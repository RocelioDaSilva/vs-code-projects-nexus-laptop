## What Petroleum Engineering Is

Petroleum engineering is the applied science of finding, developing, and producing oil and gas from underground reservoirs. It sits at the intersection of geology, physics, chemistry, mechanical engineering, and economics. No other engineering degree gives you this combination.

Your ISPTEC licenciatura covers five technical pillars:

```
PETROLEUM ENGINEERING
│
├── SUBSURFACE (underground side)
│   ├── Geology / Geoscience interface
│   ├── Reservoir Engineering
│   └── Formation Evaluation
│
├── WELLS (the connection)
│   ├── Drilling Engineering
│   ├── Well Completion Engineering
│   └── Well Integrity Management
│
├── PRODUCTION (surface side)
│   ├── Production Engineering
│   ├── Facilities / FPSO Engineering
│   └── Artificial Lift
│
├── FLOW AND TRANSPORT
│   ├── Multiphase Flow (Escoamento)
│   ├── Flow Assurance
│   └── Gas Engineering
│
└── INTEGRATING DISCIPLINES
    ├── Field Development Planning
    ├── Petroleum Economics
    └── HSE / Regulatory
```

Every role in the industry lives somewhere on this map.

## How the Disciplines Interconnect

This is the most important thing ISPTEC does not explicitly teach you: how the disciplines form a system.

```
RESERVOIR                 WELLS                  SURFACE / SUBSEA
──────────                ─────                  ────────────────
What's in the rock?  →   How to reach it?   →   How to process it?
         │                     │                       │
         ▼                     ▼                       ▼
  ER I & II               Eng. Poços            Sistemas Produção
  Simulação               Completação           Sistemas Marítimos
  Avaliação Form.         Fluidos Perf.         Elevação de Petróleo
         │                     │                       │
         └────────────┬─────────────────────────┘
                      │
                      ▼
              FIELD DEVELOPMENT PLAN
              (All disciplines meet here)
                      │
                      ▼
             PETROLEUM ECONOMICS
             (Tells us if it's worth doing)
```

**The key insight:** No discipline works alone. The reservoir engineer's forecast feeds the production engineer's tubing design, which feeds the facilities engineer's separator sizing, which feeds the economist's cash flow model. You need to understand your own discipline deeply AND understand how it connects to the adjacent ones.

## Career Progression Within Each Discipline

Your career is not a single job. It is a progression through increasing technical depth and responsibility. Here is how each major discipline progresses:

### Reservoir Engineering Progression
```
Year 1-3:   Junior Reservoir Engineer
            → Run static model updates, volumetric calculations
            → Support decline curve analysis
            → Prepare production reports

Year 3-6:   Reservoir Engineer
            → Lead reservoir studies for 2-3 wells
            → Run dynamic simulations (Eclipse/CMG)
            → Participate in reserves certification

Year 6-10:  Senior Reservoir Engineer
            → Lead full field studies
            → Chair reserves estimates for SEC/SPE-PRMS
            → Design IOR/EOR programs

Year 10+:   Lead / Principal Reservoir Engineer
            → Field Development Plan lead
            → Subsurface team lead
            → Technical authority for reserves
```

### Drilling Engineering Progression
```
Year 1-3:   Drilling Engineer / Field Engineer (Service Co)
            → Well site engineering, mud logging, MWD data
            → Cementing/perforating service on rig

Year 3-6:   Drilling Engineer (Operator side)
            → Plan and execute 2-4 wells/year
            → Manage well costs, KPI tracking

Year 6-10:  Senior Drilling Engineer
            → Lead complex well programs (HP/HT, extended reach, sub-salt)
            → Well engineering lead for a development campaign

Year 10+:   Drilling Superintendent / Manager
            → Manage rig contracts
            → Oversee 10-15 wells/year per block
```

### Production Engineering Progression
```
Year 1-3:   Production Engineer / FPSO Field Engineer
            → Daily production monitoring, well test analysis
            → Gas lift optimization, ESP monitoring

Year 3-6:   Production Engineer
            → Lead well performance campaigns
            → Artificial lift optimisation studies
            → FPSO production debottlenecking

Year 6-10:  Senior Production Engineer
            → Integrated production model (IPM) lead
            → Production optimization campaign leader
            → Artificial lift strategy for a block

Year 10+:   Production Superintendent / Manager
            → Block-level production strategy
            → Interface with reservoir and drilling teams
```

## The Strategic Career Choice: Operator vs Service Company

Most ISPTEC graduates face this choice first. Here is how to think about it:

| Dimension | Operator Side | Service Company Side |
|-----------|--------------|---------------------|
| First job type | Graduate trainee / analyst | Field engineer / technical specialist |
| Learning speed | Slower (more process-driven) | Faster (high volume of wells/jobs) |
| Technical depth | Broad across disciplines | Deep in one service line |
| Pay (entry) | $2,000–3,500/month | $2,500–4,000/month |
| Offshore time | Varies (some office-only) | Often mandatory offshore rotations |
| Career ceiling | Very high (GM, VP) | High (Principal Engineer, Regional VP) |
| Angola hiring | Competitive, fewer roles | More frequent hiring, local content rules |
| ISPTEC advantage | ANPG local content preference | Strong for technical roles |

**Recommendation:** If you want the offshore FIFO life (tseluca), start at a service company. If you want to be the engineer who decides what gets drilled and why, aim for the operator side from day one — but it may take a service company stint first.

## The Three Skills No ISPTEC Subject Teaches You

These are the skills that separate good engineers from great ones — and ISPTEC does not have a course for them:

### 1. Engineering Economics and Decision Making Under Uncertainty
Your Engenharia Económica course teaches NPV. Industry requires you to build a full probabilistic economic model with P10/P50/P90 ranges, Monte Carlo simulation, and sensitivity analysis. Learn this on your own — it is what enables you to argue for your own project.

**Self-study:** Mian's "Project Economics and Decision Analysis" Vol. 1 and 2. SPE Economics courses (free as student member).

### 2. Integrated Asset Modelling
Individual disciplines are taught in isolation. Industry requires you to connect: reservoir model → well model → flowline model → FPSO model into one integrated system. Learn Prosper and GAP (from KAPPA or Petroleum Experts).

**Self-study:** Petroleum Experts RESOLVE/Prosper tutorials (free 30-day trial on their website). SPE Applied Technology Workshops on Integrated Asset Modelling.

### 3. Subsurface Uncertainty and Communication
Being a great engineer is useless if you cannot explain your work to management, finance teams, or operations. Communicating uncertainty ("our P50 reserves are 120 MMbbl but the range is 80–180 MMbbl") is a professional skill.

**Self-study:** SPE papers on Uncertainty Communication. Any course on technical presentations. Practice with your supervisor — always present your assumptions, not just your answer.

## The Discipline You Should Start Building Before You Graduate

Given Angola's market in 2026, the fastest path to your first job combines:

1. **Your core discipline deep-dive** (choose one from the tseluca paths)
2. **Basic reservoir engineering proficiency** (even if you pick drilling or production — you need to understand the subsurface)
3. **At least one software tool** (Eclipse, CMG, Prosper, or Petrel — even at student level)
4. **Python/data basics** (even a 10-hour Python course makes you stand out)
5. **Well control certification** (IWCF Level 1 — opens doors to every rig-based role)
