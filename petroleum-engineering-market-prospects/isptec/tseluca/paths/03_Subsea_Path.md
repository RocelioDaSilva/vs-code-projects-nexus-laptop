# Path: Subsea Engineer — From ISPTEC to Construction Vessel

> **Time to first offshore rotation:** 18–36 months post-graduation
> **Typical entry company:** TechnipFMC, Subsea 7, Oceaneering, Saipem, or PAENAL (Tier 3 entry)
> **Physical environment:** Construction/installation vessel, DSV (Diving Support Vessel), or FPSO (integrity role)
> **Schedule:** Project-based (weeks to months at sea) or 28/28

---

## What Subsea Engineers Actually Do

Angola's deepwater fields don't produce from a drillship or FPSO by magic. They produce through an elaborate underwater infrastructure: dozens of subsea wellheads on the seabed at 500–2,500m water depth, connected by umbilicals (control lines), flexible risers (oil/gas transport), and flowlines to the FPSO above.

This entire underwater infrastructure — designed, installed, and maintained over 20–30 year field lifetimes — is the domain of the subsea engineer.

**Three major specializations within subsea engineering:**

### 1. Subsea Design Engineer (FEED / Detail Engineering)

Primarily onshore-based (Luanda or HQ). Works on:
- SURF design: sizing flowlines, risers, umbilicals for the production capacity
- Subsea tree and manifold specifications
- Riser analysis: fatigue, vortex-induced vibration, in-place analysis
- Interface management between subsea and topside (FPSO) systems
- Piping design, cathodic protection design, material selection

### 2. Subsea Installation Engineer

Goes offshore during installation campaigns. Works on:
- Planning and executing the offshore installation of subsea infrastructure
- Vessel operations: J-lay, S-lay, reel-lay for pipelines; crane lifts for manifolds and trees
- ROV operations management during installation
- Commissioning: pressure testing, flushing, hydraulic testing of installed systems

### 3. Subsea Integrity / Operations Engineer

Typically based on an FPSO or in the operator's onshore office. Works on:
- Regular ROV inspection campaigns of installed infrastructure
- Anomaly management: corrosion, VIV damage, anode depletion, riser wear
- Subsea equipment interventions: valve actuations, pig launchers, tree maintenance
- Production chemistry: managing scale, corrosion, wax/asphaltene deposition in flowlines

## Angola's Subsea Infrastructure Scale

Understanding the scale of what exists helps you understand why subsea engineers are continuously employed in Angola:

- **Block 15 (Kizomba):** ~40 subsea wells connected via multiple flowlines and risers to 3 FPSOs. Installed from 2003 to present. Integrity work ongoing.
- **Block 17 (Girassol, Dalia, Pazflor, CLOV):** Over 80 subsea wells across 4 FPSOs in water depths of 600–1,500m. Over 500 km of subsea infrastructure.
- **Block 32 (Kaombo):** 2 FPSOs with 59 wells in ultra-deepwater 1,200–2,000m. Installed by Saipem's Castorone pipelay vessel.
- **Block 31 (PSVM):** 4 FPSOs operated by bp in 2,000m+ water depth — one of the deepest developments in Africa.

Every one of these blocks requires continuous subsea integrity management and will require subsea intervention, flowline replacements, and tie-in of infill wells over the next 20 years.

## The Subsea Career Ladder

```
Year 0–2: Graduate Subsea Engineer / Subsea Engineer Trainee
  └── Learning SURF design basics, equipment familiarization
  └── Typically onshore initially, occasional offshore visits
  └── May start in Tier 3: fabrication yard (PAENAL, Sonamet) for equipment exposure

Year 2–5: Subsea Engineer (Installation or Integrity)
  └── Installation: offshore campaigns of 2–12 weeks, design and planning onshore
  └── Integrity: offshore ROV inspection trips, anomaly reporting
  └── Building specialization (SURF design, riser analysis, or inspection)

Year 5–8: Senior Subsea Engineer
  └── Lead engineer on installation campaigns
  └── Technical authority for integrity assessments
  └── Mentoring junior engineers

Year 8–12: Subsea Lead / Principal Engineer
  └── End-to-end responsibility for a subsea system
  └── Operators: $12,000–18,000+/month
  └── EPC contractors: $8,000–14,000+/month
```

## Entry Routes: How to Actually Get In

### Route A: PAENAL / Sonamet (Tier 3 Fabrication Yard — Recommended First Step)

PAENAL (Porto Amboim Naval Fabrication Yard) and Sonamet (Lobito Fabrication Yard) fabricate subsea structures: pipeline end terminations, manifolds, umbilical termination assemblies, suction anchors. Working here gives you:

- Hands-on familiarity with subsea hardware before you ever go offshore
- Understanding of welding, NDT, pressure testing, and dimensional inspection — exactly what you need for an installation or integrity role
- Professional references from the subsea contractors who commission the fabricated equipment

**How to enter:** Apply directly via PAENAL or Sonamet websites. The fabrication engineer or inspection engineer roles are realistic targets for a fresh ISPTEC graduate.

### Route B: Oceaneering Angola

Oceaneering operates ROVs (Remotely Operated Vehicles) on all major deepwater projects in Angola. Their ROV Technician and ROV Supervisor roles are the most direct offshore subsea positions available to entry-level candidates.

**ROV career reality:** You start as an ROV Technician — maintaining and operating the ROV on the vessel. It is technical, hands-on work. The transition to ROV Pilot, then Senior ROV Pilot, opens into project management and engineering roles. Many subsea integrity engineers started as ROV technicians.

### Route C: TechnipFMC Angola

TechnipFMC designs and manufactures subsea trees, manifolds, and SURF equipment, and also installs it via their installation vessels. Their Angola operations have graduate positions, though they are competitive. TechnipFMC's subsea tree manufacturing gives you an unparalleled understanding of subsea equipment — starting in their engineering or project management team is an excellent entry.

### Route D: Subsea 7 Angola

Subsea 7 is one of the world's largest SURF installation contractors. Their Angola operations include EPCI (Engineering, Procurement, Construction, Installation) contracts for all major deepwater blocks. They hire project engineers, installation engineers, and operations engineers.

## The SURF Universe: What You Must Know

SURF = **S**ubsea **U**mbilicals, **R**isers, and **F**lowlines

**Flowlines:** Transport produced fluids (oil, gas, water) from subsea wellheads to the FPSO. May be rigid (carbon steel pipe) or flexible (unbonded flexible pipe). Key design considerations: internal pressure, thermal insulation (flow assurance), burial depth, expansion loops.

**Risers:** Connect the flowlines on the seabed to the FPSO above. Angola typically uses **flexible risers** (in lazy-wave or steep-wave configurations to manage vessel motion). The riser must handle:
- Pressure: up to 700 bar for some deepwater wells
- Temperature: production fluid at 60–100°C
- Dynamic loads: vessel motion in swells
- Fatigue: millions of cycles over 20-year life

**Umbilicals:** Multi-core conduits that carry control signals, hydraulic power, and chemical injection from the FPSO down to subsea trees and manifolds. An umbilical for a deepwater well may contain:
- Hydraulic lines (open/close tree valves)
- Electrical cables (signal, ESP power)
- Chemical injection lines (scale, hydrate, corrosion inhibitors)
- Fiber optic cables (data)

**Subsea Tree:** The wellhead valve assembly on the seabed. Horizontal or vertical tree configurations. Controls flow from the well; provides safety barrier; houses the SCSSV (subsurface safety valve). You must be able to explain a tree schematic.

**Manifold:** Where multiple wells connect to a common gathering point before going to the FPSO. Simplifies piping. Allows flow routing flexibility.

## Technical Interview Preparation: 15 Questions

1. What is a SURF system? Describe each component.
2. What is a flexible riser? What are the structural layers of an unbonded flexible pipe?
3. What is a lazy-wave riser configuration and why is it used?
4. What is a subsea tree? What is the difference between a vertical and horizontal tree?
5. What is an umbilical? What does it contain?
6. What is flow assurance? What are the main flow assurance threats?
7. What is a hydrate and how do you prevent hydrate formation?
8. What is VIV (vortex-induced vibration) and why does it matter for risers and pipelines?
9. What is cathodic protection? How does a sacrificial anode work?
10. What is an ROV? What are the typical work classes (observation class vs work class)?
11. What is the difference between pipe lay methods: J-lay, S-lay, and reel-lay?
12. What is a pressure test? What is the difference between hydrostatic testing and pneumatic testing?
13. What is a pipeline pigging operation? Why do you pig a pipeline?
14. What is IMCA and why is it important for subsea marine contractors?
15. What is a Subsea Control Module (SCM) and how does it receive commands from the FPSO?
