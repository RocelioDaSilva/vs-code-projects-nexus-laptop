# The Offshore Reality — What Your First Rotation Actually Looks Like

> Everything in this file is based on real offshore Angola operations. Not job descriptions. Not brochures. This is what the first day, first rotation, and first year look like from the inside.

---

## Before You Go: Mobilization

**Where it starts: [SONILS](../../data/company-directory/sonangol.md) Base, Avenida 4 de Fevereiro, Luanda**

[SONILS](../../data/company-directory/sonangol.md) (Sociedade Nacional Industrial e de Serviços de Petroleo) is the logistics backbone of Angola's offshore industry. Almost every offshore rotation passes through their base.

**Morning of mobilization:**
1. **Medical check** — Blood pressure, eye check, verify your offshore medical certificate is current. If BP is above 160/100 on that morning, you are demobilized. Go back. Try again next rotation.
2. **Breathalyzer test** — Zero tolerance. 0.00 mg/L required. Positive result = demobilized, reported to your employer.
3. **Baggage inspection** — No prohibited items: no alcohol, no weapons, limited medication (declared medications require documentation)
4. **Safety briefing** — Emergency procedures for the installation you're going to
5. **Issued your PPE** — Hard hat, coveralls, safety glasses, work boots (some companies issue these; others require you to bring your own)
6. **Manifest check** — Your name must be on the passenger manifest, [BOSIET](../../docs/glossary.md) certificate verified, medical certificate verified

**If anything is missing or expired, you do not fly.** This is not negotiable and no exception exists.

## The Helicopter Flight

**Aircraft:** Typically a Sikorsky S-76 (medium-range, 12 passengers) or AS332 Super Puma (larger, longer range)

**Duration:** Approximately 90 minutes to 2.5 hours depending on the block. Block 17 is approximately 250 km offshore — roughly 2 hours from Luanda's offshore heliport (Aeroporto Internacional 4 de Fevereiro dedicated helipad area).

**The experience:**
- You wear a survival suit (immersion suit) for the entire flight — it's warm and uncomfortable
- You carry your personal survival beacon (EPIRB) — attached to the suit
- You carry your inflatable life preserver (SOLAS-approved)
- The flight is usually quiet — experienced hands sleep immediately
- Landing: the helideck is typically at the stern or bow of the [FPSO](../../docs/glossary.md)/drillship. The helideck operator confirms deck is clear. The helicopter settles. You unbuckle when told.
- After landing: helideck operator guides you away from the rotors. You are offshore.

## Your First 6 Hours Offshore

**Step 1: Induction**  
Every installation runs a mandatory arrival induction. Typically 2–4 hours with your Offshore Installation Manager (OIM) or HSE Officer. Covers:
- Muster station (where you go in emergencies — memorize your station)
- Emergency shutdown procedure (ESD): what the alarms mean
- Permit to Work (PTW) system: you cannot touch any equipment without an authorized PTW
- Your cabin assignment: where you sleep
- Meal times: typically 06:00 / 12:00 / 18:00 / 24:00 for 24-hour operations

**Step 2: Meeting your supervisor**  
You report to your direct supervisor — the senior engineer you're working with. They will brief you on the current operation status and your role for this rotation.

**Step 3: Shift assignment**  
Offshore runs 12-hour shifts, 24 hours a day. You'll be assigned either:
- **Day shift:** 06:00 – 18:00
- **Night shift:** 18:00 – 06:00

You work the same shift for your entire 28-day rotation. Night shift is brutal for the first week. Your body adjusts.

## The 28/28 Rotation Schedule

**28 days ON, 28 days OFF.** This is the standard Angola deepwater rotation.

What "28 days ON" means:
- You live on the installation for 28 consecutive days
- Every meal is provided (galley — the offshore cafeteria, open 24 hours)
- Every bed is provided (cabin — typically shared 2-person for junior staff, single for senior)
- All transport is provided (helicopter to/from Luanda)
- You work 12 hours every single day — no weekends, no days off
- Total working hours per rotation: 28 × 12 = **336 hours** in 28 days

What "28 days OFF" means:
- You are home. Completely. No emails (unless you choose to engage).
- No salary reduction — your monthly salary covers both on and off periods.
- Use this time: family, health, education, language courses, professional development.

**The financial reality:**  
Your monthly salary is paid for 12 months of the year. But you only work 6 months (6 rotations × 28 days on). You are earning a full salary for 6 months of work. Plus offshore allowances on top.

Many engineers use the 28 days off to take online courses (Coursera, edX), study for certifications, or build businesses. The discipline to use your off-days productively determines how fast you advance.

## A Typical 12-Hour Shift (Drilling Engineer Example)

**06:00** — Wake up. Galley. Eat. Brief your colleague who's finishing the night shift.  
**06:30** — Shift handover at the driller's console. Review the IADC report from night shift. What happened? Any kicks? Any stuck pipe? What's the bit depth? What's the current mud weight?  
**07:00** — Active drilling begins. Monitor: WOB, RPM, ROP, pump pressure, ECD, pit volumes (CRITICAL — rising pit volume means a kick is forming)  
**09:00** — Bit at next casing point approaching — review casing setting depth decision with Drilling Supervisor  
**10:30** — Mud engineer reports on mud properties. Review with mud logger.  
**12:00** — Lunch at galley. 30 minutes.  
**12:30** — Back on drill floor.  
**14:00** — Tripping pipe (pulling drill string out to change bit). Different monitoring requirements. Measure hole fill. Fill stat.  
**16:00** — Write your section of the IADC daily report. This accounts for every 15 minutes of rig time during your shift. Non-productive time categories: waiting on weather, stuck pipe, equipment failure, wellbore stability issues.  
**18:00** — Shift handover to night shift engineer. Brief them on everything that happened. Any anomalies, any upcoming decisions they need to make.  
**18:30** — Dinner. Done.  
**19:00–22:00** — Free time. TV room, gym (most FPSOs and drillships have a gym), Wi-Fi (slow but present via RigNet satellite), call family.  
**22:00** — Sleep.

**Repeat for 28 days.**

## Life Offshore — The Details Nobody Puts in Job Descriptions

### The Cabin

Junior staff typically share a 2-person cabin (think: a small ship cabin). You have a bunk bed, a small desk, a wardrobe, a shared bathroom. On newer FPSOs (Kaombo, Agogo) the accommodations are modern. On older FPSOs (Dalia, Girassol), less so.

**Key rule:** Day shift sleeps while night shift works, and vice versa. **Noise discipline is taken seriously** — banging around in the cabin at 06:00 when your roommate is sleeping is not acceptable.

### Food (The Galley)

The galley is the best part of offshore life. Catering companies (Newrest, Sodexo) serve 4 meals per day. Variety is usually good — local Angolan dishes alongside international food. Coffee and tea are always available. Water and juice are free. Weight gain in the first rotation is common.

### Connectivity

Most FPSOs and drillships have **RigNet or KVH satellite Wi-Fi**. Speed is limited (~1–5 Mbps shared across the installation). Streaming is often blocked. WhatsApp and voice calls work. Email works. You can video-call your family.

**Important:** There is no phone signal offshore. The only communication is via the satellite system. Plan for this with your family before your first rotation.

### Mental Health and Loneliness

Nobody talks about this enough. 28 days offshore is isolating. You miss birthdays. You miss events. You cannot be there for family emergencies in any immediate way.

**What experienced engineers do:**
- Establish a daily call routine with family before leaving (e.g., "I will call at 19:30 every day")
- Use off-shift time constructively — gym, study, music, reading
- Build relationships with colleagues on the installation — the offshore community is tight
- Know the signs of isolation fatigue: irritability, poor sleep, disengagement. Report to the Medic or OIM if it becomes serious.

Offshore installations typically have a **Company Medic** onboard (a trained paramedic/EMT at minimum, sometimes a doctor on larger installations). Mental health is a legitimate reason to seek their help.

### The Unofficial Hierarchy

On every offshore installation there is a clear social hierarchy that your org chart won't show you:
1. **The Driller** (for drillships) or **the Control Room Operator** (for FPSOs) — runs the floor. Enormous practical authority.
2. **The Medic** — universally respected. Can demobilize anyone.
3. **The Catering team** — the people you want on your good side. They control your meals.

Respect the experienced hands — the senior technicians, the drill floor crew, the deck operators — regardless of their formal rank. They know things that your degree will never teach you. Listen more than you speak in your first six months.

## The Money (Realistic Numbers)

Offshore engineers in Angola earn **total compensation** = base salary + offshore allowance (per diem) + sometimes rotation bonus.

| Level | Base Salary | Offshore Allowance | Total (28 days on) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Trainee / Tier 3 | $800–1,500/mo | $20–50/day | $1,360–2,900/mo |
| Junior Engineer (Tier 2) | $2,500–4,000/mo | $80–150/day | $4,740–8,200/mo |
| Engineer (Tier 2) | $4,000–6,000/mo | $100–200/day | $6,800–11,600/mo |
| Senior Engineer (Tier 2) | $6,000–9,000/mo | $150–300/day | $10,200–17,400/mo |
| Supervisor/Operator (Tier 1) | $8,000–15,000/mo | $200–400/day | $13,600–26,200/mo |

*Note: On-rotation, all accommodation and food are paid by the employer. Your salary is essentially pure take-home. Many engineers save 60–70% of their income during working rotations.*

**Tax reality:** Angola taxes petroleum sector income. Verify your specific situation with a local accountant. Expat engineers often have tax structuring in their home country as well. As an Angolan engineer, consult an Angola-certified tax advisor early in your career.

## What Changes in You

Every engineer who does a year of rotations describes the same phenomenon: **your relationship with time changes.**

When you're offshore, time is compressed and structured. 12 hours on, 12 hours off. No gray area. When you're home for 28 days, time expands. Some engineers struggle to use off-days productively. The most successful ones treat off-days as deliberately as on-days.

Your physical stamina also changes. 12-hour shifts in an industrial environment — heat, noise, physical movement — builds a resilience that office work never does.

And your technical knowledge compounds. After 6 rotations, you have roughly **2,000 hours of real-time operational exposure**. No lecture theatre, no textbook, no simulator approaches that. It is the best education in petroleum engineering you can receive.
