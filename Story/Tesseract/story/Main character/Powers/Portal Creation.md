In the framework of your *Tesseract* universe, **Portal Creation** is the art of spatial data stitching. It is not a magical rip in the air, but a controlled, sustained **wormhole**—a tunnel of edited spacetime that creates a continuous data-path between two non-adjacent points within the same three-dimensional reality.

Here is its breakdown, grounded in your rules of information-based reality.

### Core Definition: The Sustained Spatial Bridge
Portal Creation is the power to define two points in local space (Point A and Point B), forcefully edit the relational data between them so that they are "adjacent," and maintain that edited state as a stable tunnel.

*   **It's a Tunnel, Not a Teleport:** Unlike teleportation (which is a deletion/rewrite of *self*), a portal is a rewrite of *space itself*. It creates a new, temporary rule: "The space within this ring is physically contiguous with the space within that distant ring."
*   **A Programmed Wormhole:** You are not just tearing a hole; you are writing a stable **Einstein-Rosen bridge** protocol into reality's local data, bending the coordinate mesh. This requires constant energy to keep the "bug" in spacetime from being corrected by the universe's checking system.

### Key Mechanics & Parameters

1.  **The Anchor as a Stabilizer:**
    *   Creating a portal requires an initial, significant expenditure of **Coins** to override local physics.
    *   **Maintaining it is a continuous drain.** The longer the portal is open, or the larger it is, the more Coins it consumes per second. This makes portal use a matter of budget and efficiency.

2.  **Placement Rules:**
    *   **Line of Sight (Optional but Cheaper):** The easiest, lowest-cost method is to visually or sensorially lock onto a destination. This provides clear coordinate data for the bridge.
    *   **Coordinate Programming (Expensive & Risky):** You can open a portal to a location you know *mathematically* (e.g., "35.2km due north, 2km up"), but this requires precise data input. Any error risks the portal terminus appearing inside matter, causing immediate catastrophic **data collision**.
    *   **Portal Surfaces:** They must be created on stable, relatively flat surfaces (a wall, the ground, a large object). Creating one in mid-air is exponentially harder, as you have to define and stabilize a "frame" of nothingness.

3.  **Portal Properties (User-Defined on Creation):**
    *   **Size:** From a pinhole to a warehouse door. Larger size = exponentially higher cost.
    *   **Orientation:** Gravity's direction can be set independently for each terminus. You can create a portal on the floor that leads to a vertical wall, requiring users to step "out" sideways.
    *   **Persistence:** Can it be left open? Does it close after one pass? Is it one-way or two-way? Each condition adds complexity (cost) to the code sustaining it.

### Strategic Uses & Advantages (Over Teleportation)
This is where Portal Creation carves its niche. It is a **team and strategy tool**.

*   **Logistics & Transport:** It can move **other people, objects, and energy**. An army, a vehicle, or a stream of water can pass through. This makes it invaluable for the missions from the **Forgotten Shores**, which likely involve retrieving things or people.
*   **Tactical Field Control:** You can cut an enemy's attack in half by placing a portal in front of it, with the exit portal pointing back at them. You can redirect environmental hazards, create instant shields, or set up endless falling traps.
*   **Scouting & Escape Routes:** Create a small viewing portal to peek into a secure room before entering. Create a guaranteed exit route for a team.

### The Risks & Limitations

1.  **The Data Shear Danger:** The "edge" of a portal is a plane of violently edited spacetime data. If the portal's stability wavers (from Coin exhaustion or an external shock), this edge can become a **data-shear field**. Anything touching it may be sliced cleanly, or worse, have its data partially scrambled—a living thing could be fused with the floor or have sections of its biology translated into inert stone.
2.  **The Link is Vulnerable:** The tunnel itself is a visible, tangible anomaly. It can be attacked or disrupted by powers that affect data or spacetime, potentially causing a violent collapse.
3.  **Anchor Tether:** Maintaining a portal is a constant, draining link. It requires a fraction of the user's focus and resources at all times, making it dangerous to use in conjunction with other costly powers or if the user is already low on Coins.

### Why It Fits C-43 and Your Story

1.  **The Contrast to Teleportation:** If teleportation is a solitary, selfish power, **Portal Creation is a connective, potentially selfless one.** It reflects a choice to enable others, at greater personal cost and risk. This creates a moral and tactical axis for C-43: does he invest in the power that only saves himself, or the one that could save others but leaves him more exposed?
2.  **The Mission Tool:** As a dimensional operative completing tasks, portals are the ultimate utility tool for extraction, insertion, and manipulation of objectives. It's the power of a problem-solver, not just a fighter.
3.  **A Microcosm of Travel:** Creating a portal is a small-scale, intra-universe version of the grander interdimensional travel he performs. It's a rehearsal in bending the rules of locality, reinforcing the theme that all his powers are variations on data manipulation.
4.  **Visual Storytelling:** Portals offer incredible cinematic potential—the shimmering disc of warped space, the unsettling view of a distant location up close, the green-tinged data-scar of a closing rift. It visually represents the "seams" in reality.

**In summary, in your story, Portal Creation is the sustained, costly editing of spatial locality data to form a stable bridge between two points. It is the strategic, team-oriented counterpart to teleportation, embodying connection and utility, but at the price of constant resource drain and the peril of catastrophic spatial shear.**
