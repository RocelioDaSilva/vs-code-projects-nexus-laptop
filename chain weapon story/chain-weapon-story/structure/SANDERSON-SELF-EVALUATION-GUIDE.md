## PART 1: CORE PREMISE EVALUATION

### What Story Are You Telling? (Lecture 8, 88)

Your opening pages make promises. A reader who picks up your book expects a certain tone, pacing, and type of ending. **The #1 reason for reader dissatisfaction: the ending doesn't match the opening's promises.**

#### YOUR STORY'S PROMISED TONE & GENRE

Read your opening chapter (Tavern Opening, Age 5) and ask:

**What tone does this establish?**
- ✓ **Pragmatic, observational**: Aisen watches. Notices patterns. Not flowery or high-fantasy.
- ✓ **Grounded in ordinary**: Bar, regulars, daily rhythms. Not magical spectacle.
- ✓ **Low-stakes warmth opening**: This is safe before it breaks.
- ⚠️ **RISK**: If opening reads as cozy slice-of-life but Act III is grimdark sacrifice, readers will feel betrayed.

**ACTION ITEM—Draft Checklist**:
- [ ] First 5 pages establish: Aisen is observant, ordinary, brave but not chosen.
- [ ] Tone is **clear-eyed pragmatism**, not cynicism.
- [ ] Reader should think "this kid will survive through intelligence, not destiny."
- [ ] Small danger (robbery, hardship) feels *real* at tavern-opening scale, not comedic.

**PROMISE TEST**: 
- Does your opening feel like a story about *how ordinary people problem-solve*? ✓ YES
- Or does it feel like a story about *a chosen one finding their power*? → Misalignment
- **Expected ending match**: Aisen dies having saved people through synthesis and sacrifice. This matches if opening promised: "ordinary person, synthesis over power, protecting others costs."

#### YOUR STORY'S UNIQUE POSITIONING ("Strange Attractor")

Sanderson emphasizes: What mashup of familiar and unfamiliar makes your story pitch-able?

**Your Current Positioning**:
- Familiar: Chosen-one trope frame (Academy, mentors, magic)
- Unfamiliar: Rejects chosen-one (he's NOT special; just intelligent)
- Familiar: War and sacrifice
- Unfamiliar: Death reaches him *because* of solutions he creates
- Familiar: Found family (allies, companions)
- Unfamiliar: Explicit focus on systems and prejudice, not just personal conflict

**One-Liner Pitch Test**: 
*"Chain Weapon Story is [familiar reference] but [unfamiliar twist]."*

**Examples**:
- "It's Ender's Game, but the protagonist survives through synthesis instead of genius manipulation—and it costs him his life anyway."
- "It's a found-family war story, but the family is held together by problem-solving systems, not emotional bonds alone."
- "It's a magic academy story where being ordinary is your superpower, and the climax tests whether ordinary holds against immortal structures."

**ACTION ITEM—Before Drafting Act I**:
- [ ] Write your one-sentence pitch using Sanderson's formula.
- [ ] Share it with someone unfamiliar with your story. Does it intrigue them?
- [ ] Does your current story-opening set up that pitch? If not, adjust opening.

## PART 2: CHARACTER EVALUATION

### The Character Sliders (Lectures 16-21, 85)

Sanderson identifies three sliders that define how compelling a character is:

#### SLIDER 1: PROACTIVITY

**Definition**: Is the character the *engine* of the story, or just pushed by plot?

**Proactive characters drive events. Reactive characters react to events.**

**Your Aisen's Current Position**:
- **Act I**: Mostly reactive (robber comes; Harald arrives; Tarovin appears). *But*: He chooses to work with Harald; he chooses to synthesize telekinesis with combat. **Mid-reactive**
- **Act II**: More proactive (applies to Academy; makes allies; studies Covenant patterns). **Increasingly proactive**
- **Act III**: HIGHLY proactive (leads resistance research; makes strategic choices). **Proactive**

**INSIGHT**: Your story *intentionally* increases Aisen's proactivity across the arc. This is good—it mirrors his growth.

**ACTION ITEM—Scene-by-Scene Check**:
- [ ] In each major scene, ask: "Who makes the key decision—Aisen or plot circumstance?"
- [ ] Target: By midpoint of Act II, Aisen should make *most* major decisions.
- [ ] Red flag: If Aisen spends scenes passively receiving information (exposition dumps), he's too reactive.
- [ ] Fix: Make him *ask* for information, or let him discover it through action.

**Proactivity in Dialogue**:
- Reactive: "What should I do?" (asking for direction)
- Proactive: "I'm going to try this—watch for [specific thing]." (stating intent, asking for support)

---

#### SLIDER 2: LIKABILITY

**Definition**: Does the character have friends? Do they do small good deeds? Do they have a clear goal?

**WARNING**: Likability ≠ being nice. Likability = compelling. A character with clear drive and simple wants is likable even if abrasive.

**Your Aisen's Current Position**:
- ✓ Has friends: Elara, Kaelen, Amara, Rin (by Act II+)
- ✓ Does small good deeds: Protects tavern-goers, helps refugees, teaches peers
- ✓ Clear want: Understand how things work; protect people
- ✓ Humble but not weak: Respects strength, doesn't defer

**Likability Risk—"Competent But Detached"**: 
Aisen's observational nature can read as cold. Readers must see him *care*, not just think.

**ACTION ITEM—Draft Check**:
- [ ] In first appearance, show Aisen doing a small good deed (not instructed, volunteer).
- [ ] Example: Notices someone hurt; brings water without being asked.
- [ ] In early academy scenes: Show him mentoring someone weaker (costs him time).
- [ ] Red flag: If Aisen seems to calculate relationships, humans will reject him. Show warmth beneath pragmatism.

**Likability Test**: Read dialogue aloud. If someone says "This Aisen guy" without warmth, he's too distant. Adjust.

---

#### SLIDER 3: COMPETENCE

**Definition**: What is your character *uniquely* good at? This doesn't have to be physical—it can be emotional, logical, creative.

**Your Aisen's Core Competency**:
- **Primary**: Systems thinking + Pattern recognition. He reads how things work, not just surface facts.
- **Secondary**: Adaptation. When Plan A fails, he finds Plan B using available resources.
- **Tertiary**: Synthesis. He combines disparate skills (Harald's spear + Tarovin's telekinesis + hunting survival).

**Competence Display**:
- **Act I**: Should see him use pattern-recognition to solve early problems (e.g., recognizes robber's psychology; deduces what Harald needs).
- **Act II**: Should see him outthink prodigies by understanding systems they ignore.
- **Act III**: Should see him synthesize solutions that seem impossible (using chain + telekinesis + network coordination).

**ACTION ITEM—Scene Design**:
- [ ] Plan 3-5 scenes where Aisen's *unique* competence saves the day (not luck, not inherited power).
- [ ] Example: He deduces an Academy secret by analyzing daily patterns others ignore.
- [ ] Example: He builds a counter-strategy to an enemy by recognizing their system's flaw.
- [ ] Red flag: If Aisen wins through luck or borrowed power, his competence doesn't shine.

### The PROMS Method (Lecture 85)

Every character line should reveal something about the character's **P-R-O-M-S**: **Past, Relationships, Obligations, Motivations, Sensibilities**.

**Use this method during dialogue/scene writing to deepen character with every line.**

#### Example: Apply PROMS to Aisen in an Academy scene

**Scene**: Aisen and a prodigy debating strategy in a study group.

**Prodigy says**: "Let's use raw power. It's the fastest solution."

**WEAK Response** (tells nothing): "I don't think that will work."

**STRONG Response** (reveals PROMS):

"I've seen raw power fail. My mentor—" *[PAST: shows Harald shaped him]* *"—once fought someone stronger and lost. But when he taught me, he never used force alone. He read angles." [SENSIBILITIES: he thinks in physics/systems]* "Power's fast until it meets something stronger. Efficiency's boring but it compounds." *[OBLIGATIONS: he's studying because Tarovin earned it through practice]*

**Breakdown**:
- **P** (Past): Harald's lesson about defeat
- **R** (Relationships): Respects mentors who teach through practice
- **O** (Obligations): He's at Academy to honor mentors' teaching
- **M** (Motivations): He wants to find approaches that *keep working*
- **S** (Sensibilities): He sees the world as systems, not absolutes

**ACTION ITEM—During Draft**:
- [ ] Pick 5 major character moments (Aisen + Elara; Aisen + Rin; Aisen + Kaelen; Aisen + Harald; Aisen + Adversary).
- [ ] For each, write the dialogue incorporating at least 3 PROMS elements.
- [ ] Test: Can you remove the character name tag and still identify the speaker from voice?

## PART 3: PLOT STRUCTURE EVALUATION

### Identify Your Plot Types (Lectures 31-34, 88)

Most good books combine multiple plot types. Identify which your story uses, and verify each is **well-escalated**.

#### YOUR STORY'S PLOT TYPES

| Plot Type | Primary Timeline | Escalation Check | Notes |
|----------|---|---|---|
| **Travelog** | Journey across lands; Academy acceptance; war frontlines | Does geography feel consequential? | ✓ Each location changes Aisen—tavern safety → Academy exposure → war danger |
| **Mystery** | Understanding Covenant infiltration; learning Seraphine's plan; uncovering Flux mechanics | Does discovery drive plot? | ✓ Each act reveals system corruption layer by layer |

### The "Yes, But..." / "No, And..." Momentum Framework (Lecture 81)

**Goal**: Never let the story's momentum flatten. Every major attempt should have a cascading consequence.

**The Pattern**:
- Character attempts solution to problem
- Result is either:
  - **"Yes, but..."**: They succeed, but face a *larger* consequence
  - **"No, and..."**: They fail, and situation gets *worse*
- Reader keeps asking: "And then what happened?"

#### Example: Apply to Aisen's Act II Journey

**Problem**: Seraphine's Covenant has infiltrated the Academy. Aisen wants to understand the threat.

| Scene | Aisen's Attempt | Outcome Type | Consequence | Stakes Escalate? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Academy arrival | Make friends; observe patterns | Yes, but... | He finds allies BUT discovers Covenant goes deeper than he thought | ✓ Threat grows |
| Infiltration research | Identify Covenant operatives through observation | No, and... | He fails to find the leader AND accidentally exposes himself to an operative | ✓ Personal danger rises |
| Ally recruitment | Convince Elara to help him investigate | Yes, but... | She agrees BUT it puts her duty to family in conflict | ✓ Costs complexity |
| Limit-break experiment | Test synthesis magic to counter Covenant | No, and... | It works briefly BUT causes permanent mana damage worse than expected | ✓ Personal cost rises |
| Grand confrontation | Organize network to stop Flux acceleration | Yes, but... | They stop one cell BUT Seraphine accelerates timeline anyway | ✓ Moves into war phase |

**INSIGHT**: Notice that every outcome escalates either stakes, personal cost, or scope. None are "clean wins."

**ACTION ITEM—As You Draft Each Act**:
- [ ] After writing a major scene, ask: "Did the outcome escalate the stakes or complicate Aisen's options?"
- [ ] If the answer is "no, he solved it and now things are calmer," rewrite the outcome to escalate.
- [ ] Red flag: Losing momentum = flat middle. Escalation = engagement.

### Inciting Incidents at Each Act Boundary

Sanderson emphasizes: **Each act needs its own inciting incident that disrupts the character's current equilibrium.**

#### Act I Inciting Incident (Chapter 3: The Robbery)
- **Current Equilibrium**: Safe tavern life. Aisen watches adventurers; dreams of adventure but thinks he's ordinary.
- **Incident**: Robber threatens father and tavern. Aisen must choose: call for help or act.
- **New Direction**: Either he acts (building confidence) or freezes (forcing Harald's arrival as rescuer).
- **Question posed**: "Can I matter even without destiny?"

**ACTION ITEM—Draft Check**:
- [ ] Read Chapter 3. Does the robbery feel **personal and stakes-raising**?
- [ ] Does it force Aisen to act in a way he hasn't before?
- [ ] After this scene, should reader think "now I want to know what he does next"? (Not just safety returned)

#### Act II Inciting Incident (Chapter 14-15: Academy Arrival & First Covenant Encounter)
- **Current Equilibrium**: Aisen is respected locally; has mentors; has synthesized skills.
- **Incident**: At Academy, prodigies outmatch him instantly. He's invisible again—but also discovers the Academy itself is compromised.
- **New Direction**: He must survive among prodigies *and* uncover conspiracy.
- **Question posed**: "Does my synthesis-approach work against systemic corruption?"

**ACTION ITEM—Draft/Outline Check**:
- [ ] What event shocks Aisen's equilibrium at Academy start?
- [ ] Is it *personal* (something he learns about himself) or *external* (Academy's nature)?
- [ ] Sanderson recommends combining both: He discovers he's outmatched (personal) AND the Academy is infiltrated (external).
- [ ] After Act II inciting incident, reader should think "now the stakes are institutional."

#### Act III Inciting Incident (War Declaration / Flux Tear Acceleration)
- **Current Equilibrium**: Aisen has allies; has researched Covenant; is building resistance.
- **Incident**: Seraphine accelerates the Flux Tear. War becomes imminent. No more time for incremental change.
- **New Direction**: Aisen must choose: sacrifice or failure.
- **Question posed**: "Can synthesis and protection coexist with warfare?"

**ACTION ITEM—Outline/Draft Check**:
- [ ] When does Act III begin—at war declaration or at Aisen's decision to act?
- [ ] Sanderson recommends: The external war arrives, forcing Aisen's *internal* choice.
- [ ] After this incident, reader should think "now he's out of time."

## PART 4: PROSE QUALITY & MECHANICS

### The Window Pane Principle (Lectures 14, 15, 52, 83, 85)

Your prose is either a **clear window** (transparent; reader sees story) or a **stained glass window** (beautiful; reader admires the beauty), but it must be **intentional**.

**Your Narrative Style Target**: Clear window with selective stained glass.

#### Verify in Your NARRATIVE-STYLE.md:

- ✓ "Observant, pragmatic, slightly detached but caring"
- ✓ "Logical; asks questions; doesn't inflate situations"
- ✓ "Shows through action/notation not explanation"
- ✓ "Clear but not simplistic; complex ideas explained through practice"

**ACTION ITEM—Style Audit**:
- [ ] Draft 3-5 paragraphs of Act I scene.
- [ ] Highlight every word that's not essential (flowery description, telling-not-showing, exposition).
- [ ] Cut 10-15% without losing meaning.
- [ ] Read aloud. Should feel clear and direct, not clunky.

---

### Pyramid of Abstraction: Concrete vs. Abstract Language

**Sanderson Principle**: Foundation is **concrete details** (what you see, hear, feel). Top is **abstract concepts** (themes, explanations).

**WEAK EXAMPLE** (too abstract):
"The academy was impressive and made him feel small."

**STRONG EXAMPLE** (concrete base):
"The academy's entrance hall was a cathedral of black stone. Students in robes moved between shelves that reached the ceiling. Aisen's boots echoed on the floor. No one looked at him. The silence felt heavy."

**KEY RULE**: 70-80% concrete. 20-30% abstract reflection.

**ACTION ITEM—Concrete Language Audit**:
- [ ] Draft a scene (tavern, academy, battlefield).
- [ ] Count: How many sentences use concrete sensory detail? Aim for 7-8 per 10 sentences.
- [ ] Red flag: If entire paragraphs are exposition or character thought, rewrite with action/dialogue/sensory detail first.

### Dialogue Check: The MIRCO Method (Lecture 89)

Every character conversation should serve:
- **M** - Motive: Why is this character speaking? What do they want from this exchange?
- **I** - Individuality: Can you tell this character apart by voice alone?
- **R** - Realism: Does dialogue sound believable (not *real*, but convincingly real)?
- **C** - Conflict: Is there tension—even if subtle?
- **O** - Objective: What is this scene trying to accomplish in the story?

#### Example: Academy Confrontation (Aisen vs. Prodigy)

**WEAK dialogue** (fails MIRCO):
```
"I think your plan is bad," Aisen said.
"No, my plan is good," the prodigy responded.
"We should do it my way," Aisen said.
"No, mine," the prodigy said.
```
- M (Motive): Unclear. Why now? What's at stake?
- I (Individuality): They sound identical.
- R (Realism): No real person talks like this.
- C (Conflict): Stated but not shown.
- O (Objective): Not clear what we're learning.

**STRONG dialogue** (passes MIRCO):
```
"Your strategy's straightforward," Aisen said. "But look—" He gestured to the board. "—you're assuming they'll respond with force. They won't."

The prodigy's jaw tightened. "They're Covenant. Force is what they understand."

"Force is what they *want* you to believe they understand." Aisen tapped the pattern he'd outlined. "Three times, our scouts have reported 'direct assault.' Three times, it's actually been distraction for infiltration elsewhere."

[S]"You think you can read their moves better than someone with actual experience?" The prodigy's voice stayed level, but her hands clenched.

"I think I'm not invested in believing strength matters most," Aisen said quietly.
```
- M (Motive): Aisen wants to save lives by working smarter; prodigy wants to prove strength works.
- I (Individuality): Aisen is observational, patient; prodigy is direct, ego-driven.
- R (Realism): Natural rhythm, hesitations, body language.
- C (Conflict): Philosophical clash about approach.
- O (Objective): Establishes Aisen's thinking + shows why allies respect him.

**ACTION ITEM—Dialogue Draft Check**:
- [ ] Write 2-3 major dialogue scenes.
- [ ] For each, verify all 5 MIRCO elements.
- [ ] Test: Read aloud without character names. Can you identify speakers?
- [ ] Red flag: If characters sound identical, add verbal tics or patterns to differentiate.

### Dialogue Mechanics: Tags, Beats, and Attributions (Lecture 52)

**Sanderson's Rules**:
1. Put dialogue tags early (who said it, before the full speech)
2. Use beats instead of tags when possible (action + dialogue shows emotion)
3. Avoid "said bookisms" ("exclaimed," "shrieked," "ejaculated")
4. Use "said" or "asked" 90% of the time

#### Example: Fix Dialogue Tags

**WEAK** (late tags, bookisms):
```
"I need your help," Aisen pleaded desperately, his voice quavering with emotion as he looked deeply into her eyes.
```

**STRONG** (early tag, beat, emotion through action):
```
"I need your help," Aisen said.
Elara didn't look up from her map. The silence stretched. Finally: "You know what happens if I do."
"I know." He sat down uninvited. "I'm asking anyway."
```

**ACTION ITEM—Dialogue Tag Audit**:
- [ ] Draft a dialogue scene.
- [ ] Check: Are tags in first half of speech, not after?
- [ ] Replace 50% of tags with beats (action that shows emotion).
- [ ] Search for: "exclaimed," "groaned," "breathed," "uttered"—replace with "said" or action beats.

Every time a character breaks limits (magic, physical, mental), there must be **measurable, usually permanent costs**.

## PART 6: THE REVISION PROCESS (Lecture 73)

Sanderson maps revisions into 5 passes. Adapt this to your project timeline.

### 1.0 — GET IT DOWN (Discovery Draft)

**Goal**: Complete first draft without stopping.

**Your Stage**: Outline complete; ready to begin Act I writing.

**ACTION ITEMS**:
- [ ] Set daily/weekly writing targets (e.g., 2,000 words/week).
- [ ] Write Act I completely before judging.
- [ ] Note continuity issues and character inconsistencies but don't revise mid-draft.
- [ ] Track in file: `drafts/1.0-discovery-draft/`

---

### 2.0 — CONTINUITY (Fix Major Plot Holes)

**Goal**: Fix plot inconsistencies, timeline errors, character contradictions you noticed while writing.

**Your Stage**: After Act I draft complete.

**ACTION ITEMS**:
- [ ] Read through Act I. List all continuity issues.
- [ ] Example: "Aisen meets Tarovin in Chapter 6, but Chapter 4 implied he'd never studied telekinesis."
- [ ] Fix major issues; ignore prose-level changes.
- [ ] Verify timeline (Aisen's age progression matches MASTER-CHRONOLOGY.md).
- [ ] Check character consistency (does Elara act like her profile in every scene?).

---

### 3.0 — POLISH (Cut 15%, Show-Don't-Tell, Concrete Language)

**Goal**: Lean and vivid prose. Cut passive voice. Make abstract concepts concrete.

**Your Stage**: After you've drafted and continuity-fixed 2+ acts.

**ACTION ITEMS**:
- [ ] Read each scene. Highlight words that don't earn their place.
- [ ] Target: Cut 10-15% of total words (tighten without losing meaning).
- [ ] Replace passive voice: "The decision was made by Aisen" → "Aisen decided"
- [ ] Add concrete sensory details: Replace "Aisen felt afraid" with sight/sound/physical sensation.
- [ ] Use NARRATIVE-STYLE.md as guide—your voice should be clear and direct.

---

### 4.0 — ALPHA READERS & STRUCTURAL FEEDBACK

**Goal**: Get feedback from 2-3 trusted readers. Look for *patterns* in confusion.

**Your Stage**: After draft + continuity + polish complete (probably months ahead).

**ACTION ITEMS**:
- [ ] Select 2-3 alpha readers familiar with: fantasy, character-driven stories, Sanderson's work.
- [ ] Create feedback form:
  - What was your reading experience? (pace, engagement, emotional beats)
  - Which character felt most real? Why?
  - At what point did you predict the ending?
  - What confused you?
  - Did the ending match the opening's promises?
- [ ] Look for trends. If 2+ readers are confused by the same thing, it's a real problem.
- [ ] Make structural changes based on patterns.

---

### 5.0 — BETA READERS & FINAL POLISH

**Goal**: Final impression of pacing and character from fresh readers. Final line edit.

**Your Stage**: After alpha feedback incorporated (months ahead).

**ACTION ITEMS**:
