# Arc C: The Crown's Dilemma (Chapters 20B–64B)

## Summary
The Northern House is the one major institutional power that holds legal standing above the Covenant's reach — but only as long as it maintains its formal independence. Elara's father, the Northern King, is caught between three pressures: Covenant encroachment via legal and economic tools, the needs of the provincial population his house is nominally responsible for, and his own dynastic calculation about what a confrontation would cost his children. He is not corrupt and not cowardly — he is a man facing a genuine dilemma with no clean options.

Elara's arc within this: she begins as the family member who is present but non-influential, discovers she is actually the most analytically capable person in the House, and has to decide whether to use that capacity in ways her father explicitly does not authorize — and then live with what that decision means for their relationship.

## Scene Map (6 escalating beats)

### Beat 1 — PUBLIC TEST (Ch 20B)
**Scene**: Already written. Court breakfast, courier drop, Aldric Fenn's veiled warning. The dilemma is introduced: the Crown can resist the Covenant, but it will cost something. Elara's father does not yet know how much.
**Personal cost**: Elara has now been directly warned by a Covenant operative. She did not tell her father.
**Status**: Exists (20B-The-Two-Masks.md).

### Beat 2 — PRIVATE COST (Ch 24)
**Scene**: Elara uses the Inter-House Review Protocol to stall the Heresy Trial (see Arc A Beat 5). She does not tell her father she is going to do this. She tells him afterward, in a private meeting.
**Personal cost**: The Northern King is not angry — he is frightened. Not for himself, but because she used a card he might have needed later, for a different purpose, and she did not consult him. This reveals the gap: Elara has been making decisions at a level of consequence she has not disclosed. Her father did not know how active she was.
**Dialogue anchor**: "How long?" (He means: how long have you been doing this.) "Since the Academy." (She means: since I learned what the Covenant was doing in the provinces.) Long silence. Then: "We will discuss the shape of this. Not the principle — the shape."
**Status**: To draft (within Ch 24 — can be a short scene, ~800 words, in an Elara POV tag at end of chapter).

### Beat 3 — BETRAYAL CATALYST (Ch 33B)
**Scene**: Elara's POV B-chapter. She receives a formal diplomatic letter from the Covenant — not for her, addressed to her father, but the courier brings it to her by mistake (or by non-mistake — the Covenant's way of communicating that they know the actual decision-maker). The letter is an ultimatum: the Northern House will withdraw the Inter-House Review request, will cease supporting the provincial assessment network, and will formalize a cooperative arrangement with Covenant administrative representatives in two northern districts — *or* the Covenant will file a formal grievance against the Northern King's eldest son (Elara's brother) for a technical treaty violation from eight years ago that has been dormant and which everyone involved considered dead.
**Personal cost**: The eldest brother does not know the grievance exists. Elara has to decide whether to tell him, advise her father without full disclosure, or act unilaterally. She tells her father. He reads the letter for a long time. He does not fully tell the eldest son.
**Who pays**: The Northern King (the weight of a dilemma with no clean exit). The eldest son (in structural danger he doesn't know about). Elara (by knowing and by being the one who named it).
**Status**: To draft — PRIORITY SCENE. ~4,000 words.

### Beat 4 — ESCALATION (Ch 48B)
**Scene**: Elara's second B-chapter. Her father makes a partial concession — withdrawing one of the two Covenant requests, on the less critical one, in hopes of buying time. The Covenant accepts the partial concession. Then, fourteen days later, moves on the second demand anyway. The partial concession was only ever a way to peel off the easier piece and reassess.
**Personal cost**: Elara told her father partial concession was a trap. He made it anyway because the eldest son's exposure was a real risk. She had predicted this would happen. Being right is not the same as having a solution. They are both right about things that do not fix the situation.
**Dialogue anchor**: "You told me this would happen." / "Yes." / "What do I do with that?" / "I don't know. I think — the same as I would. You move to the next thing and you keep moving."
**Status**: Outline only.

### Beat 5 — FINAL NEGOTIATION (Ch 61)
**Scene**: A formal negotiation session — the Northern House, the Covenant, and two Crown representatives. Elara is officially present as a protocol liaison (non-voting). She has prepared exhaustively. The Covenant's lead negotiator is Aldric Fenn. Three offers are made across the session; each offer has a different cost structure. Elara cannot vote. She can advise. She writes three brief notes to her father during the session, passed via a protocol secretary: each note recommends a different response to a different offer, and each recommendation is correct.
**Personal cost**: Her father follows one recommendation and ignores two. The session ends without resolution but with a sixty-day extension. In the corridor afterward, he tells her: "You were right about the third offer." She says: "I know." He says: "Don't." She says: "That's also a choice."
**Status**: Outline only.

### Beat 6 — FRACTURE POINT (Ch 64B)
**Scene**: The territorial concession session. The Northern House is asked to formally cede administrative authority over two eastern border zones to a joint Covenant-Crown body. Elara's father is present. Her brother is present. The eldest brother, now aware of the treaty-violation file, accepts the concession as protection. Elara is not present — she was sent on a protocol errand that took her out of the building that morning, an errand that looks like coincidence and is not. When she returns, the concession has been signed. She reads the document. She sits with it for a long time in the anteroom. Then she goes back to work.
**Personal cost**: Elara. Not rage — something harder: the clear recognition that her father protected his son at the cost of the two border zones, and that this was a comprehensible human decision, and that she is going to have to operate for the foreseeable future in the territory where a comprehensible human decision produces a structural betrayal.
**Status**: Outline only.
