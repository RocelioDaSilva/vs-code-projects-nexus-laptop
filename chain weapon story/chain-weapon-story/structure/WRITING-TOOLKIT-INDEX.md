## THE SIX CORE DOCUMENTS

### **1. SANDERSON-FRAMEWORK-ANALYSIS.md**
**What it is**: Comprehensive diagnostic of your story against all 7 universal categories  
**Length**: ~12,000 words  
**When to use it**:
- BEFORE drafting Act I (read full document to understand gaps)
- DURING planning phase (identify priorities 1-5)
- DURING revision (check each category's checklist)

**What it answers**:
- "What are my story's strengths?" ✓ (Character arcs, network narrative, thematic depth)
- "What needs development?" ✓ (Dialogue distinctness, emotional pacing, climactic clarity)
- "What should I prioritize?" ✓ (Character voice bank, pacing map, climax preparation)

**Key sections**:
- 7 categories with sub-categories (character, plot, structure, theme, setting, prose, emotion)
- Strength/Gap/Recommendation format for each
- Actionable next steps in priority order
- Quick reference checklist

- "How does [Character] sound in dialogue?" ✓ (Speech patterns, catchphrases, vocabulary)

**Key sections**:

## HOW TO USE THE TOOLKIT: PHASE BY PHASE

### **PHASE 1: PRE-DRAFT PLANNING (Week 1)**

**Goal**: Understand your story's strengths, gaps, and priorities

**Documents to read**:
1. **SANDERSON-FRAMEWORK-ANALYSIS.md** — Full read (2-3 hours)
   - Highlight "Gaps" and "Recommendations" sections
   - Note which categories feel strongest vs. weakest
   - Read "ACTIONABLE NEXT STEPS" section carefully

2. **BASTARD-MASTER-SANDERSON-INTEGRATION.md** — Full read (1-2 hours)
   - Understand how your story's identity integrates with craft principles
   - Review "Bastard Master Prose Style" section
   - Note the integration matrix

**Output**:
- [ ] Create **Word count target** (by chapter or act)
- [ ] Create **Pacing Map** (estimate chapter lengths; see framework analysis for guidance)
- [ ] Create **Character Voice Guide** (pull patterns from CHARACTER-VOICE-BANK; reference during drafting)
- [ ] Create **World Rules Checklist** (confirm magical system consistency)

---

### **PHASE 2: ACT I DRAFTING (Weeks 2-6)**

**Goal**: Write Chapters 1-12 with clear character voice and efficient pacing

**Documents to reference**:
1. **SCENE-DRAFT-PROMPTS.md** — Your chapter-by-chapter outline (open while writing each chapter)
2. **CHARACTER-VOICE-BANK.md** — Check before writing dialogue
   - Before each character speaks, reference their section
   - After writing dialogue, test: could readers identify speaker without name?

3. **BASTARD-MASTER-SANDERSON-INTEGRATION.md** — Section 7 (Chapter Structure Audit)
   - After drafting each chapter, ask: "Does this chapter pass the four-question test?"
   - If NO to 3+ questions, reconsider or merge with another chapter

**Key focus areas from framework**:
- Character distinctness (each character's voice recognizable)
- Plot cause & effect (each scene flows from previous; no coincidences)
- Pacing (Act I slowest; chapters mostly 3,000-5,000 words; use vignettes for downtime)
- Setting sensory details (2-3 details per scene ground reader in place)

**Daily checklist**:
- [ ] Dialogue feels character-specific (read aloud)
- [ ] Scene advances plot OR reveals character (no fluff)
- [ ] Setting grounded in sensory detail
- [ ] Pacing matches content (action = faster; quiet = allowed to breathe)

---

### **PHASE 3: ACT II DRAFTING (Weeks 7-14)**

**Goal**: Write Chapters 13-25 with escalating tension and thematic integration

**Documents to reference**:
1. **SCENE-DRAFT-PROMPTS.md** — Academy chapters, conspiracy revelation, midpoint shift
2. **CHARACTER-VOICE-BANK.md** — Continue testing dialogue distinctness
3. **SANDERSON-FRAMEWORK-ANALYSIS.md** — Section 3 (STRUCTURE)
   - Focus on "Middle" subsection
   - Ensure Ch ~19 contains explicit midpoint shift (Aisen moves from reactive to active)

4. **BASTARD-MASTER-SANDERSON-INTEGRATION.md** — Section 11 (Theme Through Action)
   - Each scene should illustrate core theme through character choice
   - Use template provided to integrate thematic layer into plot

**Key focus areas**:
- **Tension escalation**: Each chapter raises stakes slightly (pacing faster than Act I)
- **Thematic integration**: Core theme shown through decisions, not exposition
- **Subplots intersecting**: Caspian thread, Elara thread, Covenant conspiracy thread converge here
- **Midpoint clarity**: Chapter ~19 should be unambiguous shift point

**Chapter length**: 4,000-6,000 words (escalate beyond Act I)

**Midpoint shift checklist**:
- [ ] Before midpoint: Aisen reacts to events, responds to challenges
- [ ] AT midpoint: Aisen makes active *choice* to investigate/pursue/challenge something
- [ ] After midpoint: Story flows from Aisen's initiative, not external pressure

---

### **PHASE 4: ACT III DRAFTING (Weeks 15-20)**

**Goal**: Write Chapters 26-40+ with accelerating pacing and powerful climax

**Documents to reference**:
1. **SCENE-DRAFT-PROMPTS.md** — War chapters, final confrontation
2. **CLIMACTIC-CONFRONTATION-BLUEPRINT.md** — Read FULL before starting Act III
   - Parts 1-3: Define and structure climax (required reading)
   - Part 4: Immediate aftermath structure (use for chapters 41-43)
   - Part 5: Validation checklist (use after drafting climax)

3. **CHARACTER-VOICE-BANK.md** — Final dialogue push; voices should be rock-solid by now

**Key focus areas**:
- **Pacing**: Shortest chapters; most frequent scene changes (3,000-4,000 words typical)
- **Escalation path**: Follow Act III structure from CLIMACTIC-CONFRONTATION-BLUEPRINT to climactic moment
- **Seraphine agency**: Show her making independent moves (not just reacting to Aisen)
- **Climactic sequence**: Use 3-scene structure (impossible situation → confrontation → sacrifice)
- **Post-climax**: Chapters 41-43 show network's independent choices and grief

**Climax validation checklist** (after drafting):
- [ ] Character arc tested (Aisen chooses need over want)
- [ ] Inevitability confirmed (readers saw it coming; accepted it as only logical end)
- [ ] Emotional impact present (raw, specific response from allies)
- [ ] Thematic resonance (death proves his worth was in choices)
- [ ] Prose quality strong (action followable; emotion shown through action)
- [ ] Network agency shown (post-climax chapters 41-43 demonstrate their independence)

---

### **PHASE 5: REVISION PASS (Weeks 21-28)**

**Goal**: Strengthen craft using Sanderson framework; validate entire story

**Full review cycle** (use SANDERSON-FRAMEWORK-ANALYSIS.md):

**Category 1: CHARACTER** (1 week)
- [ ] Each major character has distinct voice (use CHARACTER-VOICE-BANK for reference)
- [ ] Each character makes active choices (not passive)
- [ ] Each character's arc is earned through struggle
- Revision task: Dialogue pass—ensure voices are distinct

**Category 2: PLOT** (1 week)
- [ ] Cause & effect clear (each event flows from previous)
- [ ] Rising action escalates (pacing accelerates through acts)
- [ ] Climax tests character arc
- Revision task: Eliminate coincidences; add tension escalators to quiet chapters

**Category 3: STRUCTURE** (1 week)
- [ ] Act breaks clear and felt (passages of time shown)
- [ ] Subplots integrate with main plot (not distracting)
- [ ] Beginning hooks; middle deepens; end pays off
- Revision task: Add act epilogues/prologues; audit subplots

**Category 4: THEME** (1 week)
- [ ] Central idea illustrated through action, not exposition
- [ ] Theme explored from multiple character angles
- [ ] Theme consistent through all three acts
- Revision task: Add one internal thought per major character about core theme

**Category 5: SETTING & WORLDBUILDING** (1 week)
- [ ] Sense of place grounded in sensory detail
- [ ] World rules consistent and shown through consequence
- [ ] Setting integrated with plot (not just backdrop)
- Revision task: Add sensory grounding; ensure magic system consistency

**Category 6: PROSE & STYLE** (1 week)
- [ ] Clarity maintained (action followable; description clear)
- [ ] Voice distinctive per character
- [ ] Imagery vivid and fresh
- [ ] Reason varied for mood
- Revision task: Prose pass—tighten sentences; test rhythm aloud

**Category 7: EMOTIONAL ENGAGEMENT** (1 week)
- [ ] Empathy for protagonist (readers care about Aisen)
- [ ] Tension sustained (readers worry about outcomes)
- [ ] Surprises earned (not random)
- [ ] Catharsis delivered (climax releases emotional buildup)
- Revision task: Ensure climax is emotionally, not just plot-wise, satisfying

## KEY PRINCIPLES TO REMEMBER

As you use this toolkit, keep these Sanderson principles in mind:

### **1. Plot Flows from Character**
Every plot point should emerge from character choices, not authorial convenience. Use BASTARD-MASTER-SANDERSON-INTEGRATION.md Section 5 to validate cause & effect.

### **2. Character Needs Voice**
Character distinctness emerges through dialogue and thought-patterns. Use CHARACTER-VOICE-BANK.md before writing any major scene.

### **3. Theme Integrates Through Action**
Never *tell* readers what your story means. *Show* it through characters making choices aligned with theme. See BASTARD-MASTER-SANDERSON-INTEGRATION.md Section 9.

### **4. Climax Tests Arc**
Your climax is only powerful if it forces protagonist to choose between competing values. Validate using CLIMACTIC-CONFRONTATION-BLUEPRINT.md checklists.

### **5. Craft is Efficiency**
Your story has "bastard master" identity—every scene should serve story and character, not just worldbuilding or backstory. Use the 4-question chapter audit (BASTARD-MASTER-SANDERSON-INTEGRATION.md Section 8) liberally.

### **6. Prose Matches Philosophy**
If your story is about pragmatic efficiency, your prose should be pragmatic and efficient too. See BASTARD-MASTER-SANDERSON-INTEGRATION.md Section 11 (Prose Style).

### **7. Emotional Engagement Earns Trust**
Readers care when stakes are real and choices matter. Every scene should either build tension, reveal character, or advance plot—and ideally all three.
