# Introduction: The Journey of Writing Fantasy

Writing a fantasy novel is one of the most rewarding creative endeavors you can undertake. It's a chance to build worlds from nothing, to create characters who live and breathe, and to tell stories that transport readers to places they've never imagined. But it's also one of the most challenging forms of writing—requiring not just strong prose and compelling characters, but also intricate worldbuilding, logical magic systems, and plots that span continents and centuries.

This guide synthesizes advice from multiple published fantasy authors and experienced writing coaches. It's designed to take you from the very first spark of an idea through to a polished manuscript ready for readers. Whether you're a complete beginner or a writer looking to refine your process, this guide will provide the tools and frameworks you need.

**A Note on the Journey:** Writing your first fantasy novel is rarely a straight path. Most published authors have written multiple manuscripts before producing something publishable. This isn't discouragement—it's liberation. It means you can focus on falling in love with the craft without the pressure of immediate publication. Your first novel's purpose is to teach you how to write a novel. Every word you write makes you better.

# Phase One: Idea Generation and Preparation

## Finding Your Story Idea

### The Trope Brainstorm Exercise

Before you can write, you need something to write about. Many writers struggle with either having no ideas or having too many. The solution is to examine what you already love.

**Step 1: Identify Your Favorite Stories**
List the books, movies, games, and shows that have captivated you. Don't just list titles—identify *why* they captivated you.

**Step 2: Extract the Tropes You Love**
A trope is simply a story element—not inherently good or bad. List the specific elements you're drawn to:

- A young person discovering they have special powers
- A secret magical world existing alongside our own
- Found family dynamics
- A character rising from outcast to hero
- School or academy settings
- Heist structures
- Political intrigue
- Specific magic system concepts

**Step 3: Combine and Remix**
What happens when you merge your favorite elements? The author's first novel emerged from combining Harry Potter, Percy Jackson, Sky High, and The Avengers—creating a superhero school story.

### The Shower Test

When you have multiple ideas, pay attention to which one your mind returns to when you're not actively thinking about writing—in the shower, while driving, before sleep. The idea that obsessively bubbles up is likely the one you're most passionate about.

**Why Passion Matters:** The single biggest hack for productive writing is being obsessed with your story. When you're truly passionate, questions of motivation and discipline fade away. You'll think about your story constantly and write eagerly whenever you can.

## Choosing the Right Scope

### For Your First Novel, Simplicity Is Your Friend

**Point of View Characters:** Every additional POV exponentially increases complexity. Consider starting with one POV character for your first novel.

**Series vs. Standalone:** A standalone novel is significantly easier to complete than the first book in a planned series. You can always write sequels later.

**Length:** Shorter novels (80,000 words or less) are easier to finish and revise. A novella (20,000-40,000 words) can be an excellent entry point.

### What This Means in Practice

The author's first published novel, *Across the Broken Stars*, is:
- A standalone story
- One POV character
- Approximately 80,000 words

His later epic fantasy, *Kingdom of Dragons*, has:
- Multiple POVs
- Complex worldbuilding
- Planned as a trilogy
- Over 700 pages

He needed to write several novels before developing the capacity for the more complex project.

## The Myth of Originality

Many new writers worry excessively about whether their ideas are original enough. Here's the truth:

**Originality Is a Myth.** As author Jonathan Lethem put it, when people call something original, nine times out of ten they simply don't know the references involved.

**Your Unique Contribution:** What makes your story original is your particular combination of influences and your unique voice. Give ten writers the same prompt—"a dragon attacks a city"—and you'll get ten completely different stories.

**Execution Over Idea:** The most important thing is not the idea itself but how you execute it. A "simple" or even "bland" idea, executed brilliantly, will always beat a brilliant idea executed poorly.

## Primary vs. Secondary Worlds

### Primary World Fantasy
- Set in our world or a recognizable version of it
- Includes contemporary/urban fantasy
- Magic exists alongside the familiar
- Requires careful consideration of how magical elements would realistically impact history and society
- Example: Harry Potter, Percy Jackson

### Secondary World Fantasy
- Set in an entirely invented world
- No direct connection to our reality
- Complete freedom to create geography, history, cultures
- More labor-intensive at the start
- Example: The Lord of the Rings, A Song of Ice and Fire

**Choosing Between Them:** Consider your story's needs. Do you want the grounding of the familiar, or the freedom of the completely new? Both approaches have their challenges and rewards.

# Phase Two: Foundational Elements

## Premise Development

### What Is a Premise?

A premise is a one-sentence summary of your story that conveys:
- Your main character
- The core conflict
- The plot
- The stakes

### Examples of Strong Premises

**Ninth House by Leigh Bardugo:**
"A woman who can see the dead seeks justice for an unsolved murder by unraveling the twisted games of Yale's secret magical societies."

**The Hobbit:**
"A comfort-loving hobbit reluctantly agrees to help a wizard and a group of dwarves steal treasure from a dragon."

**Kingdom of Dragons (author's own):**
"A spy infiltrates a dragon rider academy to destroy it from within, but a forbidden romance forces him to choose between his loyalty and his heart."

### Developing Your Premise

1. **Identify what your main character wants** (external goal)
2. **Establish the stakes** (what happens if they fail)
3. **Consider including the setting** if it's particularly distinctive
4. **Keep it concise**—you're capturing the essence, not every detail
5. **Test and refine**—get feedback from others to see what intrigues them

### A Note on Premise Evolution

Your premise will likely evolve as you deepen your understanding of your story. The author's premise for *Kingdom of Dragons* continued to refine even after the book was published, as he found more elegant ways to describe it for the blurb.

## Theme and Meaning

### Defining Theme

Rather than thinking of theme as vague one-word concepts like "love" or "power" (which are actually subject matter), think of theme as:

**A moral argument about how to live, explored through your plot, characters, and world.**

### Three Categories of Theme

**Category 1: Easy to Agree With, Hard to Live**
- Example from Harry Potter: "True bravery comes from being willing to sacrifice your life to protect the ones you love."
- Simple on the surface, but profound in execution

**Category 2: I Can See All Sides**
- Example from The Final Empire: "Crime and deception are required to overthrow a corrupt system."
- Can be argued from multiple perspectives

**Category 3: The Devil's Advocate**
- Example from Hogfather: "Humans need to believe in myths and stories, even if they are lies, to maintain our morality."
- Counter to what most people would want to believe

### Theme in Practice

The author's theme for *Across the Broken Stars* is: "Redemption is possible by sacrificing yourself for an ideology that is bigger than you."

This theme:
- Came to him early in the process
- Gave cohesion to the entire story
- Informed the main character's arc
- Helped define the antagonist's opposing view
- Provided guidance when he felt stuck

### How to Use Theme

1. **Connect theme to your characters' arcs**—often the protagonist and antagonist will grapple with the same theme from opposite sides
2. **Make every side of the theme as strong as possible**—avoid straw-manning opposing viewpoints
3. **Use theme to guide decisions**—when uncertain, ask: "How can I explore this theme deeper?"
4. **Let theme emerge naturally**—it may come early or late in your process

## Character Arc Fundamentals

### The Core Components

**Ghost:** A past traumatic event that shaped your character's worldview

**Lie:** The false belief your character holds as a result of the ghost

**Want:** The external goal your character thinks will bring them happiness

**Need:** What your character actually requires for fulfillment

**Truth:** The antidote to the lie that will help your character grow

### Three Types of Character Arcs

#### Positive Arc

The character begins believing the lie and pursues the want. Through the story, they gradually understand the truth, face a pivotal decision, and ultimately choose the truth—achieving their need and rejecting the lie.

**Example: Percy Jackson (The Lightning Thief)**
- **Ghost:** Raised without knowing his father, labeled a troubled kid
- **Lie:** "I'm a powerless failure who can't do anything well"
- **Want:** "I just want to be normal and fit in"
- **Need:** To accept his true identity as a demigod
- **Truth:** "By embracing my courage, I can be a true hero"

#### Negative Arc

The character begins with the truth and their need, but is tempted by the lie. They chase their want, ignore their need, and ultimately embrace the lie—rejecting the truth.

**Example: Caul Shivers (Best Served Cold)**
- **Ghost:** Violent past as a northern warrior
- **Truth:** "I can build a new peaceful life"
- **Need:** To let go of anger and move on from violence
- **Want:** To regain respect and power
- **Lie:** "I should just be what I am—a killer"

#### Flat Arc

The character already knows the truth and has their need. The world believes the lie. The character remains steadfast, and in the climax either persuades the world to accept the truth or is defeated.

**Example: Ned Stark (A Game of Thrones)**
- **Truth:** "We must do what is right, no matter the cost"
- **Need:** To remain true to his oaths
- **World's Lie:** "Oaths should be bent for the greater good"
- **Outcome:** Executed because he cannot change

## Worldbuilding Basics

### Why Worldbuilding Matters

1. **Internal logic**—readers shouldn't get tangled in contradictions
2. **Reader immersion**—a richly crafted world invites exploration
3. **Uniqueness**—your world sets your story apart
4. **Speculative depth**—the "speculation" in speculative fiction

### The Scale Question

Before diving deep, ask: **How much of this world will readers actually see?**

- Is your story confined to one city?
- Does your character travel across continents?
- Are there multiple POVs in different regions?
- Do you need to map the whole globe, or just the relevant parts?

**The Pitfall:** It's easy to spend months building an entire world, only to realize that 90% of it never appears in your story. Build what your story needs, and let the rest remain implied.

### How to Start Worldbuilding

**Step 1: Write First (or at Least Start)**
Before committing to extensive worldbuilding, write a chapter or two. This helps you discover:
- What locations are actually central
- What details your story genuinely needs
- What questions your readers will have

**Step 2: Identify Your Starting Point**
Based on your story, determine what to build first:
- A single city or region
- A travel route between locations
- The political structure relevant to your plot
- The magic system mechanics

**Step 3: Build Through Character Lens**
Your readers will experience the world through your characters' eyes. Consider:
- What would this character notice?
- What sensory details would matter to them?
- What aspects of the world are most relevant to their daily life?
- Are they a newcomer (seeing things fresh) or an expert (noticing nuances)?

### Example: Political Intrigue Worldbuilding

In *The Traitor Baru Cormorant*, the world is explored through an economics lens because the protagonist is an accountant whose mission requires understanding political and economic systems. The reader learns about:
- Colonial structures
- Trade economics
- Political factions
- Cultural tensions

All through the lens of what the protagonist needs to know to achieve her goals.

# Phase Three: Structure and Plotting

## The Nine-Point Story Structure

This structure merges external plot with internal character transformation, creating a cohesive narrative where character and plot are inseparable.

### 1. The Hook (0-5%)

Introduce your main character, especially their flaws, desires, and key traits. Show a characteristic moment that immediately tells readers what makes this person unique and interesting.

**Example (Percy Jackson):** We meet Percy as a troubled 12-year-old with ADHD and dyslexia, feeling like an outsider. On a school trip, his teacher turns into a harpy and attacks him—but afterward, everyone acts like the teacher never existed.

### 2. The Inciting Incident (10-15%)

The ordinary world is disrupted by an event that sets the story in motion.

**Example:** While camping, Percy's friend Grover appears, warning that a monster (the Minotaur) is chasing them. Percy's mother drives them toward Camp Half-Blood, but the Minotaur takes her before they arrive.

### 3. Entering the New Realm (15-20%)

The protagonist is thrust from the ordinary world into the extraordinary world of your story.

**Example:** Percy wakes at Camp Half-Blood and discovers he's a demigod—the son of a Greek god. He learns that all Greek myths are real and the gods are alive in modern America.

### 4. The Antagonist Threatens (20-30%)

Pressure is applied to the protagonist, elevating the threat level.

**Example:** During Capture the Flag, Percy is ambushed by Ares' children but gains power from water. Immediately after, he's attacked by a hellhound—revealing he's the son of Poseidon (forbidden for the Big Three gods to have children). Chiron reveals that Hades has stolen Zeus's master bolt, framing Poseidon, and Percy must retrieve it.

### 5. Midpoint Revelation (45-55%)

The protagonist finally understands an important truth about themselves but can't fully embrace it yet. This is often a quieter, reflective moment.

**Example:** After defeating Medusa, Percy, Annabeth, and Grover rest and reflect. Percy admits: "I don't care about the master bolt. I agreed to go to the underworld so I could bring back my mother." Grover reveals Percy also wants to make his father proud—a truth Percy hasn't fully acknowledged.

### 6. Amplified Pressure (55-70%)

The protagonist's new understanding is challenged by the antagonist, intensifying the danger.

**Example:** Ares tricks the group, then reveals Percy's mother is a hostage in the underworld. Percy dreams of a monstrous voice in the pit saying: "He suspects nothing... Poseidon has played his most desperate card. Now we shall use it against him."

### 7. The Darkest Low (70-80%)

The protagonist faces their greatest suffering—the "dark night of the soul." The lie has never looked so strong.

**Example:** The trio finally enters the underworld: "The shoreline of the underworld came into view... a sound came from somewhere nearby... the howl of a large animal."

### 8. The Climax (80-95%)

The final confrontation between protagonist and antagonist. Either the truth wins or the lie wins—one is vanquished, the other reigns supreme.

**Example:** In the underworld, Percy discovers the master bolt was in his backpack all along—Ares set him up. Percy chooses to leave his mother in the underworld for the sake of the quest, returns to the surface, and confronts Ares. After defeating Ares, he learns that Luke—his mentor at camp—is the true villain working with Kronos.

### 9. The Closing Image (95-100%)

Show your protagonist living with their new truth and need. Often mirrors the opening image to highlight the transformation.

**Example:** Percy has gone from a misfit outcast to having a camp full of friends who understand him. Annabeth reconciles with her father. They shake hands and promise to face future threats together.

## Avoiding Common Plot Mistakes

### Mistake #1: Beating the Antagonist Too Early

**The Problem:** If your protagonist easily defeats the antagonist in the first few chapters, there's no suspense when they fight again later.

**The Solution:** Make your protagonist the underdog. They can fend off the antagonist or make a desperate escape, but it should be clear the antagonist is stronger.

### Mistake #2: Repetitive Scene Formats

**The Problem:** Using the same one or two scene formats repeatedly (e.g., every chapter is two characters having a conversation in a house).

**The Solution:** During outlining, note each scene's format. Ask: "Am I providing enough variation? Is the conflict constantly shifting?"

### Mistake #3: Passive Protagonists

**The Problem:** The story happens to the character rather than because of them. If you can remove the main character and nothing changes, they're too passive.

**The Solution:** Ensure your character makes active choices. If your novel feels like it doesn't "start" until page 100, that's likely where your character first acts rather than reacts.

### Mistake #4: No Consistent Through-Line

**The Problem:** Meandering, purposeless scenes or pointless subplots that deviate from the core conflict.

**The Solution:** Write down all the potential through-lines in your story, identify which is most developed, and commit to it. You can have subplots, but know which thread drives the spine of your narrative.

### Mistake #5: Soggy Middle

**The Problem:** The middle lacks progression and purpose, feeling slow and boring.

**The Solution:** Use a structure that provides more anchoring points in the middle, like the nine-point structure outlined above.

### Mistake #6: Weak Consequences

**The Problem:** Being afraid to punish your characters for failure.

**The Solution:** Ask: "What's the worst possible thing that could happen to my protagonist here?" Adversity reveals character depth. The characters you remember best are often those who went through hell.

### Mistake #7: Misaligning the Three P's

**The Problem:** The promise, progress, and payoff don't align.

**The Solution:** Ensure your opening promises what the story delivers. If you promise one type of story through tone and early scenes but deliver something else, readers will feel cheated.

### Mistake #8: Petering Endings

**The Problem:** Plot threads are resolved gradually over several chapters, creating a slow, anticlimactic ending.

**The Solution:** Compress resolutions into a single moment. Knot your plot threads together so they're resolved simultaneously, combining your firepower into one massive explosion.

### Mistake #9: Luck That Helps the Protagonist

**The Problem:** Coincidences or luck get your character out of trouble in unsatisfying ways.

**The Solution:** It's fine for *luck to help the antagonist*—throwing your protagonist into peril. But luck helping your protagonist often feels like cheating.

## The Promise-Progress-Payoff Framework

Every story consists of:
- **Promise:** What you tell readers to expect
- **Progress:** How you show movement toward that promise
- **Payoff:** How you deliver on what was promised

The progress part is crucial—it's what pulls readers through the story. You need a clear type of progress that readers can track.

### Types of Progress

**Travelogue Progress:**
- Characters move toward a destination
- Readers can track their journey on a map
- Example: The Lord of the Rings (from the Shire to Mount Doom)

**Information Progress:**
- Characters gather clues that build upon each other
- Some clues are false, leading backward
- The picture becomes clearer over time
- Example: Mystery novels

**Skill/Ability Progress:**
- The protagonist grows stronger, wiser, or more capable
- Readers can track their development
- Example: Coming-of-age stories, training arcs

**Relationship Progress:**
- A relationship develops or deteriorates
- Readers track the emotional distance between characters
- Example: Romance novels

### The Key Principle

If readers complain that your pacing is slow or your story isn't a page-turner, it's likely because you're not signposting the type of progress your story is making.

## Scene Format Variation

### What Is a Scene Format?

A scene format is a structural archetype that defines the prime action of a scene—for example:
- A romantic confession
- A stealthy infiltration
- A battle sequence
- A negotiation
- A chase
- A revelation

### How to Ensure Variation

During outlining, note the format of each scene. If you see the same format repeated too often, mix things up. Variation keeps readers engaged and forces characters to adapt to new challenges.

# Phase Four: Outlining Your Novel

## The Hourglass Method

The Hourglass Method moves from broad exploration to focused core development and back out to detailed planning.

### Phase 1: Explore the Suburb (Brain Dump)

Write down all your ideas without judgment:
- Interesting characters
- Cool scenes
- Unique worldbuilding details
- Magic system concepts
- Emotions you want readers to feel
- Potential plot directions

**Use the Triangle of Story:** Character, Plot, Setting. Identify which area you're weakest in and consciously spend more time developing that aspect.

### Phase 2: Find Your Story's Core

#### Develop Your Premise
Create a one-sentence summary conveying character, conflict, plot, and stakes.

#### Refine Your Theme
Articulate the moral argument your story explores.

#### Create Your One-Page Guiding Principles Document

Include:
- Your one-sentence premise (logline)
- Your theme
- Main character arcs
- High-level synopsis (spoiler-filled overview)
- Any other important notes

**Example Guiding Principles Document for *Across the Broken Stars*:**
- **Logline:** "Desperate for redemption, a wingless cowardly middle-aged angel must help a young fugitive—the only other surviving angel—evade a ruthless inquisitor in a quest for a mythical safe haven."
- **Theme:** "Redemption is possible by sacrificing yourself for an ideology that is bigger than you."
- **Designing Principle:** Using the physical quest for the safe haven as a metaphor for the protagonist's redemption journey.

### Phase 3: Put It All Together

Expand back out to develop:
- Detailed characters
- Plot and structure
- Worldbuilding
- Magic systems
- Scene-by-scene breakdown

## The Nine-Point Structure in Your Outline

Use the nine-point structure as your scaffold. For each point, consider:
- What happens in this section?
- How does this advance the plot?
- How does this affect the character arc?
- What questions does this raise for readers?

## Developing Character Arcs in Depth

### The Five Core Elements Revisited

1. **Ghost:** Past traumatic event shaping worldview
2. **Lie:** False belief resulting from the ghost
3. **Want:** External goal pursued in vain
4. **Need:** What the character actually requires
5. **Truth:** Antidote to the lie

### How These Map to Your Outline

**Act One:** Character believes the lie, pursues the want
**Midpoint:** Character glimpses the truth
**Act Two:** Character struggles between lie and truth
**Climax:** Character chooses truth (positive arc) or lie (negative arc)

## Building Your Magic System

### If You Include Magic

Not every fantasy novel needs a magic system, but if you include one:

**Show, Don't Explain:** Give readers a tantalizing glimpse of what magic can do, leaving mystery around its full capabilities.

**Create Tough Choices:** Magic should force characters to make difficult decisions. Example: In *Blackstone Heart*, magical robes can only be enchanted by sacrificing human souls to a demonic entity.

**Establish Limitations:** Magic without limitations removes tension. What can't your magic do? What does it cost?

**Foreshadow Later Uses:** If characters will discover new applications of magic later, plant seeds early.

# Phase Five: Writing the First Draft

## Establishing a Writing Habit

### The Five-Minute Rule

Tell yourself you'll write for just five minutes. Open your document, read what you've done, type a few sentences. If you want to stop after five minutes, you can. In most cases, you'll trick yourself into continuing because action creates motivation.

### Find Your Energy Apex

Experiment with different times:
- Morning writers often produce best before other demands intrude
- Night writers may feel more uninhibited and creative
- There's no "right" time—only what works for you

### Aim for Daily Writing

Even 50 words a day keeps the story in your subconscious, allowing ideas to percolate between sessions.

**The No Two Skip Days Rule:** If you miss a day, write the next day. Two missed days in a row make it easy to miss three, four, five.

### Track Your Progress

Keep a spreadsheet of daily word counts. This gamifies the process, letting you see small wins stack up. Many writers find this dramatically increases motivation.

### The Atomic Habits Framework

- **Cue:** Make it obvious (keep story materials visible, have inspiring artifacts around)
- **Craving:** Make it attractive (focus on what will be fun about today's session)
- **Response:** Make it easy (use the five-minute rule)
- **Reward:** Make it satisfying (track progress, celebrate small wins)

## Mindset for First Draft Success

### Shovel Sand into the Sandbox

Your job in the first draft is simply to get words on the page, not to craft perfect prose. You can shape the sand into castles later.

### Focus on Today's Step

Don't worry about publication, marketing, or future books. What do you need to write today?

### Get 1% Better Each Day

Set small challenges:
- "Today I'll write better dialogue"
- "Today I'll focus on vivid descriptions"
- "Today I'll cut redundant words"

Over a year, this compounds into significant improvement.

### Enjoy the Process

The single biggest hack for effortless motivation is writing a story you're obsessed with. If you're passionate about your story, you'll think about it constantly and write eagerly whenever you can.

## Overcoming Writer's Block

### Reframe "Writer's Block"

Instead of thinking of it as a supernatural affliction, break it into categories:

**Plot Block:** Your subconscious recognizes something is wrong. If you keep writing, you'll just create more problems to fix later.

*Solution:* Re-outline. Ask: "Is this the right direction?" Workshop ideas with other writers.

**Energy Block:** You're exhausted.

*Solution:* Get rest. Eat well. Sleep is "the greatest legal performance-enhancing drug."

**Life Block:** External circumstances are demanding your attention.

*Solution:* Process what you need to process before forcing creativity.

### Make Every Scene Someone's Favorite

When a scene feels boring, ask: "Why will this be somebody's favorite scene?" What can you add that makes it special?

The author transformed a "necessary transition" boat journey into one of his favorite scenes by adding:
- Character moments of happiness and play
- Training sequences
- A storm that capsizes the boat
- Survival challenges

### Reconnect with Inspiration

If you're stuck, go back to the books, movies, or games that inspired you. Reconnect with what made those stories great—it often provides clues for moving forward.

### Push Through to Completion

If you're 20,000-30,000 words in and thinking of giving up, strongly consider pushing through. You'll learn more from finishing a flawed 80,000-word novel than from abandoning five half-finished ones.

## Writing with Intensity

### What Is Intensity?

Intensity is your focus, speed, and engagement while writing. High-intensity writing means:
- Typing quickly
- Staying focused
- Knowing what comes next
- Not stopping to edit or tinker

### Why Intensity Matters

Writing at 100% intensity for 20 minutes is more productive than writing at 20% intensity for an hour. High-intensity writing often produces scenes that need less editing and feel more organic.

### How to Increase Intensity

- Write faster (physically type more quickly)
- Eliminate distractions (phone notifications, email, social media)
- Know your story (outline first)
- Focus on what you enjoy about the scene
- Don't stop to edit as you go
- Use placeholders for things you don't want to figure out now

### Placeholders in Practice

When you can't think of a name or detail, use a placeholder like {NAME} or {CITY}. You can search and replace later. The author had about 200 placeholders in his first draft of *Kingdom of Dragons*.

## Ending Each Session

### The Hemingway Method

"Always stop while you are going good. That way your subconscious will work on it all the time."

Stop before you exhaust your ideas, even mid-sentence. When you return, you can immediately finish that sentence and gain momentum.

# Phase Six: The Revision Process

## Let Your Manuscript Rest

After finishing your first draft, put it away for a few weeks. Work on something else—a short story, a novella, or even a completely different project. This creates objectivity.

## The Editing Workflow

### Draft 1: First Draft (Get Words on Page)

### Draft 2: Structural Fixes

Read the entire story and take notes. Change your font, transfer to Kindle, or print the manuscript to trick your brain into thinking someone else wrote it.

**Take Notes on:**
- General story impressions
- Structural issues
- Character arcs
- Worldbuilding gaps
- Plot holes
- Individual chapters

**The Wild Imagination Session:** Before committing to fixes, brainstorm every possible cool direction your story could go, even radically different ones. Sometimes this reveals solutions you wouldn't otherwise consider.

### Draft 3: Specific Editing Passes

Once structure is locked, do focused passes:

**Character Pass:** Focus on one character at a time, ensuring their voice and arc are consistent.

**Dialogue Pass:** Focus only on dialogue. In most cases, dialogue improves by condensing it.

**Hook Pass:** Ensure each chapter starts and ends with interest.

**Foreshadowing Pass:** Plant seeds early for payoffs later.

**Setting Pass:** Add sensory details, weather, and ensure scenes are set in the most interesting possible locations.

### Early Readers (Draft 4)

**When to Share:** Give your story to early readers when the bones are solid but there's still room for improvement. If you've polished too much, you'll be resistant to feedback.

**What to Ask:** Focus on readers' *experience*, not their solutions:
1. What's great? (So you don't accidentally remove it)
2. What's boring? (So you can fix it)
3. What confuses you? (So you can clarify)
4. What don't you believe? (So you can make it convincing)

**What Not to Ask:** Don't ask for prescriptive advice unless your readers are experienced editors. They may be right about what's wrong but wrong about how to fix it.

### Professional Editor (Draft 5)

**When to Hire:** After incorporating early reader feedback and you're ready for expert guidance.

**What to Look For:** An editor who has worked on similar books in your genre. Think of this as an investment in learning—the skills you gain will help you for every future book.

**Types of Editing:**
- **Developmental Editing:** Big-picture structure, character arcs, plot
- **Line Editing:** Prose style, sentence flow
- **Copy Editing:** Grammar, spelling, punctuation

For your first novels, developmental editing is most valuable.

### Final Polishes (Draft 6+)

**Read Aloud:** Use Word's read-aloud feature or read aloud yourself. This catches mistakes you'd skim over silently.

**ProWritingAid:** Editing software that helps identify:
- Adverb overuse
- Passive vs. active voice
- Glue words
- Sentence variation
- Other patterns needing attention

**Read Backwards:** Start from the last chapter and work backward, or read each chapter from the end to the beginning. This forces you to see individual sentences without the flow of the story making you skim.

## Knowing When to Stop

"Art is never finished, only abandoned." —Leonardo da Vinci

You're done when you start feeling diminishing returns from editing—when you make a pass and change very little. Then you have two choices:

1. **Seek Publication:** Submit to agents, publishers, or self-publish
2. **Start a New Project:** Some books are learning experiences, not publication-ready. Most writers need several novels before they're ready to publish.

The author's publication journey:
- 2013: First novel attempt (never published)
- 2017-2018: Several novels and novellas (some published, some not)
- 2018: First paid publication (short story)
- 2020: First novel published

Every unpublished novel taught him something that made later novels better.

# Phase Seven: Crafting a Powerful Opening

## The 10 Best Ways to Start a Fantasy Novel

Based on a survey of 400 readers, these are the most effective approaches (ranked from 10 to 1):

### 10. Introduce the Magic System

Show a slice of your magic system early, but leave mystery. Hint at tough choices characters might face later.

**Key Principle:** "If the story is about dragons, there should be dragons in the beginning."

### 9. Start with the Antagonist

Opening from the villain's perspective creates an information gap—readers know more than the protagonist, creating suspense that can sustain for hundreds of pages.

**Great Example:** *Harry Potter and the Goblet of Fire* opens with a caretaker overhearing Voldemort's plans, setting a darker tone and creating tension for the entire book.

**Critical Warning:** Avoid having the protagonist defeat the antagonist in chapter one unless the antagonist is clearly superior and the protagonist barely escapes.

### 8. Foreshadowing

Hint at what's to come in ways readers may not fully understand until later. Create both immediate hooks and long-term mysteries.

**Great Example:** *The Way of Kings* opens with a prelude that raises questions only answered much later in the series.

### 7. Set the Tone or Atmosphere

Your opening should promise what the story will deliver. If your novel is dark fantasy, the opening should be dark. If it's about dragons, show dragons.

### 6. Start with a Prologue

Some readers love prologues; some hate them. This demonstrates that you can't please everyone—focus on execution, not universal approval.

**Great Example:** *The Way of Kings* uses two prologues and a throwaway POV before introducing the main character, yet it works brilliantly.

### 5. Dialogue

Dialogue is always "concrete"—it's happening now, pushing the story forward. It's also an efficient way to establish character personalities, goals, and relationships.

**Warning:** Avoid "as you know" dialogue where characters explain things they already know purely for the reader's benefit.

### 4. Create Mystery or Intrigue

Open with a contradiction or question that readers must keep reading to resolve.

**Great Example:** *Empire of Silence* opens by revealing the protagonist destroyed a sun and committed genocide—then immediately shows him as a curious, naive young man. How did he become that?

### 3. Interesting Worldbuilding Details

Unique setting details can hook readers immediately. Even familiar settings can feel fresh through distinctive prose and perspective.

**Great Example:** The opening of *The Gut of Prayer* describes a city in a way that feels utterly original despite being a recognizable medieval setting.

### 2. Introduce the Main Character

Show your character pursuing a clear goal, facing obstacles, and making tough decisions. This establishes their flaws, traits, and potential arc.

**Important Distinction:** Don't worry about making your character *likable*. Worry about making them *interesting*. Readers will follow fascinating characters even if they're terrible people (as in *The First Law* trilogy).

### 1. Start In Medias Res (In the Middle of Things)

The most popular opening among surveyed readers. Put readers on a moving train rather than having them watch it depart.

**Not Necessarily Explosions:** "Action" can be meaningful conflict on any scale—a heated argument, a character arriving somewhere new, the day everything changes.

**Boring Action vs. Meaningful Action:** Action is interesting to the extent that it defines, develops, and expresses character. The mechanics of sword fights matter less than what the fight reveals about who's fighting.

## The Excitement Equation

To write thrilling openings, use the excitement equation:

**Meaningful Action = Character Development Through Conflict**

Ask: What does this action reveal about my character? What choice are they forced to make? What does that choice tell us about who they are?

## Your First Chapter as a Miniature

Treat your first chapter as a compressed version of your entire novel. It should give readers a clear taste of:
- The emotional experience to come
- The type of conflict they'll encounter
- The kind of characters they'll follow
- The tone and style of the prose

If your novel is full of witty banter, your opening should have witty banter. If it's about political intrigue, your opening should hint at political intrigue. The taste should match the meal.

# Phase Eight: Advanced Worldbuilding

## Beyond the Map

Worldbuilding is more than geography. Consider:

### Language and Communication

- Do people speak different languages? How does this affect trade, diplomacy, conflict?
- Is there a lingua franca? How did it emerge?
- How does magic affect communication (telepathy, translation spells, etc.)?

### Culture and Society

- What do people value? What do they consider virtuous or shameful?
- What are their customs around birth, marriage, death?
- How do social hierarchies work? Is there class mobility?
- What roles do gender, age, family lineage play?

### History

- What major events shaped the current world?
- What do people remember correctly? What have they forgotten?
- Whose version of history is taught? Whose is suppressed?

### Politics

- Who holds power? How did they get it?
- What are the relationships between nations, cities, factions?
- What are the sources of conflict (resources, ideology, historical grievances)?

### Economics

- What resources are valuable? Where do they come from?
- How do people make a living?
- Is there trade? What goods travel between regions?
- What's the economic divide like? Who's rich? Who's poor?

### Technology

- What's the level of technology?
- How does magic replace or augment technology?
- What innovations have changed society?

### Religion and Belief

- What gods or forces do people worship?
- What do they believe about the afterlife?
- How does religion interact with politics?
- Are there heretics, schisms, or persecuted beliefs?

## Geography and Climate

### The Map as Story Element

Your geography should serve your story:
- Mountain ranges create natural borders
- Rivers are trade routes and lifelines
- Deserts isolate communities
- Coastlines enable naval power

### Climate's Impact

- Harsh climates create hardy, resourceful cultures
- Fertile lands enable population growth and specialization
- Climate shifts can be plot elements (long winters, magical seasons)

## The Map Mistake to Avoid

The infamous Harry Potter world map (showing magic schools globally) demonstrates what happens when worldbuilding is surface-level:
- Europe has a high concentration of schools with no stated reason
- All of South America goes to a school in Brazil (where Portuguese is spoken, while most countries speak Spanish)
- North and South Korea share a school despite historical conflict
- All of India and China share one unnamed school (representing about 2 billion people)

**The Lesson:** Consider the implications of your worldbuilding. If you don't think through language, culture, history, and politics, your world will feel shallow.

## How Much Worldbuilding Is Enough?

**Build What Your Story Needs:** Your readers will see only a fraction of what you create. Build the parts that matter to the plot, characters, and themes. Let the rest remain implied—it adds depth without overwhelming the reader.

**The Character Lens:** Your readers experience the world through your characters. What would this character notice? What sensory details matter to them? What aspects of the world are relevant to their daily life?

# Conclusion: From Draft to Publication

## The Journey Is the Destination

Writing a fantasy novel is a marathon, not a sprint. Most published authors wrote multiple complete novels before producing something publishable. The author who wrote this guide wrote for 10 years and produced over a million words before his first novel was published.

## What Success Looks Like

If you write a fantasy novel and you're proud of what you produced, you have won. Whether it's published, whether it finds readers, whether it makes money—those are nice, but they're not the point.

The point is that you told a story. You built a world. You created characters who lived and breathed. And you learned something about the craft that you'll carry into your next story.

## Keep Writing

"The journey before destination." —The Stormlight Archive

Focus on today's writing. Focus on getting 1% better. Focus on the joy of creating something from nothing.

Keep writing. Keep striving. And when you finish, start the next one.
