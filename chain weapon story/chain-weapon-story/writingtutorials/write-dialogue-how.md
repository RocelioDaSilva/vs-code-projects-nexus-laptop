# A Comprehensive Guide to Writing Dialogue

## Introduction: What Makes Dialogue Good?

Good dialogue is one of the most challenging aspects of writing to master. Unlike real conversation, which is often messy, repetitive, and directionless, effective dialogue serves multiple purposes simultaneously while maintaining the *illusion* of authenticity.

**Good dialogue must do four things:**
1. Serve the plot
2. Be story-shaped (characters pursuing goals)
3. Be consistent with the characters
4. Feel alive (natural responses, not just information exchange)

The best dialogue doesn't draw attention to itself as "writing"—it feels inevitable, as if these characters in this situation could only speak these words.

---

## Part 1: The Foundations of Effective Dialogue

### 1.1 Every Line Must Serve a Purpose

Perhaps the most fundamental principle: **every line of dialogue must serve at least one purpose.** The mark of a great writer is crafting lines that serve multiple purposes simultaneously.

**Common purposes dialogue can serve:**
- Advance the plot
- Reveal character
- Convey exposition (carefully)
- Create humor
- Build tension
- Establish mood
- Foreshadow future events
- Reveal relationships between characters

**Example of multi-purpose dialogue (Lord of the Rings):**
> "I don't know half of you half as well as I should like, and I like less than half of you half as well as you deserve."

This single line:
- Reveals Bilbo's cheeky, witty personality
- Shows his social awkwardness and love of riddles
- Tells us about his complicated relationship with the other hobbits
- Creates humor
- Characterizes him as someone who enjoys being cryptic

### 1.2 Subtext: The Heart of Great Dialogue

**Subtext** is the difference between what a character says and what they actually mean. It's the "show, don't tell" principle applied to dialogue.

When characters announce exactly how they feel—"I'm so angry right now!"—the dialogue feels *on the nose* and amateurish. Real people rarely state their emotions directly; they reveal them through what they *don't* say.

**Simple example of subtext:**
> "Are you okay?"
> "I'm fine."

The character isn't fine. The subtext is: "I'm not okay, but I don't want to talk about it."

**Masterful subtext example (Silence of the Lambs):**
The conversations between Hannibal Lecter and Clarice Starling appear to be about Buffalo Bill, Clarice's childhood, and Hannibal's paintings. But the *real* conversation is a power struggle—each trying to gain dominance, manipulate the other, achieve psychological advantage. This underlying conflict is never stated directly but drives every exchange.

**When subtext isn't needed:**
Sometimes directness works. "Here's Johnny!" from The Shining has zero subtext—it's exactly what it appears to be. Iconic lines often break the rule, but they do so deliberately and effectively.

### 1.3 Story-Shaped Dialogue: Characters Must Have Goals

Dialogue, like every scene, must be *story-shaped*. This means at least one character in every conversation must have a **goal** they're trying to achieve through the exchange.

**Every conversation needs:**
- A character with a goal (what they want)
- An obstacle (what's preventing them from getting it)
- A mini-climax (success or failure)
- A resolution that moves the larger story forward

**Example: The Dark Knight dinner scene**
- Harvey Dent's goal: Convince others that Batman is a temporary necessity and that true justice will prevail
- Obstacle: Natasha and Bruce arguing that vigilantes become permanent threats (citing Caesar)
- Mini-climax: Harvey's line "You either die a hero, or you live long enough to see yourself become the villain"
- Result: Bruce decides to support Harvey's crusade for justice

This conversation isn't random—it's structured like a miniature story with clear stakes, conflict, and a turning point.

### 1.4 Dialogue Must Be Alive: Characters Responding to Each Other

Bad dialogue reads like a list of information the writer needed to convey, with characters speaking *at* each other rather than *to* each other.

**Signs of dead dialogue:**
- Lines don't answer or acknowledge what was just said
- Characters ignore each other's statements
- Responses feel random or disconnected
- The conversation could be rearranged without changing meaning

**Example of dead dialogue (Hallmark mystery):**
> Jane: "I'm sorry you're going through all this, but I don't think I'm the right person to help."
> Leah: "I know it sounds crazy. I don't even remember her, but I know in my heart that my mom never would have left me on purpose."

Leah's response doesn't answer Jane's statement—she's just delivering her next piece of information regardless of what Jane said.

**How to fix it:**
Have characters actually respond to what was said, adjusting their tactics based on new information while still pursuing their goal.

**Revised version:**
> Jane: "I'm not an investigator. I wouldn't even know where to start."
> Leah: "Well, who would? Is there an actual investigator here I can talk to?"
> Jane: "We sometimes work with a detective from the police department. I could put you in touch."
> Leah: "I already talked to the police. They won't listen to me."

Now each line responds directly to the previous one while Leah pursues her goal of finding help.

## Part 2: Creating Distinct Character Voices

### 2.1 The "Name Cover-Up" Test

A simple but powerful exercise: **take a dialogue scene and cover up all the character names.** You should still be able to tell which character is speaking based on vocabulary, rhythm, and speech patterns.

If you can't tell who's speaking, your characters sound the same—a serious problem.

### 2.2 Revealing Character Through Speech Patterns

A character's dialogue should evolve as they change throughout the story.

**Example: Walter White in Breaking Bad**

*Early in the series:*
> "No, no, it's... it's just heavy." (hesitant, faltering)

*Later in the series:*
> "I am not in danger, Skyler. I am the danger." (confident, domineering, dogmatic)

The change in speech patterns reflects his transformation from timid chemistry teacher to ruthless drug lord.

### 2.3 Using Vocabulary and Cadence

Characters with different backgrounds, education levels, and personalities should sound different:

- **Highly educated characters:** More precise vocabulary, complex sentence structures
- **Working-class characters:** Shorter sentences, simpler vocabulary, more contractions
- **Formal characters:** Fewer contractions, proper grammar
- **Informal characters:** Slang, sentence fragments, interruptions
- **Introverted characters:** Pauses, hesitations, shorter responses
- **Extroverted characters:** More words, interruptions, tangents

### 2.4 Caution: Avoid Overwriting Accents Phonetically

**Mistake:** Spelling dialect phonetically to the point it becomes difficult to read.

**Bad example (overwritten):**
> "Y'all gon' an' done it now, you yella-bellied varmint!"

**Better approach:** Use occasional dialect markers (like "y'all") and let the character's word choice, rhythm, and context suggest their background. Readers will fill in the accent.

**The exception:** In comedy, you can break this rule for humorous effect. But in drama, prioritize readability.

### 2.5 For Characters Smarter Than You

When writing a hyper-intelligent character, **don't try to sound smarter than you are**—it will come across as fake.

Instead:
- Use a thesaurus occasionally for precise vocabulary
- Give them moments of observation that show their intelligence
- Let other characters react to them as intelligent
- Focus on their reasoning and conclusions rather than making every line sound "smart"

## Part 3: Common Dialogue Mistakes to Avoid

### 3.1 On-the-Nose Dialogue (The #1 Mistake)

**What it is:** Dialogue where characters say exactly what they mean, announcing their emotions or stating themes directly.

**Why it's bad:** It feels artificial, patronizing, and lacks depth.

**Bad example (Star Wars prequels):**
> "You're so beautiful."
> "It's only because I'm so in love."
> "No, it's because I'm so in love with you."

This dialogue announces emotions rather than revealing them through subtext and conflict.

**Even worse example (Interstellar):**
> "Love is the one thing we're capable of perceiving that transcends dimensions of time and space."

The theme is stated directly rather than demonstrated through story.

### 3.2 Exposition Dumps in Dialogue

**What it is:** Characters telling each other information they already know for the audience's benefit.

**Bad example:**
> "Let's not talk about Cincinnati, where you were almost killed by that drunk driver and wound up in the hospital for six weeks and we all worried you'd never walk again."

No real person speaks this way.

**Better approach: Layer backstory gradually:**
> "Can we just not bring up Cincinnati?"
> *Beat.*
> "Believe me, I don't want that any more than you do."

Readers understand this is a setup with a payoff to come. Later scenes can reveal more details organically.

### 3.3 Overusing Character Names

In real conversation, people rarely use each other's names. Constant name usage sounds artificial.

**Bad example (The Room):**
> "Oh, hi Mark."

**When to use names:**
- When a character enters a scene
- For emphasis or anger
- When addressing someone in a crowd
- Rarely, for clarification

Otherwise, let context and dialogue tags indicate who's speaking.

### 3.4 Identical Speech Patterns Across Characters

**Mistake:** All characters sound the same regardless of background, education, or personality.

**Common offender:** Fantasy novels where farm boys speak with the same vocabulary and cadence as kings and scholars.

**Example (Eragon):** A farm boy who can't read at the beginning adopts the exact same speech patterns as highborn nobles immediately upon meeting them.

### 3.5 Dialogue That Only Serves Plot Exposition

**What it is:** Characters explaining things purely to inform the audience.

**Bad example:**
> "As you know, the nuclear core will explode in 47 minutes unless we disable it."

If the other character already knows this information, there's no reason for it to be said.

**Better approach:** Use dialogue when characters naturally would exchange information—during arguments, negotiations, interviews, or when one character genuinely needs information.

**Example (The Shining):** The interview scene is pure exposition, but it works because:
- It's a natural situation where information would be exchanged
- It serves both characters (Jack is interviewing for a job)
- The exposition reveals character (how Jack responds to the hotel's dark history)

### 3.6 Overusing Dialogue Tags

**Mistake:** Using elaborate tags like "he exclaimed," "she retorted," "he declared" instead of simple "said."

**Why "said" is best:**
- It's invisible—readers' eyes glide past it
- It doesn't distract from the dialogue
- Fancy tags draw attention to the writing rather than the conversation

**When to use other tags:**
- Occasionally for clarity ("she whispered," "he shouted")
- When the manner of speaking is genuinely important
- But avoid the thesaurus approach of replacing "said" with synonyms

### 3.7 Mannerisms of Attribution

**Mistake:** Using actions as dialogue tags.

**Wrong:**
> "I can't take this anymore," Jim sighed.

**Correct:**
> Jim sighed. "I can't take this anymore."

People sigh, laugh, grunt, etc.—they don't sigh words.

### 3.8 Long-Winded, Bloated Dialogue

Bad dialogue is often wordy and repetitive. Good dialogue is concise—cut to the bone.

**Bloated example (The Room):**
> "Denny, it's about Lisa. She's beautiful. She looks great in a red dress. I think I'm in love with her. Well, I don't know. She doesn't like me because sometimes she's mean to me. But sometimes when I'm around her, I feel like I want to kiss her and tell her I love her. I don't know, I'm just confused."

**Improved version:**
> "It's about Lisa. She's beautiful, but sometimes she's mean to me. Other times I want to kiss her and tell her I love her. I don't know."

Remove unnecessary words, repetition, and anything the audience can infer.

## Part 4: Best Practices and Techniques

### 4.1 Cut Dialogue to the Bone

Real conversation is full of filler: ums, ahs, repetitions, tangents. Written dialogue should be a *representation* of real speech—how people *think* they talk, not how they actually talk.

**Exercise:** Take a dialogue exchange and see how much you can cut while preserving meaning.

**Before:**
> "Well, this may be one of the craziest mistakes I've ever made. Why do you say that, Pa?"

**After:**
> "This may be my craziest mistake ever."
> "Why, Pa?"

The same meaning with fewer words, creating more power.

### 4.2 Read Dialogue Aloud

This is the single most practical technique for improving dialogue. Reading aloud forces you to hear what the reader will "hear" in their mind.

**What to listen for:**
- Does it sound natural when spoken?
- Where does the rhythm feel awkward?
- Would a real person say it this way?
- Are there tongue-twisters or difficult-to-pronounce phrases?

**For non-native speakers or younger writers:** Ask a friend or family member who's fluent to read your dialogue aloud and give feedback.

### 4.3 Embrace Realistic Interruptions and Overlaps

Real conversations are messy. People interrupt, talk over each other, start sentences they don't finish, and sometimes don't respond directly.

**Example (Marriage Story):** Adam Driver and Scarlett Johansson's argument scenes are filled with interruptions and overlapping dialogue. This makes the fight feel authentic—they're not waiting politely for their turn to speak.

**Example of non-sequiturs:**
> "Did you remember to pick up milk?"
> "The car made a weird noise on the way home."

This happens constantly in real conversation. Use it to create authenticity.

### 4.4 Let Characters Talk Past Each Other Occasionally

Perfect communication is boring. Characters should sometimes misunderstand, talk past each other, or fail to truly connect.

**Example (Raymond Carver's "What We Talk About When We Talk About Love"):** Characters tell stories they never finish, respond to things that weren't said, and never truly understand each other. This creates realism and depth.

**Example (Sally Rooney's "Normal People"):** Connell and Marianne frequently don't say what they're thinking. There's a beautiful contrast between their internal thoughts and what they actually communicate.

### 4.5 Use Silence as Dialogue

Sometimes what characters *don't* say is more powerful than what they do say.

**Technique:** Don't write "he didn't respond" or "she remained silent." If you don't indicate a response, readers will understand silence.

**Example:**
> "Well, John," Linda said, "what do you have to say for yourself?"
> John set his jaw and stared out the window.
> "I'm waiting," she said.
> He lit a cigarette.

John's silence says everything. We don't need "he refused to answer."

### 4.6 Strive for "Make My Day" Moments

Iconic lines become legendary: "Frankly, my dear, I don't give a damn." "Go ahead, make my day." "May the Force be with you."

These lines:
- Fit seamlessly in the moment
- Don't draw attention to themselves initially
- Become quotable because they perfectly capture character and situation

Don't force quotability. Write the line that's true to the character and moment; if it's great, readers will quote it.

### 4.7 Use Dialogue to Reveal Character Through Conflict

The most memorable lines often come at moments of highest conflict.

**Examples:**
- "I'll have my vengeance" (Gladiator)
- "I ate his liver" (Silence of the Lambs)
- "You have my full attention? You have the minimum amount" (The Social Network)

Conflict creates tension, and tension makes dialogue crackle.

### 4.8 The Realism Balance: Authentic vs. Actual

**Important distinction:** Good dialogue *feels* authentic but isn't actually realistic.

**Real conversation is:**
- Full of filler words (um, uh, like)
- Often directionless
- Repetitive
- Interrupted and never concluded
- Not particularly witty

**Good dialogue:**
- Eliminates filler
- Has direction and purpose
- Is concise
- Uses tangents that serve the overall scene
- Is wittier and more clever than real speech

**The Tarantino approach:** Characters go on tangents (like tipping in Reservoir Dogs) that seem unrelated but actually reveal character. The tangent folds back into the scene's purpose—Mr. Pink's refusal to tip reveals his self-centered, survival-at-all-costs mentality that drives his entire character arc.

### 4.9 For Comedy: Rules Can Be Broken

Comedy is the one genre where breaking the rules of realism can work. Meta-jokes, fourth-wall breaks, and unrealistic dialogue can be effective if they're genuinely funny.

**Example (22 Jump Street):**
> "I can't believe the Koreans wanted church back."
> "Yeah, good thing there was an even bigger abandoned church directly across the street."

This dialogue is completely unrealistic and meta, but it works because it's intentionally comedic.

## Part 5: Practical Exercises

### 5.1 The Eavesdropping Exercise

Go to a cafe, bus stop, or other public place and listen to real conversations. Better yet, record one (with permission or discreetly) and transcribe it exactly.

**What you'll discover:**
- How messy real speech is
- How often people interrupt
- How conversations wander
- The filler words and repetition
- The non-sequiturs and tangents

This trains your ear for authentic speech patterns you can then *represent* in your writing.

### 5.2 The Name Cover-Up Test

Take a dialogue scene, cover the character names, and read just the lines. Can you tell who's speaking? If not, you need to differentiate voices more.

### 5.3 The Purpose Audit

Go through a dialogue scene and ask of each line:
- What purpose does this serve?
- Could it serve multiple purposes?
- If it serves no purpose, can it be cut or rewritten?

### 5.4 The Subtext Rewrite

Take a scene with on-the-nose dialogue and rewrite it using subtext.

**Before (on-the-nose):**
> "I'm angry that you forgot our anniversary."
> "I'm sorry. I've been stressed about work."

**After (with subtext):**
> "So, did you remember what day it is?"
> "Tuesday?"
> "Right. Tuesday."

The anger and disappointment are revealed without being stated.

### 5.5 The "No Dialogue Tags" Challenge

Write a scene with no "he said," "she asked," or any attribution tags. Use action and context to make clear who's speaking.

**Example:**
> Jordan dropped onto the couch. "I've had it."
> "Long day?"
> "You could say that."
> The cushions sagged as Mary sat beside him. "Tell me about it."

This forces you to use action to identify speakers, creating more dynamic writing.

### 5.6 The Cutting Exercise

Take a dialogue-heavy scene and cut it by 30-50%. Remove:
- Unnecessary words
- Repetition
- Lines that don't serve purpose
- "Um," "uh," filler that doesn't reveal character
- Information the audience can infer

You'll likely find the scene is stronger and more powerful.

## Part 6: Formatting and Technical Considerations

### 6.1 Dialogue Tags: Keep It Simple

**Use "said" 90% of the time.** It's invisible and doesn't distract.

**Tag placement:**
- **Better:** "Joe said" (tag after name)
- **Worse:** "Said Joe" (sounds like a children's book)

**When to avoid tags entirely:**
- When action makes the speaker clear
- In two-character exchanges (alternating lines)
- When the rhythm of short lines indicates the speaker

### 6.2 The First Draft Shortcut

When writing your first draft, skip dialogue tags entirely. Format like a script:

> Character A: Dialogue line
> Character B: Response
> Character A: Follow-up

Then in revision, convert to prose with tags and action. This lets you focus on content first.

### 6.3 Contractions Are Your Friend

Real people use contractions. Formal, un-contracted language sounds stiff and unnatural.

**Bad:** "I do not want to go to the store."
**Good:** "I don't want to go to the store."

Unless a character is deliberately formal, use contractions.

### 6.4 Avoid Explaining the Obvious

Don't write: "I'm beat," John said tiredly.

The dialogue itself should convey exhaustion, or you can show it through action: "John collapsed onto the bed. 'I'm beat.'"

## Part 7: Genre-Specific Considerations

### 7.1 Genre Fiction

**Fantasy/Historical:** Be careful with anachronistic language. Words like "stressed," "traumatized," "OK," "chemistry," and "adrenaline" didn't exist in certain historical periods. Research your era.

**Romance:** Subtext is crucial. The "will they/won't they" tension lives in what characters don't say.

**Mystery/Thriller:** Dialogue often serves double duty—revealing character while conveying clues. The interrogation scene is a classic structure.

**Science Fiction:** Consider how future or alien contexts might affect language. But be careful—too much invented slang becomes unreadable.

### 7.2 Literary Fiction

Dialogue can be more experimental, with longer pauses, silences, and what's unsaid being as important as what's said. The focus is often on character revelation rather than plot advancement.

### 7.3 Screenwriting vs. Novels

**Screenplays:**
- Even more economical—every line costs screen time
- Subtext is often conveyed through visuals and performance
- Actors bring their own interpretation

**Novels:**
- Can include internal thoughts alongside dialogue
- Can use more dialogue tags and description
- Can be longer, more reflective

## Part 8: Revision Checklist

Use this checklist when revising your dialogue:

**Purpose**
- [ ] Does every line serve at least one purpose?
- [ ] Do any lines serve multiple purposes?

**Subtext**
- [ ] Are characters saying what they mean, or is there depth beneath?
- [ ] Does any dialogue feel "on the nose"?
- [ ] Could a line be cut to let subtext speak?

**Character Voice**
- [ ] Can you tell who's speaking without tags?
- [ ] Do characters with different backgrounds sound different?
- [ ] Does dialogue evolve with character arcs?

**Natural Flow**
- [ ] Does reading aloud sound natural?
- [ ] Are there interruptions and overlaps where appropriate?
- [ ] Do characters actually respond to each other?

**Conciseness**
- [ ] Has every unnecessary word been cut?
- [ ] Are there repetitions that can be removed?
- [ ] Could any line be shorter and stronger?

**Formatting**
- [ ] Are tags mostly "said"?
- [ ] Is it clear who's speaking throughout?
- [ ] Are actions separated from dialogue with periods, not commas?

**Common Pitfalls**
- [ ] No anachronistic language
- [ ] Minimal use of character names
- [ ] No phonetic over-writing of accents
- [ ] No exposition dumps
- [ ] Characters have distinct voices

## Conclusion: The Secret to Great Dialogue

The secret to writing great dialogue isn't crafting clever lines or memorizing rules. It's **staying true to your characters.**

As Quentin Tarantino describes his process:
> "I just get the characters talking to each other. What you're trying to do is create an environment where Quinton isn't doing it—they're doing it. Quinton knows the direction it's supposed to go, but then the characters take it over."

The better you know your characters—their psychology, fears, desires, backgrounds, goals—the more authentic their dialogue will feel. You're not *creating* them; you're *meeting* them and writing down what they say.

When you come back for the second draft, you'll know them even better. You can refine their voices, cut what doesn't serve them, and deepen what does.

**The final test:** Does this dialogue feel inevitable? Would these characters in this situation say anything else?

If the answer is no, you've written good dialogue.
