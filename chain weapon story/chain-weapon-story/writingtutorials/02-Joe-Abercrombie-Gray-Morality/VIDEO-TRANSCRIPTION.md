# Joe Abercrombie: Gray Morality & Moral Complexity

## Video Transcription & Master Lesson

---

### Introduction

Joe Abercrombie writes a world where good and evil don't exist. Instead, there are people making choices—often terrible choices—for understandable reasons.

His genius is making you *sympathize* with characters who do awful things. Not forgive them. Not excuse them. But understand them.

In this lesson, we'll explore how to create morally complex characters and how to make "the right choice" lead to catastrophe.

---

## Core Principle: No Pure Heroes, No Pure Villains

Every character should have:
- A moment of cruelty or selfishness
- A moment of vulnerability or fear
- A justification for their worst choices

Your protagonist doesn't get to be purely good. Your antagonist shouldn't be purely evil.

### Why This Matters

Real conflict comes from two characters, both with valid reasons for their positions, forced into opposition.

Example from your story:

General Varro is not evil. He's a military commander trying to:
- Win the war
- Protect his soldiers
- Maintain order
- Preserve the nation

His methods are cruel. His compromises are immoral. But his *reasons* are rational.

A reader can understand why Varro makes his choices without agreeing with them. That's stronger than a mustache-twirling villain.

---

## The Abercrombie Technique: Doing Good Makes Everything Worse

### The Rule

When Aisen chooses compassion, when he chooses the "right" choice, it should often backfire spectacularly.

This teaches the reader that:
- Good intentions don't guarantee good outcomes
- The world is more complex than simple morality
- Sometimes the kind choice is also the cruel choice (because it delays suffering rather than preventing it)

### Example from Your Story: The Spy Boy

Aisen: prisoner caught with a coded message

**The "Moral" Choice:** Release him with money and a map north. Give him a chance.

**The Reality:** 
- The Covenant catches him within a day
- They execute him
- His mother dies anyway (the Covenant assumes he talked)
- Aisen's "mercy" extended the boy's suffering by 12 hours and doomed his mother

**The Consequence:**
Aisen now *knows* he made everything worse. He carries that guilt. He changes as a person.

This is more powerful than if Aisen had made the "cruel" choice and executed the boy cleanly. Because now he can't reconcile his compassion with his pragmatism.

## Writing Gray Characters: Internal Monologue

The key to Abercrombie's style is letting the reader live inside the character's head while they make terrible choices.

This isn't omniscient narrator judgment. It's the character justifying themselves.

### Example:

Bad version:
> The noble ordered the village burned. It was a war crime.

Abercrombie version:
> The noble looked at the map. The village was a supply hub. If it stood, his army would starve or take it in a costly siege. Either way, soldiers died.
> 
> "Burn it," he said. His hands didn't shake. He didn't look away. "Make it look natural if you can."
> 
> He had commanded ten thousand deaths across this war. Ten thousand more. At what point did the arithmetic stop mattering? When he'd already damned himself past redemption, burning one village felt... efficient.
> 
> He didn't enjoy it. He simply accepted it as necessary.

Notice: The narrative doesn't judge him. Doesn't call him evil. Just shows the logic. The reader understands *and* feels complicit. That's the power.

## Application to Your Story

### Exercise 1: Give Aisen a Dark Moment

Write a scene (300 words) where Aisen makes the pragmatic choice over the compassionate choice. And make it *work* narratively—he solves the immediate problem.

But then show the cost. The person he abandoned. The guilt that lingers.

Example structure:
1. Problem: Aisen could save a civilian or complete a mission objective. Not both.
2. Choice: He chooses the mission.
3. Consequence: The civilian dies.
4. Internality: Aisen justifies it to himself. But the reader feels his doubt.

### Exercise 2: Make a Good Character Do Something Selfish

Amara is brilliant and generally well-intentioned. But she's also ambitious. 

Write a scene where Amara:
- Makes a choice that helps her but hurts someone else
- Justifies it to herself
- The reader sympathizes even while disapproving

Example: Amara takes credit for intelligence that got innocent soldiers killed. It advances her career. She tells herself: "If I don't rise in power, someone worse will."

### Exercise 3: Add Dark Humor to Bleak Moments

Abercrombie uses humor to release tension in dark scenes. It makes the horror bearable and more human.

Example: After a brutal battle, a soldier says: "Well, could have gone worse."

His friend: "How?"

Soldier: "We could have, all of us, still been alive to regret it."

Write one bleak scene. Then add a single line of dark humor that makes it more poignant, not lighter.

## The Abercrombie Checklist for Moral Complexity

- [ ] Does my protagonist have a moment where they're cowardly or cruel?
- [ ] Does my antagonist have a moment where they're sympathetic or vulnerable?
- [ ] Does doing the "right thing" ever backfire?
- [ ] Do characters justify terrible choices in ways that *almost* make sense?
- [ ] Have I avoided using villainy as a plot device?
- [ ] Does the narrative ever judge characters (or do I show consequences instead)?
- [ ] Have I included dark humor in at least one hopeless scene?
- [ ] Could the reader sympathize with the "enemy" faction?

## Structural Principle: The Cost of Mercy

In your story, Aisen is defined by his moments of attempted mercy that fail.

This is his tragic flaw—not arrogance or ambition, but compassion in a world where compassion is expensive and rarely effective.

By showing these failures across multiple scenes, you build a character arc where Aisen:
- Tries to save people → They die anyway
- Tries to help refugees → Systems fail them
- Tries to be kind → Evil wins anyway

By Act III, Aisen understands: He cannot save everyone. He cannot fix systems. He cannot make mercy work at scale.

So what can he do?

That's the question that drives the climax.

---

## Key Insight

Gray characters are created by showing their choices, their justifications, and their consequences—without moral judgment from the narrator.

The reader will judge. You don't need to.

This creates depth, complexity, and a sense that you're reading about real people in an impossible situation rather than heroes and villains in a simple story.

Make everyone complicit. Make everyone understandable. Make everyone pay a cost.

That's Abercrombie's approach. That's how you write moral weight.
