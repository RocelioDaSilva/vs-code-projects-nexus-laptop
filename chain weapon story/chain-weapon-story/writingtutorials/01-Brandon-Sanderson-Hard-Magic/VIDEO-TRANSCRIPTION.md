# Brandon Sanderson: Hard Magic Systems

## Video Transcription & Master Lesson

---

### Introduction

Brandon Sanderson is perhaps the most systematic writer working in fantasy today. His approach to magic systems is revolutionary because he treats magic like physics—like a law of nature that can be understood, predicted, and relied upon.

In this lesson, we'll explore his Three Laws of Magic and how to apply them to your chain weapon story.

---

## The Three Laws of Magic

### Law One: An author's ability to solve conflict with magic is directly proportional to how well the reader understands the magic.

What does this mean?

It means: **Clarity enables power.**

If your reader doesn't understand how your magic works, they can't believe it. They can't feel tension. They can't fear what might go wrong.

Example: In your story, Aisen uses telekinesis. But how much can he lift? What's the range? What happens if he overreaches? 

If you, the author, don't know these answers, the reader will sense the uncertainty. Your action scenes will feel arbitrary. Aisen will win or lose based on plot convenience, not on the internal logic of his power.

But if you've established:
- Aisen can lift objects up to 50 kilograms safely
- He must maintain line of sight
- Exceeding his limit causes nosebleeds and fever
- Recovery takes 12-24 hours

...then suddenly, the reader understands. When Aisen faces a 300-kilogram boulder, the reader *knows* it's impossible. When he does it anyway, the reader feels his desperation. When he collapses from fever, it's not arbitrary—it's inevitable consequence.

This is the power of **clarity**.

### Law Two: Limitations are more interesting than abilities.

Think about it. What's more interesting:
- A mage who can cast any spell instantly?
- A mage who can cast one spell per day, but it's incredibly powerful?

The first is boring. The second creates conflict.

Your story already understands this. Aisen cannot:
- Teleport (locked to telekinesis only)
- Heal others
- See the future
- Avoid entropy debt

These limitations are what make your story work. They force Aisen to think. They force creativity. They make him vulnerable.

When Aisen faces a wall he cannot break with magic, he must use:
- Leverage
- Timing
- Observation
- Allies
- Terrain

That's the story. That's the interesting part.

### Law Three: Expand what you already have before adding something new.

Don't create seventeen different magic systems. Master one.

Your story has the Flux-Weave. That's enough. That's a strong, complete system with:
- Hard limits (mana pool, range, line of sight)
- Clear costs (entropy debt)
- Visible consequences (fever, nosebleeds, weakness)
- Different practitioners with different approaches (Aisen's telekinesis vs. Caspian's blood magic vs. Kairos's temporal loops)

You could invent a separate fire magic system. But why? Expand Flux-Weave instead. Show a Flux user who specializes in thermal manipulation. Show how the same system produces different effects based on understanding and training.

## Applying to Your Story

### Action 1: Audit Your Magic Costs

Go through every scene where Aisen uses telekinesis. Ask:

**Does the reader understand the cost?**

- If yes: Include a visible consequence (nosebleed, tremor, fatigue)
- If no: Add one

Example before:
> Aisen threw the chandelier at the assassin. The assassin dodged. Aisen threw it again.

Example after:
> Aisen seized the chandelier with his mind. Blood trickled from his nose. The weight exceeded his limit—five times his safe load. He held it anyway, feeding mana into the telepathic grip until his vision blurred. The chandelier flew. The assassin dodged. Aisen's knees buckled.

The second version shows cost. Cost creates credibility.

### Action 2: Create a Magic Limitation that Creates Plot

Aisen's magic already has limits (range, line of sight, mana). Now use one of these limits as the central problem of a scene.

Exercise:

Write a 300-word scene where Aisen **cannot** use his strongest ability because of a limitation.

Examples:
- An enemy hides behind a curtain. Line of sight is broken.
- Aisen fights in darkness. He can't see.
- He's exhausted from overuse. Mana pool is empty.
- A magical ward blocks telekinetic force.

What must Aisen do instead? That's your scene. That's where character comes through.

### Action 3: Expand Flux-Weave, Don't Add Systems

Rather than inventing a new magic system, show how different mages approach Flux differently:

- Aisen: Telekinesis (exoskeleton of force)
- Caspian: Blood magic (internal manipulation)
- Kairos: Temporal loops (self-targeted time magic)
- Ryo: Lightning (forced energy discharge)
- Healer mages: Biological regeneration (cellular repair)

Same system. Different applications. This makes the world feel coherent.

## Checklist: Hard Magic in Your Story

- [ ] Can I explain my magic system in 2-3 sentences?
- [ ] Does every use of magic have a visible cost?
- [ ] Does the protagonist ever fail because of magical limitations (not just because enemy is stronger)?
- [ ] Are there scenes where magic *cannot* solve the problem?
- [ ] Do different characters use the same magic system differently?
- [ ] Have I resisted adding new magic systems?
- [ ] Does the reader trust that magic won't be used as a plot convenience?

---

## Key Insight

Limitations create story. Powers create problem-solving. The magic system is not the story—it's the constraint that forces the protagonist to earn their victory.

Make your magic clear. Make your costs visible. Make your limitations meaningful.

That's Sanderson's genius. That's how you write magic that *matters*.
