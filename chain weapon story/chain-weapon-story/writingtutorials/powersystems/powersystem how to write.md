## INTRODUCTION

Power systems are more than just cool abilities. They shape the world, the characters, the conflicts, and the themes of your story. Whether soft (flexible) or hard (rigid), every power system makes a statement about what your world values and how it operates. This guide provides the vocabulary, frameworks, and practical steps to create or analyze power systems that resonate with readers.

# PART 1: POWER SYSTEM THEORY – The 6 Types of Power Systems

## Type 1: WILD SYSTEMS (Soft Magic)

Wild systems operate under what is called soft magic. Meaning the rules about what can and cannot happen are barely defined, if they're defined at all. In a world with soft magic, a wizard can cast a spell to create a fireball or turn you into a frog or do any other thing the author can think of, with no limits or rules to follow.

characters powers might appear at random, change between installments, or just serve the purpose of specific scenarios. In the Mario video games, there's no rational explanation for superstars or fireflowers. They exist, so we get to have more fun playing as Mario. They don't say anything about the grander world or Mario himself. In a wild power system, there is no overarching logic binding together the fantasy of the world. And when I say fantasy or magic, I don't mean that literally. Science fiction worlds where

the powers are based on technology can absolutely have a wild system, too. Rick and Morty is a good example. Rick's science can do basically anything he wants it to, regardless of whether or not it's based in any real world technological process. Now, hearing this, you might think that wild systems are just bad or lazy, but that's not necessarily the case at all. A lot of stories with wild power systems are great as long as they account for it correctly. If the power system exists primarily to serve the tone of the

world, the spectacle of the battles, or the identity of the characters, then is absolutely acting as a positive force for the story. The emphasis doesn't need to be placed on the mechanics of the powers for the system or the greater story to be good. Let me give you some examples. In Adventure Time, the land of Oo absolutely has a wild power system. There are all types of bonkers, magic, gods, demons, technology, monsters, and none of it has any explanation. There are vague structures. We learn that

there are four elements in the world. Ice, fire, candy, and slime. But why these are the elements or how they interact with each other is never the point of the stories being told. Or what about Sonic the Hedgehog? These games take place in a world where the main characters all have unique superpowers with no explanation why. Sonic is super fast. Knuckles is super strong. Tails can fly. But why they're like that doesn't matter. We just want to play as them and do cool stuff. The Chaos Emeralds are pure soft magic. We don't

know what they are or why they work the way they do. They can basically grant wishes just because. But that's because the power system isn't the focus of the world. In a wild system, power is allowed to be strange, flexible, or unexplained because the story is about something else. The mechanics are not meant to be interrogated because they're not the focus. The only real stipulations required for a wild power system to be good are these. Is it cool? And is it consistent? The first is

self-explanatory, so I'll focus on the second one. The abilities in a wild system need to be fairly consistent. We don't need to know why Sonic is fast or exactly how fast he is, as long as he's generally the fastest guy around in every story. Mario's mushrooms don't need a backstory, but they do need to make him bigger and stronger every time he eats one. Without consistency in your power system, your story basically devolves into the babblings of a kindergartener. Batman grew a million

feet tall and blew up the sun. Then he turned his hands into ice cream and did a backflip over Jupiter. It's just nonsense. The second type of power system is definitive. Basically the opposite of wild. These systems use hard magic. There is a structured set of rules that explains the basic premise behind the fantasy elements of this world. Just like a wild system, this is a really broad net. Any power system that has any amount of explanation is technically definitive and they spread across a broad spectrum from simple to

complex. Here, power is governed by identifiable rules that are consistent, shared, and understood within the [music] world. The audience is encouraged to ask questions and make predictions based on their knowledge of how the power [music] system works. Where does this come from? What are the limits? What if this rule is broken? Those are the thoughts that the author is trying to spark in the viewer's mind to get them engaged with the story because that is the primary benefit of a definitive system. It engages your

brain. When we understand the basics of chakra control, it becomes interesting to watch Naruto train his reenon and create the rasen shuriken. If we didn't know that Chakra had natures like wind and fire, or that the rasen is an exceedingly difficult judu because of the concentrated energy and spinning force, watching an arc where he learns the rasen shuriken becomes immediately boring. It's just a guy trying and failing over and over again to do a cool thing. The context is what gets us to

care. My Hero Academia is the same. The power system of quirks is definitive. Both the characters and the audience understand basic rules of the world. Most people are born with [music] quirks. They are a fundamental part of your identity. Training a quirk can make it stronger. And hypothetically, having multiple quirks makes you really, really powerful. So, when we're introduced to All for One, a villain who can take away people's quirks and use them for himself, it immediately clicks in our

minds how powerful, dangerous, and scary he is. No one needs to gas him up for him to be a threatening villain. Our knowledge of the power system informs our opinions on the character and leads us to speculate. How many quirks do you think he has? Are there any he can't steal? Could he ever take too many? To use an example from a western story, Harry Potter is absolutely a definitive power system. While the rules can be a little loose in places, they're generally fairly rigid. Magic is nearly

impossible to do without a wand. You need to understand the spell that you're casting and pronounce it correctly. The speed at which you can cast a spell and react to others directly influences your skill in duels. And all of this is boosted even further if you're creative and can think on your feet. Harry's entire dueling philosophy, which lets him save the world, is based on the first rule. You need a wand to cast spells. So, he just gets really, really good at the one spell that makes other

people drop their wands. This is a clever cheese strategy based on the rules of the world, and it enders us to his character. The defining philosophy of a definitive power system is this. Power is meaningful because it is constrained. Abilities come with costs, limits, and drawbacks.

## Type 2: DEFINITIVE SYSTEMS (Continued)

To recap: Power is meaningful because it is constrained. What makes a definitive system shine is how the rules create tactical depth. When readers understand the rules, they can predict outcomes and get excited when characters exploit them cleverly. The best definitive systems show characters growing not through raw power, but through deeper understanding of the rules.

# PART 2: PRACTICAL CREATION GUIDE – 9-Step Framework for Building Power Systems

Now that you understand the different types of systems, let's apply these concepts to create your own. Here's a comprehensive step-by-step framework for building an original power system from scratch.

## Step 1: Choose Your System Type

First, decide which of the 6 types your system will be: Wild (soft magic, flexible) → Definitive (hard magic, rules-driven) → Thematic (philosophy-bound) → Pluralistic (multiple systems) → Synergistic (complex interactions) → Meta (breaks the narrative)

Your choice determines everything that follows. A Wild system allows flexibility but loses tension. A Definitive system creates engagement but requires more design work. Choose based on your story's needs.

## Step 2: Determine Access and Source

**Access:** How do characters use the power?
- Objects (weapons, artifacts, tools)
- Knowledge (understanding, techniques, incantations)
- Own body (martial arts, gestures, bloodline)
- Familiar (bonded animals, spirits)
- Environment (location-based, elemental)
- Emotions (willpower, fear, love)
- Hybrid (combination of above)

**Source:** Where does the power come from?
- Self-energy (mana, ki, aura, chakra)
- Psychic power (mind-based)
- External energy (ambient magic, cosmic energy)
- Supernatural forces (demons, spirits, gods)
- Objects (artifacts, crystals, minerals)
- Technology (gadgets, cybernetics)
- Bloodlines/genetics
- Training and effort
- Consumables (potions, foods)
- Gods/entities (grants)
- Contracts (binding agreements)
- Bonds with familiars
- Gifts/blessings (permanent)
- Rituals/ceremonies
- Sacrifice (health, lifespan, memories)
- Emotions and trauma
- Environmental cycles (moons, seasons, time of day)
- Hybrid sources

Combine one Access with one Source. Example: Objects (access) + Self-energy (source) = weapons powered by the user's internal magic.

## Step 3: Give Your System a Name

The power system's name is how your audience recognizes and remembers it. A great name:
- Is short and easy to pronounce
- Carries emotional weight
- Reflects the system's nature or origin
- Feels natural in dialogue
- Makes the audience curious

Look at successful series: Nen, Chakra, Ki, Tao, Cursed Energy. All are short, memorable, and feel natural. Avoid names that are too long, awkward, or too similar to existing systems.

## Step 4: Define Scaling (Progression from Novice to Master)

Scaling determines how your system grows and evolves. Characters need room to improve, and Stakes need to rise over time.

**Questions to ask:**
- What's the weakest possible use of this power? (spark a flame, lift a pebble)
- What's the strongest possible use? (burn a nation, tear reality)
- Is mastery based on talent, effort, training, or a combination?
- Are there visible markers of progression? (transformations, auras, levels)
- Can growth plateau, or is it always possible?
- Can complexity grow instead of raw power? (more techniques, not just stronger)
- How long does it take to move between levels?

The best systems have multiple dimensions of growth. A character might increase power through pure training, or they might discover new applications of existing abilities. Both feel rewarding.

# PART 3: CASE STUDIES & ANALYSIS – Learning from Existing Systems

This section explores how real systems work and what makes them great. We'll examine the frameworks further before returning to the remaining steps.

**On Classifications:** Classifications are the "branches" of your power system tree. If your system is magic, classifications might be schools of magic (fire, ice, lightning) or types of mages (warrior, scholar, priest). Ask yourself:
- Does this classification have its own philosophy or worldview?
- If someone dedicated their life to mastering this classification, how would they change as a person?

Your classifications can be based on:
- Personality traits
- Skill specialization
- Spiritual alignment
- Natural elements (fire, water, earth, air)
- Even abstract concepts (music, color, dreams)

**On System Naming:** As mentioned in Step 3, a great name should:
- Reflect the system's nature
- Be easy to remember and pronounce
- Carry cultural or mythical weight
- Make audiences curious

**On Rules and Limitations** (Step 5 – continued below): Rules and limitations keep your system believable. Rules define what's possible; limitations create tension. Without rules, you have chaos. Without limitations, you have boring power creep.

---

## Step 5: Establish the Rules and Limitations

Rules define what's possible. Limitations create tension. Together, they make fights interesting and victories feel earned.

**Questions to ask:**

- **What triggers the power?** Does it require words, gestures, mental focus, or physical actions?
- **What are the costs?** Does using the power drain energy resources, shorten lifespan, or consume materials?
- **What are the risks?** What happens if someone misuses the power or overuses it repeatedly?  
- **What are the hard limits?** Are there things the power system absolutely cannot do?
- **Can rules be bent or broken?** Are some users able to transcend the standard limitations?
- **Does context matter?** Does time of day, location, or environment affect the power?
- **How do limitations foster creativity?** When characters can't just brute-force a problem, they must think their way through it.
- **What happens when someone breaks a rule?** Is there a backlash, a cost, or a permanent consequence?

The best power system limitations make characters creative. When a character can't punch through a problem, they strategize. This creates interesting conflicts and memorable battles.

## Step 6: Give Your Protagonist an Edge

In a world where many people use the same power system, your protagonist needs something unique that allows them to overcome stronger opponents.

**The edge doesn't have to break the rules.** It can be:
- A clever interpretation of the rules (like Asa's anti-magic in Black Clover, which negates magic by understanding it)
- A unique ability or inborn talent
- An unconventional approach or perspective  
- A hidden potential that must be developed
- A combination of skills that others haven't tried

**Questions to ask:**
- What unique ability, trait, or perspective does your protagonist have?
- How does this edge align with their personality or backstory?
- Is it something they start with, or must they actively develop it?
- Does this advantage create unique strategies in battles?
- Does the edge come with drawbacks or challenges?
- Can other characters replicate or counter this edge?

## Step 7: Simplify Your System

Your power system should be easy to grasp at first glance, even if it has deep complexity underneath.

**Test:**
- Can you explain your system in one sentence?
- Can it grow naturally over time without feeling forced?
- Is it easy to understand but hard to master?

Start simple; build complexity. Your readers should feel like they "get it" after chapter 1, even if they discover nuances in chapter 50.

## Step 8: Add Advanced Techniques

Advanced techniques are the pinnacle of your power system: forbidden spells, hidden transformations, ultimate moves. These are moments of revelation—the culmination of a character's struggles.

**Examples:** Bankai from Bleach, Domain Expansion from Jiujutsu Kaisen, Rasengan from Naruto.

**Key:** Advanced techniques should feel like natural extensions of the system, not random additions.

**Questions to ask:**
- What makes these techniques special?
- How are they discovered or taught?
- Do they carry extra costs or risks?
- Do they bend or break existing rules?
- Are they accessible to everyone, or only a select few?

## Step 9: Test the Logic

Take your system and throw it into every difficult scenario imaginable. Let villains exploit loopholes. Let heroes push its limits.

**Critical rule: Never break your own rules.**

If you've spent 200 pages establishing a power system, and then a character does something that contradicts everything you've said, your readers will lose trust in you.

**Test scenarios:**
- What would a genius villain do with this system?
- What about a desperate hero with nothing to lose?
- What if someone had unlimited resources?
- What if someone lacked all talent but trained obsessively?
- Can you logically explain every major ability shown in the story?

Run thought experiments. Create sample battles. Find weak points before readers do. Logical consistency builds trust. When audiences believe in your system, they believe in your world.

## IX. Ecological Expansion: The Dungeon as a Living System

Drawing from the *Dungeon Meshi* analysis, your world’s Flux‑beasts and Void Zones can form a self‑regulating ecosystem. This adds a new layer to the power system: the environment itself fights back.

### A. The Dungeon’s Immune Response

When adventurers damage the dungeon (break walls, kill monsters, cast spells), the dungeon “heals” through **dungeon cleaners** – tiny Flux‑beasts that consume debris and secrete material that rebuilds structures. This is not magic; it is biology. The dungeon is a super‑organism.

| Element | Real‑World Parallel | In‑World Function |
|---------|---------------------|-------------------|
| **Dungeon cleaners** | Decomposers (fungi, bacteria) + immune cells (white blood cells) | Eat corpses and debris; secrete minerals that repair walls |
| **Mana veins** | Circulatory system (blood vessels) | Transport Flux from ley lines to every part of the dungeon |
| **Monster food chain** | Ecosystem predator‑prey relationships | Controls population; prevents overpopulation that would spill onto the surface |
| **Void Zones** | Scar tissue (fibrosis) | Permanent damage from overuse of magic; becomes a new habitat for corrupted beasts |

### B. How This Affects the Power System

- **Flux debt is not abstract.** When a mage casts a spell, the dungeon’s immune system is weakened. Too much magic in one area creates a Void Zone – a scar that will never fully heal.
- **Monsters have ecological niches.** The living armor is a colony of mollusks; the mimic is a hermit‑crab‑like creature that hides in chests; treasure bugs are parasitoids that lay eggs inside mimics. Each monster’s behavior can be explained by biology, not just “magic did it.”
- **Adventurers are part of the food chain.** When they die, their bodies become fertilizer. The dungeon does not care about good or evil; it only cares about balance.

### C. Character Implications

- **Senshi (if you had an equivalent)** would be a mage who learned to live *with* the dungeon, not against it. His cooking is a form of ecological stewardship.
- **Marcille’s black magic** is like introducing an invasive species – it works in the short term but destabilizes the entire ecosystem.
- **The Lunatic Magician (your Seraphine or Covenant leader)** is not just evil; he is trying to control the dungeon’s immune system for his own purposes, like a cancer that rewrites the host’s DNA.

## X. The Meta Layer (Optional Expansion)

The video introduces a **sixth type** of power system: **Meta**, where the system acknowledges that it is a system (e.g., *Undertale*’s save/load mechanics). You currently have no meta elements, and that is fine. However, you could add a subtle meta layer by having **anomalies (regressors, transmigrators)** be aware of narrative tropes. For example:

- A regressor might say, “I know how this story goes. The hero always wins in the end.” This acknowledges the trope without breaking the fourth wall.
- A transmigrator might try to exploit “power system loopholes” they remember from games, only to discover that reality is not a game – a humbling moment.

If you add this, keep it **subtle and rare**. Too much meta will destroy immersion.
