# The Chasm Vision Scene

*Ryo's consciousness fractures and reforms. The Azure Wind becomes real. Two selves become one.*

---

## The Fall

Ryo's sword shattered.

Not broke—*shattered*. Lightning arced wild, then died. The Covenant battle‑mage's final blow had caught him mid‑dash, and now he was falling. Not through air. Through something else.

The battlefield vanished. The screams of his comrades faded. He was tumbling down a dark chasm, walls of fractured memories pressing in from all sides. He tried to reach for his lightning—nothing. His mana was there, but the connection was… wrong. Distant.

*Am I dead?*

No. He was still falling. But the falling had become slow. Floating.

---

## The Flashes

He saw his arm first.

A blue glow wrapped around his forearm and torso—not lightning, but something else. A shape. A familiar shape. His sleeve had turned a deep, brilliant azure, and a white stripe ran across his chest. *No. That's not my arm. That's—*

His shoes changed next. Red. Bright red, with a white strap and a golden buckle. He remembered those shoes. He'd drawn them a thousand times in the margins of his school notebooks.

Then his head. The top of his skull tingled, and he felt it—quills. Not hair. Quills. Spiky, defiant, pointing backward like a blue flame frozen mid‑flicker.

And then he smiled.

Not his smile. Not the weary, guarded smile of a transmigrator who had seen too much. A different smile. A cocky, reckless, *I‑know‑I'm‑the‑fastest‑thing‑alive* smile.

The Azure Dash's smile.

*No way.*

---

## The Child

He landed.

Not hard—he floated down, limbs spread like a starfish, until his back touched something solid. The chasm was gone. He was lying on a wooden floor. Dim light. The smell of dust and old electronics.

He pushed himself up into a sitting position—legs curled, hands braced behind him. The Azure Dash's sitting pose. The one from the old cover art.

He looked down at his hands.

Small. Soft. A child's hands.

He was wearing a faded t‑shirt with a blue blur printed on the front. Jeans with a scuff on the knee. His old sneakers—not the red ones, the cheap canvas ones he'd worn to school.

*I'm him again. I'm the kid.*

---

## The TV

Across the room, on a low wooden table, sat a CRT television. Fat‑backed, screen dusty, power light dark. Next to it, a game console he hadn't seen in twenty years. A cartridge was already inserted. The label was worn, but he could still read it:

*Heroes of the Azure Wind.*

"No," he whispered. "That's not real. That's not—I'm not there anymore."

He stood. His legs were shaky. He walked to the TV and placed his palm on the screen.

A spark jumped from his finger. The screen flickered. Glowed. The title music began—tinny, nostalgic, a melody that bypassed his ears and went straight into his chest.

He picked up the controller. It fit perfectly in his small hands.

*I spent so long trying to be someone great. Someone else.* He pressed start. *What happened to trying to be me?*

---

## The Second Controller

He played for what felt like hours. Maybe minutes. Time didn't exist here. He raced through Green Hill Zone analogues, spin‑dashed through loops, collected rings that shimmered like memory fragments.

Then he saw it.

Behind the TV, half‑hidden in shadows, another controller. And next to it, a second cartridge.

*Azure Wind 2: Twin Souls.*

Multiplayer.

He reached for it—and another hand reached at the same time.

He looked up.

His warrior self was kneeling beside him. Taller. Scarred. The electric sword was strapped to his back, dim and quiet. His eyes—Ryo's eyes—were tired.

"You're me," the child said.

"I was you," the warrior replied. "I spent so long fighting myself. Trying to understand who I was. Who I *should* be. The me who once was. The me who is now. The me who will be."

The child looked at the second cartridge. "It didn't have to be me or me."

The warrior picked up the second controller. "It could always be us."

They sat side by side. Inserted the cartridge. And played together.

---

## The Awakening

The chasm shattered.

Ryo gasped, eyes snapping open. He was on the battlefield, face down in mud and blood. His sword was gone. His body screamed.

But above him, something glowed.

A figure made of light—blue, fierce, impossibly fast—hovered in the air. It had quills. Red shoes. A white stripe across its chest. It looked down at him with a proud, wordless expression.

Then it winked.

And threw a ring.

The golden loop arced through the smoke and fire, spinning end over end. Ryo reached up. Caught it.

The ring melted into his chest.

Not warmth — something deeper than warmth. A pressure that moved inward from his sternum and kept going, finding every crack where the two selves had been grinding against each other for years, filling them the way heated metal fills a mold. He made a sound he didn't decide to make.

The child and the warrior didn't merge into something new and alien. They simply stopped arguing. Stopped insisting they were the only real one. The argument had been the wound; the silence was the sealing of it.

They weren't two anymore.

He rose to his feet. Lightning crackled—not wild, not fractured. Controlled. Two colors now: blinding white and soft gold. True lightning and something else. Something new.

He raised his hand. Four copies of himself flickered into existence around him, each wreathed in arcs of gold and white. Each holding a blade of solidified storm.

The Covenant battle‑mage took a step back. His face did something complicated — recognition without name, the look of someone watching a thing that doesn't have a category yet.

"What are you?"

Ryo considered the question. Not because it was difficult. Because for the first time in as long as he could remember, it wasn't.

He smiled. Not the child's smile. Not the warrior's smile.

His own.

"Complete."
