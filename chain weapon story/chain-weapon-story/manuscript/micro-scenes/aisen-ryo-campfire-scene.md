# Aisen and Ryo: The Campfire Conversation

*Late in the adventuring years. Ryo reveals he is a transmigrator. Identity, sacrifice, and the cost of becoming whole.*

---

The fire had burned down to embers, casting the camp in a warm, flickering orange. Aisen sat with his back against a fallen log, his chain coiled in his lap, fingers absently checking each link for stress fractures. Ryo sat across from him, knees drawn to his chest, his electric sword planted point‑first in the dirt beside him.

They had been silent for a long time.

Ryo spoke first. Quietly. Like he was confessing something he'd never said aloud.

"I'm not from here."

Aisen didn't look up. "Not from the Heartland? Or not from the continent?"

"Not from this world."

The words hung in the air. Aisen's fingers paused on a link. Then resumed.

"I figured," he said.

Ryo's head snapped up. "You *figured*?"

"You don't flinch at the same things we do." Aisen's voice was even, unhurried. "The first time you saw a griffin, you looked at it like you were admiring a painting. Not like a threat. Most people run. You tilted your head and asked what it tasted like."

Ryo almost laughed. "That's what gave me away?"

"That, and you talk to yourself when you think no one's listening." Aisen finally looked up. His eyes were calm, unreadable. "Two voices. Arguing."

Ryo's throat tightened. "You heard that?"

"I hear everything. It's kept me alive."

The silence returned, heavier now. Ryo stared into the embers.

"You're not going to ask me the obvious question," he said.

"Which is?"

"Why should you care? Where I came from. What powers I have. If I can teach them to you."

Aisen shrugged. "You'll tell me if you want to. If not, it's not my business."

Ryo studied him. "Most people would want to know."

"Most people aren't me." Aisen leaned back against the log. "I've learned that knowledge you have to beg for is usually worth less than knowledge that's offered freely. So I wait."

Ryo was quiet for a moment. Then: "You're strange."

"So I've been told."

The fire popped. A log shifted, sending a spray of sparks toward the dark canopy above. Ryo watched them rise and fade.

"Do you ever feel like you're two people?" he asked.

Aisen didn't answer immediately. He turned a link over in his fingers, feeling the small notch where a blade had nicked it months ago.

"Sometimes," he said. "The boy who hunted in the forest. The man who's killed. They don't always agree."

"For me, it's worse." Ryo's voice was barely a whisper. "I remember a life where none of this existed. No Flux. No Weave. No monsters. Just… school. Video games. A family who doesn't know I'm gone."

Aisen's fingers stilled. "That sounds like a ghost that doesn't leave."

"It doesn't." Ryo hugged his knees tighter. "And the longer I'm here, the more I wonder—*am I him?* The kid who stayed up too late playing games? *Or am I the person I've become?* The one who's killed. Who's bled. Who's watched people die."

"Both," Aisen said simply.

Ryo looked at him. "Both?"

"You're both. You can't cut away the past without bleeding out." Aisen set the chain down and folded his arms. "The question isn't which one you are. It's whether you can carry both without breaking."

Ryo's jaw tightened. "I used to play this game. *Heroes of the Azure Wind.* I'd stay up way too late, trying to beat the final level. I wasn't good at it. But I loved it."

"You miss it."

"I miss *him*." Ryo's voice cracked. "The kid who didn't know about war. Who thought the biggest problem was a math test. I spent so long trying to be someone great. Someone else." He laughed, bitter and hollow. "What happened to trying to be me?"

Aisen said nothing. He let the silence breathe.

After a long moment, Ryo continued. "The warrior—the me of this world—he's strong. But he's also tired. He's seen things the kid couldn't imagine. And they don't agree on what to do. They fight. All the time. Over control. Over fear. Over whether to run or stand."

"So which one is you?"

Ryo's hands trembled. "I don't know anymore."

Aisen reached over and picked up his chain. The links clicked softly as he ran his thumb along the worn metal.

"I've never had that problem," he said. "I know who I am."

"How?"

"Because I built myself." He held up the chain. "Piece by piece. Every link I traded for, every scar I earned, every lesson I learned from people who had no reason to teach me—it's mine. No one else's."

Ryo stared at the chain. "That sounds lonely."

"It is." Aisen coiled it back into his lap. "But I'd rather be lonely and whole than surrounded and split."

Ryo was quiet for a long time. The fire had dimmed further, and the night pressed in around them, full of unseen sounds.

"What if there was a way to stop fighting?" he asked. "To become one person. Not the kid, not the warrior. Something new."

Aisen's eyes narrowed. "There's always a cost."

"I know." Ryo's voice was steady now, almost resigned. "Both of them would have to… let go."

"Let go of what?"

"Themselves."

Aisen studied him for a long moment. Then he nodded, slowly.

"You're not going to tell me it's a bad idea?" Ryo asked.

"I'm not you." Aisen stood, stretching his shoulders. "I don't know what it's like to carry two lives. But I know that every choice has a consequence. If you make that choice, make sure it's yours."

Ryo looked up at him. "You're a strange man, Aisen."

Aisen almost smiled. "So I've been told."

He turned and walked toward the edge of the camp, where the shadows were deepest. Ryo watched him go.

"Thank you," Ryo called after him. "For listening."

Aisen paused. He didn't turn around.

"Someone listened to me once," he said. "Pass it on."

Then he disappeared into the darkness, leaving Ryo alone with the dying embers and the weight of a choice he hadn't yet made.
