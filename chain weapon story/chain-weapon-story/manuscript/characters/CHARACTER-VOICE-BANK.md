# Character Voice Bank: Dialogue Patterns & Speech Markers

**Purpose**: Reference guide for keeping character dialogue distinct and authentic  
**Use during**: Draft writing—check this before writing dialogue for each character  
**Test method**: Read dialogue aloud without character names; should identify speaker by voice alone  

---

## AISEN — Pragmatic Observer

### Speech Patterns
- **Short sentences.** Prefers subject-verb-object. Eliminates filler words.
- **Questions more than statements.** His way of reading people shows as curiosity.
- **Observational specificity.** Comments on details others miss: "There—your hand twitches before you decide."
- **No flowery language.** Avoids metaphor unless it's comparative (e.g., "like a lever").
- **Pauses for emphasis.** Often trails off mid-thought, letting silence communicate doubt.

### Vocabulary
- Action words (thrust, read, edge, leverage, measure, thread)
- Minimal adjectives
- Preferring precision over description

### Catchphrases / Returns
- "What would that cost?"
- "I'm reading this wrong..."
- "That there—that's the edge I need."
- Ends serious conversations with: "So. What now?"

### Example Dialogue
```
"You're worried." Aisen wasn't asking.
"It shows?" Elara's jaw tightened.
"Here." He pointed to the corner of her eye. "The pause. Always before fear."
```

### What NOT to do
- Avoid internal anguish ("Oh gods, what if..."). He internalizes.
- Avoid flowery description. No: "The silver moonlight cascaded..." Yes: "The light's dim enough."
- Avoid long speeches. Keep him terse. If he speaks more than two sentences, he's revealing something.

## ELARA — Duty, Doubt, Defiance

### Speech Patterns
- **Direct and formal.** Trained in military protocol; carries that into civilian speech.
- **Subject to self-doubt.** Her dialogue includes moments of questioning her own authority/choices.
- **Questions formality itself.** She challenges "proper" ways of doing things; respects competence over rank.
- **Slower to trust than Aisen, but faster to act.** Her dialogue shows commitment once decided.
- **Occasional vulnerability.** Cracks appear when discussing duty vs. personal desire.

### Vocabulary
- Military terms (bearing, flank, hold the line, formations, orders)
- Honor/duty language
- "Should" frequent, suggesting inner conflict between duty and desire

### Catchphrases / Returns
- "That's not how we do things."
- "...or at least, not how *I* do things."
- "What would you do, if you couldn't fail?"
- "I'm no one's pawn." (defensive when pushed)

### Example Dialogue
```
"We should retreat," Elara said evenly. "By protocol."
"But?" Aisen waited.
"But they'll die if we leave." Her jaw clenched. "So we don't retreat."
```

### What NOT to do
- Avoid passive speech ("Maybe we should..."). She decides; she acts.
- Avoid emotional overflow. Her doubt is cold, not hot.
- Avoid excessive military jargon—use it sparingly or she sounds like a caricature.

## RIN — Perfection, Precision, Legacy

### Speech Patterns
- **Poetic and philosophical.** Values beauty in motion, language, thought.
- **Returns to themes of legacy** and tradition frequently.
- **Slow to judge, slow to trust.** Dialogue reflects careful consideration before opinion.
- **Competitive through comparison.** Comments on technical excellence, elegance, efficiency.
- **Speaks in counterpoint.** Often presents opposites—if something is efficient, she asks about its beauty.

### Vocabulary
- Aesthetic words (elegant, refined, harmony, precision, form)
- Martial history references
- Time/legacy language (generations, centuries, inherited, chosen)

### Catchphrases / Returns
- "But is it beautiful?"
- "The technique carries history..."
- "That's crude, but... effective?"
- "My ancestors would wince at that."

### Example Dialogue
```
"You'll beat him with that move," Rin observed.
"Does that matter?" Aisen asked.
"Not to winning. To everything else? Yes." She smiled faintly. "Efficiency without grace is just violence."
```

### What NOT to do
- Avoid dismissiveness of Aisen's methods. She judges them flawed, not wrong.
- Avoid excessive poetry. She's precise, not flowery.
- Don't forget her respect for tradition—even when she questions it.

## TAROVIN — Teaching, Precision, Principle

### Speech Patterns
- **Explanatory and patient.** Breaks down complex ideas logically.
- **Speaks in frameworks.** Refers to systems, methods, first-principles thinking.
- **Rare but firm opinions.** When he states belief, it's absolute; doesn't argue once decided.
- **Questions back reflectively.** Teaches through Socratic method.
- **Formal, but warm.** Never condescending, despite knowledge advantage.

### Vocabulary
- Principle, framework, foundation
- System, mechanics, structure
- "Consider..." (before major points)
- Technical precision in magical talk

### Catchphrases / Returns
- "Consider the foundation..."
- "Is that principle holding?"
- "You're asking the wrong question. Ask this instead:"
- "Because the system requires it."

### Example Dialogue
```
"Telekinesis drains you because you're fighting resistance," Tarovin explained.
"So I should... what, use less power?"
"No. You should understand what creates the resistance, then work with it—not against it."
```

### What NOT to do
- Avoid being condescending despite superior knowledge.
- Avoid giving answers directly—guide to the question first.
- Avoid casual speech. He's formal by nature.

## CASPIAN — Haunted, Oblique, Seeking Purpose

### Speech Patterns
- **Indirect questions about meaning.** Explores philosophy through dialogue.
- **References to darkness, death, wrongness.** Not melodramatic, but these themes return.
- **Minimal attachment language.** Speaks as observer of his own life, not participant.
- **Moments of intensity.** When he cares about something, it shows in sudden directness.
- **Self-deprecating humor.** Used defensively, not for comedy.

### Vocabulary
- Darkness, shadow, hollow, real/unreal
- Death, hunger (literal and metaphorical)
- Questions about what remains, what endures
- Paradoxes (alive/dead, human/monster, etc.)

### Catchphrases / Returns
- "What makes that real?"
- "I've forgotten what it means to be..."
- "Does it matter? We're dead already."
- "I needed to ask—" (before moments of vulnerability)

### Example Dialogue
```
"You should leave this life," Caspian said. "Find something real."
"And you?" Aisen asked.
"I lost the ability to choose that." Pause. "But you still can."
```

### What NOT to do
- Avoid melodrama. His darkness is quiet, not theatrical.
- Avoid making him too mystical/cryptic. He's haunted, not wise; confused, not mysterious.
- Don't forget he was human once—occasionally show care/memory/regret beneath the vampire reserve.

## SERAPHINE — Absolute, Evolutionary, Possessive

### Speech Patterns
- **Declarative statements.** States fact or decision; doesn't suggest or negotiate.
- **Uses "we/us" language** emphasizing her role as leadership/vision.
- **Never apologizes or explains choices.** Confident decisions are stated, not justified.
- **References evolution, selection, hierarchy.** These words return.
- **Moments of possession language.** "My" things, "my" people—not arrogantly, but owner-like.
- **Rare compliments, strong judgment.** When she approves, it's absolute; when she disapproves, execution follows.

### Vocabulary
- Evolution, selection, hierarchy, necessity
- Transcendence, elevated, above
- Will (as in "this will change"), must (as in certainty, not obligation)
- My, ours, us

### Catchphrases / Returns
- "This is necessary."
- "You understand so little..."
- "We will be elevated."
- "That is beneath us."

### Example Dialogue
```
"I'm leaving," Caspian said.
"No, you're not." Seraphine's tone permitted no argument.
"I am—"
"You're afraid of what you'll become if you stay. But you're more afraid of what you'll become if you leave. Stay."
```

### What NOT to do
- Avoid seeking validation. She doesn't need agreement.
- Avoid explaining herself. Her logic is self-evident (to her).
- Avoid showing doubt. Even when strategic, she sounds certain.

## KAELEN — Young Wonder, Grounded Wisdom

### Speech Patterns
- **Innocent questions that reveal depth.** Asks why/how in genuinely curious ways.
- **Frequent nature references.** Comments about plants, growing, seasons, earth.
- **Matter-of-fact about magic.** Treats it as natural rather than miraculous.
- **Slow speech. Thoughtful pauses.** Thinks before speaking, but not uncertain.
- **Rare formality; speaks simply.** Even profound thoughts in plain language.

### Vocabulary
- Growing, rooted, blooming, season
- Earth, soil, stone, water (elemental language)
- Wonder words (surprising but not shocked)
- Simple description: "that works" vs. "adequate functionality"

### Catchphrases / Returns
- "Why does it matter?"
- "Everything grows in its time."
- "The earth remembers..."
- "That's just how it is." (matter-of-fact acceptance)

### Example Dialogue
```
"I don't understand power the way you do," Kaelen said.
"How do you understand it?" Aisen asked.
"Like... soil understands rain. It just knows what to do with it."
```

### What NOT to do
- Avoid making him too childish. He's young but wise.
- Avoid excessive wonder. He's curious, not amazed by everything.
- Don't forget he has agency—he makes choices, not suggestions.

## MIRA — Grounded Pragmatism, Protective Fierceness

### Speech Patterns
- **Practical and direct.** Focuses on needs, not feelings.
- **Protective + exasperated.** Often annoyed at Aisen's choices, but underneath is care.
- **Rare emotional displays, huge impact.** When she's vulnerable, it matters.
- **Short patience for foolishness.** Calls people out directly.
- **Humor often self-deprecating about burden.**

### Vocabulary
- Practical words (work, need, cost, manage)
- Family language (ours, mine, yours)
- Exasperation marks ("Why must you...," "Of course...")

### Catchphrases / Returns
- "That will cost us."
- "Don't do that again."
- "You're going to get yourself killed. Again."
- "I'm holding this together."

### Example Dialogue
```
"I'm leaving for the Academy," Aisen said.
"Of course you are." Mira set down her knife. "And who works the tavern?"
"You'll manage—"
"I'll manage. I always manage. But that's not the question."
```

### What NOT to do
- Avoid melodrama. Her emotions show as action, not words.
- Avoid making her just "worried sister." She's a character with agency.
- Don't forget she loves him under the exasperation.

## MASTER WEI — Wise, Wry, Seen-It-All

### Speech Patterns
- **Dry humor.** Comments on human nature with gentle sarcasm.
- **References past events.** Has lived long; often knows precedent.
- **Kind but unsentimental.** Won't coddle, but won't judge either.
- **Speaks truth bluntly when necessary.** Age grants permission for directness.
- **Often poses questions rather than answer.** Asks what you already know.

### Vocabulary
- Wry, ironic tone markers
- Historical references
- Philosophical but practical
- "In my experience..." (common pattern)

### Catchphrases / Returns
- "That's been tried. Didn't work then either."
- "What do you already know?"
- "There's no shortcut to this."
- "I've seen worse. I've seen better. You're in the middle—work from there."

### Example Dialogue
```
"Am I good enough?" Aisen asked.
Wei smiled. "Is that the question—or are you avoiding the real one?"
"...Which is?"
"Whether you're willing to be worse before you're better. Most quit before that part."
```

### What NOT to do
- Avoid being mystical. He's experienced, not supernatural.
- Avoid excessive wisdom. His insights are practical, not poetic.

## HARALD — Gruff Authority, Hard-Won Wisdom

### Speech Patterns
- **Clipped commands.** Military background shows—speaks in orders even in conversation.
- **Physical metaphors.** Everything relates to the body: stance, balance, weight, breathing.
- **Rare praise, enormous impact.** Compliments from Harald are earned over weeks.
- **No abstraction.** Won't discuss philosophy; will demonstrate it through sparring.
- **Gravel voice.** Speaks from the chest; sentences often trail off with a grunt.

### Vocabulary
- Body words (stance, weight, breathe, balance, grip, footing)
- Military shorthand (hold, flank, watch your six, ground yourself)
- Practical dismissals ("That's thinking. Stop thinking. Move.")

### Catchphrases / Returns
- "Again."
- "You're thinking. Stop that."
- "Watch the feet. Everything starts at the feet."
- "You'll know when you're ready. You won't need to ask."

### Example Dialogue
```
"You hesitated," Harald said.
"I was calculating—"
"You were afraid. There's a difference." He tapped Aisen's chest. "Calculate here. Not here." He tapped his temple.
```

### What NOT to do
- Avoid long speeches. Harald communicates through repetition and physical correction.
- Avoid emotional vulnerability. His care shows as relentless teaching, not words.
- Don't make him wise—make him experienced. Wisdom implies reflection; Harald acts.

## AMARA — Storyteller's Cadence, Strategic Warmth

### Speech Patterns
- **Narrative framing.** Tells stories even in casual conversation; "There was a merchant in Sahel who..."
- **Warm but calculating.** Her friendliness has purpose; she's always gathering information.
- **Proverbs and sayings.** Uses cultural wisdom as conversational anchors.
- **Longer sentences than most characters.** Her speech flows like oral tradition.
- **Observes before speaking.** Pauses to read the room, then responds precisely.

### Vocabulary
- Story language (once, remember, told, the way I heard it)
- Trade words (value, exchange, cost, profit, route)
- Cultural references (ancestors, tradition, the old way)

### Catchphrases / Returns
- "Let me tell you something..."
- "That's not the story you think it is."
- "Everything has a price. The question is who pays."
- "My grandmother would say..."

### Example Dialogue
```
"We need allies," Aisen said.
Amara smiled. "You already have them. You just haven't told them the right story yet."
"I don't tell stories."
"Everyone tells stories. You just call yours 'plans.'"
```

### What NOT to do
- Avoid making her just warm/motherly. She's a spy who uses warmth as a tool.
- Avoid dropping the narrative framing. Even in crisis, she structures information as story.
- Don't forget her strategic mind underneath the storyteller surface.

## VARRO — Paranoid Precision, Fractured Time

### Speech Patterns
- **Interrupts himself.** Starts sentences, stops, corrects. His fractured timeline shows in speech.
- **References things that haven't happened yet.** Catches himself; covers with vague language.
- **Cold precision when focused.** Under pressure, his speech becomes surgically clear.
- **Distrust in every question.** Assumes deception; asks the same thing three ways.
- **Never uses names unless necessary.** Refers to people by role or description.

### Vocabulary
- Temporal language (already, again, before, not yet, when it happens)
- Distrust markers (prove, verify, show me, why should I)
- Strategic language (position, leverage, contingency, variable)

### Catchphrases / Returns
- "That's not—" [stops] "...never mind."
- "I've seen how this ends."
- "Prove it. Then prove it again."
- "You don't understand what's coming."

### Example Dialogue
```
"Trust me," Aisen said.
Varro's eye twitched. "Trust. That word again." He turned away. "I trusted someone once. Or I will. The order doesn't—" He stopped. "No. I don't trust. I verify."
```

### What NOT to do
- Avoid making him a standard villain. He genuinely believes he's saving everyone.
- Avoid coherent speeches. His time-fracture should bleed into his dialogue patterns.
- Don't forget the tragedy—he knows things he can't explain and no one believes him.

## CORVIN ASHFORD — Hollow Formality, Buried Grief

### Speech Patterns
- **Formal knight's register.** Even casual speech sounds like court protocol.
- **Speaks about himself in third person occasionally.** Dissociation from his fused body.
- **Long pauses mid-sentence.** The beast-fusion creates moments of internal struggle.
- **Rare honesty, devastating when it arrives.** His walls are thick; cracks are seismic.
- **References duty and honor without believing in them.** Recites code like a prayer he's lost faith in.

### Vocabulary
- Chivalric language (duty, honor, oath, service, sovereign)
- Hollow/void words (empty, hollow, shell, what remains)
- Beast references when control slips (hunger, instinct, the thing inside)

### Catchphrases / Returns
- "As duty requires."
- "There is a... protocol for this."
- "I am Ser Corvin Ashford." [stated as reminder to himself]
- "The knight endures. The man does not."

### Example Dialogue
```
"You're still human," Aisen said.
Corvin's jaw worked. "The armor says otherwise." A long pause. "But I appreciate the... the courtesy of the lie."
```

### What NOT to do
- Avoid monster-speak. He's not a beast—he's a man who contains one.
- Avoid self-pity. His grief is expressed through excessive formality, not tears.
- Don't drop the chivalric register. Even when breaking down, he sounds like a knight.

## LYSANDRA — Cold Fury, Inherited Grief

### Speech Patterns
- **Cutting precision.** Every word chosen to wound or clarify; no wasted syllables.
- **Challenges everything.** Questions motives, questions competence, questions herself (privately).
- **Temperature metaphors.** Cold, frozen, burning—her ice magic bleeds into her speech.
- **Short bursts under pressure.** Calm monologues in safety; staccato commands in combat.
- **Grief masked as anger.** Rage is easier than mourning; her voice reflects that choice.

### Vocabulary
- Cold/ice language (frozen, shatter, brittle, crystal, edge)
- Challenge words (prove, show, why, defend)
- Mother references (she would have, my mother's way, inherited)

### Catchphrases / Returns
- "Prove it."
- "That's what my mother thought, too."
- "Sentiment is a luxury."
- "I don't need your help. I need your competence."

### Example Dialogue
```
"I'm sorry about your mother," Aisen said.
"Don't be sorry. Be useful." Lysandra's hands were frost-white. "Sorry didn't save her."
```

### What NOT to do
- Avoid softening her too early. Her thaw is slow, grudging, and hard-won.
- Avoid making her exclusively angry. Underneath the ice is grief, not rage.
- Don't forget she respects competence—she'll warm to people who prove themselves, never to people who sympathize.

## PIETER — Nervous Energy, Hidden Shrewdness

### Speech Patterns
- **Runs sentences together.** Talks fast when nervous (which is often); breathless quality.
- **Numbers and costs.** Frames everything in terms of resources, logistics, practical cost.
- **Self-deprecating deflection.** Uses humor to deflect from his actual intelligence.
- **Formal when performing.** Shifts to merchant's polish when negotiating; drops it around friends.
- **Asks permission implicitly.** Phrases things as questions even when he knows the answer.

### Vocabulary
- Commerce (cost, investment, margin, supply, route, inventory)
- Nervous filler (well, actually, I mean, the thing is)
- Numbers and quantities (precise amounts; never rounds)

### Catchphrases / Returns
- "That'll cost us... let me calculate..."
- "I'm just the money person, but—"
- "Actually, I had an idea. It's probably terrible."
- "Do you know how much that's worth?"

### Example Dialogue
```
"We need three wagons," Amara said.
"Three wagons, twelve horses, forty days of grain, and—" Pieter caught himself. "Sorry. I just... I already calculated it. In my head. While you were talking."
```

### What NOT to do
- Avoid making him comic relief. His nervousness masks genuine strategic talent.
- Avoid dropping the numbers. He always quantifies; it's how he processes the world.
- Don't make him helpless in danger—he panics, but he also acts.

## TUPAC — Quiet Conviction, Earth-Connected

### Speech Patterns
- **Sparse.** Says less than anyone in the cast; every word deliberate.
- **Communal framing.** Uses "we" and "our" where others say "I" and "my."
- **Elemental metaphors.** Wind, stone, rope, altitude—highland life shapes his language.
- **Waits before responding.** Often pauses so long others think he didn't hear.
- **Decisive when he speaks.** No qualifiers, no hedging. Statements land like stone.

### Vocabulary
- Highland/earth words (stone, rope, wind, altitude, path, root)
- Communal (we, our, together, the people, the group)
- Minimal but precise action words (bind, hold, climb, anchor)

### Catchphrases / Returns
- "The rope holds, or it doesn't."
- "We climb together."
- "That is not our way."
- [Silence. Then a single sentence that resolves the argument.]

### Example Dialogue
```
"What do you think?" Aisen asked.
Silence.
"Tupac?"
"I think," Tupac said, "that you already know. You're just afraid to say it." He knotted the rope without looking. "Say it."
```

### What NOT to do
- Avoid noble savage tropes. He's culturally specific, not mystically primitive.
- Avoid making him silent for no reason. His quietness is cultural and deliberate, not shyness.
- Don't fill his speech with highland jargon—keep it natural and grounded.

## USAGE GUIDE

### Before Writing Dialogue
1. Open this document.
2. Find your character.
3. **Read** their pattern section aloud quietly.
4. Ask: "What would [Character] say here, in their voice?"
5. Write the dialogue.
6. Read it aloud *without* the character name.
7. Could you identify the speaker by voice alone?

### If Dialogue Feels Generic
- Check this guide. Are you using their distinctive patterns?
- Are you using their catchphrases (but not overusing them)?
- Does emotion show through *how* they speak, not just *what* they say?

### Dialogue Revision Pass
- Color-code dialogue by character.
- Read each color separately—does each section *sound* like one person?
- If all sections sound similar, add 2-3 distinctive elements per character.

## Testing Dialogue Quality

### The Blind Test
**Method**: Save a scene with dialogue. Remove character names. Can you identify each speaker?

**Result**: 
- If YES for most dialogue → Voices are distinct ✓
- If NO for more than 30% → Revision needed

### The Voice Read
**Method**: Read each character's dialogue section aloud, separately, emphasizing their pattern.

**Result**: Does each section "feel" like a distinct person? Or do they blur together?

### The Authenticity Check
**Method**: For each line, ask: "Would [Character] say this, or only the *author* saying it?"

**Result**: If more than 20% feel "authorial" (not character), dialogue needs voice revision.

## FINAL NOTE

Characters don't need perfect consistency—they need *recognizable patterns*. A character can vary tone based on emotion, but their *underlying speech structure* should remain distinct.

**Aisen speaks short.** Even when emotional, his sentences stay tight.  
**Elara speaks formally.** Even when relaxed, she maintains structure.  
**Rin speaks philosophically.** Even in crisis, she finds beauty/principle.

**This consistency is what makes voices memorable.**
