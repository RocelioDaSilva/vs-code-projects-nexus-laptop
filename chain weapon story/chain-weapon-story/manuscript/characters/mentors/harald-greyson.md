## Teaching Method: The Socratic Approach

### Philosophy of Education

**Harald's principle**: "I don't teach answers. I teach how to ask better questions."

### Teaching Structure

| Lesson Type | Method | Goal |
|-------------|--------|------|
| **Spear craft** | Physical practice + questioning "why" after each mistake | Understand principle, not memorize technique |
| **Literacy** | Read together, discuss text, argue about meaning | Make reading pleasurable, not obligation |
| **Philosophy** | Present situation, ask "what would you do?", explore consequences | Develop independent moral reasoning |
| **Self-knowledge** | Reflect on actions through questioning | Understand patterns in own behavior |

### Specific Teaching Moments

1. **Post-robbery scene (Chapter 3-4)**
   - Aisen kills in self-defense; carries trauma
   - Harald doesn't say "you were right to kill"
   - Instead: "How do you feel about what happened? What do you understand now you didn't before? What will you do differently?"
   - Helps Aisen integrate trauma into learning rather than dissociate

2. **Spear training (Chapters 4-7)**
   - 100+ techniques taught not as rote forms but as applications of principles
   - Each technique has a question: "What's your objective? How does position help that? What's the opponent's counter?"
   - Aisen learns to think tactically; spear becomes expression of understanding

3. **Literacy foundation (Chapters 4-12)**
   - Harald finds books without answers (philosophy, questions, stories)
   - Reads aloud; stops at interesting moments: "What do you think about that?"
   - Literacy becomes gateway to world of ideas, not just practical knowledge

4. **Moral compass development (Throughout)**
   - Never gives direct moral pronouncements
   - Presents dilemmas: "What troubles you about this? What's the right choice? Why?"
   - Trusts Aisen to work through moral questions with guidance, not instruction
