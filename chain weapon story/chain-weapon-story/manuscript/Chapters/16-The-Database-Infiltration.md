# CHAPTER 16: THE DATABASE INFILTRATION

The magical servers pulsed with a low blue-white rhythm, each pulse slightly offset from the one beside it, producing a visual shimmer that was almost hypnotic if you looked at it directly. Aisen didn't look at it directly. He kept his eyes on the terminal interface and navigated the directory tree.

"Four minutes twenty," Serath said. She was standing two paces behind him, her voice flattened to technical frequency — the conversational cadence entirely gone. This was what she sounded like when she was timing something. He'd known her for three days and he already recognized the register.

The server room was cold. The Academy's database infrastructure ran warm and the ventilation system overcompensated; the air had the slightly burnt ozone smell of active magical machinery running at sustained load. His fingers were two degrees cooler than normal already, which affected fine control. He'd internalized that as a variable.

The database tree was cleaner than he'd expected. Fourteen years of Torven's systematic organization. Top-level branches matched Serath's schematic almost exactly — Department Resources, Academic Administration, Restricted Administrative, and a fourth branch that wasn't in the schematic, labeled *Legacy Records*.

The micro-want was specific: provincial military allocation projections. If the Covenant had structured deployment plans for seven regions, those plans would tell the networks not just *that* suppression was coming but *when* and *where the concentrated force would be smallest* — which was the only information that would let the provincial organizations time their own structural moves correctly.

He opened Restricted Administrative. Department-level subfolders: Headmaster Affairs, Security Protocols, Staff Oversight, Military Liaison —

Military Liaison.

"Four minutes," Serath said.

The Military Liaison subfolder had six items. He scanned the filenames. *Combat Doctrine Evolution – Historical and Current.* That was a support document; he'd read secondary summaries. *Covenant-Academy Authority Integration.* Administrative framework, lower priority. *Military Strategy Framework – Covenant Operations in Resistant Regions.* Priority. *Provincial Military Allocation Projections – Semester 3.* Priority, exact target. *Headmaster Emergency Protocols.* Secondary. *Weapons Development Summary – Current Status Overview.* Possible.

He flagged Combat Doctrine and Military Strategy first — largest, slowest to transfer, needed maximum remaining window. Initiated. The transfer counter appeared in the corner of the interface: 1.2MB/s, which was slower than Serath had estimated.

"Both files transferring," he said quietly.

"I see them," she said. "That rate is lower than projected. We'll need to decide about the third file at two minutes."

He moved to Provincial Allocation while the first two transferred. Opened a summary view: regional headers with attached operational sub-documents. The regions listed were named not by the regular provincial designations the Academy used in academic materials but by Covenant internal codes — Sector 4-North, Sector 7, Sector 11-South — and cross-referenced against numbered Force Allocation Units. Seven sectors. Projected initial operation windows with staggered start dates across a fourteen-week period.

His hand was over the file before he'd decided to open it fully. He caught the involuntary motion and stopped.

Document first. He initiated a screencapture of the summary view, flagged the primary allocation document to transfer as file three.

"Three minutes fifteen," Serath said.

The Combat Doctrine file finished. Military Strategy was at 73%. Provincial Allocation queued.

He scanned the Interface while Military Strategy was transferring. The Legacy Records branch at the top level caught his attention again — it hadn't been in Serath's schematic. Three days of preparation versus whatever Torven had been building for fourteen years; there would be gaps. He moved to the top-level branch and opened it.

Subfolder names: *Decommissioned Operations*, *Pre-Covenant Reference Materials*, *Sealed Historical Documents*. Forty-seven items in the sealed subfolder alone, each marked with access restrictions two levels above the Military Liaison materials. He photographed the subfolder listing with the camera on his right index finger without opening anything — the filenames were visible. Cynthia's analysts would have context for them that he didn't.

"Two minutes thirty," Serath said. "Three seconds on Military Strategy."

Military Strategy completed. Provincial Allocation at 44%.

He had two files he hadn't touched: the Headmaster Emergency Protocols (Serath had flagged these as potentially critical if the network ever needed to understand how the Academy would respond to an incident) and the Weapons Development Summary (large enough that the summary was still 600KB). He checked the transfer rate. Provincial Allocation would complete at one minute forty. That left twenty seconds for one more file.

Emergency Protocols: 1.4MB. Too large.

Weapons Development Summary: 600KB. Twenty seconds at this rate: possible.

He made the decision before he reached the thought about it — initiated the Emergency Protocols transfer at reduced priority, flagging Weapons Development Summary for immediate queue behind Provincial Allocation.

"Two minutes," Serath said. Her voice had a quality in it — not concern, something closer to attention sharpening to a point.

Provincial Allocation completed at one minute fifty. Weapons Development Summary began.

He caught himself staring at the provincial sector data in the screencapture he'd just taken — Sector 4-North's projected window was the earliest, eight weeks from a date labeled *Current*, which meant if *Current* was now, eight weeks. His attention stayed on the number for half a second longer than was useful and he moved it deliberately back to the transfer counter.

600KB. One minute twenty remaining.

Emergency Protocols at 22%, too slow — he aborted it. File entry would show in the transfer log unless Serath's node was masking that cleanly. "Are incomplete transfers visible?"

"No. Node masks all non-completed pulls." A pause. "One minute."

Weapons Development Summary at 81%.

His fingers had gone cold — had been cold since he'd seen the seven sector codes with staggered operation windows. He let it register without assigning a label and kept his attention on the interface.

"Forty-five seconds," Serath said. "Prepare to exit."

94%.

"Thirty."

100%. Transfer complete.

"Exit."

He closed the interface windows in sequence — Military Liaison subfolder, Restricted Administrative main tree, session terminal. Didn't attempt the Legacy Records session close because he hadn't opened any files there (the screencaptures were local to the camera). Moved back from the workstation. Serath was already at the door with the transfer node disengaged, the device reduced to its crystal-communication cover.

They exited the basement through the service pathway she'd mapped: service corridor, northeast access ramp, two turns to the administrative building's side exit into the outer courtyard. Each section of corridor they passed was empty — the communal gathering's noise was audible from the main quad, students still dispersing.

At the side exit, he held the door for her as though they were two people who'd had reason to be in an administrative building in the evening, which they were. They separated in the courtyard. He walked toward the student residential quadrant through the dispersing crowd, passing sixteen people in the first ninety seconds, none of whom looked at him with any interest.

---

The garden on the Academy's eastern perimeter was colder at this hour than the interior paths, the stones retaining the day's warmth only in the uppermost surface. He sat on the low wall at the garden's inner edge. Kael was already present; Serath arrived three minutes after him.

"All transfers completed," Serath said. Her technical cadence was gone — she was back to the normal register. "No security alerts. Backup completion logged as routine. Unless they conduct a manual review of the node protocols, we're clean."

"Provincial Allocation," Aisen said. "Seven sectors. Staggered fourteen-week window. Sector 4-North is earliest — approximately eight weeks from now, based on the date notation."

Kael received this without expression, which with her was the expression that mattered. "That matches our field intelligence on conscription timing." She looked at Serath. "How long to full analysis?"

"Preliminary on the military strategy and allocation by tomorrow evening," Serath said. "Full breakdown of doctrine materials, four days."

"Tomorrow evening on the regional windows," Kael said. "The networks in Sector 4-North don't have eight weeks of runway in their current model." She stood. "This was well done. Both of you." She and Serath left through the garden's east access.

Aisen stayed on the wall.

He was reviewing the sector summary screenshot in his memory — the headers, the force allocation unit codes, the staggered dates — not because there was anything left to determine but because his attention kept returning to it the way it returned to any data that had not yet resolved into an implication he could act on. Seven sectors. Some of them, by force allocation logic, were going to receive heavier concentration than others. Some of the networks in those sectors were going to survive the initial military phase. Some were not. The provincial allocation document would tell Kael's team which was which before the operations began.

That was what the calculation had been for.

---

"You look like you're doing arithmetic," Vex said.

He'd heard her approach two seconds before she spoke — familiar weight-on-stone cadence from the east access. She sat beside him on the wall in the space Kael had just left.

"I am," he said.

"Stop."

He stopped.

"Tell me what it felt like instead," she said.

He considered the question as she'd asked it, which meant she wanted experiential report not analysis. "Cold server room. Transfer rates slower than projected. Had to cut the Emergency Protocols file. The seven sector codes with the staggered windows — that's when my hands went still."

"Because?"

"Because it became specific," he said. "Military preparation is general. Seven sectors with start dates is a calendar."

Vex turned the small stone she'd picked up from the garden path between her fingers — a habit she had when thinking. "You understand what's different now about your position."

He did. Serath had the transfer node. Kael had the networks. Caris had the analysis chain. Aisen had navigated the directory structure, which was recoverable forensically if someone with Archive-level authority ever examined the backup protocols carefully enough, and his presence in the administrative building during commun gathering hours was physically traceable if Torven's physical access logs were cross-referenced. The infiltration had been clean, and if the Covenant's attention was elsewhere, it would stay clean indefinitely.

If the Covenant's attention turned toward the Academy, specifically, in the next six months — which the provincial deployment window now suggested was plausible — it would not stay clean.

"I understand it," he said.

"I'm not telling you it was a wrong decision," Vex said. "The provincial allocation data is going to save specific people that would otherwise die because they didn't know to move their organizational structures in time. That's arithmetic you can actually finish."

"And the other arithmetic?" he asked. Meaning himself.

She set the stone down on the wall between them. "If they find you, they'll know exactly what you are. Not a curious student. Someone who knew what he was looking for and found it." A pause. "Which is what you are."

He picked up the stone she'd put down, turned it once, set it back. "Is that your assessment or a warning?"

"Observation," she said. "You're already past the point where you can go back to being something else. The question is what you do from where you actually are." She stood. "The semester ends in four weeks. After that, everything outside the Academy starts moving faster."

She left via the east access. He sat on the wall another ten minutes, not doing arithmetic, and then walked back to the dormitory through the dispersing crowd.

The seven sector codes stayed ordered in his memory like something he was supposed to translate and hadn't yet.

He slept five hours, which was enough.
