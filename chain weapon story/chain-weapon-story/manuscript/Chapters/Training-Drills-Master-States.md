# Training Drills - Master States

Master Wei taught that techniques fail when students mistake movement for state.

A movement is visible.
A state is sustained.

The Academy yard curriculum below trains practitioners to manage magic units (MU) under stress, integrate chain modules with telekinetic control, and recover before debt collapse.

## Core Doctrine

1. Spend less than you can, not less than you want.
2. Every anchor requires a recovery plan before activation.
3. Precision beats spectacle in all contested environments.
4. Team timing is a force multiplier greater than raw output.

## Baseline Metrics and Safety

Before any drill block:

- Resting tremor check (hands and jaw)
- Focus check (name, date, sequence recall)
- MU capacity estimate from previous session
- Heat index and hydration status

Stop conditions:

- visual bloom or double image,
- delayed command response,
- involuntary tremor over three breaths,
- inability to release anchor on cue.

If any stop condition appears, training halts and student enters supervised recovery.

## Drill Block I - Grounding and Micro-Pushes (Low MU)

Purpose: establish reliable grain-scale control.

### Exercise A: Pebble Balance

- Hold one pebble at palm height using micro-push only.
- Maintain for five breaths.
- Repeat ten sets.

Coaching cues:

- Shoulders down.
- Elbow unlocked.
- Breath leads force pulse.
- Correct drift with minimal push, not panic surge.

Failure patterns:

- overcorrection oscillation,
- facial strain indicating hidden MU overspend,
- breath hold during correction.

### Exercise B: Feather Trail

- Move a feather through marked lane on table.
- No contact with lane edges.
- Sustain smooth velocity across three passes.

Progression:

- widen lane familiarity,
- narrow lane precision,
- add ambient distraction (sound, movement).

Training objective: teach students to maintain output while attention is contested.

## Drill Block II - Tether Relay (Medium MU)

Purpose: transfer force across linked nodes without collapse.

### Exercise C: Three-Station Relay

- Anchor at station A.
- Transfer impulse to station B.
- Transfer from B to C with <0.5s gap.
- Repeat for timed sequence.

Scoring:

- transfer delay,
- anchor stability,
- MU consistency across repetitions.

### Exercise D: Chain Catch and Redirect

- Launch spare link on controlled arc.
- Mid-air catch via telekinetic tether.
- Lock module and redirect moving target.

Coaching cues:

- eyes track endpoint, not chain blur,
- command words short and consistent,
- release before fatigue spikes.

Common error: students hold too long to "prove" control and trigger unnecessary MU debt.

## Drill Block III - Sustained Anchor and Recovery (High MU)

Purpose: maintain team function under load while preventing collapse.

### Exercise E: Gate Hold

- Two practitioners establish anchored corridor.
- Team passes weighted sandbags through corridor.
- Hold 20-30s, then controlled release.

Risk point: panic grip during load surge causes anchor snapback.

### Exercise F: Rotating Anchor

- Four-practitioner team rotates anchor duty.
- No practitioner exceeds safe MU window.
- Rotation timing is fixed before start and cannot be improvised mid-load.

Evaluation criteria:

- release cleanliness,
- handoff timing,
- post-drill recovery speed.

## Practical Sequences

### Sequence A: Line Defense

Use short anchor and micro-push deflection to redirect incoming projectiles, then sweep lane with loose chain segment to disrupt advance.

Applied lesson: combine block I precision with block II transfer.

### Sequence B: Relay Evacuation

Create moving corridor for stretcher passage under intermittent pressure. Anchors provide handholds and hazard dampening.

Applied lesson: prioritize civilian movement over offensive output.

### Sequence C: Last Vigil

Simulate prolonged extraction under contested conditions. Team must maintain anchor rotation while evacuating one impaired member.

Applied lesson: schedule power, protect weakest link, preserve mission continuity.

## Instructor Notes on Aisen's Cadence

Aisen's preferred training cadence emphasized conservation.

- shorter output bursts,
- strict release discipline,
- repeated recovery drills,
- anti-showmanship correction for advanced students.

He frequently repeated one line to novices:

"If your technique only works while you feel invincible, it does not work."

## Maintenance Protocol

- Inspect links before and after rain cycles.
- Remove rust at first sign, not visible failure.
- Rebalance warped shafts before next drill block.
- Log all equipment anomalies in shared yard ledger.

Technique quality degrades when maintenance is deferred.

## Closing Principle

Master State is not a peak moment. It is a repeatable operating condition.

A practitioner who can produce moderate output repeatedly with clean recovery is more valuable than one spectacular discharge followed by collapse.

Train for the second hour of conflict, not the opening display.
