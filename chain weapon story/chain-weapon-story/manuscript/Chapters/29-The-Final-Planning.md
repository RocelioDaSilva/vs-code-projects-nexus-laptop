# CHAPTER 29: THE FINAL PLANNING

The regulatory review board admitted Amara's analysis for formal review on day one hundred and twenty-seven.

He learned this from Amara during a brief encounter at the morning formation checkpoint. She was standing at the documentation table — she was sometimes assigned there — and she set a filing stamp on the table with a specific orientation: parallel to the table edge, which was not the standard resting position. It was a signal they hadn't discussed explicitly but that he'd learned from her habit over fifty days.

He noted it and continued to his post.

He didn't ask her about it directly until three days later, when he had a scheduled meeting with her for the technical documentation exchange. The flanged tip second-iteration notes — he'd made adjustments to the mass curve based on the overextension problem he'd identified.

"Formal review admitted," she confirmed. "It's now in the regulatory board's priority queue, not the holding stack. Timeline to decision: six to fourteen weeks."

Six to fourteen weeks was a long time in operational terms. He thought about what that time would contain: the continued binding program application, the eastern district network completion, whatever Serath's Amara-message intelligence had set in motion at a level above his visibility.

"What does formal review mean for the second-year relabeling decision?" he said.

"It means the regulatory board will examine whether the deployment authorization has adequate formation and capability assessment as a precondition," she said. "The board can require additional assessment before deployment proceeds. The board cannot reverse the deployment authorization — that's a command decision. They can require documentation of readiness."

Documentation of readiness was a delay mechanism. Not protection but bureaucratic friction. He knew Vex's pending staff-classification review could exist in that friction window.

"What's your assessment of likelihood?" he said.

"The analysis is strong," she said. "Whether strong analysis produces useful board action depends on whether the registry oversight officer's regulatory mandate gives her leverage in the board deliberation." She was quiet for a moment. "I'd estimate a forty percent probability of meaningful outcome."

He held that number.

"The Registry Inspector's recommendation — to admit the analysis — was that helpful or neutral?" he said.

"Helpful," she said. "He's an efficient validator. When he recommends admission, the board's procedural threshold is satisfied. Without his recommendation, the submission would have spent another six weeks in holding."

The Inspector had asked him three questions and made two notes. The second note followed something Aisen had said about Section 6 — a connection the Inspector had either already made or constructed in the moment. His recommendation to admit the analysis was either genuinely procedural or a specific strategic choice. Both possibilities were live.

"The Inspector," he said. "Is he Registry-central or Registry-field?"

"Field administration with regulatory oversight portfolio," Amara said. "Not intelligence-operational. His mandate is compliance and accountability in provincial enforcement." She looked at him. "Why are you asking about his affiliation?"

He thought about what to give her. "He is not related to a similar-named archive official I knew at the Academy," he said. "I wanted to confirm I wasn't making a false connection."

She accepted that.

On day one hundred and thirty, Kael sent a message that was longer than her normal compressed format.

He translated it slowly, carefully: *M-network contact confirmed as flag-active under hereditary classification (ancestry from pre-Fourth Circle period, eastern province). Contact not currently detained or bound. Flag is active-dormant — requires trigger event to initiate processing. We have moved contact to non-operational category: no transmissions, minimal public presence, no network-identifiable behavior. Assess: does the regulatory review alter the flag-activation threshold?*

This was a specific question. She was asking whether Amara's regulatory analysis, by surfacing the hereditary flag structure, would cause the Registry to accelerate flag-activation processing before the review board could constrain it.

He thought about this carefully. The regulatory review had been admitted to the priority queue, but "formal review" didn't suppress ongoing operations. The Registry's enforcement arm and its regulatory compliance arm were separate branches. A regulatory review in one branch wouldn't automatically trigger a policy halt in the enforcement arm — especially if the enforcement arm wasn't formally notified.

But the Inspector's role was regulatory oversight of provincial enforcement. His recommendation to admit the analysis meant he'd reviewed the contents. Which meant the enforcement arm's flag-activation behavior had now crossed a regulatory official's awareness.

Whether that created an accelerant or a dampener depended on the Inspector's specific mandate interpretation.

He encoded a response to Kael: *Regulatory admission creates oversight record, not operational halt. Flag-activation threshold: unchanged in short term. Registry oversight awareness may create longer-term constraint if review produces binding outcome. Non-operational status for flag-active contact is correct protocol. Recommend maintaining through review timeline.*

He also encoded a separate, shorter query: *Request assessment: does reg-oversight awareness of enforcement pattern typically precede or follow enforcement acceleration?*

He held this in draft for six hours. He was asking Kael a question about Registry's institutional behavior that she probably had better visibility into than he did — because she was running a distributed network that had accumulated longer observational history than his deployment tenure.

He sent it.

---

On day one hundred and thirty-five, the third meeting with Kael in Ashfall.

Same craft-sector building. She had changed the entry protocol — he arrived to find a chalk notation on the doorframe indicating a shift in entry timing by eleven minutes. He waited at a supply point three blocks away for ten minutes and forty-five seconds, then went to the building.

She was already there. She was holding a different carrying bag than the previous meeting — heavier, from the shoulder drop.

"The flag-activation question," she said, before he'd settled. "Registry oversight awareness historically precedes enforcement acceleration by three to six weeks. The oversight official's record creates a documentation pressure — enforcement agencies complete pending actions in the window before a formal review constrains them."

He sat across from her at the workbench. "Three to six weeks takes us inside the review board timeline."

"Yes."

"The flag-active contact needs additional removal from the observable environment," he said.

"Already in process," she said. "The contact's currently documented location will appear consistent through the review period. Administrative footprint maintained. Physical presence relocated." She set the carrying bag down carefully — the weight settled deliberately. "I also need to tell you that we have a decision point."

He waited.

"The Kael network's interior Covenant source," she said. "The three-month-old limited access. That contact has been approached by Registry counter-intelligence in the past week. A compliance interview — not an accusation, but a pattern-assessment conversation. Standard protocol for individuals with access to operations-relevant information."

He understood what this meant. The interior access was at risk of compromise. Once a counter-intelligence compliance interview occurred, the source had two weeks maximum of continued safe operation before their access pattern would be flagged if they hadn't already been identified.

"If the source's access is terminated or the source is compromised, it changes what I can provide to your Serath arrangement," he said carefully.

"Yes," Kael said. "But that's a secondary consequence. The primary decision is how we handle the interior access's exit. There are two options: controlled withdrawal — the source reduces access frequency gradually over ten to fourteen days, creating a pattern of natural operational reduction rather than abrupt cessation. Or immediate cutoff — full information silence from the source, immediately, with administrative cover for the absence."

"What are the risks of each?" he said.

"Controlled withdrawal maintains some access but increases exposure duration. If counter-intelligence is actively monitoring, ten to fourteen days is additional data for possible identification." She met his eyes. "Immediate cutoff protects the source but creates a data gap in our Covenant-command awareness at the same time the Registry is executing its flag-response window."

He thought about the three-to-six-week flag-acceleration window she'd just described. A data gap in Covenant-command awareness during exactly that window.

"What is the source's assessment?" he asked.

"The source prefers controlled withdrawal," she said. "They believe the compliance interview was procedural, not targeted."

"And your assessment?"

"I believe the compliance interview was targeted," she said. "But I can't prove it, and overriding the source's judgment about their own situation requires a level of certainty I don't currently have."

He thought for eight seconds.

"Controlled withdrawal with a five-day window instead of ten to fourteen," he said. "Reduces exposure duration while maintaining some continuity. The source's preference is partially honored — not immediate cutoff — but the duration is constrained by your assessment of the risk level."

Kael was quiet. He could tell she'd considered this option and set it aside for reasons she hadn't disclosed.

"The source's current access runs on a nine-day reporting cycle," she said. "A five-day window means one partial cycle. The interior access would close before the next scheduled report window."

"Is the scheduled report critical?" he said.

"It would include the Covenant's provincial operations review for this quarter," she said. "Command allocation decisions, deployment rotation updates, flag-activation processing schedule."

He held that. The flag-activation processing schedule — which would tell them exactly when the three-to-six week window would close specific flag actions. Missing it meant navigating the acceleration window without visibility.

"Five days plus access to the partial-cycle report before cutoff," he said. "If that's operationally possible."

Kael held his question for a long time. "Possible," she said. "But the source takes additional risk for every day of continued access."

"Then they decide," he said. "I'm not going to commit the source to additional risk without their informed choice."

Kael looked at him with the quality he'd observed in her before: not surprised, but recalibrating. "I'll relay the option with the risk assessment."

She left through the north workshop exit. He stayed four minutes.

On the way back to barracks, he passed the east market and stopped at the grain stall, not because he needed anything but because the behavioral pattern of regular non-network stops at familiar locations reduced frequency-centrality in any observation record. He bought a small amount of dried grain. He gave the seller the standard exchange. He left.

He thought about the source taking additional risk.

He thought about seventeen-year-olds being relabeled as deployable cadets.

He thought about Soren spending eight years in a library documenting a suppression program that had then been used as an operational template against the people he'd documented it to protect.

These things were not the same problem. They pointed at the same thing.

He went to sleep at a standard time. He did not know if the source would accept the five-day option. He would find out when Kael told him.
