# Chapter 1 — Opening: The Tavern
**Anchor:** The Wandering Stag tavern; Aisen (age 5); coin-jar opening and early household economy.

## Scene 1: The Empty Coin Jar

The tavern smelled like boiled barley and bent spoons; it smelled like warm mouths and the slow, sticky sweetness of stew that had been ladled through the same wood for as long as Aisen could remember.

He sat on the low bench with his knees pulled to the grain, a small heap of legs and elbows that fit between a man's chair and the hearth. Father was a silhouette of elbows and patient hands behind the counter, a thumb that could pass a coin from one palm to another without thinking. Aisen knew the sound of that thumb better than the sound of his own name.

"Stay near the tankard," Father said, not looking up. His voice was the kind that folded itself into the room and did not startle the rafters. Marta—Marta with her laugh like a windchime that had lost a bell—moved between tables, carrying a tray that hissed when a spill fell into it. She reached down and patted Aisen's head the way she always did, leaving the faint smell of lemon soap in her wake.

She set a coin on the table in front of him—a small, nicked coin that caught the light in a way real coins did, the kind that felt like salt and a promise. "For your lessons," she said, and the words were as big as a chair to a child. "Don't eat it all at once."

Aisen's fingers closed around the coin. It was still warm from her hand.

The tavern was full this evening—traders from the south, merchants counting stock, soldiers in garrison grays nursing ale like it was water from the only well left in the world. The air was a wall of sound: negotiation, laughter, argument, affection, the clink of cups and the scrape of chairs on wood.

Aisen watched Marta count coins at the tavern's closing, three hours later. The jar wasn't as full as expected for the season. She frowned at it, turning coins over in her fingers like they might multiply if she was gentle enough.

She mentioned her siblings back home. She mentioned the farm. She mentioned someone's agent buying out the family holdings.

Father was listening from behind the counter, the way he always listened—with his hands and his silences instead of his voice.

"It will be fine," Father said, but he was looking at the coin jar like it was a problem he did not yet know how to solve.

Aisen held the nicked coin Marta had given him and did not yet understand that the first signs of catastrophe were quiet things. Small things. The kind of things that a child could hold in his hand and never realize they meant the world was already breaking.

## Story Opening Context

This is the beginning of **Chain Weapon Story**, a three-act narrative following Aisen Korv from age 5 through 23 across three distinct periods:

- **Act I: The Hidden Years** (Ages 5-15) — Aisen learns to hide what he knows in a world where knowledge of advanced Flux magic is punishable by death.
- **Act II: The War Years** (Ages 16-21) — Aisen is conscripted into war, crosses paths with other rule-breakers, and develops his philosophy through increasingly complex moral choices.
- **Act III: The Final Stand** (Ages 22-23) — Aisen faces the climactic confrontation with the forces hunting him.

The story explores themes of **class, prejudice, potential, and systemic change** through a protagonist whose greatest skill is not heroic action but the careful observation of patterns—how systems work, what people will choose given their constraints, and whether one person can change anything at all.

### Core Narrative Principles

**Sanderson's "Hard Magic"**: Every spell has a visible cost. Aisen's telekinesis burns mana efficiently, but overuse causes entropy debt—his body pays the price.

**Abercrombie's "Moral Ambiguity"**: Aisen's compassion often leads to worse outcomes. He must live with the guilt of failed mercy and choices between atrocities.

**Jemisin's "Systemic Conflict"**: The real antagonist is not a person but systems of scarcity, war, and ecological collapse. Individual kindness cannot solve structural problems.

**Rothfuss's "Layered Mystery"**: Aisen discovers strange things early—a fishing line in a dead mage, for example—and five years later understands their significance.

**Martin's "Political Machinations"**: Power flows through networks, choices have factional consequences, and betrayal is often the only moral option available.

### This Chapter

This opening chapter establishes:
- The tavern as Aisen's anchor point and the story's narrative nexus
- Father as a figure of quiet competence and careful observation
- Marta and the merchant community as sources of care and economic vulnerability
- The first hints that systems are failing (coin jar running low, farms being bought out)
- Aisen as an observer who watches carefully but doesn't yet understand the implications

The chapter sets the tone for everything that follows: quiet moments of observation that contain vast amounts of meaning, the awareness that small changes cascade into catastrophe, and the tavern's role as a place where ordinary people navigate the breaking world.
