# Scene: Night Before Leaving for Aldric's Crossing

The night before he left, the tavern ran as usual.

No speeches.
No farewell feast.
No dramatic promise to return as someone larger than himself.

The Wandering Stag did not pause for one boy's departure. Travelers needed beds. Teamsters needed stew. A wheelwright from South Road needed his invoice corrected because he could not read numbers after ale.

Aisen worked through all of it with a packed satchel hidden under the back stairs.

Near close, his father called him to the ledger table.

"Sit," he said.

The old man pushed over a leather pouch that looked flatter than it should.

"Count it."

Aisen poured coppers into his palm.

Twenty-three, plus two small silver cuts shaved thin enough to pass only in certain markets.

"This is too much," Aisen said.

"It is not enough," his father replied. "Which is the same thing, depending on weather."

He handed over a folded paper with names.

Three inns that would not cheat too badly.
One quartermaster cousin in Aldric's Crossing who hated paperwork and therefore owed favors.
One warning written twice: do not borrow from uniformed men.

His mother arrived with a clay jar sealed in wax.

"Salve," she said. "Cuts, burns, bad stitching, and stubbornness."

"You made enough for a regiment."

"Then do not be the reason I was accurate."

Mira pretended she had no gift until final sweep, when she cornered him by the stove and shoved a wrapped bundle into his coat.

Inside was the carved stag from years ago, newly sanded and darkened with oil, plus a cord to wear it under his shirt.

"You keep losing small things," she said. "Now it stays tied to you."

Aisen turned the carving in his fingers. The short antler remained. Mira had refused to "fix" it.

"Still lopsided," he said.

"Still ours," she answered.

After close they ate standing up, bowls in hand, because nobody trusted themselves at the table.

His father adjusted the straps on Aisen's pack twice, then once more.

"Show me what you are carrying."

Aisen laid it out.

Two shirts.
Needle kit.
Waterskin.
Harald's practice spear wrapped in oiled cloth.
Mother's salve.
Ledger paper.
The stag.
The coin pouch.

His father pointed at the spear. "Tool."
At the ledger paper. "Work."
At the stag. "Anchor."

"Keep all three," he said. "People fail when they drop one."

Late night, when the last lamp was lowered, Aisen went outside to the yard where carts were stacked under tarps. The town bell rang midnight. He tried to imagine Aldric's Crossing and could only picture roads converging and people he did not yet know deciding who he was.

His mother came out without a cloak, ignoring the cold.

"Cannot sleep?" she asked.

"No."

She stood beside him a moment.

"Good," she said. "Sleepy people miss details. Details keep you alive."

He laughed softly. "Is this your comfort voice?"

"Yes. You are welcome."

Then she touched his cheek once, quick and practical, and went back inside before either of them made it sentimental.

He slept little.

At dawn he found his father already in the kitchen sharpening a knife that did not need sharpening.

No speech this time either.

His father held out his hand.
Aisen took it.

"Write when you can," his father said.

"I will."

"And if you cannot write, send a mark through someone honest."

Mira appeared with a heel of bread and a strip of dried apple wrapped in cloth.

"For the road," she said. "Do not trade it for stories."

"What if it is a good story?"

"Then trade half."

He stepped into morning mist while the town was still half asleep. Behind him, pots began clanging in the kitchen. Ahead, wagons creaked toward the eastern road.

At the gate he looked back once.

The tavern sign swung in wind.
Smoke rose from the chimney.
A normal morning.

That steadied him more than any blessing could have.

He touched the stag under his shirt, adjusted the spear on his shoulder, and started toward Aldric's Crossing counting his steps in groups of fifty so fear had something mechanical to do.

By the time the sun cleared the ridge, he had stopped counting.

The road had taken over.
