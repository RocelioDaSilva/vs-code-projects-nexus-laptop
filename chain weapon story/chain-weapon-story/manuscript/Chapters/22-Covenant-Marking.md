# CHAPTER 22: COVENANT MARKING

The binding ceremony was conducted in the administrative building's east annex, in a room that had been a municipal records office before the occupation made it something else. Three Covenant Registry officials had arrived from the capital three days prior. They had equipment: portable wards, a carrying case with several crystalline implements, and a ledger that appeared to catalogue identities in a format Aisen didn't recognize.

He learned about the program from Serath during one of their weekly intelligence exchanges. She told him as contextual information — something he should understand to calibrate his analysis correctly. The Covenant had authorized a "containment designation protocol" for captured resistance leaders: rather than immediate detention and interrogation, they would be bound and released. The binding would constrain operational magical action and would report magic-use events back to Registry monitoring.

"They track them after release," Aisen said.

"The Registry receives notification whenever a binding triggers," Serath confirmed. "If the bound individual attempts combat magic, resistance-related communication magic, certain forms of information encoding — the binding sends a passive alert. They then have six hours to triangulate the location and respond."

He processed this. "What's the threshold for triggering?"

"Intent and magnitude, from what I understand. Low-level domestic magic doesn't trigger. Direct resistance-operational magic does." She watched him for his reaction. "Does that change your analysis of network structure?"

"Yes," he said. This was one of the situations where his operational knowledge and his intelligence channel converged at a point where he couldn't cleanly separate them. If Kael's network had individuals who'd been bound and released, those individuals were compromised regardless of their loyalty — the binding would report them.

He needed to know if any of them had been applied to network contacts. And he needed Kael to know the binding existed and what it did.

Micro-want: get the binding protocol specifics from Serath without revealing why he needed them at this level of detail.

Ticking clock: the first bound-and-release individuals would be back in the general population within forty-eight hours.

"What are the implementation parameters?" he asked. "Who qualifies for binding versus standard detention?"

"Organizational leadership tier. If Registry classification identifies someone as a cell coordinator or above, they're eligible for binding protocol rather than detention." She paused. "Mid-tier and below go to standard interrogation and release or detention depending on intelligence value."

He noted the gap. Kael had designed cell communication to minimize visible hierarchy — coordinators presented externally as logistics handlers. The question was whether Covenant Registry could identify a coordinator from behavioral and social patterns rather than formal hierarchy designations.

"The classification criteria," he said. "How specific are they?"

"Registry uses pattern-based classification," Serath said. "Communication frequency centrality, decision-pattern analysis across observed interactions, magical signature consistency with resistance-operation frequencies. It's probabilistic — they have a confidence threshold below which they use standard protocol."

Pattern-based. Which meant it could catch coordinators without formal hierarchy data. He needed to know more.

"Can I see the Registry criteria documentation?" he asked.

Serath studied him for a moment. "That's outside the intelligence-sharing framework I described."

"I'm asking because the binding program intersects with resistance network analysis," he said. "If bound individuals are released back into the general population, they affect the network's structural behavior in ways that aren't visible without understanding the binding parameters. The analysis you're asking for requires knowing what's modified."

She held the question for four seconds. Then she opened the incident folder on her desk and extracted a two-page classification summary. She set it on the table.

She didn't give it to him. She left it visible. He photographed it with the index camera in seventeen seconds.

---

That evening he re-read the classification criteria in the photograph. The pattern-based system used four indicators in combination: communication frequency (how often an individual appeared in multiple-party observation), spatial centrality (how often an individual was in locations used for coordination), magical exertion profile (whether their magic-use pattern suggested operational rather than domestic application), and behavioral consistency (whether their activities showed the systematic repetition of someone conducting planned operations versus unsystematic behavior).

Individually, none of the four would identify a Kael-method coordinator. Kael had specifically designed lateral networks where no individual showed frequency centrality — each coordinator communicated with a small number of contacts on non-repeating schedules. But the combination score was what mattered, and the combination score accumulated over time.

He encoded the full classification summary and the binding-protocol description and transmitted it in six segments over two days, each segment routed through a different dead-drop chain. This exceeded his standard transmission security protocol. He used every extra step he'd been holding in reserve for high-risk content.

The response from Kael came on the third day through an unusual channel — a market vendor contact he hadn't used in six weeks, which meant Kael had shifted to backup routing: *Protocol acknowledged. Three compromised. Two contain. One already triangulated and relocated. Binding analysis confirmed. Network adjusting threshold criteria.*

Three compromised. One already triangulated — meaning the Registry had already tracked one bound-and-released coordinator by magical signature, and Kael's people had moved them in time. Two being contained, meaning their communication abilities were being restructured to work within binding constraints.

He sat with the three number for a long time.

The binding program had been operational for how long before he'd learned it from Serath? The Registry officials had arrived three days before his exchange with Serath. The first individual processed under the protocol had been released the same day as the officials' arrival, which was six days ago. Which meant six days of contamination before the network had warning.

He pressed his thumbnail to his inner wrist and counted. Not to twelve. Longer.

---

On the sixty-third day of deployment, he was assigned to support duty at the east checkpoint — perimeter documentation, civilian movement logging, nothing that required his specific skills. He had been deliberately appearing in low-priority assignments regularly to avoid the pattern-centrality that the Registry's own classification criteria would flag.

At the checkpoint, mid-morning, a man in his mid-fifties came through from the residential district. He was carrying a bundle of cloth goods — a legitimate trader's load. He had the specific quality of someone performing ordinary behavior with slightly too much precision.

Aisen logged the man's passage. Standard documentation.

The man's eyes met his for exactly one half-second and found the stone wall behind him. Normal non-contact behavior. No signal, no recognition.

But on the back of his right wrist, below the cloth cuff, Aisen had seen the edge of a marking: a small sigil, burn-healed, the color of old scar tissue.

A binding mark.

He logged the man's documentation number and entry time. He continued the morning's checkpoint duty for four more hours.

He did not transmit anything about the cloth trader.

What he knew: the man had been bound. What he didn't know: which tier had produced the binding (coordinator, logistics, or Registry error resulting in wrongful binding of a civilian adjacent). What he couldn't determine: whether the man was still operationally active, had been isolated from the network per Kael's containment protocol, or was one of the three compromised cases he'd already been told about.

Transmitting an unverified observation about a bound individual was more dangerous than not transmitting it. If the man was innocent of network connection and Aisen's transmission identified him with network-specific framing, that itself created a record.

He held the observation in a closed-unknown column.

---

That evening, Vex arrived.

He hadn't been told she was coming. The contact who reached him was a new dead-drop placement — a chalk notation on the east alley wall that meant: *network courier present, standard market contact window, third hour of morning*.

Vex was at the grain stall at third hour. She was presenting as a supplies researcher for an educational institution conducting nutritional access assessment in the occupied territories. It was better cover than the Academy exchange program — harder to follow up on, institutional enough to be plausible, regional enough to explain her presence.

They didn't have a supervised meeting window this time. The grain stall was loud and trafficked, which was more protection than a monitored room.

"The binding program is Soren's work," she said. She was examining grain samples with the professional interest of a researcher. "From his archive research. He spent eight years documenting how the Covenant's predecessor institutions controlled magical practitioners in pre-unification territories. The binding protocol is based on a fourth-century containment method that the heresy archive documented."

He had not known that. He held it still.

"Soren is still alive?" he said.

"Yes. He's removed himself from the Archive. The binding program activation was too close to his research signature for him to stay in position." She set down a sample. "He wanted me to tell you: the fourth-century containment protocol has a structural failure point. The binding tracks magical-use events but not magical-capacity suppression. Someone who learns to perform necessary operations below the binding's detection threshold can operate without triggering it."

"What's the threshold?" he asked.

"Below intended-resistance frequency," she said. "Which is why the bindings are most effective on combat mages and coordinators who use operational magic. Less effective on network adapters — people who work primarily through indirect and non-magical channels." She moved to the next sample. "Kael already knows. But she asked me to confirm it reached you."

He understood. Soren had documented the binding's structural weakness in those eighth years of archive research and Kael had received that information through a channel he hadn't had visibility into. The network was better informed about its own containment than he'd realized.

A weight settled low in his chest: the recognition quality, not alarm.

"Three contaminated," he said.

"Two are cleared," she said. "The third is in a city two sectors north. They're not operational but they're not relocated yet. We're working on the movement window." She looked at him briefly — not looking at the grain, actually looking at him. "Serath's approach is holding?"

"For now," he said.

"The Registry is pushing Serath to increase binding program application," Vex said. "The second wave of bound individuals will be processed this week. Broader coverage — they're expanding the tier criteria downward." She set down the last sample. "The network needs the updated threshold criteria if you have them."

He had them. The photograph of Serath's classification summary was still in his encoded record.

"I'll transmit by evening," he said.

She bought two measures of grain for the researcher cover and left via the west market exit. He stayed at the stall for six more minutes. A different seller gave him change with a specific finger position that told him the east cell was still operationally independent, which was the same signal as twenty days ago and therefore confirmed continuity.

He went back to barracks and encoded the classification criteria transmission that evening. He slept four hours. That was fewer than he needed and more than he expected.
