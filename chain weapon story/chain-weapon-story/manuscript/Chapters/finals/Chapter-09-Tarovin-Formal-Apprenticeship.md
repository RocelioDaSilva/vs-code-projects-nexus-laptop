Spring arrived in the border town like a debt being repaid—slowly, with reluctance, and only after the earth had been convinced it would not be cheated again. The frost gave ground by inches. Meltwater carved new conversations into the ditches along the north road, and the air carried that particular mineral sweetness that meant the river was swollen and the soil was remembering how to be soft. Aisen noticed these things the way he noticed most things now: involuntarily, catalogued, stored against some future need he could not yet name.

He was thirteen and had begun to understand that noticing was not the same as knowing. The distinction mattered in the same way that watching a river and swimming in it were not the same skill.

Tarovin arrived on the second day of thaw, walking the north road with a satchel that sagged with the weight of papers and the particular exhaustion of a man who had been arguing with institutions. His robe was different from the one Aisen remembered—heavier, with a scholar's pin at the collar that caught the watery light—but his gait was the same: measured, deliberate, as if each step were a sentence in a paragraph he was still composing. His hands trembled faintly at his sides, the way they always had. Aisen had once thought it was cold. He understood now that it was something else: the cost of holding precision in a body that wanted to be imprecise.

Tarovin smiled when he saw Aisen in the doorway of the tavern. It was the patient smile of someone who kept promises to future selves.

"You're taller," Tarovin said.

"You're grayer," Aisen replied, and immediately felt the small heat of having been too familiar. But Tarovin laughed—a sound like a book being closed gently—and set his satchel on the bar as if returning to a desk he had never quite left.

He produced a letter. It was folded into a careful square, the creases sharp enough to suggest it had been folded and refolded many times in consideration. The seal on the outside was the academy's: a glyph of interlocking circles that meant, Aisen had learned from one of his small notebooks, *knowledge through application*.

"This is for you," Tarovin said. "Not to open now. To keep."

"What is it?"

"A recommendation. When the time comes, it will speak for you."

Aisen took the letter and felt the weight of it—not heavy, but dense in the way that intentions are dense. He wanted to open it immediately and had to press that impulse flat like a crease in linen. "What does it say?"

Tarovin's eyes crinkled. "It says you notice things. That matters more than any born advantage."

He said this with the plain certainty of someone stating arithmetic, and Aisen felt the words settle into a place in his chest that had been waiting for them without knowing it. Not pride, exactly. Something more structural—a beam being laid across a gap he had been measuring for years.

---

The awkward arithmetic of choices began the following morning.

Harald had been training Aisen since the robbery, three years of muscle and movement and the slow education of a body learning to listen. Harald's lessons were physical grammar: footwork as syntax, balance as punctuation, the river-thinking that said *work with forces, not against them*. By thirteen, Aisen could read a room the way his father read a customer—the lean of a shoulder, the distribution of weight, the small betrayals of intent that lived in a person's feet before they reached their hands.

Tarovin brought something else entirely. Where Harald taught through the body, Tarovin taught through the mind's architecture. Magic, in his framing, was not a gift or a force but a language with conjugations and cases, a physics with discoverable laws. The candle demonstration seven years ago had planted the seed; now Tarovin had returned to see what had grown.

Aisen stood between the two of them—not literally, but in the way a plumb line hangs between two walls, pulled by gravity toward the center while each side insists on its own geometry. Harald offered competence. Tarovin offered the kind of understanding that could redefine what competence meant. The selfish part of Aisen—the part that counted advantages the way his father counted coin—wanted both. The honest part knew that wanting both might mean doing neither well.

He did not say this aloud. He was thirteen, and thirteen-year-olds in border towns learned early that admitting uncertainty was an invitation for others to make your decisions for you.

---

Tarovin's first formal lesson took place at the long table in the tavern's back room, after the morning trade had quieted and the air smelled of cooling bread and the iron tang of the kettle left too long on the stove. He cleared a space with the careful authority of a man who believed all surfaces were potential classrooms and laid out his materials: a shaving of pale wood, thin as a fingernail; a length of hemp string, perhaps two hands long; and a small brass weight no bigger than a hazelnut.

"Watch," he said.

He tied the string to one end of the wood shaving, let the brass weight dangle from the other end, and balanced the whole assembly on the edge of a copper coin standing on its rim. The wood flexed. The weight swung in a tiny arc, pendulum-slow.

"This system is in equilibrium," Tarovin said. "All forces balanced. The wood wants to fall, but the string pulls back. The weight wants to swing, but friction holds. Everything is arguing, and the argument has reached a truce."

His trembling fingers hovered near the brass weight. Aisen watched for the telltale hum—the faint disturbance in the air that meant telekinesis was happening—and caught it: a thin vibration, less a sound than a pressure against the inner ear, like the moment before a storm decided which direction to move.

The weight shifted. Not dramatically—a hair's breadth, no more. But the shaving of wood tilted, the string's tension changed, and the entire assembly slid off the coin's edge and clattered softly onto the table.

"Torque," Tarovin said. "One minimal impulse, applied at the right point, and the system finds a new equilibrium. That is telekinesis at scale. Not the brute shoving of objects. The finding of leverage points where a whisper does the work of a shout."

Aisen stared at the fallen assembly. In his mind, he was already translating. *This is what Harald means when he says to find the place where a man's balance becomes a question instead of an answer.* The language was different—Tarovin spoke in physics and Harald in river-water—but the grammar underneath was the same.

"You're thinking about your other teacher," Tarovin observed. His mouth twitched with something that was not quite amusement—closer to recognition.

"The same lesson," Aisen said. "Different words."

"Yes." Tarovin gathered the materials with hands that shook faintly as they worked. "The academy teaches students to see these as separate disciplines. Martial practice over here, theoretical magic over there. That is a failure of imagination dressed as organizational convenience. The world does not draw lines between its own laws."

He looked at Aisen directly. His eyes were the color of water over dark stones—clear, but with depth that suggested things settled at the bottom. "You see across the line. That is rare. That is what I wrote in the letter."

---

The test came after lunch, when the sun had warmed the cobblestones outside the tavern and the smell of the season's first wildflowers competed with the permanent fog of rendered fat and old beer that lived in the building's timbers.

Tarovin stood in the clearing behind the tavern—Harald's training ground, the earth packed hard and smooth by years of footwork—and performed a simple movement. A step, a pivot, a reach. Nothing martial, nothing magical. Just motion: clean, deliberate, stripped of wasted gesture.

"Read it," he said. "Then do it with fewer movements than I used."

Aisen watched twice over as Tarovin repeated the sequence. The step carried a slight drag in the back foot—unnecessary, a habit. The pivot overcommitted by a few degrees before correcting. The reach extended a finger's width past its useful range.

He tried it. His body found the step easily enough—Harald's three years of footwork made the ground feel cooperative—but when he pivoted, he added his own excess: a hitch in the hip, a compensating lean. He knew it the moment it happened. The air felt wrong around him, like a sentence with one too many words.

"Again," Tarovin said.

The second attempt was worse. Aisen, overcorrecting, stripped too much and stumbled where the pivot needed a commitment he'd removed. The ground punished him with a scraped palm and the particular embarrassment of failing at something that looked simple. The taste of dust and frustration mixed on his tongue.

"You're mimicking the shape," Tarovin said, without criticism. "Stop mimicking. Read the *pattern* underneath. What is the movement trying to accomplish?"

Aisen stood up, brushed the grit from his hands, and thought. Not about the step or the pivot or the reach, but about what they served. Redistribution. The movement was a way of shifting weight from one state to another—from standing to reaching—with the minimum necessary disruption. It was the same principle as the wood shaving and the brass weight. Find where the system is willing to change and apply the smallest force there.

He tried a third time. This time he did not copy Tarovin's sequence. He found his own version—a step that was shorter but landed where the balance wanted to go, a pivot that gave only what was needed, a reach that stopped at the exact edge of useful range. The motion felt clean. It felt like a sentence without filler words.

Tarovin was silent for a moment. Then he nodded, and the nod contained more praise than any speech Aisen had received from the man.

"You don't learn by copying force," Tarovin said quietly. "You learn by finding patterns. That is not common. Most students at the academy spend their first two years unlearning the habit of mimicry. You never acquired it."

"Is that good?"

"It is dangerous and useful. Like most things worth having." Tarovin folded his arms, his hands tucked against his elbows to still their trembling. "The academy's enrollment opens in autumn. When you are ready—not now, but when you are ready—I will endorse your application. Formally. My signature carries enough weight for a border-town boy to be considered."

The words *border-town boy* should have stung, but Tarovin said them the way he said everything: as facts to be accounted for rather than insults to be suffered. The academy was a place where bloodlines and connections wrote the first page of every student's story. A recommendation from a scholar-cleric would write a second page. After that, Aisen would have to write his own.

"I'll think about it," Aisen said, because that was the true answer and because the habit of honesty Harald had drilled into him over three years was stronger than the vanity of an immediate yes.

Tarovin smiled. "Good. Thinking is the correct response to an offer of power."

---

That night, the tavern closed early. A spring storm rolled through the valley, pressing rain against the windows in sheets that made the glass sing. The hearth threw shadows that jumped and settled and jumped again. Aisen's father wiped down the bar with the slow, methodical care of a man who believed that cleanliness was the last defense against chaos, and the smell of lye and old oak filled the room like a second hearth.

Aisen took a candle to the back clearing. The rain had stopped but the earth was soft, and his boots left prints that filled slowly with dark water. The air tasted of wet stone and the ozone edge of the retreating storm.

He stood where Tarovin had stood and began to move.

Not the test sequence. Something of his own. He started with Harald's footwork—the foundational steps that had been drilled into his body until they were less technique than reflex. The weight transfer from back foot to front. The way the knees absorbed shock like the pilings of a bridge absorbing current. He felt the ground answer through the soles of his boots: soft, yielding, cooperative in the way that spring earth cooperated with seeds.

Then he layered Tarovin's principle over it. Minimal impulse. He moved through the sequence and asked himself, at each transition, *where is the smallest force that accomplishes this?* He found the place where a step didn't need to be a step, where a shift of weight from the ball of one foot to the heel of the other did the same work without lifting the sole from the ground. He found the place where a turn didn't need his whole torso, where a rotation of the hips, small and precise, put him where he needed to be with nothing left over.

The candle flame wavered in the after-storm breeze. Aisen reached—not with his hand, but with the thread of telekinetic awareness that lived behind his breastbone like a second pulse—and steadied it. A micro-correction. The minimal impulse applied to a system that wanted to stay lit. The flame held, patient and obedient, and the small warmth of it reached his face like approval.

He felt something settle in him. Not certainty—he was too young and too honest for certainty—but the architecture of an approach. Harald's body-knowledge and Tarovin's mind-knowledge were not competing dialects. They were the same language spoken at different registers. The body found the pattern; the mind found the principle beneath the pattern; and somewhere between the two was a grammar that was entirely his own.

He practiced until the candle burned to a stub and the puddles in the clearing reflected a sky that had cleared to show the first spring stars. His muscles complained. His telekinetic thread—that thin, low-powered gift that would never win a contest of raw strength—hummed with the pleasant fatigue of having been used precisely. His hands did not tremble the way Tarovin's did, but he understood now why Tarovin's did: the cost of holding precision was paid by the body, and the body's currency was always trembling and time.

When he returned to the tavern, his father looked up from the bar and said nothing, which was Harald's way of saying he'd seen and approved. Aisen hung his damp coat by the fire and sat in the chair that had been his since he was old enough to reach the seat without climbing.

"I want to keep training with you," he said. "And I want to take Tarovin's classes when he offers them."

Harald set down the cloth. "That's two roads."

"It's one road," Aisen said. "Walked with two feet."

His father considered this with the slow, tidal patience of a man who had spent his life reading people. Then he nodded. "Tarovin's a good teacher. Different from me, which is the point. You'll find things with him that I can't show you, and things with me that he can't explain. The trick will be the stitching."

"The stitching?"

"Making them hold together when it matters. Theory falls apart under a fist. Muscle forgets to think when it's tired. The place where both survive—that's where you'll need to live."

Aisen thought about this. He thought about the shaving of wood balanced on the coin's edge, about the way a minimal impulse could rearrange a system without breaking it. He thought about the river and the rock and the patience that outlasted both.

"I can do that," he said, and meant it in the particular way that thirteen-year-olds mean things: with total conviction and no evidence, which is sometimes the only honest way to begin.

He went to bed with the letter still folded in his pocket and the stub of candle wax on his thumb. Outside, the spring night settled into the sounds of water finding new channels and earth remembering how to grow. In the morning, he would start again—two languages, one grammar, and the slow, painstaking work of becoming someone who could translate between them.

It was not a dramatic beginning. It was not meant to be. The most important decisions, Aisen was learning, were the quiet ones: the choice to learn two things instead of one, to stand between two teachers and refuse to let go of either, to trust that the synthesis would come even when the parts seemed to argue.

He slept, and the letter sat against his ribs like a second heartbeat, folded and patient and full of a future he would have to earn.
