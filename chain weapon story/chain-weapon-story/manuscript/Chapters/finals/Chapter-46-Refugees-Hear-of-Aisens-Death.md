# CHAPTER 46: REFUGEES HEAR OF AISEN'S DEATH

The bread was wrong.

Emma knew it by touch before she knew it by taste—the crust yielding a fraction too early beneath her thumb, the interior holding its warmth unevenly, the grain's structure compressed in a way that spoke of flour milled too coarse and water added too fast. She had baked bread for twenty-three years. Her hands knew the language of dough the way a musician's hands knew the language of strings: by pressure, by resistance, by the specific conversation between the material and the maker that produced, when both participants were honest, something that could sustain a body.

This bread was not honest. But it was bread.

She broke it and distributed the pieces to the children gathered at the long table in the refectory tent—a word too grand for the structure, which was canvas stretched over a frame of green pine poles, the canvas patched in three places with sailcloth and the poles still weeping sap that caught the lamplight in amber beads. The children received the bread with the automatic hands of people who had learned not to examine food before eating it, because examination was a luxury that belonged to a life before, a life where bread came from ovens with names—*Emma's, on the square*—and the bread had a provenance and the provenance was a form of trust and the trust was a form of home.

There was no home now. There was the camp.

The camp was a place the way a scar was a place—defined not by what it was but by what had happened to produce it. It occupied a stretch of floodplain above the Miren delta, the ground flat and damp and given to a mineral smell that rose from the clay when the rain came, which was often. The tents numbered in the hundreds. The population shifted—families arriving, families dispersing to relatives or work or the grey anonymity of cities that absorbed refugees the way rivers absorbed tributaries, by dilution. Emma had been here for fourteen months. She was, by the camp's accelerated calendar, an elder.

She was also, by the camp's informal taxonomy, a baker. Not officially—officially she was Displaced Person 4,417, Thessaly District, Female, Category B (Able-bodied, Skilled Trade, No Dependents Above Age of Labour)—but functionally. She baked. She had three ovens, built by her own hands from the clay of the floodplain and fired with the same technique her grandmother had taught her grandmother: a slow cure, the heat rising in controlled increments over three days, the clay learning its shape through the gradual education of fire. The ovens were not her mother's ovens. They were not the stone-floored, three-chambered ovens of the bakery on the square in Thessaly, the village, not the province. But they baked bread, and the bread fed people, and the feeding was the thing that she could do, the single act of competence that the upheaval had not taken from her.

The news arrived on a Tuesday.

Not dramatically. Not carried by a rider or announced by a herald or delivered in the formal language of institutional communication. It came the way all news came to the margins: sideways, through the channels of the displaced, the informal network of travellers and traders and the simply restless who moved between camps and settlements carrying information the way wind carried seeds—without intention, without agenda, by the mechanics of movement through a populated landscape.

A woman named Darya, who sold cloth remnants from a cart she had salvaged from a failed textile workshop in Greenvale, mentioned it at the water pump.

"The quiet one," she said. She was filling a bucket, her hands red from the cold, her voice carrying the flat declarative tone of someone reporting weather. "The one with the chain. They say he fell at the western ridge."

Emma's hands stopped. She was rinsing flour from her forearms, the water cold enough to ache, the flour dissolving into a thin paste that the current carried away. Her hands stopped and the water continued and the paste continued dissolving and the dissolving was indifferent to the stopping, because the world did not pause when a fact arranged itself inside a person's understanding and changed the architecture of what that person knew.

"When?" she asked.

"A week. Maybe more. News travels slow."

A week. Maybe more. The quiet boy who had taught strangers to survive had been dead for a week, maybe more, and the world had continued baking and washing and filling buckets and dissolving flour, and the continuation was not cruelty but reality, the planet's stubborn insistence on rotating regardless of who had stopped rotating with it.

---

The news moved through the camp the way heat moved through bread—from the centre outward, transforming what it touched. By midday, most of the long-term residents had heard some version. The versions varied. In one telling, he had died fighting a hundred soldiers. In another, he had been assassinated in his sleep. In a third, he had sacrificed himself to save a thousand civilians. The numbers inflated with each retelling, the details growing more dramatic as they travelled from mouth to mouth, because grief without facts fills the empty spaces with grandeur, and grandeur was easier to hold than the small, specific, unadorned truth.

Emma did not participate in the inflation. She had met him once—once that mattered, once that she remembered with the clarity that marked the moments in which a person's trajectory changed direction. Chapter twenty-one of her displacement, by her internal reckoning. The night by the fire when her name became her name again.

She remembered his hands. Not his face—the face was harder to reconstruct, the features blurred by time and by the particular quality of his attention, which was the thing she remembered most clearly and which had nothing to do with features. His attention had been a physical fact. He had listened to her speak about Thessaly—about the bakery, the ovens, the door that stuck in wet weather—and the listening had not been the polite simulation of listening that she had encountered in every administrative interaction since her displacement, the head-nodding, eye-contact-maintaining theatre of institutional compassion. He had listened the way her dough listened to her hands: by receiving the pressure and responding to it, by taking the shape of what was given without resistance and without distortion.

He had taught her things. Small things. Ordinary things. The night after the interview, when the fire was low and the camp was settling into the uneasy quiet that passed for rest, he had sat beside her at the edge of the light and spoken about logistics—about how to organise a food distribution that minimised waste, about how to read a queue for signs of tension before the tension became conflict, about how to position a water station so that the filling and the carrying did not create a bottleneck.

"You already know this," he had said. His voice had carried the particular quality of a person stating a fact, not offering a compliment. "You ran a bakery. You managed supply and demand and customer flow every day. The skills are the same. The scale is different."

The skills were the same. She had not believed it at the time. She believed it now.

---

That evening, in the refectory tent, the camp's informal council gathered. Not a council in any institutional sense—no seals, no seats, no authority beyond the authority that competence and consistency conferred. There was Yosef, the carpenter, who had built most of the camp's durable structures and who spoke with the economy of a man who measured words the way he measured timber: by what was needed, not by what was available. There was old Margit, who had been a district nurse in Greenvale before the consolidation and who still carried her medical bag, the leather cracked and the instruments incomplete but the knowledge intact. There was Davan, a shepherd from the uplands who had arrived with forty sheep and a dog and had become, by the logic of pastoral economy, the camp's primary source of wool, milk, and profanity.

They did not eulogise. Eulogies required a relationship with the dead that was personal, and their relationship with Aisen had been practical—mediated through the things he had taught and the systems he had helped establish and the small, specific improvements to daily existence that his network had facilitated. The rope technique for securing tent poles in high wind, which someone had learned from someone who had learned from someone who had sat in a training circle he had organised. The observation game he taught children—the exercise in noticing details, in reading a room's temperature by reading its occupants' postures—which had become, in the camp's adaptation, a way of monitoring for signs of illness or distress before they required intervention. The distribution system for rationed supplies: the numbered tokens, the rotating priority, the accountability ledger that prevented theft not through punishment but through visibility.

These were his monuments. They did not know his name was attached to them. Some did. Most did not. The attachment was irrelevant. The monuments functioned.

"The rope hold he showed us," Yosef said. He was whittling a peg, the knife moving in strokes so small they were nearly invisible, the shavings curling away from the blade like whispered syllables. "Three passes through the loop before you stake. I've taught it to every new arrival since the spring."

"The children's game," Margit added. Her hands were folded in her lap, the knuckles swollen with the arthritis that the damp exacerbated and that she treated with a willow-bark compress she prepared each morning with the same methodical care she had once given to sutures. "The noticing game. I use it in triage. I teach the older children to play it with the younger ones—count the things that are different today, count the things that are the same. They think it's play. It's surveillance. It catches the early signs."

"His distribution system saved us three disputes in the winter," Davan said. He was not a man given to sentiment. His grief, if it was grief, expressed itself as a slight increase in the precision of his speech—the words arriving more deliberately, as though each one were a sheep being counted through a gate. "Three disputes that would've split us. The tokens. The ledger. It's not dramatic. It works."

*It works.* The phrase settled over the tent like a blanket drawn over sleeping shoulders—not warming, exactly, but covering, providing the basic insulation between the body and the cold. It works. The methods work. The methods work because they're ordinary. Anyone can—

The sentence completed itself in Emma's mind with the echo of a voice she had heard once and remembered always, the way she remembered the sound of her mother's oven door opening—not the words but the quality, the timbre, the specific frequency of a person speaking a truth they had tested against reality and found sound.

---

She baked the next morning at dawn.

The air above the floodplain was cold and held the mineral smell of clay and river water, and the sky was the grey that preceded colour, the horizon carrying the faintest suggestion of warmth at its eastern edge where the sun would arrive in its own time, unhurried, indifferent to the daily negotiations of the people who lived beneath it. The ovens had been fired since the previous evening—the coals banked, the heat sleeping in the clay the way memory slept in the muscles, ready to be called upon when the call was made.

She worked the dough. Flour and water and salt and the wild yeast she cultivated in a crock beside her cot—the starter was a descendant of starters, a lineage of fermentation that connected this morning's bread to the bread her grandmother had baked in the bakery on the square, and the connection was not symbolic but biological, the yeast strains carrying their ancestors in their cellular memory the way Emma carried hers in her hands. She kneaded. The dough resisted and then yielded and then resisted again, the rhythm of the kneading a conversation between what she demanded and what the material could give, and the conversation was honest, and the honesty was the thing that had not been taken from her.

The children came first. Then the old. Then the able-bodied, who had work to do and did it and who paused at the refectory table to take their bread and nod and move on, the nod carrying everything that the morning did not have time for: the acknowledgement of sustenance received, the gratitude that was not spoken because speaking it would slow the morning's machinery, the understanding that the bread was there because someone woke before dawn to make it and the making was a form of care and the care was the infrastructure on which everything else was built.

Emma watched them eat. She watched the way the children held the bread—some in both hands, protective, as though the bread might be reclaimed; others in one hand, casual, the children who had been in the camp long enough that bread had become reliable and reliability had become invisible, the way all successful infrastructure became invisible, its presence noticed only in its absence. She watched and she thought of the quiet boy who had sat beside her and told her that the skills were the same, and she understood now what he had meant, and the understanding was not revelation but recognition, the way recognising a face in a crowd was not discovering the face but locating it among the known.

The camp continued. Not unchanged—change was the camp's resting state, the constant arrival and departure, the daily improvisation of survival performed by people who had been given no script and were writing one in real-time with the materials at hand. But continuing. The tents held. The bread baked. The children played the noticing game and counted the things that were different and the things that were the same. The rope holds secured against the wind that came from the northwest on Wednesdays and from the southwest on Saturdays and that could not be argued with, only prepared for. The distribution system churned its tokens and its ledger and its quiet, functional accountability.

A man was dead. The methods lived.

Emma set the next batch to rise. The dough breathed beneath its cloth, expanding in the warm air beside the oven, the yeast performing its ancient alchemy of conversion—taking sugar and returning gas and alcohol, the simplest chemistry, the most ordinary miracle. She covered the crock and wiped her hands and stepped outside into the grey morning where the camp was waking and the river was moving and the sky was opening its slow eye above the floodplain, and the world was the world, and the world went on, and the going on was not a betrayal of the dead but a fulfilment of everything the dead had worked to make possible.

She went on. They all did. Not heroically. Not with the self-conscious grandeur of people performing their own survival for an audience. Ordinarily. With bread, and rope holds, and the small game of noticing played by children who did not know they were carrying forward the legacy of a man they had never met but whose methods had reached them through the chain of hands that hands created when they taught other hands how to work.

The bread rose. The camp continued. The morning arrived in full.
