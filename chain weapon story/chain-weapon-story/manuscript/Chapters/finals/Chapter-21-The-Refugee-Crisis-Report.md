The woman was sitting against the granary wall when Aisen found her.

Not sitting—that word implied choice, the selection of a place and the decision to occupy it. What the woman was doing against the granary wall was something closer to arrival, the final collapse of forward momentum into stillness, the body reaching the end of its capacity for movement and simply stopping where it stopped, the way a stone thrown across water eventually stops skipping and sinks. She had a child in her arms. The child was asleep—or unconscious, Aisen could not tell from this distance, the difference between the two states visible only in the quality of the breathing, the depth of it, and the evening light was too poor for that kind of reading. The woman's head was tilted back against the rough stone, and her eyes were open but not seeing, fixed on a middle distance that contained nothing because it was not a place she was looking at but a place she was looking away from.

The town of Ashenmere sat three miles down the hill from the academy, connected by a road that the institution maintained just well enough to signal that the town existed within its sphere of influence and just poorly enough to signal that the town's needs were secondary to the academy's purposes. Students went there for market-day provisions, for the kind of gossip that did not circulate within institutional walls, for the tavern on the square that served beer so bad that drinking it constituted a minor rebellion against the academy's standards of excellence. The granary occupied the town's eastern edge, where the buildings thinned and the land began its slow concession to the scrublands that stretched toward the border. It was not a place that drew attention. That, Aisen understood with a clarity that felt like weight settling in his chest, was the point.

He had come to Ashenmere for ink—the blue-black variety that the academy's supply office stocked irregularly and that the stationer on the square kept in consistent supply, the kind of practical errand that gave a student reason to be off campus without requiring explanation. But the ink had been a secondary motive grafted onto a primary one. For three days, reports had been filtering through Rin's information channels—whispered fragments gathered from market traders, from a carter she had cultivated as a source, from the shifting patterns of road traffic that she read the way Kaelen read root systems—that people were moving through the border scrublands in numbers that the official reports did not account for. People moving at night. People moving without roads. People moving in the direction of anywhere that was not where they had been.

Aisen approached the woman. His footsteps on the packed dirt were deliberate—audible enough to announce himself, slow enough to not alarm. He had learned this calibration in the tavern years ago, the careful introduction of presence into the proximity of someone who had reason to fear the approach of strangers. The woman's eyes tracked the sound before they tracked him, her head turning a fraction of a second after her gaze, the sequence reversed from the way alert people moved—she was hearing the world before she was seeing it, functioning on the senses that required less effort, the body conserving what it could.

"There's a well behind the cooper's workshop," Aisen said. "Forty paces south. The water's clean."

The woman stared at him. Her face was thin in the way that faces become thin not from a day's hunger but from weeks of it, the fat reserves beneath the skin consumed so that the architecture of the skull became legible—cheekbones, jaw, the orbital ridges that usually live beneath the surface of a face but were now, in this woman's face, the dominant features, the structural truth that flesh normally conceals. She was perhaps thirty. Perhaps forty. The kind of exhaustion she carried made age a speculation rather than an observation. On her neck, below the left ear, a burn scar twisted the skin into a knot of tissue that was still pink at the edges—recent enough to be tender, old enough to have begun its permanent reshaping of the surface it occupied.

"The water's clean," Aisen repeated, because the woman had not responded, and because repetition was sometimes the bridge between hearing and comprehension when exhaustion had widened the gap between the two.

"I had water," she said. Her voice was hoarse and flat, pitched in the register of someone who had stopped modulating for expression and was simply conveying information at the minimum cost of effort. "At the river. Three days ago. I had water there."

Three days. Aisen let the number settle. The nearest river was the Selthe, which ran sixteen miles east through the scrublands before turning south toward the border. Sixteen miles in three days, carrying a child, without water since the crossing.

"Stay here," he said. "I'll be back."

He found Kaelen at the southeast edge of town, where the scrublands pushed against the last garden plot like a slow tide against a failing levee. Kaelen was crouched among the wild plants that grew in the margin between cultivated ground and waste ground—the interstitial space that interested him more than either extreme, the place where domesticated soil and wild soil conducted their negotiations. His fingers were in the earth. His green-gold eyes had the half-focused quality they took on during communion with root systems, the expression that Aisen had learned to read as concentration rather than absence.

"There's a woman at the granary," Aisen said. "With a child. She hasn't had water in three days."

Kaelen's hands lifted from the soil. The transition was immediate—the gentle focus of the nature-mage replaced by the sharper attention of someone being told a practical thing that required a practical response. Dirt fell from his fingers in small cascades. "Food?"

"I didn't ask. But from the look of her, longer than three days."

Kaelen stood. He looked at the wild margin where he'd been working, and Aisen watched the calculation happen—Kaelen's particular kind of calculation, which was not mathematical but ecological, an assessment of what the land held and what could be taken from it without damage. "There's burdock root along the Selthe path. Dock leaves. Wild garlic if you know where. I can gather enough for a meal in twenty minutes." He paused. "A hot meal needs fire. Fire needs a place that isn't visible."

"I know a place," Aisen said, though what he meant was: *I know someone who knows places.*

---

Rin arrived at the granary within the hour, moving along the alley behind the cooper's workshop with the soundless economy that was her natural state of locomotion. In the deepening twilight, the star-marks on her forearms glowed with their faint silver luminescence—she had pushed her sleeves up for the walk, and the marks pulsed gently, a rhythm that Aisen had learned to associate with heightened alertness, Rin's body broadcasting on frequencies that most people could not receive.

"The old drying barn," Rin said, before Aisen could speak. "South side, past the tanner. It's been empty since the tanner moved his operations closer to the river. The roof leaks but the walls are solid and the sight lines from the road are blocked by the hedgerow." She looked at the woman against the granary wall. Something shifted in Rin's face—not softening, exactly, because Rin's face did not soften in observable ways, but a reorganization of the guardedness, the professional assessment incorporating a new variable. "How long?"

"She says three days without water. Longer without food. The child—"

"I can see the child." Rin crouched beside the woman with a fluidity that made the movement look like something between sitting and kneeling, a posture that placed her at eye level without looming. "My name is Rin. We're going to move you somewhere warmer. Can you walk?"

The woman—who had watched these arrivals with the numb attention of someone past the threshold where new information produces new reactions—looked at Rin's forearms. At the star-marks. Something flickered in her expression, a recognition or a memory, gone before Aisen could catalogue it.

"I walked from Thessaly," the woman said. "I can walk forty paces."

---

The drying barn smelled of old leather and dried herbs and the particular mustiness of enclosed spaces that have been empty long enough for the air to thicken with its own age. Lysandra was waiting inside—Rin had sent word through channels Aisen did not fully understand, the message traveling through the network of small alliances and careful trusts that Rin maintained the way other people maintained friendships, with investment and attention and the occasional strategic withdrawal. Lysandra had brought blankets from the academy stores, folded inside a canvas bag that looked, to anyone who might have noticed, like laundry.

"I liberated them from the infirmary surplus," Lysandra said, her voice carrying the mountain calm that was her default register—unhurried, pragmatic, warm in the way that stone is warm after absorbing a day's sun. Her grey-blue eyes assessed the woman and the child with the evaluative care of someone trained to triage. "The child needs warmth first. Then water, then food. In that order."

Kaelen arrived minutes later with his forage: burdock roots scrubbed clean in the stream behind the tanner's, a bundle of wild garlic wrapped in dock leaves, a handful of late blackberries that he'd found along the hedgerow, their skins splitting with ripeness. He built a small fire in the barn's old hearting pit with the efficiency of someone for whom fire-making was not a survival skill but a collaboration—the kindling arranged with the same attention to structure that he gave to root systems, the flame coaxed rather than forced, the smoke guided upward through the barn's ventilation gaps by what Aisen suspected was a subtle redirection of the draft, magic so minor and so integrated into the physical act that distinguishing one from the other was impossible.

The woman ate. She ate the way people eat when eating has become reflex rather than pleasure—methodically, without tasting, the body performing its function while the mind occupied itself elsewhere. The child woke when the warmth of the fire reached them and ate from the woman's hand, small fingers closing around pieces of burdock root with a grip that was fierce and animal and entirely without self-consciousness. The child was three, perhaps four. The age where language exists but has not yet become the primary tool, where the body speaks first and the mouth follows. The child did not speak.

Aisen sat against the barn's far wall with his journal open on his knees. He had not asked permission to write. He was not writing yet. He was waiting for the space between emergency and conversation to open, the gap that appears when the body's immediate needs are met and the mind, no longer occupied by survival, begins to process what survival has required.

The woman's name was Emma.

She said it the way people say their names when they have been unnamed for too long—with the slight hesitation of someone reclaiming something that has been set down and left unattended, the name requiring a moment of recognition before it could be spoken as belonging to her again. "Emma Vasik. From Thessaly. The village, not the province." A distinction that mattered to her, a precision that Aisen noted, because precision in speech after extremity meant that the speaker was still organizing their world, still insisting on the categories that chaos had tried to dissolve.

"Thessaly was a baking village," Emma said. The fire had warmed her enough that her body had begun to relax its defensive compression—her shoulders dropping, her spine settling against the blanket Lysandra had folded behind her, her hands unclenching by degrees. The burn scar on her neck caught the firelight and held it, the damaged skin reflecting at a different angle than the skin around it, a permanent alteration. "That's what we did. Baked. The province had wheat fields, and we had ovens, and the road between the two was sixty years old and everyone on it knew where the bread came from." She paused. The child stirred in her lap, resettling. "I had a bakery. My mother's. Her mother's before that. Three ovens, a stone floor, a door that stuck in wet weather. That was—it was a life. A real one. With a name. Emma's, on the square."

The fire crackled. The wild garlic Kaelen had thrown on the coals released its pungent sweetness into the closed air, layering over the leather-and-herb smell of the barn with an immediacy that was almost aggressive, the scent of food overpowering the scent of abandonment. Lysandra shifted where she sat near the door, her position chosen for sight lines—she could see the approaching path through the gap between the barn doors, her body angled in the watchful posture of someone standing guard without making the guarding obvious.

"When did they come?" Rin asked. She had positioned herself near Emma but not beside her—the calculated distance, the space that offered proximity without pressure. Her voice was even, professional, the voice of someone conducting an interview, but beneath the professional surface Aisen could hear the effort the evenness required, the discipline of keeping the questions neutral when the answers were not.

"Six months ago. Seven. I stopped counting." Emma's eyes were on the fire. "They called it consolidation. That was the word. The Covenant sent administrators—not soldiers, that's important, they sent *administrators*—with papers that had seals and numbers and language that took a full day to read and three days to understand. The papers said that Thessaly was being consolidated into the Greenvale administrative district. It said that resources—our resources, our ovens, our granary, our seed stores—were being redirected to support the district's strategic priorities."

"What were the strategic priorities?" Aisen asked. His pen was moving now—the words going into the journal in the small, precise script he used for testimony, the handwriting different from his analytical notes, tighter, more controlled, as if the physical act of recording demanded a formality that his own thoughts did not.

"They never said. That was the design, I think. If they'd said 'we need your grain for the army,' people would have understood. People can make sense of a reason, even a bad one. But 'strategic priorities'—" Emma's mouth twisted, the muscles of her face performing an expression that was not a smile and was not grief but something between, the face attempting to process an absurdity that the mind had already categorized. "Strategic priorities means everything. It means nothing. It means whatever they need it to mean on the day they need it."

Kaelen was listening from his place by the fire, feeding it pieces of scrap wood at intervals that maintained the heat without raising the flame high enough to send smoke above the barn's roofline. His face held the particular stillness that Aisen had come to recognize as anger translated through Kaelen's temperament—not the hot, visible anger that most people expressed, but the deep, root-level anger of someone who understood systems and was hearing one described in its most destructive function.

"They separated the families first," Emma continued. Her voice had found a rhythm now—the flat, precise cadence of someone who has told this story in their own mind a hundred times and is now, for the first time, giving it to someone else's ears. "Not all at once. Gradually. The men were assigned to labor details in Greenvale—road work, construction, the kind of work that required them to be somewhere else for days, then weeks. The women and children were told to remain in Thessaly for processing. Processing." She tested the word the way one tests a loose tooth. "They processed us for four months. Forms. Interviews. Assessments of our skills, our property, our—they assessed the *ovens*. My ovens. Someone came with a ledger and wrote down the dimensions of my ovens."

The child in her lap had fallen asleep again, breathing in the deep, unmeasured rhythm of a body that had found warmth after cold, the respiratory system surrendering to comfort with the absolute trust of a creature too young to know that comfort might be temporary. Emma's hand rested on the child's back, the fingers splayed, the touch both protective and habitual—the touch of someone who had been holding a child for a very long time without setting them down.

"When the land started dying, they stopped pretending it was about administration." Emma's voice went quieter. "The fields outside the village—the wheat fields, the ones that had fed Thessaly for three generations—they just... stopped. Not drought. Not blight. Something else. The soil went strange. Grey. The wheat came up twisted, or it didn't come up at all. Animals at the edge of the forest started behaving wrong—aggressive, confused. One of them—" Her hand went to the scar on her neck, the gesture involuntary, the body remembering what the mind was describing. "Something came out of the tree line that looked like a dog but wasn't. It was *wrong*. The proportions were wrong. It burned. Not with fire. With something inside it, something that came through its skin."

Silence in the barn. The fire consuming its fuel with the quiet patience of a process that does not hurry. The garlic scent fading as the coals settled.

"After that, the administrators said Thessaly was no longer viable for habitation. They used those words. *No longer viable for habitation.* As if we hadn't lived there for sixty years. As if viability were a thing decided in an office rather than demonstrated by a life." Emma looked up from the fire for the first time, and her eyes found Aisen's—not because he had positioned himself to be found but because he was the one writing, the one holding the pen, the one whose attention was taking the shape of a record. "They offered relocation. To a processing camp near the border. They said it was temporary. They said it was for our safety." Her mouth formed the word *safety* with the exaggerated care of someone handling something fragile and fraudulent. "My neighbor went. She had two children and believed them. I took my daughter and walked the other way."

Aisen wrote. He wrote every word, and between the words he wrote the things that were not words—the pauses, the gestures, the quality of the silence that followed certain sentences, the way Emma's hand tightened on her daughter's back when she said *processing camp*, the way her eyes went flat and distant when she said *no longer viable*. He wrote these things because they were evidence, and because testimony that included only words was testimony with the humanity removed, a skeleton without the tissue that made it a body.

When Emma finished—not with a conclusion but with a trailing off, the story reaching the present moment and having nowhere further to go—the barn was dark except for the fire's embers. Lysandra had not moved from the door. Rin had not moved from her position near Emma. Kaelen had not moved from the fire. The four of them occupied the barn's space in a configuration that was, Aisen realized, the geometry of their first coordinated action—Lysandra securing the perimeter, Rin gathering intelligence, Kaelen providing sustenance, Aisen recording. Each of them doing the thing they could do, in the place where that thing was needed. It was clumsy. It was small. It was, in the scale of the crisis Emma described, almost nothing.

But Emma and her daughter were warm. They were fed. They were, for this night, in this barn that smelled of old leather and wild garlic, in a town three miles from an institution that taught the systems responsible for their displacement—they were safe. Temporarily. The word *temporarily* was important. Aisen did not let himself forget it.

"We'll find somewhere more permanent tomorrow," Rin said. Her voice was steady, but her star-marks were pulsing fast—faster than Aisen had seen them, the luminescence cycling through its patterns with an urgency that suggested calculations happening beneath the professional surface. "I have contacts in the western quarter. A woman who takes in borders, no questions. It's not free, but it's not expensive."

"I can supplement her food," Kaelen said. "The hedgerows and wild margins around town produce enough for one person if you know what to look for. I know what to look for."

"And I can help with the mountain routes if you need to move further north," Lysandra added from the door, her voice carrying the quiet authority of someone offering a skill they had tested against the world's difficulties and found sufficient. "The passes are hard, but they're unwatched. The Covenant doesn't patrol what it considers impassable."

Aisen looked at the three of them—at Kaelen with his dirt-stained hands and his knowledge of living systems, at Rin with her star-marks and her networks, at Lysandra with her mountain-trained body and her understanding of terrain—and saw, for the first time, not just individuals with complementary skills but the preliminary architecture of something that could act. Not an organization. Not yet. Something smaller and simpler and more frightened than that: a group of people who had seen a specific suffering and decided, without discussion, without a plan, without knowing what it would cost them, that seeing was not enough.

He closed the journal. The ink was still wet on the last page—Emma's words, Emma's pauses, Emma's silences, transcribed into a record that would outlast this evening, that would become evidence if evidence were ever needed, that was already, in its mere existence, an act of defiance against the administrative language that had reduced Thessaly to a word on a form and Emma Vasik to a number in a ledger.

"This changes things," Aisen said. He did not say what it changed, or how. He was not certain yet. But the direction was clear—the same direction that the bramble in the garden had been guided, not by cutting but by redirection, the slow recognition that a system needed to be answered not with understanding alone but with the specific, clumsy, insufficient, necessary act of doing something about it.

Outside the barn, the night had settled over Ashenmere with the weight of a season turning. The air smelled of wood smoke and cooling earth and, faintly, of wild garlic—the trace of what they had given, lingering in the air above what they had done.
