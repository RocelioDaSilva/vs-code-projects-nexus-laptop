The border town of Gresik sat on the map like a bruise—a discolored sprawl of timber palisades and mud-brick warehouses where three trade roads met and none of them wanted to stay. It was the kind of place that existed because geography demanded it, not because anyone had chosen to build something beautiful there, and it wore that reluctance in every plank and nail. The air tasted of livestock and the sulfurous tang of the hot springs that fed the public baths, and beneath that, a mineral sharpness that Aisen had learned to associate with altitude—the mountains were closer here, pressing their cold weight against the sky like something watching from a height.

He was thirteen, nearly fourteen, and he had come with Harald on what his father called a supply run, though Aisen suspected the word concealed a more complicated errand. The wagon carried the usual provisions—salt, lamp oil, the coarse wheat flour that baked into bread dense enough to serve as currency in the lean months—but Harald had also packed two sealed letters in his coat pocket, and he had asked Aisen to wear his good boots, the ones with the reinforced soles. These were the kinds of details that did not survive in isolation. Paired together, they meant that someone in Gresik expected them, and that Harald anticipated the possibility of leaving quickly.

The town's market was louder than Valdimere's by an order of magnitude—not busier, precisely, but unregulated in a way that gave each voice permission to compete. Drovers shouted prices over the bleating of goats penned in wooden crates. A farrier's hammer rang against an anvil in a rhythm that syncopated against the clatter of wagon wheels on cobblestones worn smooth by decades of traffic going nowhere it wanted to go. Aisen catalogued these sounds the way he always catalogued them: ingredients in a recipe he was still learning to cook. The noise told him things. A market that shouted was a market where people did not trust the quality of their goods to speak for itself.

Harald moved through the crowd with the particular alertness of a man who had learned to be comfortable in uncomfortable places. He delivered his letters—one to a dry goods merchant with a limp, one to a woman who ran a cartwright's shop and spoke to Harald in a dialect Aisen could not follow—and then steered them toward the northern edge of town, where the palisade gave way to open ground and the road climbed into terrain too rough for wagons.

"We'll eat here," Harald said, nodding toward a wayhouse that squatted near the gate like a toad waiting for flies. "Stay close to me today."

It was the second sentence that sharpened Aisen's attention. Harald said *stay close* the way other fathers said *I love you*—rarely, and only when the weight of it could not be carried by silence alone.

---

They were halfway through a meal of smoked river trout and flatbread—the fish tasting of char and the cold clean mineral of snowmelt—when the sound outside changed. Not louder, but different in texture. The market's chaotic overture narrowed into something with a single focal point, the way a room full of conversation will go quiet when someone draws a blade. Aisen heard it in the absence first: the farrier's hammer stopped. Then the drovers' shouts thinned to murmurs. Then a silence settled over the northern quarter of Gresik that was not peaceful but pressurized, like the air before a river decides to flood.

Harald set down his fork. His hand did not move to his knife, but his posture changed—shoulders squaring, weight shifting to the balls of his feet. It was the geometry of readiness without the declaration of intent, and Aisen had spent enough years watching his father to know the difference between caution and alarm. This was something between the two: a door Harald had hoped to find locked, found standing open.

"Don't stare," Harald said quietly. "But look."

Aisen looked.

The figure was coming through the northern gate, and the first thing Aisen noticed was that it moved wrong. He had spent years learning to read the grammar of human movement—Harald's river-thinking, Tarovin's precise economy, the thousand variations of stride and stance that populated The Crossing's common room every evening. He understood gait the way a musician understood tempo: instinctively, structurally, as a language that bodies spoke whether they intended to or not. And this body was speaking something he had never heard.

The figure wore plate armor—dark, unornamented, the kind of functional steel that announced no allegiance and invited no admiration. A Covenant sigil was embossed on the left pauldron, small enough that you had to know what you were looking for. But the armor was almost incidental to the motion underneath. A man in full plate moved with a particular cadence: deliberate, weighted, each step a negotiation between the body's desire for fluidity and the metal's insistence on rigidity. This figure moved as if the armor were skin. No negotiation. No rigidity. The stride was long and liquid, with a predatory rolling quality in the hips that suggested a spine more flexible than human architecture typically allowed. The shoulders shifted with each step in a way that was almost feline—not graceful in the courtly sense but efficient in the way that a hunting animal is efficient, every joint and muscle organized around a single purpose that had nothing to do with beauty and everything to do with the closing of distance.

Aisen felt something tighten in his chest that he could not immediately name. It was not fear, though fear was a component. It was the recognition of a pattern that should not exist—human form moving with inhuman fluency, armored weight carried with predatory lightness. Like watching a river flow uphill. The eye understood what it was seeing while the mind refused to file it correctly.

The figure crossed the market square without pausing, and the silence followed it like a wake follows a ship. People stepped aside—not hurrying, not panicking, but rearranging themselves around the figure's trajectory with the unconscious coordination of a school of fish parting for something larger. A woman with a basket of onions pressed herself against a wall. A drover pulled his goats closer. A child was lifted and carried without a word spoken. The town had clearly done this before.

"Sit," Harald said, though Aisen hadn't moved. Perhaps he had leaned forward. Perhaps Harald's instruction was preventive rather than corrective. "Eat your fish."

Aisen ate his fish. It tasted of nothing now, the smoke and mineral overwritten by the adrenaline that was doing interesting things to his palate. Through the wayhouse window—narrow, set deep in the stone wall like a suspicious eye—he watched the armored figure move down the road toward the northern palisade, where a detachment of local militia had arranged themselves with the studied casualness of men who wanted desperately to be somewhere else.

"Who is that?" Aisen asked.

Harald was quiet for the length of three breaths. Aisen counted them because counting was what he did when a silence was trying to tell him something.

"His name is Corvin Ashford," Harald said. "He's a Covenant enforcer. Hunts deserters—soldiers, scholars, anyone who tries to leave the Covenant's service without permission." Harald's voice was level, but his hands were still on the table, and Aisen could see the tendons standing in relief beneath the skin. Not gripping anything. Just ready to. "Locals call him the Hollow Knight."

"Why hollow?"

Another careful silence. "Because he gave something away to become what he is. Something that doesn't grow back."

---

Aisen wanted to leave the wayhouse, to find a vantage point where he could watch the Hollow Knight without the narrow frame of the window compressing his observations into a keyhole's worth of data. Instead, he sat. He ate. He listened to the town recover its voice in increments—first the farrier's hammer, then a drover's curse, then the gradual resurgence of haggling and complaint that meant the market had decided to continue existing despite what had passed through it.

It was an hour later, when they were loading the wagon at the dry goods merchant's yard, that Aisen saw Corvin again. Closer this time—across the width of the yard, perhaps forty paces, close enough. Corvin stood speaking to a Covenant administratrix in gray robes, his helmet removed and held under one arm. The face beneath was younger than Aisen had expected, and that wrongness added to the catalogue of wrongnesses he was building. Late twenties, perhaps thirty. A strong jaw, high cheekbones, skin that had been weathered by campaign rather than age. But the eyes were what held Aisen's attention and would not release it.

They were flat. Not angry, not tired, not focused—flat in the way that a lake is flat when the wind dies and the surface becomes a mirror that refuses to show you what lives beneath it. Aisen had seen exhaustion flatten a person's gaze before; he'd seen grief do it, and drink, and the long erosion of disappointment that made some of The Crossing's regulars look like they were watching something behind whatever was in front of them. This was different. This flatness was architectural. It had been built into the structure of the face as deliberately as a wall is built into a house—not the absence of something but the presence of an absence, a space where something had been removed and the removal had been load-bearing.

Corvin's hands, Aisen noticed, moved in a repetitive pattern at his sides—a flexing and unflexing of the fingers, rhythmic, unconscious, like a pulse translated into gesture. The motion had the quality of memory: not a tic but an echo, as if the hands remembered reaching for something that the mind had been told to forget.

A merchant loading crates nearby—an older man with the sunburned complexion and permanently squinted eyes of someone who had spent decades on the southern trade roads—caught the direction of Aisen's attention and shook his head.

"Don't watch too long, boy. He can feel it."

Aisen looked away. Then back. The merchant was securing a rope with hands that knew knots the way Aisen's knew a pen. "You've seen him before?"

"Twice. Once in Kellmarsh, once on the Dusken Road." The merchant spat, not with contempt but with the reflexive need to expel something that tasted wrong. "They say he was a man once. A full man, I mean—loved a woman, had friends, laughed at things that were funny. Then the Covenant offered him something, or he asked for something, and whatever bargain he struck took the part of him that could care."

"Took it how?"

The merchant's hands paused on the rope. He looked at Aisen with the particular expression of an adult deciding how much truth a child's architecture could bear. "Beast-fusion, they call it. Stitching the body to something other. Gives you strength, speed, senses no human was meant to carry. Takes the price from somewhere the healers can't reach." He returned to his rope. "I heard he loved a woman once. If you told him that now, he'd hear the words, understand the grammar, and feel nothing. Like reading about rain when you've never been wet."

The simile struck Aisen like a physical thing—a small, cold stone lodged somewhere behind his ribs. He looked again at Corvin Ashford and saw the armored figure with new information layered over it like a transparency laid on a map. The predatory gait was not grace; it was the body running on a different engine, one that burned a fuel no human being manufactured naturally. The flat eyes were not discipline; they were the windows of a house where someone had moved out and taken the furniture. The hand-flexing was not readiness; it was the body remembering a life the mind had been emptied of, the way a phantom limb aches for a hand that is no longer there.

---

Harald found Aisen by the wagon, writing in his notebook. This was not unusual—Aisen wrote in his notebook the way other boys fidgeted, as a method of processing the world's surplus of information—but something in the quality of the writing must have told Harald what he needed to know, because he sat on the wagon's tailgate and waited.

"He was a person," Aisen said finally. He did not look up from the notebook. His handwriting was smaller than usual, the letters compressed, as if the page were not quite large enough for what he was trying to contain. "Before. He was a complete person."

"Yes."

"And he chose to stop being one. For power."

"For what the power allowed him to do. Or what the Covenant convinced him it allowed." Harald's voice carried the careful weight of a man choosing each word like a step across unstable ground. "There's a difference between choosing and being chosen, and the Covenant has always been skilled at making the second feel like the first."

Aisen turned this over. He thought of Tarovin's trembling hands—the cost of precision carried in the body's protest against its own constraints. He thought of Harald's knuckles, scarred from years of teaching hands to do what hands were not designed to do without complaint. Every form of power he had witnessed extracted its price in the currency of the body. But this—what Corvin Ashford represented—was a price extracted from somewhere deeper, a payment made not in flesh but in the architecture of self. You could survive the loss of a finger and still be who you were. Could you survive the loss of the capacity to love and still be anyone at all?

"Is he still in there?" Aisen asked. "The person he was?"

Harald looked toward the town, where Corvin's armored figure was no longer visible but whose presence still lingered in the way people moved—slightly off-center, slightly too aware of the spaces between themselves and the northern gate.

"I don't know," Harald said. "That's the honest answer. The older stories say beast-fusion is a door that only opens from one side. The Covenant's scholars say it's refinement—cutting away what doesn't serve the mission. The people who've seen the results say it's emptying."

"What do you say?"

Harald was quiet for a long time. The market had fully recovered now—the noise of ordinary commerce filling the square like water returning to a channel—but the quiet between father and son occupied its own space, separate and insulated.

"I say that a man who can't feel the weight of what he does will never choose to stop doing it. And a weapon that doesn't know it's a weapon is the most dangerous kind." He put a hand on Aisen's shoulder. The weight of it was familiar, calibrated, the same hand that had corrected Aisen's stance a thousand times and steadied him twice as many. "There are many kinds of strength. What you saw today is one. It is effective. It is also a grave."

They left Gresik in the late afternoon, the wagon heavier with supplies and Aisen heavier with something that did not register on any scale. The road south unwound through stands of birch and pine, and the air cooled as the altitude dropped, carrying the sweetness of tree sap and the green exhalation of growing things—smells that belonged to a world operating on principles that had nothing to do with the Covenant or its monstrous bargains. Aisen sat in the back of the wagon and watched the road shrink behind them, and the border town contracted to a point on the horizon, and somewhere in that shrinking geometry Corvin Ashford was still moving with his liquid predatory stride, still flexing his hands in the rhythm of a memory his mind could no longer access.

Aisen opened his notebook to a fresh page. At the top, he wrote a heading he had not planned: *Kinds of Strength.* Beneath it, in the compressed handwriting of a boy trying to fit too much meaning into too little space, he wrote:

*There is a kind of strength that costs everything and knows it. There is a kind that costs everything and doesn't. The second kind is not stronger. It is only more complete in its loss. A weapon that cannot feel itself cutting does not cut less deeply—it simply never chooses to stop.*

He closed the notebook. The sunset bled through the birch trees in long amber slashes, and the wheels of the wagon found the ruts of the south road, and the rhythm of travel settled over Aisen like a second skin. But beneath that rhythm, something new had taken root—a small, cold knowledge, lodged like a seed in winter soil, that the world contained forms of power that did not merely demand sacrifice but consumed it, and that the distance between becoming powerful and becoming empty might be narrower than any map could show.
