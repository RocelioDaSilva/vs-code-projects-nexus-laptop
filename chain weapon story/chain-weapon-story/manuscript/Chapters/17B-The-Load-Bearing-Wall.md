# CHAPTER 17B: THE LOAD-BEARING WALL

The engineering brief said the eastern annex extension was complete and ready for occupancy.

Vex stood in the connecting corridor between the old building and the new one and tapped the junction wall with two fingers. The sound was wrong. A complete junction wall in load-bearing masonry should return a low, solid knock — dense material, good contact along the full bearing length. This one gave a mid-range hollow that told her the mortar contact was partial, probably seventy percent fill on the interior coursing, which meant the load path from the new annex roof was transferring through approximately five-eighths of the intended bearing area. Not dangerous. Substandard.

She wrote it in her survey notes — fourth finding in forty minutes, all of similar grade — and kept moving.

Vex's formal role at the Academy was *Structural Assessment Practicum, Third-Year*. She had a faculty supervisor who signed her work orders and a report format she followed and access to building plans stamped *Academy Maintenance — Authorized Personnel.* The role was genuinely useful: older stone buildings had ongoing maintenance needs, junior architects and engineers were chronically in short supply, and Vex was very good at finding the places where structures were doing things their designers had not intended. The tap-and-listen method she used was old — pre-Academy, learned from a master builder in the city's outer districts who charged his clients by how long it took him to find problems, which incentivized speed and accuracy in equal measure. She had worked with him from age eleven to sixteen, carrying tools and listening. When she came to the Academy she brought the method with her.

She also brought her habit of documenting everything.

The new annex had six rooms on the ground floor. She had the original plans — dated fourteen months ago, submitted for approval under a standard maintenance-and-expansion brief, approved by the Academy Facilities office in the usual three-week cycle. The plans showed four rooms: a storage room, two multipurpose teaching spaces, and a staff study. That was what the original brief described. That was what the approval was for.

The actual construction had six rooms.

Vex stood at the threshold of the fifth room — the one that should have been the eastern edge of the second teaching space but was instead a separate, walled space with a door that had a different lock grade than everything else on the floor — and tapped the partition wall.

Load-bearing. Not a dividing partition wall. Someone had modified the structural design during construction to make this room a permanent, load-bearing enclosure.

She opened her field notebook to the floor-plan comparison page she had started preparing. On the left: the approved design. On the right: the as-built, sketched from this morning's survey. The discrepancy was clear and documented. She looked at room five and what it contained: a long table, two-thirds the room's length. A set of document holders along the wall, the kind with sliding compartments for sorting papers. A lamp stand with three lamp positions, indicating multiple workers. A calendar board with columns that were, from where she was standing, too small to read.

She took three steps into the room to read the calendar board.

The columns were labeled by enrollment cohort year. The rows were labeled by what she recognized, after a moment, as District designation numbers from the Provincial Corridor — the same district numbering system that appeared in the regional census records she had referenced last term for a structural load analysis of a provincial granary. The cells of the calendar grid were filled with names.

She stepped back out of the room into the corridor.

Her pulse was slightly elevated. She noted this, the same way she noted the hollow mortar joint and the non-compliant room count: as data that required accurate recording.

Room five was a conscription processing space. The document sorters and the district-coded name lists made this clear. Someone at the Academy — someone with enough authority to modify a structural design during construction without amending the approved plans — had decided that a load-bearing permanent enclosure was needed for conscription administration. Probably the same someone who was correlating student enrollment by provincial district of origin.

She knew the enrollment data existed because she had filed her own. Every student at the Academy filed district-of-origin as part of their enrollment record. The data was supposed to be used for regional distribution analysis and resource allocation. She looked at the name columns again in her memory: full names, district codes, what looked like a classification notation in a third column that she had not been close enough to read.

She went to room six.

Room six was the room that should have been the second teaching space. It was currently being used as a teaching space, or had been recently: chairs in rows, a lecture stand, papers still on the side table as if someone had stepped out for a short break. She walked to the side table. The papers were student assessment records. Upper Academy, third-year cohort. She looked at the top page without touching it.

Standard assessment format: name, cohort year, instructor, performance score, comments. The comment field on the top record read: *Provincial affiliation confirmed. Category 3 recommendation — see Annex.*

She walked back to the corridor.

She had been conducting this structural survey for forty minutes. In that time she had found four substandard construction details of a maintenance-reportable grade, one non-compliant room count against the approved plans, and one room that appeared to be an administrative processing space for correlating student identity data with provincial conscription categories. She had not been looking for any of these things. She had been looking for load path issues and mortar quality.

Her notebook had all of it. It was her habit — she documented what she found, without judgment, because judgment came after documentation. The master builder had taught her this. *Write what you see. The wall does not care what you think of it. Write what you see, and see accurately, and the wall will tell you everything it knows.*

She had been writing what she saw for seven years.

She understood, in this moment, that she had been very efficiently creating a record.

---

The student's name was Perren. He was in Vex's third-year cohort, not a close acquaintance but a recognizable face — she had seen him at the weekly open seminar several times, sat near him once during the fire-load assessment practicum. He had come from the eastern Provincial Corridor, she knew, because he had mentioned it once in passing when they were discussing drainage gradients in high-rainfall zones, something about his home district's flood management problems.

She saw him being walked out of the dormitory building by two officers from the Academy's Administrative Security section at 0840 that morning. She was crossing the courtyard to the reference library — had the survey notes in her bag, was going to compare them against the original approved plans before deciding what to file and with whom. She was not directly involved. She was ten meters away.

Perren had a single bag with him. The kind of bag you were allowed to bring when you were given approximately twenty minutes' notice. The officers' body language was efficient and impersonal — not aggressive, not deliberately degrading, simply the practiced economy of people who did this regularly and had concluded that speed was the most humane approach.

She stopped walking and watched until they turned the corner.

Perren was from District 4. She had seen District 4 on the calendar board. Column four, multiple names. She had not been close enough to read all of them.

She went to the reference library.

She pulled the original approved plans for the eastern annex and laid them on the reading table.

She put her survey notes next to them.

She looked at them for a long time.

Her notebook had documented the room accurately, objectively, in the way she had been trained to document structural features: dimensions, features, materials, contents. The documentation was accurate. The documentation had recorded the contents of room five. Anyone who read her survey notes would know about room five. Anyone who acted on her survey notes using the standard facilities management process would file a report with the Academy Facilities office, which was the same office that had approved the annex construction in three weeks, which was the same administrative chain that — she did not know this for certain, only probabilistically — had approved the room count discrepancy.

She had documented. Her documentation was potentially evidence of something. She did not know yet for whom.

She looked at the floor plan.

There was a particular floor tile in the corridor outside room five — she had noticed it during the survey because it had a slightly different settlement pattern than the surrounding tiles, a 4mm depression, not enough to be a trip hazard but enough to be measurable and she had measured it. The tile was distinctive: slightly darker grout, a small V-shaped chip in the upper-left corner.

She took a fresh sheet from her notebook, wrote *Corridor — S.E. junction — tile deviation 4mm* and the tile's exact position in coordinates relative to the room five door. She wrote it as a structural maintenance note in the same format as the rest of her survey. She folded it into the main survey document, in the same section, non-highlighted.

If she filed the full survey, that note was in it. If someone else read the survey, they would know where to look.

If someone who understood why it mattered read the survey, they would find the tile. And if they looked at the tile and at its position relative to room five, they would understand that Vex had stood there and seen everything and had left a marker to say so.

She closed the survey document. She put it in her bag. She did not yet decide whether to file it.

She walked back across the courtyard past the place where Perren had been.

She did not know what had happened to him. She did not know if knowing would make her decision easier or harder. She suspected the answer was harder, and she suspected she would find out anyway, and she filed that too under the heading in her mind that said *things that will need to be documented eventually.*

---

She ate lunch alone at the long table near the eastern windows of the refectory.

She pulled out her structural notebook and wrote a clean version of the annex survey. Load path analysis, mortar quality findings, room count discrepancy. No mention of the calendar board. No mention of Perren. No mention of Category 3 recommendations. Clean structural data, clear and accurate and technically complete and missing half of what she had actually found.

She put the clean version in the formal report envelope.

She put the full version in the locked compartment of her survey bag, which she had acquired at age fourteen because she had been working in districts where what you documented and what you showed were two different questions, and she had understood early that there was sometimes a gap between them that you had to manage with hardware rather than hope.

She tapped the table once with two fingers, absently. The sound came back solid. Good table. Dense material. Full contact.

She ate her lunch.

The full survey stayed in the bag.
