# Scene: The Beast in the Hollow

Aisen went into the north woods for meat and came back with scars and purpose.

Morning fog sat low over the roots, and the pines held that metallic smell that came before weather shifts. He carried a long practice spear, a coil of rope, and enough dried bread to stay out until dusk. Hunting was work, not sport. If he missed, someone at home went hungry.

At first the forest read clean.

Rabbit sign near old stone.
Boar track at the creek bend.
No fresh wolf scat.

Then the pattern broke.

Birdsong cut out all at once.
Moss was torn in long strips where no hoof should have landed.
A patch of bark looked scorched from the inside.

Aisen crouched and touched the ground. The soil still held warmth though sun had barely risen.

He should have turned back then.
He knew it.
He kept moving.

In a shallow hollow north of the creek, he found the kill site: half a deer, dragged and abandoned. Too much meat left behind, which meant the predator had been interrupted or unwell.

He had just risen when he heard breath behind him.

Not wolf breath. Larger. Wet.

He turned into teeth.

The beast hit him shoulder-first and drove him into a root wall. It looked wolf-shaped at a distance but wrong up close, as if two anatomies had been forced together without agreement. Pale eyes, patchy hide, rib cage moving in uneven pulses.

Aisen thrust with the spear. The point glanced off jawbone and sank shallow behind the foreleg. The beast barely noticed. It snapped the shaft near the middle like kindling and came again.

He ran.

Branches whipped his face. Blood warmed his side where claws had opened cloth and skin. He vaulted a fallen trunk, slipped in mud, and felt the beast's weight hit where his spine had been half a heartbeat before.

No clean line. No heroic stance. Just raw math.

Distance.
Terrain.
Anything that could become leverage.

He spotted a low branch over a narrow gully and threw his rope with the chain link weight tied to the end. First cast missed. Second cast caught. He wrapped once around his forearm, planted both feet, and swung hard across the gap.

The branch cracked but held long enough.

The beast launched after him, hit the far lip badly, slid, and climbed in a frenzy that made the soil seem to boil.

Aisen stumbled into a camp perimeter he had not known was there.

"Down!" someone shouted.

Two travelers with short spears moved in from left and right. A woman in patched armor took point with a hooked polearm. Behind them, an older cleric began a chant that smelled of bitter herbs and warm metal.

The beast charged.

The woman set her hook, turned the head, and shouted for anchor. One traveler took the hit and rolled. The other drove a spear butt into the creature's knee joint. The cleric's light flared at exactly the wrong time and still worked, a pulse that staggered the beast long enough for the hook to catch throat.

It died ugly and loud.

Aisen sat down without meaning to and discovered he was shaking so hard his teeth clicked.

The cleric knelt, pressing cloth to his side.

"Hold this," he said.

"I am," Aisen answered.

"Harder."

The cloth came away red.

They got him under a tarp awning while rain started in fine needles. The woman introduced herself as Captain Liora, though she said "captain" like a joke that had survived several bad campaigns.

"You hunt alone often?" she asked while the cleric stitched him.

"When we need food," Aisen said through his teeth.

"Need is not a plan," she said.

The cleric snorted. "It is where plans begin."

His name was Brother Kel. His healing was real but costly; by the third stitch sweat stood out on his brow and his hands trembled.

"Magic does not erase damage," Kel told Aisen. "It negotiates with it."

Aisen watched every motion.

The way Kel anchored his breath before each pulse.
The way he touched wrist, not wound, to read circulation.
The way he stopped before overreach.

"Can you teach me that?" Aisen asked, voice thin.

Kel tied off the thread. "Not today. Today I teach you not to faint."

He did not faint.
He did vomit in the bushes and then apologize to a shrub.

The camp let him stay two nights while fever passed.

On the second evening Kel showed him a coin trick that was not a trick: balancing a copper on edge, then nudging it with tiny directed force without touching it. A telekinetic micro-push so small it looked like breath.

"Most people want dramatic effects," Kel said. "Dramatic effects kill amateurs. Learn control at the grain scale."

Aisen tried and failed eighteen times.
On the nineteenth the coin shivered half a finger width.

It was nothing.
It changed everything.

When he left camp, Liora returned the broken half of his spear and handed him a short staff cut from ash wood.

"Until you earn something better," she said.

Kel gave him a wax-wrapped packet of salve and a stern look.

"If you see silence in the woods like that again, walk away. Curiosity is expensive."

Aisen nodded.

He walked home slower than he had left, side bandaged, thoughts sharp.

At the tavern, his mother cursed him in three dialects before hugging him hard enough to reopen one stitch. His father said nothing for a long time, then set a bowl in front of him and added extra meat without comment.

That night Aisen cleaned the broken spear shaft and kept it.

Not as trophy.
As accounting.

The world had shown him a debt he could not pay with courage alone. Skill, preparation, allies, and disciplined force were the only currency that counted when things went wrong.

He swore, quietly and without witnesses, that next time chance would get less of a vote.
