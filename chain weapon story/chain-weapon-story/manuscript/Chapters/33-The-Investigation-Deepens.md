# CHAPTER 33: THE INVESTIGATION DEEPENS

The intelligence coordination desk faced a window that looked onto the supply depot yard, and from it Aisen could see the task force personnel without appearing to watch them.

They'd arrived nine days ago — three Covenant Intelligence specialists in the grey coats with the double-loop collar emblem that marked them as investigators, not administrators. They'd taken the east briefing room and removed the standing maps and replaced them with their own boards. They moved through the building with the contained, purposeful quality of people who were not on a time limit but who understood that information had a half-life. He'd tracked their movements in his peripheral attention while appearing to work on supply chain variance reports.

The supply container. One of his. Or rather: not his — the analysis documents distributed through it had been created by him, but the container itself had been a provincial resistance acquisition. He hadn't anticipated an interception. No one had.

On the morning of the tenth day, Colonel Serath closed the command post door and said his name.

"I wanted to brief you personally." She sat, not at the command table, but in the chair across from his desk — which meant this was not a briefing, exactly. Her hands were folded in her lap. She did not have the incident folder. "Internal security is investigating the evidence materials network. They're working on the theory that Academy personnel were involved in creating and distributing the analysis documents."

The lamp on the desk gave everything a close, amber quality. Outside, two task force personnel crossed the yard.

"Do they have specific targets?" Aisen asked carefully.

"Not yet," Serath said. "But they're conducting systematic review of Academy personnel with Archive access. Cynthia is on their list. So are several other scholars."

"What are you telling me?" Aisen asked.

"I'm telling you that if this investigation connects to Academy network operations, it will cascade through the entire institutional structure," Serath said. "And I'm telling you that you should understand the implications for your own position."

"My position as a military officer?"

"Your position as someone who understands both military operations and resistance network structures," Serath said directly. She was being blunt now—no more careful operational obscurity between them.

"I understand," Aisen said. "Are you asking me something?"

"I'm asking whether you think the investigation will reach Academy network operations or whether the evidence materials can be traced only to provincial resistance sources," Serath said.

It was a critical question. He heard it land the way critical questions did — not louder than other questions, but with a different quality. The room's amber light held still.

"The documents reference specific Archive material organizational patterns," Aisen said. "If investigators conduct detailed analysis, they might identify the Archive as the source. But if provincial resistance claims credit for the analysis and presents it as independent strategic work conducted by provincial networks, the Covenant might accept that explanation."

"But do you think they will?" Serath pressed.

"I think there's significant probability they won't," Aisen admitted. "The analysis is too sophisticated to have been created purely at provincial level. It references historical patterns that require archive-level historical knowledge. Investigation will probably conclude that institutional personnel were involved."

Serath was quiet. Not thinking — he recognized when she was thinking. This was receiving, which was different.

"I'm going to leverage my authority to recommend that the investigation focus on identifying what actually compromised the supply system rather than attempting to trace the analysis documents to Academy sources," she said. "I'll argue that pursuing institutional allegations could damage military effectiveness during a critical operational phase. I might not succeed in redirecting the investigation, but I'll try."

"Why?" Aisen asked.

"Because I want you to continue providing intelligence analysis," Serath said. "As long as you're not actively compromised and detained by Covenant security, your position in military command structure is valuable to me. And because your network has successfully created evidence that makes provincial networks more strategically capable, and that's good for someone who thinks military suppression should have boundaries."

---

Serath's attempt to redirect the investigation had limited effect. The Covenant Intelligence task force continued their work in the east briefing room: interviews, Archive access log reviews, research project output audits. Each evening one of the grey-coat specialists carried a sealed case to the communications room and returned without it — routing reports east, to the capital.

By the third week they had narrowed their focus to approximately seven Academy personnel. Aisen learned this through the task force's own requisition trail — they had requested personnel files on seven specific names, and his position gave him sight of that requisition before it was processed.

He read the seven names twice. Committed them.

Cynthia was one of them.

Word reached him through the covert channels that same night — a dead-drop response, clipped and precise: she was moving resources out of her Academy position. Preparing for potential extraction to provincial territory if necessary. Consolidating critical information, the kind of archive knowledge that couldn't be rebuilt if she disappeared.

The message included specific instructions: if she was detained, Aisen was to assume leadership of intelligence operations. Transition to protective mode. Preserve archive knowledge from institutional access. The contingency they'd planned for.

---

Cynthia was taken on the fourth day after that.

Early morning — the shift before the Academy's day officially began, when corridors were quiet and the ambient surveillance thinner. He learned it from the secondary dead-drop, a folded courier slip with the single-word indicator that meant she was in custody: *transferred*. He read it in the east corridor stairwell and remained standing on the same step for forty-three seconds before he folded the slip and destroyed it.

She was in a military detention facility now, under Covenant Intelligence supervision. *Interview regarding procurement of restricted materials*, the official Academy communication had said. But everyone involved — Caris, Vex, every node in the network still operational — understood that *interview* meant interrogation under conditions designed to extract operational network information before she could assess what to reveal and what to protect.

Caris activated the protective protocols within hours. Active intelligence gathering suspended. Network personnel instructed to minimize visibility. The entire structure that had taken two years to build folded itself into a posture of cautious dormancy, like something alive that had learned to look dead.

And then the emergency message reached Aisen through the backup channel: *Extract if possible. Window before intensive questioning: narrow.*

He looked at the message for a long time.

Cynthia was in a military detention facility with Covenant Intelligence supervision. He was a junior military officer with intelligence access and exactly no authority over detention operations. The request was, by any functional measure, impossible.

But the cascade if she broke — Caris, Vex, the archive access records, two years of careful document trails — that would be worse than impossible.

Then Colonel Serath appeared at his desk with an unexpected offer.

---

"I need you to conduct intelligence analysis on the detained scholar," Serath said. "We want to understand what she actually knows about military operations and whether she represents a genuine security risk to our occupation operations."

It wasn't phrased as an order. The way she looked at him made it an order anyway.

"What analysis do you want?" Aisen asked.

"Interview her," Serath said. "Understand what's actually in her head versus what intelligence specialists are imagining. Then report back to me whether she represents a genuine military intelligence threat or whether she's simply someone who became involved with provincial resistance structures without deep understanding of military implications."

He held the offer for exactly the amount of time it required. Serath was watching him the way she sometimes did — not waiting for an answer, but noting the shape of how he received it. He understood what was actually on the table. Access to a detained network contact before intensive questioning. An hour, possibly. Enough to know what she'd already given them. Enough for her to know what to protect.

"When can I conduct the interview?" Aisen asked.

"Tomorrow morning," Serath said. "Before Intelligence specialists conduct more intensive questioning."

She left his desk without waiting for further response.

He sat with the intelligence coordination report in front of him and wrote nothing for four minutes.
