A caravan arrives in winter, wrapping the tavern in a new rhythm of trade and rumor. Among the merchants is Amara—young, watchful, and clearly organized. She notices patterns in people like a clock reads hours: who pauses, who lingers, who avoids eye contact. Aisen watches her watch the room and learns a different kind of observation—information as armor.

A side scene shows a trader's ledger being shorted; a deliberate miscount that hints at a local siphon. Amara draws Aisen aside and explains, in spare words, how networks form: small favors exchanged, debts hidden in kindness, channels of information that move like water under river ice. She offers an aside to Aisen about choices: protecting a network requires hard arithmetic—who to save, who to expose, when to run.

Amara's presence seeds the larger heresy plot: information itself is dangerous. Later, when Aisen tries to replicate her mental map, he fails. He realizes observation isn't only seeing; it is also sorting, trusting, and knowing when to withhold. Amara departs with a wooden giraffe carving for Aisen—an odd talisman that marks the moment their threads cross.

The chapter establishes Amara's practical ethics and the idea that for some people power is the ability to collect truth and make impossible choices from it.
