Seraphine sat in a chamber of ice and mirrors, each reflection another way to measure loneliness. Her reign had been long; her power grafted from centuries of choices and purchases. A life of possession had built a throne of methodical cruelty. 

When the alarms rose, she felt more irritation than fear. She had a plan for intruders, as she had a plan for hunger and for the way Caspian answered to her. Yet the presence of Aisen's peculiar tactics—his network made of small choices—was a novelty. 

Her thoughts drifted to Caspian, to the boy who'd been kind in a snowbank so long ago. The memory loosened a seam. In a critical moment, she hesitated—not because she lost aggression but because the sight of someone who once acted with kindness touched a buried tendon. That hesitation would tilt events. 

Her isolation, centuries in the making, became both her armor and her flaw.
