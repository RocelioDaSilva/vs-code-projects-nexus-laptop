Years later the world looks different but familiar. Trade routes rerouted, policies reformed, and small institutions hardened against central abuse. A historian notes how grassroots networks preserved knowledge and saved lives; their records mention Aisen not as myth but as an architect of ordinary methods. 

Communities rebuilt, and the Covenant's control fractured into many small authorities and local checks. The historian's tone is careful: systems that democratize power are messy but durable. 

The chapter closes on a classroom where a teacher reads a fragment of Aisen's notebook to children—practical lessons on observation and care. Eyes in the room listen like seeds to water.
