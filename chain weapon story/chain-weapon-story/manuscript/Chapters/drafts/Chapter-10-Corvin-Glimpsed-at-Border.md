Aisen goes with Harald to the border to perform a small escort and sees Corvin for the first time: a man larger than life and oddly cut into pieces of myth. Corvin moves with an animal rhythm—jagged and efficient—and his presence suggests a cost in flesh and memory. The villagers whisper about the price of his survival: a body stitched to beasts, a soul nudged toward monstrous focus. 

The fight, brief and ugly, occurs when a gang tries to press the escort; Corvin intervenes not to save the caravan but to redirect a violent moment into a controlled end. He tears and remakes space with brutal economy. The scene gives readers a visceral answer to "what is a rule-breaker?"—someone who accepted structural sacrifice to gain power, and who paid a personal toll that leaves a hollow in his eyes.

Aisen's observation skills register the little tell: Corvin's hands make a repetitive motion, as if remembering a rhythm of many lives. Harald explains in quiet that Corvin is proof that survival sometimes hinges on the wrong bargain. The scene ends with Corvin disappearing back into the borderland, leaving a sense that power extracted from self is never free. Aisen is left with two impressions: the effectiveness of monstrous strength, and the human cost etched in the margin of victory.

This chapter builds moral texture: not all wins are wins, and the forms of power available in this world demand payment.
