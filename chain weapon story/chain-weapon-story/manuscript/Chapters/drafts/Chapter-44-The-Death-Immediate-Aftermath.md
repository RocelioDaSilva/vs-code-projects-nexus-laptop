Aisen fell not as a heroic banner but as a man who chose a specific weight. The fatal moment was granular: the release of an anchor that saved hundreds but left him exposed to a final blow. Amara watched him go down and carried him from the field with hands that trembled but did not falter. 

The immediate aftermath was intimate: bandaging where a wound could be tended, the small litany of names whispered into the night. The wider battle continued around them, but the personal grief was ruthless and precise. Amara felt the fullness of a life removed from the world—no miraculous return, no posthumous crowning, just the absence of a person who had made a choice. 

The network moved quickly: statements drafted for the public narrative, a plan to shield the most vulnerable, and a promise that Aisen's lessons would be institutionalized without mythologizing. They would not turn him into legend; they would keep his methods alive. 

Amara's scene is both mourning and mobilization—grief folded into work.
