The training arena smells of sweat and oil; targets line the walls like neutral witnesses. Aisen strapped the chain-spear and ran through the routine Harald drilled into him: positions, breath, smallest motions first. Tarovin watched with the quiet attention of a scholar testing a hypothesis. 

When he released a practiced motion the chain responded not as a blunt instrument but as an architect of space—a tether here, a restraint there. Instructors murmured. Some saw only improvisation; others saw a new grammar. 

An examiner asked for explanation. Aisen tried to speak the mechanics—torque, anchor points, micro-impulses timed to breath. They recorded his words like a curious species. Approval came in small increments: a raised brow from a senior master, a notecard put into a file. Suspicion came wrapped in questions from a chairly dean. 

That night he wrote the sequence down with meticulous care. If the academy would not teach this exact approach, he would refine it himself. The chain had become a problem-solving tool rather than merely a weapon.
