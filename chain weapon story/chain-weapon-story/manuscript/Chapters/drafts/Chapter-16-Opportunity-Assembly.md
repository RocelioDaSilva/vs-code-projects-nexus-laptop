The assembly hall smelled of polished wood and pretense. The dean extolled a new "opportunity" program for elite students—advanced studies, fast tracks, and sponsored posts after graduation. Faces lit up or fell the way lanterns might in wind. 

Aisen listened to the names called and the subtle ways invitations were framed. He wasn't among them. No one expected him to be. The exclusion felt like a small relief; invisibility protected him from recruitment's soft coercion. 

He took note of the teachers who praised the program, then of the ones who watched the students with a different expression—calculating, not proud. Tarovin's lesson echoed: precision matters more than spectacle. 

Afterward, in the courtyard, Amara slipped him a small paper with a shorthand note: "They are choosing for reasons that are not merit." He folded it into his pocket. The assembly had shown him how decisions were made—not for ability but for use.
