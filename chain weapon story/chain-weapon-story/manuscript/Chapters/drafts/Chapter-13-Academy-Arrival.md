The academy gate was larger than any doorway Aisen had seen; its ironwork suggested scholarship and surveillance in equal measure. He arrived with a single pack, Harald at his side for escort, and Tarovin's reluctant blessing folded into his pocket like a spare coin. 

The first day was a study in observation: uniforms, ranks, subtle nods of recognition between nobles, the polite but invisible walls that kept commoners in their lanes. Aisen noticed everything—the way a noble's sleeve frayed at the seam, the way a scholar adjusted his posture when a dean passed. 

He met his roommate, a loud boy from the south who loved to tell stories, and a quiet girl who ate slowly and watched the room like a tally. The dorm smelled of chalk and oil and nervous ambition. 

An early assembly introduced an opportunity program for elite students; Aisen listened and felt the tug of exclusion. He didn't belong to that circle, and already that absence became an asset: no one expected him to be dangerous. He filed the information carefully.

By night he sat in the courtyard and wrote observations in a small notebook—names, gait, who avoided whom. If power here was about pedigree, he reasoned, his power might be about reading the room. That thought felt like a plan.
