Caspian stood at the edge of his old life and the possibility of release. Seraphine's grip had long been his world—safety traded for appetite. In one flash of memory he saw the warm biscuit and the cloak he had wrapped around Aisen in snow. 

Freedom required a violent unraveling: to leave Seraphine was to be cast out, hunted, or destroyed. Yet the memory of being truly seen by Aisen carried weight heavier than fear. He chose the moment of fracture. 

In action, Caspian did not fight like a monster; he moved like someone reclaiming a language. He opened a path for Aisen's team, choosing to end his own servitude even if it cost him his life. 

His last words were not a curse but a quiet benediction, an acknowledgement that someone had once asked if the hunger hurt—and had meant it. His death reawakened compassion as a weapon.
