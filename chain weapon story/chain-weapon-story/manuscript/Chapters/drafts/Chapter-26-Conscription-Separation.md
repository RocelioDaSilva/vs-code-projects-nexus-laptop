Conscription felt like a formal erasure. Aisen was given new clothing, a number, and an order to report to the north border. Harald clasped his shoulder not as mentor but as someone who understood the bureaucracy of survival. 

The camp smelled of metal and damp canvas. Men queued for orders, families whispered farewells, and officers read lists like liturgy. Aisen realized how the Covenant's reach turned citizens into units—calibrated members of a war economy. 

Separated from the academy, his network reduced to a few messages and promises, he learned the soldier's rhythm: standing watch, learning the feel of frost, and recognizing the small alliances that kept men sane. The war wasn't noble; it was logistics and fear. He swore to learn the systems so he could find the seams.
