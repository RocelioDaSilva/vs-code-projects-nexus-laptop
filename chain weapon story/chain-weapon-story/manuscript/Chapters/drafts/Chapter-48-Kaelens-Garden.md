Kaelen returned to the grove and planted new seeds as a communal act. The garden became a place of repair—plants used to feed refugees, petals used in ritual care, roots traded for favors. He tended with the patient rigor of someone who knew how small cycles made big changes. 

The garden became a training ground for a different kind of craft: how to grow a community's resilience. Kaelen taught children how to tend living systems and how to read soil the way Aisen taught his students to read opponents. 

It was a gentle legacy, a green architecture that honored the fallen by restoring life.
