Tarovin returned in spring with a letter folded into a careful square and a patient smile like someone who kept promises to future selves. He'd been away at an abbey and then the academy, reading, arguing, collecting small notations on glyphs and motion. The letter—meant for Aisen—was a recommendation, not for skill but for curiosity: "This boy notices. That matters more than any born advantage." 

The chapter opens with the awkward arithmetic of choices. Harald's lessons are muscle and river-thinking; Tarovin brings the idea that magic is syntax and physics. Aisen stands between the two teachers feeling the tug of a plumb line: one side promises practical competence, the other precise understanding that could alter what competence meant.

Tarovin's first lesson after returning is small and dry: a demonstration of torque and leverage using a shaving of wood and a length of string. He explains that telekinesis at scale is about micro-corrections—finding the minimal impulse that nudges a system toward a new equilibrium. The same lesson in Harald's terms is a way to unbalance an opponent with a single minimal motion. 

A social beat follows: Tarovin invites Aisen to a test—read a simple movement, then repeat it with fewer wasted gestures. Aisen fails twice, then finds the rhythm. Tarovin recognizes the boy's method: he learns by noticing patterns rather than mimicking force. The academy's application process is mentioned—Tarovin offers to endorse Aisen when he's ready.

A small scene cements the decision. At night, Aisen practices by himself, applying Harald's footwork to Tarovin's idea of minimal impulse. He discovers satisfaction in the marriage: a move that does not break things but rearranges them with quiet accuracy. The chapter ends with Aisen accepting a tentative plan: continue Harald's apprenticeship and enroll in Tarovin's occasional classes. It's the first time he consciously begins to synthesize two languages into one personal grammar.

This chapter frames the book's recurring tension: power as force versus power as craft. Aisen's choice to learn both sets the stage for later synthesis moments where his ordinariness becomes tactical advantage rather than limitation.
