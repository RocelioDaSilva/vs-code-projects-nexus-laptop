Tholen's copies were crude: ink blotches, marginalia in different hands, a map of how names were changed and treaties reinterpreted. The pre-Covenant records described a different approach to crisis—cooperative measures rather than consolidation. 

Aisen read a line that reframed everything: "Consolidation as a response was a choice, not a necessity." The phrasing made the Covenant's strategy look like political calculus, not destiny. 

Tholen warned of the danger in releasing such texts; he had lost colleagues for smaller infractions. But he also recognized that truth could be wielded carefully: leaked into merchant manifests, cited in court papers, planted in a sympathetic professor's lecture. 

They planned a slow dissemination, one that would not trigger immediate suppression but would seed doubt. The archive had become a weapon: not against soldiers but against the narrative itself.
