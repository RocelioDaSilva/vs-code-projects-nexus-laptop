Kaelen stepped into the academy with soil still under his nails and a head full of the grove's patient rhythms. The formal halls felt like a different ecology—tile instead of loam, rules instead of seasons—but he listened for life where it persisted. 

Reuniting with Aisen was both relief and adjustment. They traded small confidences: Kaelen's grandmother had died quietly; Aisen described the odd rituals of institutional learning. Kaelen attended classes where instructors clipped the living language of his practice into diagrams; he felt the friction of two logics meeting. 

In the garden, Kaelen quietly tended a corner of plants that the academy had relegated to a gritty patch. He shielded roots from inquisitive hands and found allies in unexpected places: a kitchen servant who loved herbs, a librarian who smuggled seed lists. He learned that the academy's insistence on uniformity made gentle magic a scandal rather than a system; he resolved to keep the grove's grammar alive.

By the end of his first week he had planted a small token of his grove beneath a courtyard bench—a quiet sign that tending could survive among tomes.
