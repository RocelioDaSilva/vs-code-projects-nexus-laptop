Amara presented her findings in a cellar, ledger pages lit by a single lamp. She spoke in increments: who paid whom, how shipments rerouted, who profited when crops failed. The pattern was deliberate—conflict manufactured to concentrate resources and justify harsher rule. 

Her network had traced payments to front companies and to donors who had influence in both markets and the academy. The border tensions were not accidents; they were cultivated for benefit. Aisen listened, the contours of his suspicions forming into architecture. 

They planned small disruptions: false manifests, diverted shipments, and targeted leaks to sympathetic journalists. The plan was not heroic on a grand scale—it was a set of inconveniences that would, over time, erode the Covenant's smooth narrative. It was network work: patient, procedural, and precise.
