Corvin's armor was rumor made manifest: iron plates braided into flesh, a gait that read like a ledger of battles. Stationed at a command post, he was an instrument the Covenant used when bluntness was required. 

From Corvin's perspective, orders were the only stable thing left. He reflected on choices—the bargain that had fused beast and man—and felt the residue of what he'd traded: small memories missing like torn pages. 

When he watched men suffer at the camp, a hollow formed inside him that no power could fill. Aisen's network fascinated and annoyed him; the boy's commonness was contemptible and, paradoxically, dangerous. In one small exchange, Corvin hesitated to strike a fleeing family; that hesitation hinted at a fracture within the machine he'd become.
