A child finds a carved giraffe in a market stall. The vendor tells a small tale of a person who taught people to see the world and to act with ordinary care. The story is simple and practical: observe, weigh, act. 

The coda ties the novel to the ordinary: the work of living, teaching, and maintaining networks continues. The historian's margin note insists that legacies are not monuments but practices, repeated until they shape institutions. 

The last line circles back: "The world remembers those who taught it how to keep itself." The book ends with small, ongoing life—a domestic scene, a repaired tool, a child learning to notice.
