# CHAPTER 22B: THE PUBLIC HEARING

The hearing room had been the Academy's east library before the administration decided to cooperate. Now it was configured for a formal proceeding: the prosecution table on the raised platform where the reference stacks had been, the defense table below on the reading floor, the observer gallery behind a rope barrier along the eastern wall. The rope was a recent addition. Someone had measured it with care. The spatial politics were legible to anyone who had worked long enough in institutional structures: elevation communicated authority, and the borrowed authority of the Academy's own architecture was being used against it.

Aisen arrived fourteen minutes before the formal start and spent those minutes in the observer corridor.

He was not a party to the proceeding. He had been summoned as an informational witness — "technical clarification requested regarding standard Academy research documentation protocols," which was the language in the summons, and which was specific enough to mean he would be asked about something he knew precisely, and vague enough to mean he would not know what that something was until he was already standing in it.

He used the fourteen minutes to observe who else was already seated.

Defense table: Professor Celdwyn, who ran the experimental methodology section with the particular patience of someone who had argued with institutional frameworks for long enough to know how they broke. Beside him, a junior faculty advocate named Dara who had formal proceeding training and was young enough that her primary visible affect was careful. Two student researchers who were listed as supporting witnesses — both of them would testify about their own work, which was a procedural technique the defense had chosen to humanize the research program. Aisen noted that one of the student researchers — a second-year named Cashen — had not been seated when Aisen arrived but appeared from the side door three minutes before start, slightly too quickly, in the way of someone whose previous activity had been either anxious or physical.

Prosecution table: two Registry officials who had been in the Province for six days. They had arrived with their own equipment, their own documentation, and a deposition from a Covenant research review board that Aisen had not seen. Beside them, a Covenant legal specialist named Aldric Fenn — Aldric was the kind of person Aisen assessed on contact as someone who understood exactly what he was doing and had thought carefully about the costs he was willing to pay for it. He was competent in a way that was distinct from merely intelligent. His notes were organized in a physical ledger rather than a standard document case, and he did not refer to them during the first twenty minutes of the proceeding. He already knew what was in them.

Observer gallery: faculty, students, two provincial administrators who had no obvious institutional reason to be present, and Elara Valorin seated at the far right end of the second row.

Elara's presence registered as significant before Aisen had articulated why. She was technically a visitor at the Academy under a Northern House liaison designation that had been filed three weeks prior — the kind of administrative filing that created a formal record without requiring any specific explanation. She was in the observer gallery behind a rope barrier she could not have crossed without becoming part of the proceeding. She was watching the prosecution table with the focused patience of someone who had assessed it already and was now gathering secondary data.

He noted this and moved to his assigned position.

---

The first ninety minutes were technical.

Celdwyn led the defense presentation with a summary of the synthesis-magic research methodology: its origin in standard Academy research practice, its documentation according to Registry protocols, its peer review within the experimental methodology section, and its compliance with the institutional review framework that the Academy and the Covenant had both agreed to in the current operating charter. He was thorough. He was precise. He was not, Aisen thought, speaking to anyone in the room.

He was speaking for the record.

Aisen recognized this technique. When you are faced with a proceeding whose outcome is not determined by procedural compliance but which must appear to be — you comply procedurally and you document your compliance. The record is not the victory; the record is the thing you take with you after the loss, because the loss is coming and you have known that since before you arrived.

The Registry officials asked clarifying questions about three specific research notations. The questions were competent and did not misrepresent the technical content. Aldric Fenn asked no questions during the first ninety minutes. He sat with his ledger open to the same page for the entire period, and Aisen watched his eyes twice: both times they were on the room, not the text.

He was also watching for something.

The break came at the ninety-minute point. Aisen was positioned in the witness annex — a former reading alcove, two small chairs and a water jug — when Dara appeared in the doorway.

"Can I ask you to review something?" she said.

"Yes."

She handed him a single page: the outline of the defense's technical argument regarding the research documentation timeline. It was the working draft she had been using to prepare his testimony questions. He had seen the first version of it eleven days ago; this was a revised version.

He read it. It took him three minutes because he was reading carefully. There were four substantive changes from the first version he had seen.

Two of those changes were consistent with the kind of revision that happened when a junior advocate worked through the material and found better precision. One change was an addition she had told him about verbally two days ago — a structural clarification he had suggested.

The fourth change was a specific example case. He did not recognize it.

"The Morveth case," he said. "That wasn't in the first version."

"I added it yesterday," Dara said. "It's stronger precedent than the Leval notation I was using."

"Where did you find it?"

"The Archive cross-reference index. Cynthia helped me trace the citation."

He held the document for a moment longer.

"Is there anything unclear?" Dara asked.

"No," he said. "It's good work."

She left. He sat with the page for the remaining eight minutes of the break.

The Morveth case was good precedent. Better than the Leval notation — he agreed with her assessment. It was also specific, and it was new, and it was a change made after the defense preparation file had been completed and distributed to three people.

He knew which three people had seen the full preparation file.

He also did not know, with certainty, which of those three had seen it last.

---

When the hearing resumed, Aldric Fenn opened his ledger.

"I would like to enter preliminary evidence regarding research documentation and institutional affiliation," Fenn said to the Registry officials. The officials nodded in the way of people who had received this signal already and were providing it a formal recorded acknowledgment.

"Proceed," the senior official said.

What followed was eleven minutes of systematic documentation entry. Each document was described, logged, and placed in the proceeding record. Most of them were routine — cross-referenced research filings, standard methodology reports, the kind of paper that accumulated around any institutional project over three years.

Then Fenn entered document sixteen.

"This is a summary of the research defense preparation strategy filed with the Academy's internal review panel on the fourteenth," Fenn said. "I would like to note for the record that the filing is dated three days ago."

The room went still in the way that stillness replaced a sound no one present had made but everyone present had felt the absence of.

Third-year student Cashen, sitting at the defense table, made a very small movement — not visible from the observer gallery, but legible from where Aisen was standing. His hand moved half an inch to the right and stopped.

Aisen was already calculating.

The preparation strategy had been completed and distributed seven days ago. It had been seen, in full, by three people: Celdwyn, Dara, and Cashen. Dara's revised draft — the version she had handed him during the break, the one containing the Morveth case — had been prepared two days ago. The version Fenn had just entered was dated three days ago, which meant it predated Dara's revision.

Three days ago.

Three people had seen the preparation file seven days ago. The version they had seen had not yet contained the Morveth case. But the version Fenn was holding would contain the earlier Leval notation.

Which meant Fenn's copy was not the current version. It was an earlier version, shared before Dara made her final revisions.

Of the three people who had seen the full preparation file, all three had received it at the same time. But only one of them would have shared a three-day-old copy rather than waiting until the final revision was distributed.

He didn't know which one.

He knew the calculation. He did not know the result.

---

"Objection." Celdwyn's voice was level. "The document referenced is internal defense preparation material. Under institutional proceeding protocol, internal preparation materials are not subject to pre-evidence submission."

"The objection is noted," the senior official said. "The document has been submitted as evidentiary context, not as primary evidence. The defense preparation strategy is not under examination. Only the research methodology it describes is under examination. The document establishes that the defense itself characterized the methodology in a specific manner, which is relevant to the proceeding."

This was technically accurate. Aisen had checked the proceeding protocol before arriving. Fenn had found the one procedural gap that allowed him to enter the document.

Celdwyn argued the point for eight minutes. He was precise and he was right. He also was not going to win this one, and by the fifth minute his voice carried the specific quality of argument that had already accepted its outcome and was completing itself for the record.

The document was admitted.

Fenn spent fourteen minutes with it. He walked through the defense's own characterization of the research methodology, asked three questions that appeared to be technical clarifications and were actually structural concessions — each question accepted an answer that reframed the methodology's institutional compliance in slightly smaller terms, and each reframing was technically accurate so the defense could not object without appearing to contradict their own preparation document.

Dara saw it on the third question. Her hand was flat on the table. In Aisen's assessment she had approximately sixteen seconds before the implications crystallized into something she could not maintain without it showing.

She maintained it for nineteen. He noted the three seconds as professional.

The fourth question came to Celdwyn, not Dara.

"Professor," Fenn said, "your own preparation notes characterize the synthesis-magic methodology as, quote, 'a non-traditional application of standard research frameworks.' Would you agree with that characterization?"

"I would agree it is one possible characterization," Celdwyn said. "The document was produced for internal discussion. The word 'non-traditional' in that context referred to the integration of cross-disciplinary—"

"Non-traditional," Fenn said again, placing the word precisely in the record a second time. "Thank you."

---

Aisen was called at the four-hour mark.

He walked to the brief witness position — a chair placed between the prosecution platform and the defense floor, slightly lower than either table — and gave his name and institutional designation for the record. The Registry official confirmed the summons terms. He agreed to provide accurate information.

Fenn led.

The first six questions were exactly what the summons had described: technical clarification of standard Academy documentation protocols. Aisen answered them with precision. He knew the documentation protocols thoroughly, had applied them correctly in his own work, and had no reason to characterize them as anything other than what they were. He said exactly what was true.

The seventh question turned.

"In your understanding of Academy documentation protocol," Fenn said, "which research activities would be required to file a separate institutional affiliation disclosure?"

"Activities that involved direct collaboration with non-Academy institutional partners," Aisen said.

"And if such collaboration occurred without an affiliation disclosure being filed?"

"That would be a documentation compliance gap."

"In your assessment — " Fenn's ledger was still open in his hands "— would the synthesis-magic research program have required an affiliation disclosure if any of its practitioners had been receiving methodological guidance from sources outside the Academy?"

The question was specific. It was also general enough that a direct answer committed nothing. Aisen recognized the construction: the question embedded a factual premise — *if any practitioners had been receiving guidance from outside sources* — and the only answer that did not concede the premise was to object to the conditional itself.

Which would look, to anyone taking a surface reading of the proceeding, like he was avoiding the hypothetical.

He sat with the question for two seconds.

"If the scenario you describe had occurred," he said, "yes, a disclosure would have been required. Whether it occurred is a separate question from whether the disclosure protocol is clear."

"Of course," Fenn said, in the tone of someone who had received exactly the answer he needed and was choosing not to appear pleased about it.

---

The testimony ended at the two-hour point.

Aisen returned to the witness annex.

The annex was empty. He sat in the smaller of the two chairs and thought about the room he had just left. He thought about Cashen's hand. He thought about the specific timing: three days ago, before the final revision, after the initial distribution.

Seven days ago, three people had the full file: Celdwyn, Dara, Cashen. Fenn's copy was three days old. Which of the three had Fenn's copy come from?

Celdwyn had been at the Academy for fourteen years. His institutional loyalty was not in question by anyone except the Covenant, who would question the institutional loyalty of any person with the temerity to run a research program they hadn't authorized. The profile was wrong for the leak.

Dara was new — she had been brought in for the proceeding specifically for her formal-proceeding training. But the leak could only have come from someone who had the three-day-old version rather than the current one. Dara was the one who had revised it two days ago. She would have shared the current version, not the old one.

That left Cashen.

He sat with that calculation for a long time.

He did not know Cashen. He knew his name from the research program listings, knew he had Archive access, knew his coursework was in secondary methodology. He was a second-year student. He would not have been a natural target for Covenant recruitment — unless there was something else. A leverage point that was not visible in the institutional record.

The Ghost was in the network. Rin had said as much in her systematic way — she would not have documented five candidates without being certain there was someone to document. Ghost was not Covenant by ideology. Ghost was afraid of something.

Cashen was twenty years old. What was a twenty-year-old student afraid of?

He did not know what Cashen's family situation was. He did not know if there was a provincial connection, a garrison assignment, a previous record, a debt. He did not know if the person who had given Fenn the three-day-old document was acting under coercion or calculation or both.

He also could not name Cashen in that room without evidence.

He did not have evidence. He had a timeline and a three-second movement of a hand and a document dated three days ago. That was not proof. That was the kind of reasoning that was correct seven times out of ten and that produced the same devastation in the three cases where it was wrong.

He wrote nothing down.

---

The hearing concluded in the late afternoon.

The outcome was procedurally neutral: no immediate finding, a thirty-day review period, the research program allowed to continue under restricted documentation conditions. This was not, technically, a loss. It was also exactly what Fenn had structured it to be — not a decisive action but a liminal state, during which the Covenant would apply the additional pressure that a decisive action would have made visible too early.

Aisen stood in the observer corridor afterward and watched the room clear.

Celdwyn and Dara were at the defense table, speaking quietly. Their body language was the combination of composed and exhausted that indicated they had performed their function correctly and knew the outcome had been predetermined.

Fenn was at the prosecution table, exchanging documentation with the Registry officials with the efficiency of someone who had achieved what he came to achieve and was now in the transition phase of moving to the next objective.

Cashen was not visible in the clearing room.

From the far end of the observer gallery, Elara was watching Fenn.

She caught Aisen's eye across the room. It was not a social acknowledgment — too brief, too specific. It was the exchange between two people who had been observing the same proceeding from different vantage points and were noting that the other person had been there.

She had watched the prosecution table for four hours. She would have a different read than his. Different access point, different visibility on the room. What she had seen might clarify or complicate his three-day calculation.

He filed the variable under *follow-up required* and moved toward the exit.

He did not go to her. She did not come to him. The corridor had Covenant officials in it, and whatever they had observed together, they had not observed it together in any way that could be documented.

Outside, the afternoon was getting cold. He stood at the edge of the Academy's east courtyard for a moment, letting the air clear the close-room quality of four hours in a proceeding he had not been able to steer.

He had not named Cashen.

He did not know if that had been correct.

He knew that if it had been wrong — if Cashen was not the source, or was the source under coercion with a situation that exposure would have made catastrophic — naming him would have destroyed a twenty-year-old student on the basis of a timeline calculation in a room where the outcome was already set.

He also knew that Ghost was still inside the network, and would be at the next proceeding, and the one after that, and would continue to be there until the calculus of exposure changed for whoever was keeping that secret. And that every proceeding Ghost participated in was a proceeding that the network walked into having already lost the room.

He had made a choice. He did not think it was right. He thought it was the only choice that did not guarantee a specific harm while accepting an ongoing general one.

The difference between those two things was not the same as the difference between good and bad.

He walked back to his quarters and filed the hearing in the section of his working memory labeled *costs*, in the subsection he had started thinking of as *the things I have not named*.

The section was getting fuller.
