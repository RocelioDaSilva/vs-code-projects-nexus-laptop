# CHAPTER 29B: THE BLOCKADE

The formal complaint was filed on a Thursday.

She knew this because the regulatory clerk who processed it noted the day and time at the top of the document when he stamped it received: *Day 127, 14:32, Commerce Registry, Provincial Corridor Division.* She saw this stamp because she was one of six people who received a notice copy — not the complaint itself, but a procedural notice of filing, which was standard practice when any complaint involving military supply logistics was formally entered.

She received the notice at seventeen minutes past fifteen, while standing at the documentation table at the morning formation checkpoint.

She read the cover notation: *Covenant Authorized Forwarding Consortium, Ltd. v. Seventeen Named Provincial Supply Operators: Documentation Compliance Failure, Routes 7-East, 11-South, 14-East-Connector, pending sixty-day route hold.*

She set the notice face-down on the documentation table and finished the current documentation batch — four personnel transit forms and a supply manifest — before she picked it up again.

---

She had run the numbers in March.

The analysis had been part of the deployment-hold submission: a sixty-day disruption to autumn supply routes in the Provincial Corridor would produce a gap of approximately forty percent in expected seasonal goods delivery. She had built the model from three years of Provincial Corridor supply data, seasonal stock documentation from eleven distribution nodes, and the population density figures from the administrative census, which were eleven years old and therefore conservative by approximately eight to twelve percent.

The forty percent figure was therefore an underestimate.

She had noted this in the submission. She had written, in the footnotes of Section 4, that the census data's age introduced a downward skew of approximately eight to twelve percent, and that the actual gap should be modeled at forty to fifty-two percent depending on which population correction factor was applied. She had cited three correction methods and said why she preferred the middle one.

The submission had been filed six copies in six different office chains.

The submission was in the formal review queue. The regulatory board had admitted it for priority review twenty-two days ago.

The formal complaint was dated today.

She picked up the notice and read the complaint parameters.

Seventeen named operators. She recognized nine of the names — she had used them in her analysis as the independent merchant operators whose capacity was critical to the autumn supply sequence. The other eight she had not specifically named but could identify from the route listings: they covered the connector routes between the seven main supply roads, the kind of carrier that didn't appear in high-level analysis because individually they were small, but whose aggregate contribution was roughly fourteen percent of the eastern distribution chain.

The documentation violation: non-compliance with Commerce Registry Directive 44-C, which had revised the compliance form requirements for registered supply operators. The directive had been issued sixty-three days ago. The transition window had been forty-five days.

She looked at the transition window number for a moment.

Forty-five days. The directive had required operators to file updated compliance forms within forty-five days. The smaller operators — the ones who used part-time documentation services or filed their own paperwork — would have needed the full forty-five days to process the change, assuming they had received the directive notification promptly.

She pulled the regulatory filing calendar from the documentation stack and found Directive 44-C.

The directive had been issued through the Commerce Registry's secondary bulletin rather than the primary commercial notice chain. The secondary bulletin went to certified commercial agents. Operators who filed independently — who were not registered with a certified agent — received notifications through the regional distribution offices. The regional distribution offices had a fifteen-day internal processing lag before they generated local notices.

Fifteen-day lag plus forty-five-day transition period equaled sixty days.

The complaint was filed sixty-three days after the directive.

She set down the filing calendar. She looked at the notice for a long moment.

The complaint was technically valid. She had checked this because she checked everything, and the answer was: yes, technically valid. The operators had not filed the updated compliance forms. They had not filed the updated compliance forms because they had, in practical terms, received approximately thirty days of actual transition time rather than forty-five. And thirty days was not enough time for a small operator to file updated compliance paperwork during the peak late-summer preparation period.

But the complaint was technically valid.

The sixty-day route hold was therefore enforceable.

---

Her second calculation was the supply officer.

Lieutenant Corvyn was the supply officer for Provincial Corridor Distribution at the Field Command level. He was competent, methodical, and had been promoted by institutional momentum to a position that required him to make decisions he was not temperamentally structured to make. He was not a bad officer. He was an officer who experienced the gap between "technically valid" and "practically disastrous" as a personal intrusion and preferred not to think about it.

She found him at the supply depot at the end of the morning rotation.

"The route hold," she said.

He was reviewing a materials ledger. He looked up with the expression of someone who had seen the notice too and was hoping the conversation would be brief.

"I'm aware of the filing," Corvyn said.

"The autumn supply window opened four days ago," she said.

"The routing disruption is within legal parameters," he said. "Commerce Registry has jurisdiction over commercial operator certification."

"This is the number I projected in March," she said. She had the original analysis page with her — she had updated Section 4 with the actual complaint filing date and the revised gap estimate. She set it on the ledger beside his hand. "The supply gap I modeled at forty to fifty-two percent. The complaint covers seventeen operators, nine of which are on the primary autumn routes. The gap, given the seasonal timing, is now approximately forty-six to fifty-five percent."

He looked at the page. He looked at the gap percentage. He looked back up at her.

"Lieutenant Amara," he said, "the hold is within legal parameters."

"Yes," she said.

There was a silence that had a specific quality to it: the quality of a person who knew they were on the wrong side of an argument but could not move because moving required action and action required authorization they did not have. She was familiar with this quality. She had been in it herself.

"What would you like me to do?" he said. "Commerce Registry does not answer to Field Command. The route hold is their authority."

"I know," she said. "I wanted to make sure you'd seen the March analysis numbers."

"I've seen them now."

"Yes," she said. She picked up the page and left.

---

The two she was thinking about most were Wellen and the boy with the oxen.

Wellen ran a flour mill supply route: she moved grain from three smallholder farms in the east corridor through two distribution nodes to six mills. She had been running that route for eleven years. Her husband was the certified agent for the district; he handled her paperwork. But he had been in the capital for the late-summer trading session and had not received the secondary bulletin from the district office until the fifteenth day after the directive, which left, practically, thirty days.

Amara knew about Wellen because Wellen was one of the nine operators she had named in the analysis. She had studied Wellen's route as the example of the critical connector function - the route that seemed small but stitched the supply chain together in ways that only became visible when it stopped.

The boy with the oxen — his name was Davan Osse, and he was twenty-two years old, and he ran a livestock-feed carrier that served four villages in the southeastern connector — she knew about because his name appeared on the complaint. She had looked up his certification record after reading the notice. He had filed the old compliance form because he had not known there was a new one. The secondary bulletin had reached his regional distribution office on day sixteen and the notice had been generated on day twenty-two and it had arrived at his address on day thirty-one.

He had been given fourteen days. The compliance form took nine days to process if you used the provincial filing system, which he would have to because he was not registered with a certified agent.

He had five days of actual time.

She thought about Davan Osse for most of the morning.

She thought about what his feed carrier represented: four villages, livestock over winter, feed for the animals that sustained the smallholder operations, the animals that produced the labor that made the planting possible in the spring. The supply chain for a village's agricultural year was not a single commodity; it was a sequence of dependencies, and the livestock feed was early in the sequence, and Davan Osse ran that particular link.

She did not know if Davan Osse had found another route or made arrangements. She might never know. He was twenty-two years old in a complaint with sixteen other people, and the complaint was technically valid, and the sixty-day hold was legally enforceable, and there was nothing particular about him that made him more important than the others except that she had spent seven minutes calculating his timing and could now put a name on what had happened.

She put his name in her notes.

---

At the end of the afternoon, she wrote the second report.

This was the kind of report that could not be buried in a single office queue because she was going to put it in six chains simultaneously, and those six chains overlapped with the same six chains as the March analysis submission, which meant that anyone who wanted to file this particular report in a holding pile would have to coordinate with five other offices to do it, and coordination required time and documentation, and documentation left a record that someone would eventually be able to trace.

The report was forty-two pages. She had written the first thirty-four pages as the analysis, which she had substantially ready from March. The last eight pages were the updated section: the complaint filing, the directive timeline, the transition-window calculation, and the gap revision from forty-to-fifty-two to forty-six-to-fifty-five.

She added a section she had not included in March because in March it had been a projection.

The new section was titled: *Confirmed Impact: Named Supply Operators.*

Under that title she listed eighteen names. Seventeen from the complaint, plus Wellen, who had not been named because her certification was technically in order — her husband had filed correctly, eventually — but whose route was part of the ecological cluster that the complaint disrupted. She listed each operator's route, the communities they served, the specific seasonal supply function they provided, and, where she could determine it, the practical timeline they had received versus the forty-five days the directive had officially allowed.

For Davan Osse she wrote: *Livestock feed carrier, four southeastern communities. De facto notice window: 14 days. Compliance form processing time: 9 days minimum. Route hold effective: Day 127.*

She did not add a commentary beyond the facts. The facts were sufficient.

She sent the report to the six chains at twenty minutes past seventeen.

---

Three days later she received an acknowledgment from one of the six chains: the magistrate's office, which had forwarded it to the Commerce Registry oversight review board.

The other five chains sent nothing.

---

She did not see Corvyn again for eleven days. When she did, it was brief — a formation checkpoint overlap — and he looked at her with the expression of someone who had not forgotten the conversation and had been thinking about it in the spaces between other work.

He said nothing.

She said nothing.

They both knew the number. Forty-six to fifty-five percent. It was moving through the Provincial Corridor in the form of emptier granaries and smaller market stalls and the specific quality of tension that settled on a community when people started calculating how long reserve stores would last.

She had projected it correctly.

Being correct was not the same as having changed anything.

She had sent the report to six chains. She had named the operators. She had calculated the timeline. She had spent nine minutes on Davan Osse specifically because he was young and because fourteen days was a specific kind of impossible.

The supply gap was happening anyway.

She thought about what Corvyn had said: *the hold is within legal parameters.* She thought about the precise accuracy of that statement and the precise uselessness of it. She thought about the person in the Covenant Forwarding Consortium who had looked at the calendar and chosen the sixty-three day filing date — after the transition window, before the autumn supply window closed — and had made that choice in full knowledge of what the filing calendar meant.

She thought about the gap between "technically valid" and "practically disastrous" and the person who had chosen to operate precisely in that gap.

She thought about the fact that the person who had made that filing choice was good at institutional mechanics. As good as she was. Possibly better, in some respects, because they had chosen their leverage point earlier than she had chosen hers.

She filed this as information and not as comfort.

The March analysis was in the formal review queue. The second report was in six chains. The magistrate's office had acknowledged. One contact thread was live.

It was not enough.

It was what she had.

---

*On the forty-second day of the sixty-day hold, the provincial distribution network records a seven-percent shortfall in stored feed grain at the southern corridor nodes. The number is within the range she had projected. The number, when it reaches Corvyn's desk, is stamped with the date and filed without comment. Two weeks later, Kael will find her in the analysis room after formation, and she will show him the same page she showed Corvyn, and he will read it differently — not as a logistical problem but as an intelligence problem — and begin asking questions she has not been asked before.*
