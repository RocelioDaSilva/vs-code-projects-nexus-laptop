# CHAPTER 30: ARCHIVE INFILTRATION

On day one hundred and forty, the Covenant's interior source sent the partial-cycle report.

The source had accepted the five-day option. Kael relayed confirmation through the dead-drop on day one hundred and thirty-six — four days after their meeting. The relay format told him the outcome: a specific notation on the chalk wall indicating channel continuation, which meant the source was proceeding. He had spent those four days completing normal duties without visible adjustment.

The partial report arrived through a chain he'd never used before. It came via the Caris channel — an unexpected routing — in a heavily encoded format that Caris had translated before forwarding. The translation was incomplete: Caris had flagged three sections she couldn't decode and had noted that the encoding pattern was interior-Covenant rather than standard resistance cipher architecture. The source was using the Covenant's own encoding system, which Caris partially understood but hadn't fully mapped.

What he could read:
- *Provincial operations review confirmed: Sector 4-North, 6-East, and 7-South currently active deployment.*
- *Deployment rotation: first-wave provincial trainees scheduled for relief at day 180. Rotation will include second-year cohort deployment-track personnel.*
- *Flag-activation processing schedule: dormant-active classification review to complete by day 160 for pre-identified provincial conflict designates.*

Day one hundred and sixty. He was currently at day one hundred and forty. Twenty days.

He needed to transmit to Kael immediately. Twenty days to flag-activation processing for the hereditary classification contacts. Twenty days of the window Kael was already operating in with her flag-active contact relocated and operationally isolated.

He encoded and transmitted the flag-processing timeline to Kael before he went to sleep that night. He also encoded a second segment to Caris: *Interior source cutoff protocol activating. Flag-processing timeline transmitted to K. Can you attempt decoding the three flagged sections in next 5 days?*

Response from Caris came in two days: *Will attempt. Cannot guarantee. The encoding architecture uses a rotation key I have partial mapping of. Best estimate: one section translatable, two require key material I don't have.*

He accepted that.

---

On day one hundred and forty-two, he learned what was in Serath's files.

He had not planned to access them. It happened through standard duty rotation: he was assigned as documentation officer for a command-post record transfer — an administrative task that involved moving physical files between secure storage locations when command posts relocated. This was the fourth such task he'd been assigned, and the previous three had been genuinely mundane.

This one was not.

The file bundle he was handed contained transfer documentation for a command post shift — Serath's post was relocating from the current building to a consolidated command facility two streets away. The bundle included administrative correspondence, personnel movement orders, and a sealed intelligence file marked with a priority retention code.

The sealed intelligence file was not part of his document transfer manifest. It was present because whoever had assembled the transfer bundle had included it by error.

He noted this for three seconds.

The file was sealed with a wax-adhesive closure — standard Covenant intelligence protocol. He had the document seal tool issued to documentation officers for legitimate filing. Breaking and re-applying the seal was detectable by experienced intelligence staff. The re-application left microscopic irregularities in the wax surface.

He had eighteen minutes before the next record transfer checkpoint.

He knew three things: he was almost certainly on duty rotation at a specific time and location that would be logged. He was in a building with established camera observation points (he'd mapped them during his first week). He had access to a sealed file that, by its retention code, contained operational intelligence rather than administrative records.

He thought about whether opening it was within his partition model with Serath.

The partition was: structural/pattern analysis only, no individual targeting. The file could contain either. He didn't know which.

He also thought about twenty days to flag-activation processing. About the flag-active contact Kael had relocated. About a Registry Inspector who'd asked him specific questions about Section 6 eight days before the interior Covenant source's compliance interview.

He opened the file.

The file contained four pages. He photographed all four in thirty-one seconds, re-applied the seal with the documentation tool's wax adhesive function, and replaced the file in the transfer bundle at the correct position.

He completed the document transfer checkpoint at the scheduled time.

---

He did not read the photographs until that evening.

The file contained: a Registry coordinated-action order for provincial sectors 4, 6, and 7; a command-level assessment of the regulatory review board's likely decision timeline and recommended pre-review enforcement actions; a personnel flag list for "accelerated processing pending regulatory outcome"; and a coordination schedule.

The personnel flag list had twelve names.

He did not know most of them. Two of them he recognized: a contact identification number he knew as associated with a dead-drop marker in Kael's network, and a name in the staff-category academic deployment register — faculty from the Academy.

The name in the staff-category register was Vex's.

He read it three times to ensure he wasn't constructing a false pattern.

Name, staff category, Academy unit, "embedded advisory deployment-track, Sector 4-North coordinated assignment, accelerated processing."

Accelerated processing under the flag list meant she was scheduled for binding-protocol application as part of the flag-activation sweep before the regulatory review could constrain it.

Twenty days.

He sat with this for eleven seconds.

He did not transmit anything that evening. He ate at the standard time. He completed evening formation. He went to the equipment maintenance station and spent ninety minutes on chain-tip calibration work — the flanged tip, the overextension problem, adjustments to the collar weight distribution. This was legitimate work and he needed it to be legitimate work because he needed to be somewhere that didn't look like transmission planning.

He thought about the four pages.

The coordinated-action order was Registry-level — above Serath's command tier. She may or may not have seen it. The file was in her transfer bundle, but the retention code indicated it had been routed through her post as a relay point, not as a destination. She might be acting on it. She might have had it forwarded through her without reading it.

He didn't know.

He thought about what he could do.

He could transmit the flag-list personnel identifiers to Kael. She could cross-reference against her network and act on the ones relevant to her. This was within his partition model with Serath: structural intelligence about Registry enforcement patterns, no individual targeting. A flag list was structural — it was the Registry's architecture, not Aisen identifying resistance members.

He could transmit the staff-category academic flag entry to Vex. She needed to know she was on a binding-protocol processing list. "Accelerated" meant it was happening in the twenty-day window, before she could rely on the regulatory review.

He could transmit the coordinated-action order to Caris as contextual support for the regulatory review analysis — evidence of pre-review action that Amara's board submission was specifically designed to prevent.

Three transmissions. Three different channels. All of them defensible within one or another of his existing frameworks.

He also thought about whether they were all correct to make.

The flag list: sending identifiers to Kael without knowing which were Kael network contacts and which were bystanders caught in the classification sweep meant transmitting information about potentially-innocent individuals. Even if the transmission was for their protection rather than targeting, his partition model had been built on the principle that he did not transmit individual-level information.

He held that.

Then he thought about Soren. The sealed container in Section 6. The identities inside it — Fourth Circle era practitioners whose descendants were now in dormant-active classification because of records maintained for three hundred years. He'd photographed the archive documentation. He'd used the pattern to help Caris support a regulatory analysis. He'd never transmitted the names.

The flag list was the same architecture. He knew the names because he'd opened a file he shouldn't have opened. The transmission of those names, even for protective purposes, was the kind of action that, performed repeatedly and under increasing pressure, became the mechanism by which people turned from what they had decided to be into something else.

He did not transmit the flag list.

He transmitted two things: the coordinated-action order to Caris, with a notation that it documented pre-review enforcement acceleration. And a single-channel message to Vex: *Staff-category flag. Accelerated processing. Twenty days. Do not wait for administrative review.*

He did not specify the source.

He went to sleep.

At some point before dawn, he realized he was counting from one and had been for at least twenty minutes. He stopped counting.
