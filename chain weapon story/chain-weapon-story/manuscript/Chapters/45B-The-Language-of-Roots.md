# 45B: The Language of Roots

The blocking operation would hold for one day. Perhaps two.

Kaelen had positioned five hundred soldiers across a sixty-mile stretch of the southeastern frontier, dispersed in depth across three fortified ridgelines. The Covenant forces pushing through the Pass of Sorrows numbered twice that, but they moved slowly—supply lines stretched thin, command structure fractured by the sudden arrest of regional commanders, intelligence networks ablaze with confusion about who had betrayed whom.

One day. Maybe two.

That bought Aisen time to distribute the heresy evidence across the border cities. It bought Rin time to finish whatever it was the Celestial Kingdom's spymaster was arranging. It bought Elara time to—

Kaelen didn't let himself finish that thought. Pragmatism required not asking what Elara was doing. Pragmatism required focusing on the tactical problem in front of him.

The southeastern ridge was the weakest point. He'd reinforced it with the best of his people—two companies from the Blackwood holdings, seasoned cavalry, three mage-practitioners who could channel the Flux in combat bursts. Not enough. Never enough. But enough to delay.

The first assault came at dawn on the second day.

Kaelen watched from the command post as the Covenant vanguard crested the lower slope, five hundred strong, moving in tight formation. Standard doctrine. Efficient. They'd lose maybe fifteen percent in the approach—acceptable losses, from their perspective. They weren't fighting for pride. They were fighting because the Heresy Archive had been opened, and if the knowledge spread, the entire power structure of the Covenant would collapse like a building with its load-bearing walls cut through.

"Contact," his second, Dalla, said quietly beside him. "Ridge Three, front formation."

The mage-practitioner on the ridge—Sasha, a woman who'd learned the Flux through seventeen years of field medicine, not academy instruction—opened her hands to the wind. The Flux hummed, a vibration Kaelen felt in his teeth.

Three of the Covenant soldiers fell, their muscles locked in spasm. Not dead. Paralysis, localized to the nervous system, lasting maybe thirty seconds. Long enough for the cavalry to wheel and strike.

In thirty seconds of perfect coordination, the cavalry inflicted thirty casualties. The Covenant formation broke, reformed a hundred yards back, and—

"They're adapting," Dalla said.

She was right. The next assault spread their approach thinner, predictable counter-positioning against a known threat. Sasha couldn't paralyze dispersed targets effectively. The second wave lost only twelve soldiers but broke through the forward picket line.

By midday, the forward ridge was untenable.

Kaelen gave the order to withdraw to Ridge Two. A controlled retreat—soldiers moving in disciplined units, laying caltrops and wire as they fell back. The Covenant forces followed, consolidating, regrouping. They were buying time too. Not just buying—*spending* it. Every hour they pushed forward was an hour the rest of their army couldn't move, couldn't strike at the interior fronts.

Kaelen understood that calculus perfectly.

It was the human cost he was beginning to misunderstand.

By the second day of the withdrawal, his casualty count stood at eighty-three dead, one hundred forty-seven wounded. The wounded filled the makeshift hospital tent—converted barracks, no proper supplies, three medics trying to address injuries that should have had better attention. One soldier, a boy named Teven who couldn't have been older than nineteen, had a compound fracture of the femur. Without proper setting and time, he'd lose the leg. Dalla was trying to explain this to him in a voice carefully stripped of all emotion.

"Will I still be able to fight?" Teven asked.

"Not with a sword," Dalla said. "You could retrain for other roles."

Kaelen knew that was a lie. A man with one leg had no place in a peacetime army, let alone one fighting for survival. He'd be pensioned, sent to some provincial town with enough stipend to starve slowly. That was military pragmatism. That was the logic that kept armies functioning.

It was the logic that made Kaelen's hands shake.

He stepped outside the tent and found Sasha sitting with her back against the canvas, her eyes closed. The Flux-casting had left marks on her—not visible ones, but Kaelen had learned to read the signs. Her hands were shaking. Her breathing was shallow. She'd burned herself out, poured more of herself into that morning's paralysis spells than any one person should.

"Two more casts," she said without opening her eyes. "After that, I'll start killing my own people instead of theirs."

Kaelen sat beside her. "I know."

"You know what? That I'm breaking? Or that I was always broken, and you're just now seeing it?"

"That the calculation works both ways," Kaelen said quietly. "They lose men so their supply lines don't stretch further. We lose men so their advance stays slow. And nobody—nobody—benefits from that trade except the people making the trade."

Sasha opened her eyes. They were the color of winter iron. "You're developing a conscience, Commander. In the middle of a war. Bad timing."

"Is it?" Kaelen asked. "Or is it the only time it matters?"

She didn't answer. After a moment, she closed her eyes again.

---

The third assault came in the afternoon, and this time the Covenant forces brought siege equipment. Not full siege towers—nothing that massive could be moved through the mountain passes quickly—but field artillery, improvised ballista frameworks designed to rain stone and iron onto entrenched positions. Military innovation born of desperation. Kaelen recognized it because he'd done the same thing six months ago, facing a different enemy, in a different war that somehow had become this war.

There were too many wars running parallel. That was the real problem. The Covenant against the Crown. The Crown against the provinces. The provinces against each other. The Flux corrupting the ecology, creating beasts that destroyed settlements indiscriminately. And underneath all of that, the grinding, invisible war between pragmatism and something else—something that Kaelen was discovering he didn't have a name for.

The ballista fire came in waves, and soldiers died in ways that didn't allow for clean tactical analysis. A stone strike wasn't a duel. It wasn't a place where skill mattered. It was randomness applied with intent, and it stripped away the last layer of Kaelen's distance from what he was doing.

Twenty-two soldiers killed in the artillery barrage. Eleven more wounded so severely that Dalla quietly moved them to the back tent, where the three medics and Sasha were managing the transition to whatever came after survival.

Kaelen watched the calculations happening in his own mind, the part of him that had been trained since childhood to think in terms of acceptable losses and strategic advantage. Twenty-two dead was acceptable because it kept two hundred alive. Eleven dying was acceptable because it meant the Covenant couldn't advance. The math was inexorable.

And it was making him someone he didn't recognize.

---

The withdrawal to Ridge One happened at dusk. The final line. Behind them, the interior valleys, the main roads to the Crown cities, the possibility of losing the entire war in a single collapse of the southeastern flank.

Kaelen stood at the height of Ridge One and watched the Covenant forces consolidate below, preparing for the final assault. They'd done it cleanly. Professional military operation. By tomorrow morning, they'd have positioned enough forces for a coordinated push that would overwhelm the ridge and break through to the interior.

One more day was possible. Maybe one more day after that.

That would be enough time.

Dalla found him at the edge of the ridge, looking down at the enemy encampment where fires were being lit, where soldiers were being fed, where men like him were having the same calculations Kaelen was having.

"Sasha's saying she can do one more cast," Dalla said. "A big one. She's asking if it's worth the cost."

"Cost to her?"

"To her first. To us second. If she burns that deep, she'll lose herself for days. Maybe longer. And we'll still lose soldiers in the assault."

Kaelen closed his eyes. The language of roots, his mentor had called it once, years ago in a classroom in the Blackwood holdings. The way a tree doesn't grow taller by ignoring its roots—it grows by understanding exactly how deep they go, how far they reach, what nutrients they're drawing from the earth. Understanding the foundation.

The foundation of who Kaelen was: a man trained to make these calculations. A man who'd been given authority precisely because he could separate the emotional from the tactical. A man for whom every soldier's life was already translated into a number in a larger equation.

And the foundation he was now discovering: the recognition that those numbers had faces. That Teven's lost leg was a real loss, not an acceptable one. That Sasha's burned-out hands were her hands, not strategic resources. That Dalla had been asking him not "can we afford this" but "is this who we are?"

"Tell Sasha no," Kaelen said.

Dalla was silent for a long moment. "That costs us the ridge. Maybe costs us more than the ridge."

"Yes."

"Same number live. Same number die. You weren't the variable, Kael."

It took him a moment to recognize the quote—Dalla's own words, from a conversation Kaelen didn't remember having with her. Or maybe she was reminding him of what he'd once said to someone else. The pragmatism going full circle, the lie completing itself.

But underneath it, Kaelen heard the real message: *You're allowed to break. That's allowed too.*

---

The ridge held for six hours after dawn.

The Covenant forces made their final assault with coordinated precision, no heroic charges, just systematic application of superior force. The southeastern flank crumbled first—soldiers falling back through prepared positions, slowing the advance by creating killing zones that cost the Covenant time and soldiers but didn't stop them.

By noon, the ridge was lost. By mid-afternoon, Kaelen was organizing a fighting retreat toward the interior valleys. By sunset, he was reorganizing three hundred and forty-seven survivors from the original five hundred.

One hundred and fifty-three dead. Fifty-four too wounded to continue. Numbers that would be names and faces and families getting letters written in careful military script.

Same number lived. Same number died. But those were different same numbers than Kaelen had calculated before he understood the language of roots.

He sat in the new command post—a burned-out inn, ironically, from some previous war, some previous losing general trying to hold a ridge—and wrote casualty reports. Dalla brought him water. Sasha, still shaking, still burned out, sat outside on the steps and didn't ask him if it had been worth it.

Because it hadn't been. Not in any way that mattered.

And that, Kaelen realized, was the moment the Lie died. The Lie that pragmatism could exist without cost to the soul. The Lie that authority meant you could avoid the weight of every body that fell because of your orders.

The truth was heavier. And it was the only weight that made him feel real.

The lead figure stopped at what Kaelen estimated was six meters from where the resistance delegation stood. Aisen was to Kaelen's left. Rin behind and to the right, positioned where she could observe without being the focal point — he'd noticed she always did this, placed herself at the edge of attention rather than its center. There were four others in their party: Serath's security detail, keeping distance at the treeline.

The six meters of space between the two groups was not empty. The mycelial network ran through it in a dense, active lattice. Kaelen could feel it pulsing — faster now, the chemical signals accelerating as if the network itself was anticipating the interaction.

He realized, with a clarity that felt like stepping into cold water, that the beast-kin were *reading the network.* Not passively. They were receiving the same information he was — the root-transmitted state data, the chemical signals, the topological map of connection and disruption. And they were receiving it with a facility that made his four years of study look like a child learning to sound out letters.

"Kaelen," Aisen said quietly. "They're waiting."

"They're not waiting," Kaelen said. "They're listening."

"To what?"

"To the ground."

He stepped forward. Not to the six-meter midpoint — that felt like a human calculation, a symmetry that belonged to negotiation rooms and treaty tables. Instead, he knelt again. Placed his palms on the soil. Closed his eyes.

The network's state shifted. He could feel the three beast-kin's root-connection signatures responding to his touch — a cascading chemical adjustment that propagated through the mycelial web from his position to theirs in approximately four seconds. They were receiving his signal. They were receiving *him* — not his words, not his diplomatic intentions, but his physiological state transmitted through the medium both parties shared.

His heart rate. His soil-contact history (years of greenhouse work, his hands permanently embedded with organic particulates). His emotional state, translated into hormonal markers that the network carried as chemical data.

He was being read like a text.

The lead beast-kin made a sound. Low, resonant, somewhere between vocalization and the subsonic hum of a large animal at rest. Kaelen felt the sound in his chest before he heard it with his ears — because the mycelial network carried the vibration through the soil faster than the air transmitted it.

He understood. Not the content — the format. The sound was the beast-kin equivalent of placing hands on the ground. It was a declaration of presence. It was saying: *I am here. The network knows me. Now it knows you.*

He hummed back.

It wasn't a conscious decision. His body responded to the vibration the way a tuning fork responds to a struck note — sympathetic resonance, the frequency finding its match. The sound he produced was higher, thinner, recognizably human in its timbre, but it carried the same structural information: *I am here. I am listening. The medium between us is alive and I know it.*

When he opened his eyes, the lead beast-kin had moved. Not closer — they had *knelt.* Both palms on the ground. A mirror of his posture. The distance between them hadn't changed, but the topology of the interaction had shifted fundamentally. They were no longer two groups facing each other across space. They were two nodes in a shared network, both connected to the same substrate.

---

The conversation lasted three hours and Kaelen understood perhaps a tenth of it.

What he understood was this: the beast-kin communicated through a layered system where vocalization was the smallest and least important channel. The primary channel was ecological — root-network signals, soil-chemistry modulation, plant-response patterns. The secondary channel was somatic — body position, foot pressure, the specific way weight was distributed and shifted during interaction. The tertiary channel was vocal, and it functioned primarily as *emotional coloring* rather than information transfer.

Humans had built their entire communicative apparatus on the channel the beast-kin considered decorative.

The lead figure — who, when Kaelen asked through a combination of root-signal and gesture how to address them, transmitted a complex ecological-state pattern that he mentally transliterated as *Thorn-Who-Bridges* — communicated the following, as near as Kaelen could reconstruct:

The ecological crisis was not a natural phenomenon. The Flux Tears were the surface expression of a deeper disruption — something happening in the magical substrate that the beast-kin had been monitoring for three generations. The disruption correlated precisely with the expansion of human magical infrastructure. Not all human magic — specifically, the kind of large-scale magical operations the Covenant conducted: mass Flux channeling, long-distance magical enforcement, the contract-vault systems, the heresy-detection networks.

The accumulated magical throughput was degrading the ecological substrate that both species depended on.

Kaelen had suspected this. The synthesis research pointed to it. But hearing it confirmed — *feeling* it confirmed, through the root network, as the beast-kin's monitoring data translated into chemical states he could read — was different from suspecting.

It was the difference between reading about drought and feeling the soil crack.

"How long?" he asked. Not vocally — he had learned, within the first forty minutes, that vocal questions were less effective than root-transmitted ones. He pressed the question into the soil through a combination of synthesis magic and the emotional weight of genuine urgency.

Thorn-Who-Bridges's response came through the network: a temporal pattern that Kaelen interpreted as approximately one generation at current rates. If human magical infrastructure continued expanding, the mycelial network supporting the eastern forest would collapse within thirty years. The secondary forests within fifty. The agricultural zones within seventy.

The beast-kin were not facing a political problem. They were facing extinction.

---

Aisen had been standing throughout the conversation, watching.

Kaelen knew what Aisen was doing — cataloging. Counting the communicative exchanges, mapping the interaction patterns, building a structural model of how the two species were relating to each other. Aisen processed everything spatially, converting experience into architecture. Kaelen processed everything organically, converting experience into ecology. They were two cognitive frameworks operating on the same dataset and producing complementary maps.

"I need to tell you what they're saying," Kaelen said during a break in the communication, when Thorn-Who-Bridges had stepped away to consult with their companions.

"You've been telling me," Aisen said. "Your body language changed at approximately the forty-minute mark. You went from curious to frightened and then to something I'd describe as professionally devastated. Whatever they told you about the ecological timeline was worse than our projections."

Kaelen looked at him. "How do you do that?"

"How do you talk to a root system?"

"Fair."

He told Aisen the timeline. Aisen went still — the particular stillness that meant he was processing, his body becoming a container for rapid internal calculation. His eyes moved. Kaelen had seen him do this a hundred times and it still unsettled him slightly, the way Aisen's consciousness seemed to leave his face and retreat to somewhere behind his eyes where the real work happened.

"One generation," Aisen said.

"At current rates. If we stopped all large-scale magical infrastructure today — stopped everything — recovery would take approximately two generations."

"We can't stop everything today."

"No."

"So the question is what can we stop, and what can we replace with something that doesn't degrade the substrate."

"That's the synthesis question," Kaelen said. "That's what I've been researching. Small-scale, ecologically integrated magic instead of large-scale, extraction-based magic. The theory works. I've demonstrated it in the greenhouse. But greenhouse scale and continental scale are—"

"Different problems."

"Different orders of magnitude."

Aisen was quiet for a moment. "They know this. The beast-kin. They know the solution exists in theory but not in practice."

"They've known for longer than we have. They've been watching humans develop synthesis techniques — Thorn mentioned, I think, that they've observed my greenhouse work through the root network. They've been *monitoring our research.*"

"Through the ground."

"Through the ground."

Aisen processed this. "Then they know what we're capable of. And they know it's not enough. And they came to this meeting anyway."

"Yes."

"Why?"

---

The answer came in the second hour.

Thorn-Who-Bridges returned with the companion who had been carrying the woven container. They opened it. Inside was soil — dark, dense, rich with a particular iridescent quality that Kaelen had never seen in any sample he'd worked with.

Thorn knelt and placed the container between them. Then they pressed both hands to the soil inside it and transmitted a root-signal that was, even with Kaelen's limited fluency, unmistakable in its emotional register: *this is what the land was. Before.*

Kaelen touched the soil.

The mycelial network in that small container was dense beyond anything in his experience. Not just dense — *complex.* The fungal threads weren't merely connecting root systems; they were operating as a computational substrate, processing environmental data at a resolution that made the clearing's network look like a rough sketch compared to a detailed map. The soil supported dozens of microorganism populations he couldn't even identify, all in dynamic equilibrium, all contributing to a web of ecological relationship so intricate it made his synthesis experiments look like arranging blocks on a table.

This was what the world's ecology had been before human magical infrastructure disrupted it.

This was what they were losing.

"Why should we trust the species that poisoned our land?"

The question came through the root network, transmitted from all three beast-kin simultaneously — a harmonic signal that resonated through the container soil and into the ground beneath Kaelen's knees and up through his spine. It wasn't hostile. It was precise. It was asking exactly what it asked, with no diplomatic softening, no room for evasion.

Why should we trust you?

Kaelen had no answer.

He had spent years developing synthesis magic — ecologically integrated, small-scale, regenerative instead of extractive. He had demonstrated that humans *could* do magic differently. But he had not stopped the large-scale operations that were causing the damage. He had not even slowed them. He had tended his greenhouse while the continental ecology collapsed.

His pacifism — his refusal to engage in the violent, confrontational work of actually *stopping* the Covenant's infrastructure — had been his Lie, and here, kneeling in front of people whose world was dying because of his species' choices, the Lie was visible.

Growing things wasn't enough. You also had to protect what grew. And sometimes protection required destroying what was killing it.

"I don't have an answer," he said. Through the root network, so the words carried his full emotional state: the shame, the recognition, the genuine helplessness. "I can show you what I've built. I can show you that some of us are trying. But I can't tell you it's enough. It isn't."

Thorn-Who-Bridges received this. All three of them received it — Kaelen could feel the chemical cascade as his transmitted state propagated through the network to their positions. They were reading his honesty the way they read soil composition. There was no diplomatic performance layer to penetrate. The medium didn't support lying.

A long silence.

Then Thorn transmitted something Kaelen had to process three times before he understood it. A conditional statement, ecological in structure: *If the damaged area is treated honestly — not healed, because healing requires time we may not have, but honestly assessed and honestly tended — then the root network can be maintained even in degraded condition. The question is not whether you can fix what your species broke. The question is whether you will stop breaking it.*

"Will you?" Thorn added. Vocally, this time. In Standard Common, heavily accented but perfectly clear. The first words any of them had spoken aloud to each other.

The clearing went silent.

---

On the walk back to the forward camp, Rin fell into step beside him.

She had been present throughout the meeting but had not participated — her role was observation, pattern-mapping, the kind of intelligence assessment that she performed constantly and shared selectively. Kaelen had noticed her watching Thorn-Who-Bridges with the intensity she applied to novel information sources.

"You understood them," she said.

"Partially. Maybe a tenth of what they were communicating."

"That's more than anyone else would have managed."

"It's not enough."

They walked in silence for a while. The forest around them was alive with the root network's background activity — Kaelen could feel it through his boots, a constant low hum of ecological state-data that he was starting to read automatically, the way you read the emotional tenor of a room when you walked in.

"The dead zones," Rin said. "The three areas where the network terminates. Those correspond to—"

"The Flux Tear sites. Yes."

"I mapped those from intelligence intercepts. The Covenant's internal reports classify them as 'contained instabilities.' Their assessment is that the ecological damage is a manageable side effect of necessary magical infrastructure."

"Manageable," Kaelen said. The word tasted like ash.

"Their word. Not mine." She paused. "I have reports — intercepted Covenant documentation — that detail the rate of ecological degradation around each infrastructure site. I haven't shared them with the full network because the source methodology is..." She trailed off.

Kaelen looked at her. In the years he had known Rin, she had never trailed off. She completed sentences. She delivered information. She did not hesitate.

"Is what?" he asked.

"Celestial Kingdom intelligence methods. If I share the source data, the analytical pattern reveals my homeland's surveillance capabilities, which I am not authorized to expose." She looked straight ahead. "But the data would help them. The beast-kin. It would validate their monitoring and give them a human-framework translation of what they already know through the root network."

"Why are you telling me this?"

She was quiet for several steps.

"Because you told them you didn't have an answer," she said. "And you meant it. I could tell because they could tell, and their response changed after you said it. Honesty through that medium is — it's not like honesty in a briefing room. It's structural. The root network doesn't support performance."

"No. It doesn't."

"I spend most of my operational life performing," Rin said. "Concealing. Controlling what others know about me and my sources and my methods. It is the skill that keeps my people safe and it is also—" She stopped walking.

Kaelen stopped with her.

"It's also lonely," she said. Quietly. As if the word was a door she was opening for the first time.

He didn't say anything. He had learned, from years of working with growing things, that some processes needed silence more than they needed response. You didn't rush a seed. You didn't narrate a root system's expansion. You waited, and you stayed present, and you let the organism do what it was going to do.

Rin looked at him. The white strand in her hair caught the late afternoon light.

"I will share the intelligence data," she said. "With you. Not through the network — with you directly. You can translate it into the ecological framework and present it to the beast-kin without exposing the source methodology."

"That's a risk for you."

"Yes."

"Why?"

She looked at the forest. At the ground. At the root network they were both standing on.

"Because the medium doesn't support lying," she said. "And I'm tired of media that do."

---

They walked the rest of the way in a silence that was, for the first time in his experience of Rin, comfortable rather than operational.

Kaelen thought about Thorn-Who-Bridges's question. *Will you stop breaking it?* He thought about his greenhouse, his synthesis experiments, his careful, patient, non-violent approach to changing the world through demonstration rather than confrontation. He thought about what Rin had just done — shared something that cost her, that made her knowable in a way she had spent her entire career avoiding.

He thought about the dead zones in the root network. Three of them. Expanding.

Some things must be cut before they can grow. He had believed this about plants for years. He was beginning to understand it applied to everything.

He pressed his palms against his trouser legs, felt the dirt embedded in the creases of his skin, and walked toward the camp where the rest of them were waiting to hear what the ground had said.

The math is already clear to him. The cost will be enormous. The alternative is worse.*
