# CHAPTER 10: THE LAST ORDINARY DAY

He left the flat at six forty in the morning, three hours before the Academy's intake period opened, because he wanted to walk.

Not the surveillance-walk, not the cover-pattern walk, not the operational-errand walk with the calibrated pace and the precise route designed to produce no observable associations with anything significant. Just walking. Which was, he was realizing at six forty in the morning three hours before anything became irrevocable, something he had spent three years doing with so much deliberate purpose that he had nearly forgotten how to do it without purpose.

He put his bag down outside the building's door, on the step, because carrying it would have activated the operational posture and he was specifically trying not to do that, and stood in the street for a moment.

The morning was cool and overcast, the sky a uniform grey-white that gave the street's stone surfaces a flat illumination without shadow. It would not rain — this was the still-overcast quality, not the rain-building quality — but it would not be warm until afternoon. He could smell the river two streets west, the particular cold-mineral smell of deep water in cool air, and beyond that the earlier-this-morning smell of the bread bakery on Merchant's Cross that had been operating since four.

He stood in the street for two minutes.

Then he picked up the bag and walked south.

The market district came alive at six — the serious buyers were there at five, professionals and household managers who needed first selection, and by six they were gone and what remained was the second-wave traffic: people buying for the day rather than the week, people walking through rather than shopping, and Aisen, who had no present purpose except to be here while he still was.

At the stall on the corner of Verin and Meridian, the fruit merchant whose name he had never asked was arranging the day's stock with the specific precision of someone whose arrangement mattered to them in a way that had nothing to do with sales efficiency. Each apple at the same angle. The cloth underneath smoothed and smoothed again. The arrangement followed a visual pattern Aisen had observed in the market's older Andean-descended vendors — fruit placed in the pattern of the terrace-step geometry from the highland traditions, which was an unconscious inheritance, something passed through family habit until the person doing it could not tell you why it looked right, only that it did. Aisen had watched this man arrange stock for six months and only this morning, for reasons he could not entirely articulate, found it beautiful. He had not previously allowed himself the reaction. He had been cataloguing.

He bought an apple. The merchant said the price. Aisen paid it and stood at the edge of the stall and ate the apple in the way you ate something when you were trying to actually taste it rather than simply consume it, which was a harder process than it sounded because it required a deliberate quieting of the operational cataloguing — the part of his attention that noted the merchant's position, the sightlines, the exit points — which did not quiet without effort.

The apple was cold and slightly under-ripe and tasted of the cloth it had been stored in and also of apple.

He dropped the core into the refuse bin at the stall's edge, said nothing to the merchant, and continued west.

At the corner where the market district ended and the administrative quarter began, he paused at the intersection without pretending he was looking at anything specific and let himself look at the specific street that contained the Archives Library. From here he could see its northeast face: the old stone with the narrow windows, the second-floor lamp bracket that was always unlit, the small sign above the door that read *CITY ARCHIVE — PUBLIC READING HOURS* in official script. He had walked into and out of that building approximately three hundred times in six years. He knew the exact sound its door made at the point where the hinges needed oiling, which had been the same since he was twelve and had presumably been the same since before he was twelve.

He thought about Soren, in the restricted chamber on the building's east side, with the collection in boxes J through R on the lower east shelves.

He thought: *I'm carrying that now.*

Not in any way that had resolved into a clear plan or a specific obligation — he did not yet know how to relocate the collection, did not yet know what his position at the Academy would be capable of supporting, did not yet know which of the approximately infinite future scenarios would be the one where the collection's relocation became necessary and possible. What he was carrying was the knowledge that it existed and the understanding that when the time came, this would be a part of his responsibility.

He had not eaten the apple the way a person who was not cataloguing would eat an apple. He'd noted the stall's exit routes, the merchant's position, the foot-traffic pattern — all of it running before he'd decided to turn it on.

He was at the bridge. The one the Covenant had widened six years ago. He put his hands on the parapet and looked at the river.

The river at six-fifty in the morning was the color of pewter, moving slowly east, the surface textured by the wind in a regular pattern that shifted every few seconds. He counted six birds on the water before he noticed he was counting and stopped.

He stayed there for perhaps eight minutes before the operational layer fully retreated and he was left with just the water. Grey and moving and present and requiring nothing from him.

Something loosened in his chest that he hadn't realized was tight. His hands on the parapet stone were shaking, very slightly, and this had nothing to do with the Flux and everything to do with the fact that in three hours he would walk into a building designed to identify people like him, and he would stay there for years, and if he made a mistake the size of a misplaced word or an unguarded expression, he would die before twenty-five, and his father would find out through the absence of a letter rather than the presence of one.

He gripped the parapet until the stone's cold went through his palms and into his wrists and the shaking stopped. He did not know, and would not know for several more years, why gripping stone over moving water had this effect specifically — why it was more grounding than gripping wood or metal or brick. The river below was a Flux conductor, its friction over the riverbed generating a slow ambient current that bled outward into the bridge's stone. The cold that went through his palms was not only temperature. It was the baseline regularity of something that had been doing exactly what it did for longer than the bridge had stood, and his nervous system, trained to read the ambient as information, received it as: *this is a system operating exactly as it should.* Not comfort in the sentimental sense. Evidence. The world had not stopped working.

*That's allowed,* he thought. *You're allowed to be afraid. You just can't afford to be afraid and visible at the same time.*

He walked the rest of the way to the Academy slowly.

At the Academy gates, forty minutes before intake opened, he stood with his bag and looked back at the city's skyline over the bridge, and it looked the way it had always looked: rooftops and the river and the Archives' northeast face and the market district's smoke from the bakery and the sky of this particular morning.

He turned and went through the gates.

There was nothing dramatic about this. The gate was an arch, stone, slightly worn at the left side of the threshold where years of foot traffic had produced a subtle concavity. He felt the concavity through his shoe sole. The courtyard beyond was empty at this hour, the morning intake candidates not yet arrived, the Academy's early risers still inside.

He stood in the courtyard by himself and breathed the air, which smelled of cut stone and dew-wet grass from the gardens to the left and, faintly, of the resin the Academy used when waterproofing the lecture-hall floors. And something else. He registered it in the first ten seconds and then spent the next twenty seconds verifying it was not imagination: a higher ambient density than the city outside. Not dramatically higher — not the elevated mountain-region quality of a high-Flux geological zone. But noticeably above the city's baseline, the mineral warmth pressing slightly more present, the way a room with a fire felt warmer than a room without one even before you were near the fire. The Academy was built where Harald's map had indicated: at the intersection of two underground Flux channels. Six years ago Aisen had understood that as political information. Standing in the courtyard at six forty in the morning, he understood it as something more direct: they had built the institution of control at the place where the ambient made control easiest. Where practitioners would naturally be more capable, more sensitive, more susceptible to the environment's influence.

It was a very efficient design.

He was here.

He had not brought any particular emotion with him for this moment, which was probably the appropriate thing. He had preparation. He had the understanding of what he was about to enter. He had the specific practical knowledge of contacts and procedures and emergency channels and the location of Box J through R and the correct way to move through a room without identifying yourself as someone who knows how to move through rooms.

He had thirty-five minutes before the first intake staff arrived.

He found a bench, sat down, set his bag at his feet, and watched the empty courtyard in the early morning light, and tried to do it without cataloguing it, and managed it for approximately two minutes before his attention began its inventory anyway, and he let it, because it was part of what he was, and the question was not how to eliminate the attention but how to choose, deliberately, when to use it.
