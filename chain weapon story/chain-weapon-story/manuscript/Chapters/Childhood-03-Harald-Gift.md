# Scene: Harald's Gift

The guard yard woke before the town did.

At first light, frost still on the railings, Sergeant Harald ran his unit through footwork drills that looked boring until you noticed how little wasted motion they contained. Aisen watched from outside the fence, pretending to wait for a delivery cart that never came.

On the fourth morning Harald called a halt and pointed at him.

"Fence boy. In."

Aisen obeyed before fear could argue.

Harald tossed him a blunted practice spear. The shaft was scarred and smooth where old hands had gripped it. It felt heavier than the broom handles Aisen used at home and lighter than the responsibility of being seen.

"If you are going to imitate professionals from outside a fence," Harald said, "you can at least imitate correctly."

The guards laughed. Not cruelly.

"Stance," Harald said.

Aisen copied what he had watched.

"No. That is a pose. I asked for stance."

Harald kicked his rear foot two inches wider and tapped his front knee until it bent.

"You are not trying to look dangerous. You are trying to remain standing when someone bigger disagrees with you."

They spent twenty minutes on nothing but grip transitions.

Rear-hand control for thrust.
Lead-hand slide for recovery.
No white-knuckle tension.
No dramatic lunges.

"Distance is your friend," Harald said. "Pride is not."

After drill, as the others dispersed, Harald waved Aisen to the water barrel.

"Name."

"Aisen."

"Work?"

"Tavern chores. Hunting when needed."

Harald grunted like this confirmed a private theory.

"You can come watch twice a week. You do not speak during unit drill. You do not interfere. After, I give you ten minutes. If you waste those ten minutes with flourishes, I take the spear back."

"Yes, Sergeant."

"Also," Harald added, "if you call me sergeant outside this yard, I pretend I do not know you."

The arrangement lasted months.

Ten minutes became fifteen when Aisen proved he would do boring work without complaint.

Harald taught him the fundamentals that saved lives and never appeared in songs.

How to reset footing after a miss.
How to absorb impact through angle, not strength.
How to withdraw without turning your back.
How to check the shaft for hairline fractures after rain.

"Weapon care is self-respect," Harald said, handing him oil cloth. "Neglect your tool and your tool will negotiate your funeral."

Some evenings Harald brought tea in dented tins and said little. Some evenings he told stories from campaigns with names Aisen did not recognize and casualties Harald remembered by first name.

"You think combat is bravery," Harald said once while tightening a spearhead collar. "Mostly it is logistics, fear management, and not lying to yourself about fatigue."

Aisen listened like each sentence might become rope later.

One storm night he practiced alone in the alley behind the tavern, working thrust-recover-pivot until blisters opened under both thumbs. Harald appeared under the awning without warning.

"Show me your hands," he said.

Aisen did.

"Good," Harald said. "Now show me your footwork. Pain makes people skip fundamentals."

Aisen ran the sequence anyway.

Harald watched, then nodded once.

"Still ugly. Less dangerous."

That was high praise.

By autumn, Aisen had outgrown the idea that weapons made people strong. The spear was a lever. The person made the decisions. Harald hammered that truth into him with relentless repetition.

"Do not confuse capacity with permission," he said. "Having a point does not mean you thrust."

When rumors spread that Aisen might test for Academy placement in a year or two, Harald stopped him after drill and led him to the equipment shed.

From the back rack he pulled an older practice spear, one he had clearly repaired more than once.

"This one tracks true," Harald said. "Slight warp near the third hand-width, but predictable if you know it."

He held it out.

"Take it."

Aisen stared.

"I cannot pay for-"

"It is not a sale," Harald said. "It is an investment in fewer stupid deaths."

Aisen took the spear with both hands.

"Thank you," he said, and meant it with his whole body.

Harald shrugged off the moment, embarrassed by gratitude.

"Rules remain. You maintain it. You do not showboat. You do not use it to win tavern arguments. And if you ever become important enough to forget where you learned this, I come find you and make you run forms in the rain until you remember."

"Understood."

Harald turned away, then looked back once.

"One more thing. Skill is not character. Plenty of skilled people are disasters. Build both."

Aisen carried the spear home wrapped in canvas like contraband.

Mira tapped the shaft and said, "So now you are dangerous?"

"No," Aisen said. "Now I am responsible."

His father checked the balance point and gave a slow nod of approval.

Aisen spent that night oiling the wood, smoothing a rough patch near the grip, and memorizing the slight warp Harald had warned him about.

The spear was not perfect.
That was part of the lesson.

Perfect tools were fantasies. Reliable tools and disciplined habits were how people survived long enough to matter.
