# Chapter 04 — Frostfang Incident
**Anchor:** Academy field calibration at Frostfang; unseasonable thaw exposes unstable silt‑lattice and triggers a fatal rupture during the Resonance Trial.

# Frostfang — The Exam That Broke the Ice

They called it a field calibration. Officially it was the Resonance Trial: students paired off, given a site with an anomalous flux trace, and asked to map, stabilize, and extract a small slate of samples. It was a ritual that sounded academic on paper and lethal in practice.

He knew the mountain as an old bruise on the map: Frostfang, a ridge where ice gathered in teeth and wind ate at the edges of footholds. A week of unseasonable thaw had exposed veins of silt-lattice the masters had never seen before. Professor Maren's notes had said 'instability' in a hand that trembled with restraint. The provost had still signed the release.

Varro chose the team like a man arranging trophies. He had influence and a grin that smelled of wine. "A chance for commoners to prove their worth," he said, and the Hall of the Common Stave hummed with complicated hunger. The expedition left in a swirl of banners and disclaimers. The protagonist—Aisen—was there because Varro needed a guide and because Harald's letter had opened a door no money could fit.

The mountain was wrong before they began. Water clung to air and then slid away. Stones hummed low when they were touched, like a throat clearing. The early scouts found ridgelines carved with frost blooms that glowed faintly in the dark. They were not simple crystals; they were something like old wounds that had been stitched and had not held.

The trial began with the issuing of instruments and the arrangement of the group's formation. Masters had watchers in the rocks; some of that was safety and some of it was the performance of safety. The students loved the performance because it meant they could pretend the danger was only for learning. Varro loved the cameras because they showed faces worth remembering.

They split into teams at the first ring of knolls. Aisen's band moved with the clumsy efficiency of inexperienced soldiers: blankets over packs, probes and small resonance lamps, the requisite careful steps of the uninitiated. He walked the edges of the group, more because his feet had learned to read terrain than because he had any love of proving himself. The mountain rewarded basic caution with seconds.

It was the third hour when the silt quivered. A student with a soft mouth named Marta bent to take a swab. Her hands were steady; she had impressed tutors in the symposium. She hummed the standard clearance phrase and the probe sank into the ground and found something that was not stone.

The field flared. It did not behave like a proper flux return. It oscillated and then thudded, a message in a language none of them read. The masters shouted. Cameras tilted. The trial was supposed to teach caution; instead it taught immediacy.

Varro's instinct was to command, not to listen. He called for a full sample and the formation to hold. Aisen called for distance. The two impulses collided—authority pushing forward, practice pulling back. The ground under Marta's feet shed. She was a voice and then not.

Something in the silt had been pressurized by the thaw—an old pocket of flux gassed and then ruptured like a wound being opened. The rupture threw out a tracer field that licked at the students' calibrators and made them scream in a way that sounded like pulses. The academy watchers scrambled for film and for a narrative.

Several students were caught. The cameras kept rolling.

In the next moments Aisen did only what came easiest to him: he covered, he redirected, he used the only tools he trusted—chain and the small, honed telekinesis that a cleric had started in him in the tavern years ago. He thought in vectors. Where a powerful mage might have grabbed the world and thrown it aside, he picked words and edges: *a pull here, a nudge there*. He held a tether on one student's falling spear with a finger's magic and used the chain to loop another into a safe snag.

The cost was visible as it happened. Telekinesis required small units of effort—MU in the academy ledger—and Aisen had been counting them since Frostfang. A jolt to keep one student's arc safe cost him 1 MU. A tether held a child's fall: 3 MU and a thin burning at his temples. He kept track by the way his fingers burned and the way the world focussed down to a single hinge.

He recalled the cleric's pebble and thought of leverage: a torque applied to an unstable mass could turn a disaster into a redirect. He toggled a five-link module of the chain—4 MU to lock a segment as a pole—then sent it like a harpoon into frozen ground. The pole held, crooked and defiant, a little island that bought two breaths.

Not everyone was so lucky. A boy with hands like a mason's—steady, precise—took a blast to his lungs and went quiet. The masters moved like men choosing a script: triage, notation, blame. Varro's face did the thing of men who had chosen a narrative; he did not look like a man who had seen a life end because he had insisted on spectacle.

The aftermath smelt of melted grease and bitter wine. Evidence would be scrubbed and filmed and argued. The Provost whispered in two languages, and Master Wei, who had always had the patience of fossil study, shuffled paperwork with hands that trembled like anyone else's. There would be a hearing. There would be a scapegoat. There would be tapes.

But on the slope the work was simple: bind the injured, pick up the dead, move the rest. Aisen walked among the bodies and the living, and his hands knew the geometry of a rescue even when his heart refused the arithmetic of immortality. The chain hung heavy across his back; it had done what he had asked of it. The mountain kept quiet as if it had been calmed for the evening and then decided it had only been waiting.

When they returned to Aldric's Crossing the political machines engaged with the neat cruelty of well‑oiled cogs. The Heartland demanded inquiry. An island hall sent a harsh envoy. Houses drafted letters. The academy convened in the cold light of a dozen rooms.

It mattered less to the men in suits whether he had saved three students with small, tired science and a rope than it mattered to the others that he had done it at all. He had made small miracles of conservation—he had paid energy to make a child's world bend by inches rather than be swallowed.

The diplomatic scandal would ripple; politicians would argue about provenance and patrons and whether the academy should have been allowed to hold a trial in a thaw. For him there was something else: a sharpened certainty that the world would take your best if you let it be spectacle, and that sometimes the only answer to showmanship was patient, ugly work.

In the weeks after the Conclave hearing—where the footage was shown and the provost kept his face like a stone mask—he practiced the arithmetic of small pushes and small holds. He learned the cost of a nudge and the burn of a tether. He measured the world in MU and in who owed him nothing but the quiet of a saved life.
