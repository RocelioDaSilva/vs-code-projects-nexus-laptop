# CHAPTER 36: CONSCRIPTION – THE WAR ARRIVES

The notice arrived on Aisen's eighteenth birthday.

All able-bodied citizens of adult age are required to present for military conscription evaluation. Failure to present within fourteen days constitutes treason. The Academy has been instructed to release all conscriptable students for evaluation. This requirement is absolute and non-negotiable.

Seraphine had done it. Not suddenly. Not without warning. But with the systematic precision that Aisen had come to recognize as her operational signature—she'd been building toward this moment since the beginning of the Academy year, positioning pieces, moving alliances, creating the situation where the government had no choice but to conscript. Where refusal was literally impossible.

The war wasn't real, of course. Or rather, it was as real as the Covenant's intelligence reports made it—exaggerated threats, inflated dangers, everything designed to justify the requirement for expanded military control. Aisen had confirmed this through Rin's intelligence network: the supposed border threat that necessitated conscription was approximately 70% fabrication, 20% manipulation of isolated incidents, and 10% actual genuine concern.

But people didn't need real threats to be conscripted. They needed the government to say there was a threat. And the government was under Covenant control.

"We knew this was coming," Kaelen said, sitting in their private study room on the fourth floor of the west tower—the one room in the entire Academy where magical dampening created a space that surveillance couldn't penetrate. "We prepared for this."

"Preparing and experiencing it are different things," Amara said. She was the newest member of their core network, arriving in the second semester through careful introduction by Kaelen's family contacts. Her specialty was strategic coordination—understanding not just what needed to happen, but what needed to happen in what order, and to whom, and with what resources. She was precisely the kind of person they'd needed and never found. And now she was essential. "Once we're conscripted, we're under military control. That changes everything."

"We go," Aisen said flatly. "We go to conscription evaluation. Most of us pass the physical assessment. They place us in military units. We continue our work from within the military structure."

"That's assuming they let us maintain contact," Serath said. As the combat specialist, he'd been the most vocally opposed to conscription. "Military units are separated. Soldiers don't get to choose their companions."

"But soldiers do know how to pass messages," Rin said quietly. She hadn't looked up from the document she was analyzing—a requisition form she'd acquired from the conscription office. "There's already a communication system in place. Soldiers write letters. Soldiers go on leave. Soldiers have contacts in other units." She finally glanced up. "We maintain the network. It becomes distributed, more resilient maybe, but maintainable."

"And if they test us?" Amara asked. "If they identify people with anti-Covenant leanings and actively separate them?"

"Then we've already failed," Aisen said. It was brutal, but it was true. "If the Covenant's intelligence is good enough to identify us, then we never had a chance to begin with. We have to assume we're invisible enough that they don't know the network exists."

He didn't mention that he wasn't certain about this assumption. He didn't mention that Harald—who had stopped meeting with him two years ago for exactly this reason, to avoid creating a visible connection—had warned him that Covenant intelligence was better than most people believed. He didn't mention that conscription might be specifically designed to scatter them in ways that would prevent him from ever seeing Kaelen again.

What he said instead was: "We go. We serve. We survive. We continue the work."

But after the others left—carefully, at different intervals, through different passages that honeycombed through the Academy like the circulatory system of something living—Aisen sat alone in the magical dampening room and allowed himself the luxury of fear.

Conscription evaluation took place over two days. Aisen arrived at the military assessment center with two thousand other young people from the academy district, all of them nervous, all of them understanding that their futures had just been seized by forces that didn't require their consent.

The evaluation itself was designed to be overwhelming. Physical testing first—endurance, strength, speed, the kinds of measurements that could be quantified and ranked. Aisen performed at exactly the level he'd calculated: above average, not remarkable, competent enough to be useful but not so competent that he'd be marked for special training.

The magical assessment came next. A series of tests designed to measure magical potential and affinity. Aisen had spent weeks training himself to project exactly the right level of magical sensitivity—enough that they would classify him as a combat mage, not enough that they would flag him as possessing unusual capability. The tests involved channeling small amounts of magic through specific patterns, demonstrating control, showing the kind of technical competence that the military valued.

He passed all of them at exactly the median level.

"Designation: Combat Mage, Third Class," the evaluator announced. "Assigned to the Seventh Regional Battalion, supporting role."

It wasn't the unit where Kaelen had been assigned. He'd heard Kaelen had tested higher and was designated as a specialist mage, which meant military officers would consider him more valuable and therefore more carefully controlled. It wasn't where Serath had been placed—his combat skills had landed him in a front-line unit where his physical abilities would be most useful.

But the Seventh Regional Battalion was under the command of a colonel named Merren, who Rin's intelligence network had identified as someone with genuine concerns about Covenant overreach. Not an ally, not secretly opposing them, but someone who could be reasoned with.

The conscription made it official: Aisen would be reporting for active duty within thirty days.

He spent his last month at the Academy finishing his coursework with mechanical precision, maintaining his relationships with his networks, and meeting with a rotating series of people who needed to brief him on critical information before the window closed. There was a specific document he needed to understand—the archive records that Soren, the elder scholar, had been protecting for forty years. There was the covenant's actual organizational structure, which Rin had spent months mapping. There was the heresy classification system, which turned out to be far more extensive and controlling than anyone had anticipated.

And there was the truth about what Seraphine actually wanted, which was perhaps the most important piece of all.

"She doesn't want power," Amara said during their final strategic meeting. "That's what makes her so dangerous. She genuinely believes that control is benevolent. She sees the Covenant's systems as necessary structures for keeping people safe from their own mistakes."

"A benevolent totalitarian," Kaelen said.

"Yes. Which means she can't be threatened or reasoned with in normal ways. You can't appeal to her greed or her fear. She has a fundamental belief that her system is correct." Amara leaned forward. "That means the only way to defeat her is to prove her wrong. Not just militarily. Philosophically. Structurally. You have to break her system in ways that demonstrate that people can survive, even thrive, without her control."

"Which means we need to not just win," Rin said quietly, "we need to survive. The network needs to survive. Because once Aisen's gone—and he will be gone, we all understand that—someone needs to keep building the evidence that a better system is possible."

The unspoken thing hung between them: that Aisen, as the strategist, as the coordinator, as the person holding the pieces together, would be the most dangerous to the Covenant. Which meant he would be the most targeted. Which meant the possibility that he wouldn't survive conscription was not a theoretical risk—it was a practical certainty that they all needed to plan for.

"Then we structure the network for succession," Aisen said calmly. "Kaelen, you understand the healing and restorative sides. Rin, you handle intelligence. Serath, you manage combat and security. Amara—"

"I hold it together," Amara said. "After you're gone, I make sure the pieces still function as a system instead of falling apart into individual pieces."

They didn't discuss what "after you're gone" would look like in practical terms. They didn't have the luxury of that conversation. What they did instead was spend the next week documenting everything—all the knowledge, all the strategies, all the careful work that they'd done. They created encrypted copies of Rin's intelligence files. They created detailed organizational guides. They created contingency plans for every scenario they could imagine.

And they created a system that could continue functioning if the people who'd built it stopped being available to run it.

On his final night at the Academy, Aisen walked to the library and found a letter waiting in the location where messages were left. It was from Harald, written in code they'd established years ago.

*"I am proud of you. I regret that I made you strong enough to do what you're about to do. The world will need what you are about to sacrifice. I ask only that you make them earn it. Make the Covenant pay full price for what they've chosen. Not for revenge. For the principle of the thing—that if tyranny costs you everything, those who benefit from tyranny should have to acknowledge that cost. Make it visible. Make it undeniable. Make it matter."*

Aisen sat in the library until the candles burned down, reading Harald's words until he had memorized them. Then he burned the letter, scattered the ashes, and walked back to his dormitory.

The next morning, he reported for active duty.

The Seventh Regional Battalion was stationed forty miles from the capital, in a facility designed to feel simultaneously like a military compound and a prison. Aisen and two hundred other conscripts arrived in coordinated transport, and immediately the Academy year felt like a dream—something that had happened to someone else.

The soldiers who processed them were not kind, but they weren't cruel either. They were professional. They said the rules, assigned barracks, distributed uniforms, and made it clear that the world had changed and would not be changing back.

Aisen's bunk was in a dormitory with thirty other combat mages. Most of them were scared. Some were excited. One—a dark-haired woman who responded to the name Vex—watched the entire processing with an expression of amused detachment, like she was observing something mildly entertaining rather than watching her freedom evaporate.

That evening, after the initial chaos of arrival had settled, Vex sat down next to him while they ate.

"You're the one they've been trying to recruit," she said without preamble. "The strategic thinker from the Academy. Kaelen's friend."

Aisen set down his fork. This was the moment where everything could go very wrong. This could be a Covenant plant. This could be someone sent specifically to identify him.

"I don't know what you're talking about," he said carefully.

"Of course you don't," Vex said. "And I'm not going to tell anyone you do. That would completely defeat the purpose of having you alive." She smiled, and there was something both predatory and welcome about it. "I'm part of the network. I arrived at the Academy last semester specifically to be positioned in the conscription draft. I'm your connection to the external structure."

"And if you're lying?"

"Then I'm extremely dedicated to this cover story," Vex said. "And good luck with what you're about to do, because you won't have external support." She stood up, returned to her meal, and never spoke to him directly about the network again.

But she was there. Somehow. Through coded messages in laundry arrangements, through careful positioning in training exercises, through the subtle ways that information got passed in military environments, Vex maintained the connection between Aisen's unit and the larger structure.

By the end of the first week, Aisen understood something he hadn't quite accepted before: he was in a cage now. The work would continue, but it would be harder, more dangerous, more constrained. He would be constantly watched. He would be expected to follow orders.

And somehow, despite all of that, he had to figure out how to bring down the system that had caged him.

It was impossible. He understood that with crystal clarity.

Which meant it was exactly the kind of thing worth trying.
