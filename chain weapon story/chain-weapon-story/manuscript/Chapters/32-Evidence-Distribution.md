# CHAPTER 32: EVIDENCE DISTRIBUTION

Three months into the military occupation of Southeastern Territory, the provincial resistance networks were successfully operating using the strategic analysis documents that had been distributed from the Academy. The historical precedent analysis had proven genuinely valuable—the resistance networks understood the specific Covenant tactics because they had studied how those same tactics had been used against predecessor movements.

But the resistance needed more. They needed current operational intelligence about what military deployments were actually planned, where military resources were concentrated, what officer commands were most likely to implement brutal suppression tactics and which were more flexible.

This was where Aisen's reports became crucial.

Kael had established a secondary meeting location in Ashfall—an underground space used for resistance network coordination—where she could receive direct intelligence briefings from Aisen. The meetings occurred approximately once per week, during periods when Aisen could claim military business took him to regions where resistance contact would be plausible.

"The military's timeline is being accelerated," Aisen reported during one of these meetings, speaking directly about information he'd learned during command briefings. "Higher command wants full institutional replacement completed in six months rather than the original nine-month projection."

"What does acceleration mean for civilian operations?" Kael asked.

"It means increased military presence in civilian administrative spaces," Aisen said. "More intensive interrogation operations. Faster implementation of Covenant administrative structures. They're going to compress the occupation timeline and intensify suppression operations simultaneously."

"How many additional military personnel?" Kael asked, clearly running calculations.

"Approximately thirty percent force increase," Aisen replied. "Deployed to Ashfall and three surrounding regional centers. They're concentrating overwhelming force in urban areas while maintaining lower presence in rural and frontier territories."

Kael smiled slightly. "That's actually strategically preferable for provincial resistance operations. Rural networks have better survival prospects if military occupied only major urban centers. We can maintain organizational infrastructure outside intensive military surveillance zones."

"The Covenant thinks urban concentration is necessary because roughly seventy percent of provincial population is urban-distributed," Aisen said. "They're making logical assumptions about where to position force to control the majority of the population."

"But they're not thinking about how distributed networks operate," Kael said. "They're thinking about conventional occupation, which requires controlling major urban centers. We can let them do that while maintaining organizational infrastructure outside their surveillance perimeter."

She pulled up intelligence documents that showed resistance networks already establishing rural operational bases—locations outside primary military concentration zones where resistance structures could maintain administrative functions, supply storage, and personnel coordination without direct military engagement.

"We've already positioned for this," Kael said. "I want to accelerate the timeline. We need rural networks fully established before the military force increase occurs. How much advance notice do we have?"

"Approximately four weeks before acceleration deployment begins," Aisen said.

"That's sufficient," Kael confirmed. "We can implement full rural network activation within that timeframe."

---

Over the following weeks, Aisen watched the provincial resistance networks transform. What had been relatively decentralized cell structures became organized into regional coordination networks. Supply lines that had been improvised became systematized. Personnel assignments shifted from tactical coordination to administrative infrastructure building.

The resistance networks were no longer focused on disruption—at least not primarily. They were preparing for long-term engagement, understanding that military suppression phase would eventually conclude and what mattered then was whether organized resistance structures had survived.

The transformation was visible to military operations if anyone was looking carefully. But most military personnel interpreted it as signs of resistance movement destabilization—networks fragmenting under military pressure.

Only Aisen, because he understood both perspective, recognized what was actually happening: the provincial networks were positioning themselves for sustainable long-term resistance operations rather than short-term disruption tactics.

---

The second major component of evidence distribution involved actual physical material transfer. Some of the analysis documents couldn't be distributed through encoded message systems—they were too detailed, too comprehensive, too important to be transmitted gradually through covert channels.

Physical copies needed to be moved to provincial network locations.

This was where Amara became crucial again.

She had retained her military command authority and was overseeing supply logistics for military forces in Southeastern Territory. She had access to supply convoys that moved between military installations. And she had authority to allocate transportation resources for "military administrative needs."

The plan was elegant: the resistance network would provide blank supply containers that would be incorporated into legitimate military supply convoys. These containers would travel to military installations throughout Southeastern Territory. At those installations, military personnel who were resistance sympathizers would retrieve the containers, extract the analysis documents, and distribute them to provincial resistance networks at those locations.

From the military monitoring perspective, supply containers were moving through military supply chains—nothing suspicious. From the resistance perspective, careful analysis documents were being distributed directly to regional commands that needed them.

The system worked for seven different supply rotations. The analysis documents reached resistance networks in six of the seven regional centers where military forces were deployed.

Then, during the eighth rotation, something went wrong.

---

The supply convoy carrying the eighth batch of containers was intercepted by military internal security—not the regular military forces, but the Covenant Intelligence division that was embedded within military command structures.

Amara reported it to Aisen immediately.

"The container was intercepted at checkpoint inspection," she said quietly, meeting him in a secure space in military command post. "Internal security is examining its contents. They've classified the container as suspicious and flagged it for higher-level analysis."

"How sophisticated are the documents?" Aisen asked. "Could they trace them back to Academy sources?"

"Possibly," Amara said. "The documents reference historical Archive materials. If Covenant Intelligence conducts detailed analysis, they might recognize the specific source materials."

"Which leads to investigation of who accessed the Archive during the material consolidation phase," Aisen said, continuing the logical chain. "Which leads to Cynthia. Which leads to Academy network investigation."

"Exactly," Amara confirmed. "I'm recommending immediate operational pause on physical material distribution. The system worked longer than expected, but it's functionally compromised now."

"How long before Intelligence analysis identifies the Archive source?" Aisen asked.

"Unknown," Amara said. "Anywhere from a few days to a few weeks depending on how thoroughly they analyze the materials and how many of their specialists understand historical Archive organizational systems."

---

Aisen immediately encoded a message through the standard covert channels and sent it back toward the Academy: **System compromise likely. Archive materials source may be detected. Recommend immediate protective operations on identified personnel.**

The message reached Caris approximately one week later. By that time, Cynthia was already moving into protective protocols—reducing her visible activity at the Academy, positioning herself for potential extraction if necessary, beginning to transfer her Archive access privileges to carefully selected students who could continue research operations under reduced scrutiny.

The physical distribution system was compromised, true. But the fundamental work had been accomplished—the analysis documents had reached most of the provincial resistance networks during the preparation phase. The evidence was distributed before the Covenant could consolidate control sufficiently to prevent it.
