# Scene: Tavern Night - The Wandering Stag

By ten, Aisen knew the tavern by sound better than sight.

The Wandering Stag changed shape through the evening. At dusk it was all elbows and wet cloaks, men arriving with road dust still on their boots and women counting coin with lips pressed thin. By full dark it softened. Voices lowered. Dice clicked slower. Someone always tuned a fiddle and never tuned it well.

Aisen worked in the spaces between larger people.

He scraped bowls.
Refilled broth.
Moved mugs before hands reached for them.
Kept out of the way and therefore saw everything.

His father called this useful invisibility. "If you learn where eyes do not go," he said, "you will not starve."

That night rain hit the shutters in hard diagonal lines. The room smelled of yeast, smoke, wool, and pepper from his mother's stew pot. Mira sat near the hearth darning a torn sleeve while pretending not to listen to the front room stories.

Near closing, one traveler remained.

He sat in the corner table that had a short leg and a permanent wobble. Cloak dark, beard rough, boots recently resoled by someone careful. Not noble, not beggar. The kind of man who carried his own needle and trusted almost no one.

When Aisen brought him the last ladle of stew, the man did not look up at first.

"You move quiet," the traveler said.

"Trying not to get yelled at," Aisen replied.

That got half a smile.

"Name?"

"Aisen."

The traveler tasted the word like he was checking if it belonged in his mouth.

"Road teaches strange names," he said. "Sit, if your father allows it."

Aisen glanced toward the bar. His father gave the smallest nod.

He sat on the edge of the bench, ready to stand the instant work called.

The traveler ate slowly, watching firelight throw shadows across the beams.

"You ever leave this town?" he asked.

"To the ford. To the mill. Once to South Lane market," Aisen said. "That is all."

"Enough to know roads go somewhere," the traveler said.

Aisen had heard plenty of road talk in the tavern, most of it bragging and half of it lies. This man did not brag. He listed weather and bridge conditions and which magistrate in which district took coin openly and which took it through cousins.

Useful truth.

"You have children?" Aisen asked before he could stop himself.

The traveler turned the spoon in his hand.

"Had a boy once," he said. "Could be alive. Could be under a different name. Roads split families faster than swords do."

Aisen did not know what to say to that. He thought of his own family sleeping in the rooms above the kitchen and felt protective in a way that made his shoulders tighten.

"I help here," he said finally. "So they do not have to carry all of it."

"Good," the traveler said. "Keep that habit."

When he finished, he set two things on the table.

One copper coin.
One small carving of a stag, thumb-length, whittled from cheap pine and polished with oil until it warmed in the hand.

"Coin for the bowl," he said. "Stag for the girl by the fire who pretends she is not listening."

Mira made a face from across the room that said she had heard every word.

Aisen picked up the carving. One antler was shorter than the other. The back was rough where the knife had slipped. It was imperfect in the way hand-made things were imperfect.

"Why give this away?" Aisen asked.

The traveler stood, rolling his shoulders as if preparing to carry bad weather.

"Because someone gave me a knife when I had nothing," he said. "Because small gifts keep people human. Because one day you will have little and still choose to give something."

He pulled his hood up.

At the door, he paused.

"When the road asks what kind of man you are, answer with what you keep for others."

Then he was gone into rain.

Aisen stayed by the table a long moment, palm closed around the stag.

His father came over and collected the bowl.

"Was he trouble?" he asked.

"No," Aisen said. "Just tired."

His father looked at the carving and grunted approval. "Keep the coin in the emergency jar. Give your sister the stag in the morning."

"Can I keep it one night?"

"One night," his father allowed.

After closing, Aisen swept the floor while Mira stacked plates and sang softly off-key. In bed he put the carved stag under his pillow, then took it back out because he was afraid he would break it in his sleep. He set it on the windowsill where moonlight touched the short antler.

Outside, carts rolled through puddles and a late bell rang from the watchtower.

Inside, the tavern settled into creaks and quiet breathing.

Aisen lay awake thinking about roads, about missing sons, about small things passed from hand to hand that kept people from hardening entirely.

He did not have language yet for duty or institutions or resistance.

He had this instead:

A coin in the jar.
A carved stag in the light.
A decision, made without witnesses, that if he ever had enough to give, he would not wait for abundance first.
