# 41: Transition Decision

The depot at Kestrel Ridge smelled like oil, wet rope, and cold iron.

Aisen liked it for exactly that reason. Nothing about the place encouraged illusions. You could not mistake it for moral ground. It was logistics and chain of command, numbers and fuel and weather. Useful things. Dangerous things.

Kaelen arrived near dusk with two escorts and no small talk.

"You have forty-eight hours before this becomes a choice made for you," she said.

Aisen waved her toward the campaign table. "Report first."

She did.

Evidence had reached four provincial capitals and two major trade leagues.
Covenant messaging was no longer synchronized.
Military orders were being interpreted locally.
The Crown had begun offering "stabilization frameworks" to frightened governors.

Aisen listened, then traced a route marker across the map where Crown couriers would move next.

"They are already building the replacement hierarchy," he said.

"Yes," Kaelen answered. "Which is why you cannot keep both roles."

Aisen looked up.

"Say it plain."

"If you remain in military command, you keep access and leverage but become part of Crown consolidation. If you leave now, you lose strategic visibility but keep operational independence with the civilian network. Staying is complicity. Leaving is abandonment. Pick which guilt you can carry."

The wind hit the shutters hard enough to rattle the glass. In the yard below, quartermasters shouted over mule teams and rain.

Aisen had made decisions under fire before. Those were easier. Bullets did not pretend to be ethical.

"What does Amara want?" he asked.

"She says you were never built for institutional residence," Kaelen replied. "Her exact words were: the moment he is forced to stay, he is already becoming what he meant to resist."

Aisen almost laughed, but it came out as a breath through his nose.

He had spent years inside systems he distrusted, telling himself proximity was temporary and necessary. It had been true. It had also become habit. The danger now was not immediate corruption. The danger was adaptation.

He pictured the next year if he stayed.

He would brief Crown liaisons.
He would shape personnel integration.
He would approve "temporary" emergency powers.
He would tell himself each compromise prevented worse outcomes.

He would be useful.
He might even be right.

And at the end of it he would wake one morning fluent in a grammar of harm that sounded like governance.

"I leave," Aisen said.

Kaelen did not celebrate. "Then we lose your access at the worst possible moment."

"We also lose one more excuse for centralization," Aisen said.

"That is not comforting."

"No. It is clean."

They worked until midnight drafting transition steps.

Deputy command to Marshal Joric with explicit constraints.
Open supply accounting for the next ninety days.
No use of emergency detention policy without dual sign-off.
Courier packet to Amara with live chain contacts.

Kaelen watched him sign the final transfer page.

"You know this may hand the Crown an advantage."

"I know," Aisen said. "But if the only way to win is to become permanent inside their machine, then we are not winning."

Three days later he conducted his last command review in the rain.

Soldiers stood in crooked lines, boots in mud, eyes on him for reasons that had little to do with doctrine. They had followed him through operations where official orders and actual ethics did not always align.

"Command transfers at second bell," he told them. "Your duty remains to the people under your protection, not to slogans, not to panic, not to any office that asks you to forget what you have seen."

No applause. Just nods.

When it was done, he returned to his quarters and found one sealed note from Amara.

Do not mistake uncertainty for error.

He folded it into his jacket and left before dawn with no escort and a mule that hated steep roads.

At the ridge line he looked back once.

The depot below was already smaller than he expected, as if distance had begun editing the story before he was ready. Kaelen rode up beside him and said nothing for a long minute.

"You will question this for years," she said at last.

"Good," Aisen replied. "Certainty is how people like us become dangerous."

They turned west toward civilian routes, where authority was thin and consequences were immediate.

By evening he reached the first district council hall, a converted storehouse where thirteen delegates were arguing about floodgate repair budgets and grain theft.

No banners. No strategy theatre. Just problem sets with names attached.

Aisen stepped inside, mud to his knees.

"If you are done shouting," he said, "show me your ledgers."

One woman squinted at him. "You military?"

"Not anymore," he said.

She pushed a ledger across the table. "Then welcome to the hard part."

He opened it and started reading.

She was right.
