# Therryn — Economy, Politics, Religion & Conflicts

## Economy

### The Two-Track System

Therryn runs on two parallel economies that intersect but never merge:

**Track 1: Prestige Economy (Scholar-Officials)**
- Currency: Publication citations, examination rankings, debate victories, position titles.
- A Grand Council member with no personal wealth is still the most powerful person in a province.
- Scholarly works are the primary "product"; their value is in recognition, not sales.
- Knowledge monopolies: Discovering and publishing a new geometric principle gives you citation income (other scholars must credit you) for life.

**Track 2: Monetary Economy (Merchants & Commoners)**
- Standard coin (the "mark") based on silver weight.
- Trade-focused: Therryn controls the continent's most sophisticated merchant fleet.
- Major exports: Printed texts, precision instruments, engineered goods, mathematical treatises, medicinal compounds.
- Major imports: Raw materials (metal, timber, luxury foods), military equipment, magical reagents.
- Taxation: ~20% on trade revenue + 15% on agricultural production + licensing fees for commercial operations.

### Economic Tensions

**The Patronage Trap**
- Merchants fund scholarship because it's the only way to buy influence.
- Scholars accept because they need money.
- But funded scholars become beholden to merchant interests.
- A scholar who publishes unfavorable findings about a patron's business loses funding.
- Result: Scholarship increasingly serves commercial interests while claiming objectivity.

**The Knowledge-Property Problem**
- Therryn recognizes intellectual property (unique among all nations).
- A mathematical discovery is "owned" by its discoverer for 20 years.
- This incentivizes discovery but also incentivizes secrecy before publication and litigation after.
- Patent disputes consume ~15% of the judicial system's bandwidth.
- Foreign scholars (especially Umaran) find the concept of "owning" knowledge morally abhorrent.

**Wage and Price Reference**
- Common laborer: 2-3 marks/day
- Skilled artisan: 5-8 marks/day
- Junior scholar-official salary: 15 marks/day (comfortable but not wealthy)
- Provincial governor salary: 50 marks/day + housing
- Merchant house head income: 100-500 marks/day (vastly outearning scholar-officials)
- One meal at a market stall: 0.5 marks
- One year of examination preparation school: 500-800 marks
- Private tutor: 2,000-5,000 marks/year
- A good sword: 50-100 marks
- A printed textbook: 5-15 marks

(For reference: a laborer earns ~800 marks/year. Examination preparation costs a year's laborer income minimum. This gap is the engine of class reproduction.)

## Politics

### Factional Landscape

**1. Traditionalists (Scholar-Conservatives)**
- Defend the examination system as sacred.
- Oppose merchant influence, military autonomy, and foreign innovation.
- Strongest in the capital and old university cities.
- Motto: "The examination system has served for four hundred years."
- Weakness: Increasingly disconnected from provincial reality.

**2. Reformists (Scholar-Progressives)**  
- Want to modernize examination content, expand access, reduce patronage influence.
- Propose examination scholarships funded by merchant taxation.
- Strongest among younger scholars.
- Motto: "Merit must be real, not theoretical."
- Weakness: Their reforms threaten BOTH traditional scholars and merchants — few allies.

**3. Mercantilist Faction**
- Want formal political representation based on wealth/commercial contribution.
- Push for a "commercial chamber" alongside the scholarly Grand Council.
- Strongest in port cities and trade hubs.
- Motto: "Those who fund the nation should govern the nation."
- Weakness: Viewed as crass by scholarly establishment; lack examination legitimacy.

**4. Military Faction**
- Professional soldiers demanding recognition and self-governance.
- Want military career path equivalent to examination (merit through service, not test scores).
- Strongest in border provinces.
- Motto: "Those who bleed for the nation deserve its respect."
- Weakness: Smallest faction; scholars and merchants both want military subordinated.

**5. Underground Heretical Schools**
- Radical scholars who challenge examination system, knowledge restrictions, and institutional power.
- Some aligned with Covenant (unknowingly); some genuinely independent.
- Strongest in diaspora communities and among failed scholars.
- Motto: "Knowledge belongs to everyone."
- Weakness: Disorganized, paranoid (justifiably), infiltrated.

### Political Dynamics
- Traditionalists hold power but are losing legitimacy.
- Reformists have ideas but no coalition.
- Mercantilists have money but no mandate.
- Military has swords but no political tradition.
- Heretical Schools have energy but no structure.
- **Covenant strategy**: Fund all factions simultaneously. Ensure none achieves satisfying reform. Maintain creative gridlock that prevents adaptation.

## Religion & Philosophy

### Structural Realism (State Philosophy)
- Not a religion in the worship sense. More a philosophical framework that functions as civic religion.
- Core belief: The universe operates according to mathematical principles. Understanding these principles is the highest human activity.
- The examination system is simultaneously a political, educational, and spiritual institution.
- Scholars who achieve deep mathematical insight are considered to have touched the sacred.
- **Ritual**: Astronomical observation ceremonies at solstice/equinox. Mathematical demonstrations as liturgy. Public lectures as sermons.

### Sacred Geometry (Elite School)
- Subset of Structural Realism with more mystical emphasis.
- Believes mathematical beauty is evidence of divine design.
- The most elegant proof isn't just correct — it's holy.
- Practitioners create geometric mandalas (mathematical meditation tools) that also function as magical amplifiers.
- **Link to magic**: Sacred geometry directly informs Therryn's magical technique. Geometric formations aren't metaphors — they're engineering.

### Practical Ethics (Popular Philosophy)
- The commoner's philosophy. Less interested in cosmic geometry; more interested in how to live well.
- Emphasizes reciprocity, honest dealing, community responsibility.
- Borrowed heavily from Umaran relationship ethics (though scholars won't admit the influence).
- **Function**: Provides moral framework for people who'll never sit an examination and don't care about sacred geometry.

### Heretical Undercurrents
- **Empirical Materialism**: Claims mathematical models are tools, not truths. The universe doesn't "know" geometry — we impose it. Denied by establishment because it undermines the spiritual basis of the examination system.
- **Umaran Influence**: Growing interest in relational magic and oral tradition among younger scholars. Viewed as intellectual regression by the establishment.
- **Forbidden Techniques**: Mathematical frameworks can derive forbidden magical applications. Some scholars "independently discover" techniques that the Covenant has deliberately leaked.

## Conflicts

### Internal Conflicts

**1. Examination Crisis**
- The exam tests 400-year-old content. What made a good governor in Year 200 doesn't make one in Year 600.
- Most dangerous failure mode: Officials who score highest are often least effective because they've optimized for the test, not for governance.
- Proposed reforms are blocked by those who succeeded under the current system (and therefore believe it works).

**2. Merchant-Scholar Cold War**
- Not violent but intensely hostile. Fought through patronage withdrawal, strategic publication, social exclusion, and commercial pressure.
- Rising tension as merchants approach raw economic power sufficient to simply bypass the scholarly establishment (hire their own administrators, build their own schools, create parallel governance).
- If the merchants formally secede from scholarly authority, the empire fractures.

**3. Provincial Disconnection**
- Remote provinces effectively self-governing. Central directives arrive, are acknowledged, and are ignored.
- Provincial populations develop local traditions, local allegiances, and local solutions that don't match capital orthodoxy.
- The rotation system (governors moved every 3-5 years) means no official develops provincial loyalty — but also no official understands provincial needs.

**4. Military Discontent**
- Professional soldiers commanded by scholar-strategists who earned their positions through examinations, not combat.
- During the last border conflict, scholar-designed tactics failed because the mathematical models didn't account for terrain conditions the scholars had never visited.
- Growing mood among border garrisons: "We die for their theories."

### External Conflicts

**1. Valdimere Border Friction**
- Therryn's intellectual superiority claim vs. Valdimere's military superiority. Both nations believe the other is barbaric (Therryn = "crude empire of paperwork"; Valdimere = "ivory tower with no army").
- Trade benefits both, but espionage is constant. Valdimere wants Therryn's mathematical innovations; Therryn wants Valdimere's military intelligence.

**2. Shukei Isolation**
- Shukei's refusal to engage with Therryn's examination system is interpreted as intellectual cowardice.
- Therryn scholars who visit Shukei are baffled by the embodied magic tradition ("How can you practice what you can't prove?").
- Cultural contempt runs both ways: Shukei sees Therryn as producing scholars who can calculate a sword strike but not survive one.

**3. Umara Philosophical Challenge**
- Umaran relational magic works. This is philosophically threatening because it operates without mathematical framework.
- Therryn scholars either deny Umaran results ("anecdotal"), attempt to model them mathematically (forcing Umaran techniques into Therryn categories), or quietly study them (heretical).

**4. Covenant Penetration**
- Therryn is the Covenant's easiest target. Scholarly competition creates natural secrecy. Forbidden knowledge is irresistible to ambitious scholars. Failed scholars are desperate.
- The Covenant feeds mathematical frameworks that lead to forbidden techniques. The scholar "discovers" them independently. By the time they realize they're being manipulated, they're too compromised to report it.
