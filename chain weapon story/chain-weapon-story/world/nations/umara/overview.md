# Umara — National Overview

## Identity

Umara is not a nation in the conventional sense. It is a vast network of interconnected communities, trade routes, and kinship systems spanning savannahs, river deltas, and coastal forests. Where other nations define themselves by territory and government, Umara defines itself by relationships. Power is held by whoever maintains the densest, most trusted network of obligations.

**Real-World Inspiration**: Mali Empire + Ashanti Federation + Igbo village democracy + Somali xeer customary law + West African Dyula and Hausa trade diasporas.

## Geography

- **Core territory**: Savannah grasslands, seasonal river systems, and forested coastal regions. Climate varies from semi-arid north to tropical south.
- **Seasonal landscape**: The rainy season transforms the geography — rivers swell, grasslands bloom, spirit-wells activate. Dry season contracts everything. Umaran life is structured around this cycle more than any calendar.
- **Key features**: The Griot's Seat (no permanent capital — the governing council meets wherever the senior griots convene), the Spirit-Well Network (sacred aquifer points that concentrate Flux), the Trade Crossroads (market cities that serve as neutral meeting points for kinship networks).
- **Flux geology**: Seasonal rivers and ephemeral wetlands create spirit-wells — shallow aquifers where Flux concentrates during the rainy season and lies dormant otherwise. This seasonal flux behavior is why Umaran magic emphasizes relationship with environment rather than control over it.

## Core Values

1. **Relationships over hierarchy**: Your identity is defined by who you're connected to, not what title you hold. A person without community is not poor — they're incomplete.
2. **Reciprocal obligation**: Every gift creates a debt. Every debt creates a bond. The network of obligations IS the social structure.
3. **Memory as power**: The griots who remember the past control the narrative of the present. History is not written — it is spoken, performed, and lived.
4. **Adaptive pragmatism**: What works is good. What doesn't work should be changed. Umaran culture prizes results over theory.
5. **Communal surplus**: Wealth isn't what you accumulate — it's what you distribute. A rich person is one who has given the most.

## Government Structure

### No Central Authority
Umara has no emperor, no capital, no standing government. Instead, overlapping networks of authority maintain order:

### Authority Networks

**1. Elder Councils**
- Each community has a council of elders (age 50+). Decisions require consensus through discussion, not majority vote.
- Elders don't command — they facilitate. Their authority comes from demonstrated wisdom, not appointment.
- Multiple communities coordinating: elder councils send delegates to regional gatherings. Regional gatherings send delegates to seasonal congresses. It's consensus all the way up.
- **Limitation**: Extremely slow. A decision that affects 50 communities might take months of deliberation.

**2. Griots (Historians/Storytellers)**
- Griots hold cultural authority equal to or exceeding political authority.
- They maintain the oral history of every family, every community, every alliance, every betrayal.
- A griot who declares "this is how it happened" creates historical fact. Challenging a griot's account requires producing your own griot with a competing version.
- Griots also serve as mediators, diplomats, and intelligence gatherers (they travel between communities and collect information naturally).
- **Training**: Apprenticeship from childhood (age 5-6). Full griot status at 25-30 after demonstrating mastery of at least 200 genealogies and 50 historical narratives.

**3. Merchant Princes**
- Trade network leaders who accumulate influence through commercial success.
- They don't govern in the political sense but control resource flows that communities depend on.
- A merchant prince who withdraws trade from a community can collapse it economically without lifting a weapon.
- Their authority is economic; they need elder approval for political legitimacy.

**4. Spiritual Leaders**
- Custodians of spirit-wells, seasonal rituals, and environmental relationship.
- They authorize (or refuse) major magical workings, seasonal ceremonies, and land-use decisions.
- Spiritual authority intersects with but doesn't override elder, griot, or merchant authority.

### How Decisions Actually Get Made
In practice: Someone proposes. Elders discuss. Griots provide historical context ("The last time we tried this, it ended badly"). Merchant princes assess economic impact. Spiritual leaders check environmental/magical implications. If all networks agree, it happens. If any network disagrees, more discussion until consensus or abandonment.

**Result**: Very few bad decisions — but also very few decisions at all. The system's strength (thoroughness) is its weakness (speed).

## Military

### Structure
- No standing army. Warriors emerge from communities when needed and return to civilian life after.
- Military leadership: Whoever the community considers most capable in the specific conflict. Permanent military rank doesn't exist.
- Fighting style: Guerrilla warfare, ambush, terrain exploitation, strategic retreat. Umaran forces never win pitched battles against organized armies — they make pitched battles impossible.

### Strengths
- Decentralized, nearly impossible to destroy (no army HQ, no supply chain to cut)
- Intimate knowledge of local terrain
- Intelligence networks (griots, merchants) provide excellent information
- High individual initiative — each warrior makes tactical decisions independently

### Weaknesses
- Cannot project power (no logistics for expeditionary warfare)
- Coordination between communities requires days/weeks of messengers
- No siege capability
- No standardized training — quality varies wildly between communities
- Vulnerable to "divide and conquer" (Covenant can approach communities individually)

## Religion & Spirituality

### Animist-Relational Worldview
- The land, water, air, and seasons are not things to be used — they're relationships to be maintained.
- Spirit-wells are sacred sites where the boundary between human community and natural world is thinnest.
- Ancestor veneration: The dead remain part of the community. Their wisdom is accessed through griot narratives and seasonal rituals.
- No organized priesthood: Spiritual leadership is earned through demonstrated connection to land and community, not through institutional appointment.

### Seasonal Ceremonies
- **Rainy Season Opening** (Spirit-Well Awakening): Communities gather at spirit-wells as they activate with seasonal Flux. Rituals acknowledge the returning abundance and renew obligations to the land.
- **Midsummer Confluence**: The largest gathering — multiple communities meet at trade crossroads. History is recited, obligations settled, marriages arranged, alliances renewed.
- **Dry Season Preparation**: Communities deliberate on resource allocation for the scarce months. Surplus is redistributed. Debts are renegotiated.
- **Ancestor's Night** (Deep dry season): Smallest, most intimate. Each family recounts their genealogy to the youngest generation. Missing a single night is culturally devastating.

### Relationship to Death
- Death is not an ending but a transition within the relationship network. The dead remain obligated and are owed obligations.
- Funeral: Multi-day event. The community gathers to recount every obligation the deceased held — both owed and owing. These obligations transfer to surviving kin.
- A person who dies without community to remember them suffers the true death — not physical, but relational. This is why exile is the ultimate punishment.

## Magic Tradition: Relational/Entheogenic

### Core Approach
Magic emerges from relationships — with the land, with ancestors, with community, with Flux itself as a living system. Where Therryn calculates and Valdimere standardizes, Umara negotiates. A ritual isn't a formula — it's a conversation.

### Key Practices
- **Spirit-well communion**: Practitioners meditate at activated spirit-wells to attune to seasonal Flux patterns. Extended sessions produce visionary states used for diagnosis, prediction, and community decision-making.
- **Relational casting**: Magic performed by groups who share deep kinship bonds is significantly more powerful than solo casting. The bonds themselves become channels for Flux.
- **Environmental attunement**: Umaran practitioners adjust their techniques to match local Flux conditions rather than imposing standardized approaches. Same practitioner, different location = different technique.
- **Oral preservation**: Magical knowledge is transmitted through stories, songs, and apprenticeship — never written. This makes it resilient to institutional censorship but vulnerable to community destruction.

### Strengths
- Most adaptable tradition (no rigid framework to restrict improvisation)
- Community-empowered (group casting multiplies effectiveness)
- Ecologically integrated (practitioners sense environmental damage before other traditions detect it)

### Weaknesses
- Cannot be scaled through institutions (requires personal transmission)
- Vulnerable to community destruction (kill the community, kill the knowledge)
- Appears "primitive" to other traditions (no mathematical proof, no standardized formula)
- Difficult to verify claims (without written documentation, anyone can claim mastery)

## Current Crises

1. **Covenant community-targeting**: The Covenant approaches communities individually, offering aid, trade, or protection. Once obligated, communities are maneuvered into Covenant service through the very reciprocal-obligation system that defines Umaran culture. Using the culture against itself.
2. **Griot succession crisis**: Several senior griots died in the last decade (natural and suspicious causes). Their knowledge wasn't fully transmitted. Gaps are appearing in the historical record. Younger griots are reconstructing from fragments — introducing uncertainty into a system that depends on certainty.
3. **Trade route disruption**: Valdimere's border expansion and Federation tariff changes are cutting traditional trade routes. Merchant princes are losing leverage. Communities that depended on trade income are struggling.
4. **Refugee influx**: Displaced populations from Valdimere's frontier collapse and Shukei's internal conflicts are arriving without kinship networks. The obligation system isn't designed for people who arrive with nothing and no connections.
5. **Youth-elder tension**: Younger Umarans exposed to Therryn texts and Valdimere structures are questioning the consensus system. "Why do we need five months of discussion when Valdimere can decide in a day?" The answer is that Valdimere's decisions are often wrong — but speed is seductive.
