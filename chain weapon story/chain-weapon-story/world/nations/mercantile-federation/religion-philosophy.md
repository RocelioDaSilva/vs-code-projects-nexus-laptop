# Mercantile Federation — Religion & Philosophy

## Contractualism: The Civic Religion

### Core Doctrine
Not a religion — a philosophical framework that functions as civic religion. The Federation has no sacred text, no founding myth, no deity. It has the Compact.

**Central claim**: The contract is the only reliable social mechanism. Trust without contract is naive. Contract without enforcement is theater.

**Ethical framework**: A fulfilled contract is a moral act. A broken contract is an immoral act. The quality of the contract's terms is secondary to the act of fulfillment itself. This produces a society where procedural morality (did you follow the agreement?) replaces substantive morality (was the agreement just?).

### Four Philosophical Pillars

| Pillar | Meaning | Practice | Risk |
|--------|---------|----------|------|
| **Sovereignty** | Within contracted bounds, individuals are free | Contract negotiation as primary life skill | Freedom without power is meaningless |
| **Reciprocity** | Contracts bind both parties; obligations are mutual | Equal parties, equal terms (in theory) | Drafting advantage destroys equality |
| **Enforcement** | Agreements without consequences are worthless | Magical enforcement of all registered contracts | Enforcement without justice is tyranny |
| **Pragmatism** | What works matters more than what's ideal | Diverse canton governance; tolerance of difference | Moral relativism; no floor of decency |

### The Sacred Contract (Federal Compact)
- Treated with religious reverence
- Annual "Compact Day" celebrations: reading original text aloud, renewing oath-contracts, processing through Arbitration Hall
- Some citizens genuinely believe the Compact is divinely inspired
- Most view it as a pragmatic achievement deserving respect
- **Function**: The Compact is the Federation's creation myth, holy text, and constitution combined

## Traditional Religions (Canton-Variant)

### Pre-Compact Traditions
Before the Federation, each canton had local religious traditions:

| Canton Group | Tradition | Current Status |
|-------------|-----------|----------------|
| Coastal cantons (4) | Maritime folk religion; sea-spirits, storm-prayers, sailor blessings | Maintained alongside contractualism; weakening |
| Interior cantons (5) | Earth-and-harvest tradition; seasonal festivals, crop blessings | Integrated into canton culture; non-political |
| Border cantons (3) | Valdimerian-influenced ritual; ceremony-and-hierarchy | Largely replaced by contractualism |
| Free cantons (2) | Heterodox mix; religious pluralism as civic value | Thriving diversity; Freeport especially |

### Current Religious Landscape
- No state religion; contractualism functions as civic framework alongside private belief
- Canton governance determines religious tolerance (varies from permissive to restrictive)
- Religious guilds exist (Temple Guild, Healer's Covenant) — operate under standard guild contracts
- Foreign religions tolerated within contracted limits; proselytizing regulated

## Philosophical Schools

### Dominant: Strict Contractualism
- The contract is the contract. Terms are terms. If you signed, you agreed. Judging the fairness of freely-signed contracts is subjective, dangerous, and corrosive.
- **Adherents**: Patriciate, Arbitration Court majority, conservative guild masters
- **Practical effect**: Life Contracts are legal; exploitation is legal; the system is self-consistent but morally hollow

### Heterodox: Natural Rights Contractualism
- Contracts must comply with natural rights — rights that exist before and independent of any agreement. A contract that violates natural rights is void regardless of signatures.
- **Adherents**: Abolitionists, progressive guild reformers, some Arbitration Court judges
- **Problem**: What counts as "natural rights"? No consensus. But the argument provides moral vocabulary for reform.

### Radical: Anti-Contractualism (Freeport Philosophy)
- Contracts are instruments of power disguised as instruments of freedom. The powerful write contracts; the powerless sign them. Freedom comes from community, not paperwork.
- **Adherents**: Freeport intellectuals, escaped contract-bound, underground movements
- **Status**: Officially tolerated (within Freeport); socially marginal; intellectually influential among young people

## Values & Vices

### Cultural Values
| Value | Expression | Who Embodies It |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|
| Fair dealing | Honored contracts; transparent negotiation | Guild members (aspirationally) |
| Self-reliance | Individual achievement through personal effort | Merchants, artisans (practically) |
| Innovation | New methods, new products, new contracts | Freeport community (creatively) |
| Pluralism | Tolerance of difference within contracted framework | Federation culture (officially) |

### Cultural Vices (Unacknowledged)
| Vice | Expression | Who Exhibits It |
|------|-----------|-----------------|
| Procedural morality | "It was legal" as justification for exploitation | Patriciate, court system |
| Contract fetishism | Believing signed paperwork = genuine consent | Institutional culture |
| Social atomism | No obligation beyond contracts; community = weakness | Urban elite |
| Selective blindness | Not seeing contract-bound suffering because it's "legal" | All tiers above 5 |

## The Moral Crisis

The Federation's philosophical crisis is the contract-bound problem:
- **Strict contractualism** says: they signed; they're bound; the system works as designed
- **Natural rights** says: no person can contract away their fundamental freedom; Life Contracts are void
- **Anti-contractualism** says: the system itself is the problem; contracts ARE power structures

This isn't abstract philosophy — it's the lived experience of 10% of the population in permanent servitude. The Federation's ability to resolve this crisis determines whether it survives as a functional society or collapses into the thing it claims to prevent: rule by power without consent.
