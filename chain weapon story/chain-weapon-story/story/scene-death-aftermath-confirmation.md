Renard found him by the ash-stained hedge, where the earth had been disturbed into a little hollow like a fist.

He had followed scent and hesitation more than sight; the trail was a language of small betrayals—trampled grass, a dropped strap, the faint oily smear of a man's blood on stone. The cloth they had wrapped him in had been torn open by weather and movement; when Renard brushed it aside the face beneath was a map of the life he had lived. It was not the violent, cinematic ruin of ballads. It was the ordinary quietness of finality: breath gone, the line of the jaw slack, a depression where the chest had been mobile.

Renard did not howl. There was no sound in him that matched the size of the loss. He set his forehead to the man's temple and let the cold of the air press in like an answer. He smelled smoke and cordage and the faint, dry tang of iron. He breathed as if listening for something that would not come.

He went to find Mira. She stood at the edge of the field with eyes that had been rubbed raw by too much seeing. When Renard spoke the single word that meant 'here' in their shared language, she came like a ship to a harbor—quick, certain, and without fanfare. She looked at the body and the shape of her face changed in a way that was not private; grief had become a public grammar.

Kaelen was the one who came next, as he always did when the world demanded tallying. He arrived with maps folded under one arm and the look of a man prepared to write the list of what had been taken. When he knelt and pressed his hand to the man's sternum, it was in the practiced manner of someone looking for a miracle. He found none. The sound that left his throat was a small, private break.

They did the hard, unromantic work that proves absence. Renard closed the hands with careful fingers so that the body would not say things in the looseness of death. Mira closed eyes that would not open, smoothing the lids with a tenderness that seemed to resist the legislation of fate. Kaelen checked pockets for signs—letters, a token, anything that might be a proof to bring home. In the inner pocket they found a folded scrap of paper, edges burnt where heat had licked it. The handwriting was Aisen's, hurried and slanted.

It read: If this is what I am for, then let it be small and quick. Tell Mira—tell them—remember the brook. Forgive me.

Mira held the paper as if it might still move. She did not cry immediately; she made a sound like a tide retreating—quiet, inexorable. She folded the scrap and put it inside her own shirt so that, for a little while, the thing would be tucked to the body in the way people tuck names into their hearts.

The camp needed a formal act. There was always the worry that someone would not believe absence without a ceremony. Kaelen went to the tent and returned with a length of white cloth; it was the only thing he could find that looked ceremonial without being false. They wrapped the man's head with care and then unrolled a spare banner to lay beneath him. Renard brought a small handful of the earth from the hedge and let it fall into the grave-shaped hollow as if a gesture could make the shape legal.

When word reached the town, it moved like a cautious rumor at first, then a bell. Mira went into the market to tell the ones who had known and the ones who had known of him. She did not dramatize. She said, quietly, 'He is gone,' and the town received the sentence in a manner only towns can: someone wiped a counter, a baker lowered an oven lid, a child was pulled away from a game.

They made the plaque the way small communities make monuments—not with grand marble but with something honest. A piece of wood was cut, sanded, and burned at the edges. Mira carved letters into it with a knife so her hands would remember the angles as if memory could be held in the rhythm of carving. She nailed the plaque over the tavern door. When she hammered the last nail, the sound was a punctuation that did not need an author.

Renard watched as people came to put stones at the base of the tavern's door: a child left a small clay bird, an old woman pressed a sprig of lavender into the mud. The town's memory accumulates in little acts like that—shards of meaning laid like mortar. It was less than a shrine and more than a rumor.

Mira stood in the doorway once the plaque was up and watched the road. She had folded Aisen's last scrap of paper into her palm and kept it there. There were no grand words left to say. She breathed in the smell of wood and stew and the ordinary dust of the world and let it be enough for a time.

They had proof of absence now, and in that proof lay all the cruel work of living: to continue, to remember, to carry forward the small pledges that have weight enough to bear a day.
