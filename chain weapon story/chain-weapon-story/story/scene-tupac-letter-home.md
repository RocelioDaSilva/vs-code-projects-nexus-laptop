The rope lay coiled at his feet, and Tupac could feel the mountain in it.

His hands had learned two languages in the years since leaving: the highlandry dialect of knots he had carried from childhood, and the loose, practical lashing style of the lowlands where everything was sudden and required fast solutions. He had showed Aisen the difference once—how the highland knot held through pressure and how the lowland knot held through adaptation. Aisen had listened with the attentiveness of someone who understood that in technique lives philosophy.

Now, in a room that smelled of lamp oil and foreign paper, Tupac prepared a letter. The paper was thin, the kind that merchants used because it was cheaper. He preferred it; it felt honest in its limitations. He had learned to write during his first years at the lowland academy, a skill the highlands had not deemed necessary. Writing was for record-keepers, his family had said. The highlands kept their story in rope and song.

He began:

*To the Knotmasters of Tupac's Line* —

He paused. Formality felt wrong. He crossed it out and began again:

*To my mother:*

*I am well. The work is good and hard, and the man I travel with is someone you would respect, though he is not highland. He listens the way a rope-reader listens—paying attention to what is not said as much as what is. I think you would approve of him.*

*I have learned something in the lowlands that I did not expect. The lowland knots are not inferior to ours; they are simply different. They hold through change rather than through resistance. I have begun to think that perhaps the highlands and the lowlands are both right—that some truths require a knot that cannot slip, and some truths require a knot that can dance.*

He paused and listened to the street outside. A vendor called out prices; someone laughed at a distance. The lowlands were louder, more willing to announce themselves.

*I have been asked to bring lowland knowledge back to the highlands when my time here ends. I have said I would try. But I am uncertain whether the highlands will want what I have learned, or whether my own hands have become too accustomed to the loose knots to tie the old patterns correctly anymore.*

*I miss the mountains. I miss the way the silence there is particular and earned. In the lowlands, silence is only the absence of noise.*

*I have a friend who carries a chain at his throat. It is an inheritance from his father, and it ties him to the world in a way that rope does not. I wonder if I should carry something similar—a reminder of home that is also a weight.*

*I do not know if I will come back. The world is larger than I understood. I wanted to see it and I have seen it. But I do not know if seeing is the same as understanding.*

*If you are reading this, please know that I think of the high valleys often. I think of the way the rope-songs sound at dawn. I think of your hands teaching my hands to feel the tension before it breaks.*

*I am learning to tie knots that hold through change. I will try to bring that knowledge home, if home will have me.*

*Your son,*
*Tupac*

He sealed the letter without reading it again—if he read it, he would lose his nerve and make it smaller, safer. He found a courier and paid for it to be sent northward along the merchant roads, through three border towns and up into the high passes where the mountains began to speak their own language.

He picked up the rope again and ran it through his fingers in the highland pattern. His hands were slower than they used to be; the muscle memory was there, but it was layered over with lowland habits. He tied a knot—the old kind, the kind that held through resistance—and felt something in his chest ease slightly.

When Aisen found him later, sitting by the window with the coil of rope, he asked what Tupac was thinking about.

'Home,' Tupac said. 'And whether I can carry both homes at once.'

'You can,' Aisen said simply. 'You're carrying them now.'

Tupac smiled at that—at the quiet certainty. He wondered if Aisen had one home or many, or if perhaps there were people for whom home was less a place and more a way of tying things together so they wouldn't scatter.

He would find out in time. For now, the letter was on its way, and his hands remembered two languages, and that was enough.
