Father Aldous kept the refugee lists in two books.

The official book—leather-bound, stamped with the parish seal, stored in the vestry drawer where the quarterly inspector could find it—recorded names, dates of arrival, and services rendered: meals, medical attention, temporary bedding in the parish hall. This book was meticulous. The handwriting was small and regular, the columns aligned, the totals summed at the bottom of each page. It was the book of a man who understood that institutions survive through documentation and who had served the Church long enough to know that being beyond reproach required looking reproachable in precisely the right way.

The second book was hidden beneath the loose flagstone behind the confessional. It was smaller, ragged, bound in oilskin, and written in a cipher Aldous had devised from a combination of old liturgical shorthand and the vegetable inventory codes his mother had used at her market stall. This book recorded the other names—the ones who could not appear in the official ledger because their presence in the parish was, by the terms of the Covenant's Resettlement Directive, a crime.

Seven names this month. Three families. Two individuals traveling alone. One child who had arrived without adults and who would not speak and who slept under the altar and whom Aldous had listed in the official book as a *novice chorister in training*, which was technically accurate if you defined training as *teaching a mute child to hold a candle and stand near other children who can sing.*

He was forty-seven years old. He had been ordained at twenty-two. He had served three parishes, buried hundreds of parishioners, married hundreds more, baptized so many infants that he sometimes dreamed of water—not the holy kind, just water, rushing, as if a dam were giving way inside his sleep.

His hands bore the marks of his office. The right palm carried a scar from the ordination rite—a small cut made to symbolize the wound of service, healed imperfectly, a ridge of white tissue that pulled when he made a fist. He wore the mark the way a soldier wears a medal: visibly, without comment, daring anyone to ask.

Tonight the parish was quiet. The refugees were in the cellar—the real cellar, not the one on the building plans—eating the stew that Sister Margret prepared with the parish food stores plus the supplementary provisions that arrived, unsigned, from a sympathetic farmer three leagues east whose own family had been relocated two years ago. The child sat in the corner of the cellar with a blanket around their shoulders, holding a piece of bread with both hands like a document that might be revoked.

Aldous sat in the nave alone. The moon put squares of light on the floor through the high windows, and the silence was the kind that parishes accumulate over centuries—a silence made of prayers, not the absence of sound but the residue of it, as if every word ever spoken here had soaked into the stone and dried there.

He was reading the latest diocesan circular. It had arrived that morning, folded into thirds, sealed with the bishop's wax in the colour that meant *general distribution, no response required*—which was the diocese's way of issuing instructions it did not wish to defend.

*To all clergy of the Western Diocese,*

*In accordance with the Covenant's Spiritual Alignment Initiative, all parishes are directed to integrate the following into regular liturgical practice:*

*1. The Blessing of National Purpose (text attached) shall be recited at the conclusion of Morning Prayer, replacing the traditional communal benediction.*

*2. Parish registries shall include notation of congregants' Compliance Status (categories: Full, Provisional, Under Review). Clergy may consult their regional Covenant liaison for guidance on classification.*

*3. Seasonal festivals of non-standard cultural origin are to be reviewed for alignment with Covenant educational objectives. Festivals deemed inconsistent with national pedagogical goals may be suspended pending review.*

He read it twice. Then he folded it and set it on the pew beside him and pressed his scarred palm against his thigh until the ridge hurt.

The Blessing of National Purpose. He had seen a draft months ago—circulated informally among the clergy who paid attention to the shape of things before the shape became mandatory. It was a prayer that praised order, discipline, and the wisdom of governance. It did not mention God. It mentioned *the divine architecture of civil harmony*, which was not the same thing, in the same way that a window painted to look like sky is not the same as sky.

Compliance Status categories. *Full, Provisional, Under Review.* He imagined writing those words next to the names in his parish registry—next to Widow Marsh, who had attended Morning Prayer for thirty-one years and whose son had been conscripted and who, by any honest measure, was *Under Review* only in the sense that she had publicly wept during the conscription ceremony and called the recruiting officer a *butcher in a pressed coat.*

The festivals. The Harvest Blessing. The Spring Fires. The Midwinter Road, where the parish children walked between bonfires carrying candles and the oldest woman in the village spoke the names of the year's dead. These were not Covenant-inconsistent *festivals*—they were the muscle and cartilage that held the community to its own bones.

Aldous stood and walked to the altar. He knelt—not for prayer, not yet, but because his body needed to be lower, needed the stone against his knees, needed the ache that comes from kneeling on cold floor in a cold building in the middle of a night when no one is watching.

He had served the Church for twenty-five years. He had believed—genuinely, with the kind of faith that does not require evidence because it has already survived the absence of evidence—that the Church was a vessel. Imperfect, yes. Slow, yes. Prone to the corruptions of any human institution. But oriented, fundamentally, toward care. Toward the irreducible proposition that every person who walks through the door is deserving of shelter.

And now the diocese was telling him to categorize every person who walked through the door by their Compliance Status and to replace the communal benediction with a prayer to national purpose.

*When did it happen?* he thought. *When did the infiltration begin?* Not with this circular—this circular was the fruit, not the root. The root was older. The root was in the selection of bishops who were amenable, the appointment of diocesan administrators who were practical, the quiet reassignment of clergy who asked inconvenient questions to parishes far from the capital where their inconvenience would echo less.

He had watched it happen. That was the part that burned. He had watched it happen over years, noting each small concession as reasonable, each adjustment as pragmatic, each alignment as *temporary*, and now he was kneeling in a nave lit by moonlight with a circular that told him to classify his own congregants, and the cellar beneath his church held seven people whose names he had written in a hidden book in a cipher his mother taught him for counting vegetables.

The confessional booth stood in shadow at the nave's west end. He looked at it and thought about what it meant to sit inside it every week and hear people confess their sins while his own sin—the sin of *not yet acting*—grew heavier with each circular that arrived, each directive he read, each name he added to the hidden book without telling anyone except Sister Margret and the farmer three leagues east.

He rose from his knees. He picked up the diocesan circular. He carried it to the vestry, where the lamp was still burning, and he sat at his desk, and he did three things.

First, he wrote a response to the diocese, on official stationery, acknowledging receipt of the circular and noting that implementation would proceed as directed. This was lie number one.

Second, he opened the official refugee book and added a notation beside the mute child's entry: *Chorister training progressing well; scheduled for integration into Sunday choir.* This was lie number two.

Third, he pulled the loose flagstone behind the confessional and retrieved the hidden book and added, in his mother's cipher, the names of three people who would arrive tomorrow—forwarded to him by a contact in the next parish who had received them from a contact in the parish before that, a chain of clergy whose official books were immaculate and whose hidden books were filling page by page.

He replaced the flagstone. He returned to the nave. He knelt again, and this time he prayed—not the Blessing of National Purpose but the old benediction, the one the Covenant's circular had replaced, the one that began *The peace that passes understanding* and ended with a silence that was not strategic or structural but simply the sound of a man who has run out of words offering the space where words would have been.

In the cellar, the child finished the bread. Sister Margret tucked the blanket tighter and blew out the candle, and the dark received them the way Aldous's church had received them—without Compliance Status, without notation, without any category except *here.*
