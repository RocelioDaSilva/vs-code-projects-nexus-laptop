She came through the door sideways, as if the habit of making herself small had fused into her bones. The cane struck the threshold twice before she trusted the floor, and even then Lysine kept one hand pressed flat against the doorjamb, reading the wood with her palm the way blind people read faces.

Aisen watched from behind the counter where he was stacking dried cups. His father had already moved—a chair pulled out, a clean cloth laid across the table nearest the hearth—and the woman lowered herself into it with the careful economy of someone who has learned that sitting down costs more than standing.

"Broth," she said. Not a question. Not a request. Just a word dropped like a coin on the table, as though she had counted how many words she had left and was spending them with precision.

His father brought the broth. Aisen brought bread without being asked—he had noticed the tremor in her hands, the kind that came from skipped meals rather than age, and he cut the bread into pieces small enough that she wouldn't need both hands to eat.

She noticed. Her eyes came up and found him with the sharpness of someone accustomed to watching for threats in acts of kindness.

"Clever boy," she said. Not warmly. An observation.

He went back to his cups. But he kept watching. He was twelve and had already learned that the most interesting travelers were the ones who didn't want to talk, because when they finally did, the words had weight.

The broth disappeared in silence. She held the bowl with both hands after it was empty, letting the ceramic warm her fingers. Outside, the autumn wind threw handfuls of grit against the shutters, and the fire popped, and the tavern held its breath around her the way rooms sometimes do around people carrying invisible wounds.

"You're not from the valley," his father said, wiping down a table that was already clean. It was his way of standing nearby without hovering.

"No."

"Traveling through?"

"Traveling away."

His father nodded. That was enough for him—he understood *away* as a destination, had served enough travelers who were running from rather than toward. He refilled her broth without asking and moved to the other side of the room.

But Lysine kept looking at Aisen. He felt it the way you feel someone standing in a doorway behind you—not threatening, but deliberate.

"You cut the bread," she said.

"You were shaking."

"Most people pretend not to notice."

"Most people don't look at hands first."

Something shifted in her face. Not a smile—more like the place where a smile would have been, if she still had the energy for one. She reached beneath her cloak and drew out a pouch, setting it on the table with the gentleness of someone handling something alive.

"Do you know what veincap is?" she asked.

He shook his head.

"It grows in the cracks where flux seeps into limestone. A thumb-sized piece can reduce fever for three days. Two thumb-sized pieces, administered together, will stop a heart." She touched the pouch. "Everything that heals can harm. That's not philosophy—it's botany."

Aisen sat down across from her. He had a sense, the way he sometimes did, that this was a conversation worth the price of his father's mild disapproval for abandoning the cups.

"A man came to my dispensary in Thornfeld," Lysine said. She was not looking at him now. She was looking at the broth, at the small light the fire threw across its surface. "Not a soldier. Soldiers at least have the honesty of a uniform. This man wore a scholar's coat. He was very polite. He called me 'grandmother' and he brought flowers—autumn sage, which blooms at exactly the wrong time for gift-giving, which told me he had researched what healers value and gotten it slightly wrong."

She drew a breath the way people do before diving underwater.

"He said the Covenant was developing field protocols for combat medics. He said my research on nerve-thread mending had been cited in their literature. He said they wanted me to consult on a project that would save lives." She paused. "He said *save lives* the way a man says *fine weather* when it's raining. With absolute conviction that saying it could make it true."

"What did they actually want?"

"They wanted me to reverse-engineer my nerve-mending technique into a nerve-severing one. Targeted paralysis. A spell that could freeze a mage's casting hand without killing them—leave them alive but magically crippled." She lifted the broth bowl again, though it was empty, and held it like a shield. "They called it *humane restraint*."

The fire cracked. A log shifted. In the quiet, Aisen heard his father pause in his cleaning and then resume, as though he had decided that hearing this was its own kind of cost.

"I said no." Lysine set the bowl down. "I said no, and the polite man stopped being polite. Not immediately—he gave me a week. *To consider the opportunity.* And during that week, my dispensary was inspected three times. My herb stores were catalogued. A young woman I mentor was questioned about her immigration papers."

"Threatened."

"Efficiently. Without raising a single voice."

She opened the pouch. Inside were dozens of small cloth bundles, each tied with a different colored thread. She touched them the way Aisen had seen his father touch the keys to the tavern—the way you touch things that mean you still have a door to lock behind you.

"I left Thornfeld on the third night. Took what I could carry. Left the dispensary, the garden, the students I was teaching." Her voice flattened. "Twenty years of work. The bone-growth technique I developed for children with flux-warped spines. Gone, because I wouldn't give them a weapon."

"Is there—" Aisen stopped. He was going to ask if there was anyone who could help, some authority to appeal to, but the question dissolved before it reached his mouth. He was twelve, but he understood already that the question itself was naive. The Covenant *was* the authority.

Lysine watched him arrive at the answer without speaking it, and for the first time something like approval crossed her face.

"That's why I'm talking to you instead of your father," she said. "He would have asked the question. You knew not to."

She stood, slowly, gathered her pouch, leaned on her cane. At the door she paused and looked back at him.

"If they come here asking about an old woman with herbs, you never saw me." Then, softer: "Learn to cut bread for trembling hands, boy. The world will need that more than it needs swords."

She was gone into the grit and wind. Aisen sat for a long time at the table, looking at the empty bowl, the small crumbs where he'd cut the bread. His father came over and cleared the table without a word.

It was three years before Aisen understood what Lysine had been telling him—not about healing, not about the Covenant, but about the arithmetic of refusal. The equation that every person who says *no* must solve: what you lose, divided by what you save, multiplied by how long you can keep running.

He would see her again. But that was later, and she would be different, and so would he.
