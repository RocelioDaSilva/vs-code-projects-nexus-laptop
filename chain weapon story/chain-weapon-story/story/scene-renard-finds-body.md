The mist came first. Then the silence.

Renard had learned long ago that an ending was not quiet. It was loud with absence—the sound of voices that were no longer speaking, the noise of blood that no longer moved through veins. But this silence was different. It was the silence of purpose fulfilled.

He found Aisen in the clearing where they had made their final stand.

The body lay exactly where it should have—at the center of the devastation, surrounded by the wreckage of the Heresy's collapse. Aisen had not fallen like a hero in a story. He had fallen like a person who had finished the thing he meant to finish and then stopped, as simply as laying down a tool. His chain was coiled around him, still, the way it had been for twenty-three years.

Renard approached slowly. Not from fear—fear was behind him now—but from reverence.

The body was still warm. That was the difficult part—the warmth suggested that Aisen might still be somewhere in this arrangement of flesh and bone and blood, that consciousness might return if the right words were spoken or the right magic invoked. But Renard had seen enough death to know the difference between a body and a person. The warmth was just temperature leaving slowly. The silence was absolute.

He knelt beside Aisen and did what needed to be done: he closed the eyes.

Aisen's eyes had always been the thing you noticed first. They held multitudes—every observation he had ever made, every small detail he had noticed, every failure and choice written somewhere in the iris. Renard had seen those eyes shut in sleep, in concentration, in the moment before a strike. He had never seen them shut in death.

He did it gently. The eyelids were surprisingly soft.

"You said you were not special," Renard whispered. It was the first sound he had made since the final strike. His voice was rough with disuse. "You were wrong about that. You were special in the truest way—you chose to try. You chose, again and again, when the easier choice was to stop choosing."

Renard reached for the chain. His fingers found a broken link—the one that had been severed in the last exchange with the Archdemon, the moment when Aisen had disconnected the final piece of his power and given everything away. Renard worked it free. The steel came loose from the tangle with a small sound, a whisper of friction.

He held it up to the light that was not quite light in the mist. The link was still whole despite the break. That was what Renard would remember: that even the broken parts held shape. Even severed from the whole, the link stayed itself.

He tucked it into the pouch at his belt. A token. A promise.

Aisen's body was cooling now. Renard could feel the shift—the warmth had departed, leaving only flesh. The person was gone completely. There was nothing here but the architecture of a boy who had decided, once, that he could make a difference with small edges. The architecture was empty now.

The beast came out of the mist. It had stayed back through the final moments—a promise kept—but it could not stay away anymore. The wolf form that was Renard's companion nuzzled the body once, carefully, the way an animal touched something precious and fragile. It made a small sound in its throat. Not grief, exactly. The beasts did not experience emotion the way humans did. But recognition. Acknowledgment. The sound of one creature saying goodbye to another.

Renard reached down and picked Aisen up.

He was lighter than Renard had expected. That was the shock of death—how little weight a person's life actually carried. Twenty-three years of choices, and in the end, the body was just mass that could be shifted from one place to another.

Renard carried him to the edge of the clearing, where the mist thickened into something almost solid. The beast padded alongside, keeping pace with a companion's burden.

"I will tell them," Renard said. It was not clear to whom he was speaking—Aisen, the earth, the mist itself. "I will tell them what you were. Not the story that the powerful will tell. The true one. The one about a boy who learned to see, and who paid the price for seeing, and who decided that was not a reason to stop."

The mist received them at the boundary of the clearing—Renard carrying the body, the beast walking close, the broken chain link heavy against Renard's ribs.

They walked into the white, and did not turn back.

Behind them, the clearing lay empty. The mist settled over the place where Aisen had fallen, and within moments, the surface was smooth again, featureless, without memory. But Renard carried the memory now. The beast carried it. All those who had fought, and all those who would survive to tell what had happened.

The body would be given to fire, to the old beast-kin rite that honored the dead by returning them to the elements they had moved through in life. Aisen had moved through air and earth, through water when crossing rivers, through the fires of conflict. He would be returned to all of them.

But first, Renard would carry him through the mist. First, Renard would keep the promise—to bear witness, to remember, to tell the story of a person who had chosen his own way.

The mist swallowed them completely.

And in the clearing, there was only silence, and the slow settling of ash, and the cold that comes after fire, when everything has been burned and there is nothing left but the knowledge that something extraordinary had happened here. Something that had changed the shape of the world, even if nobody built monuments to it.

That would be enough.

Aisen had always believed small was enough.
