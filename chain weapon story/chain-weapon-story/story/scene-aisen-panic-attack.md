They had made it out. That was the important fact. They had made it out of the compound with the documents before the guards had locked down the inner courtyard. They had made it across the bridge before the alarm had reached full intensity. They had made it to the safe house with no pursuit.

Aisen had done this correctly.

So why could he not breathe?

He was sitting in the safe house kitchen—Pieter was pouring water, was asking if he was hurt, was doing all the things people did after danger had passed. Aisen heard the words. He understood them. But the connection between understanding and response had been severed.

His breath was coming in short, sharp gasps. Not panting. Something sharper. Something locked.

"Aisen?" Pieter was closer now. He had set down the water. "Aisen, you're not hurt. Look. No blood. Nothing is broken."

*Nothing is broken*, Aisen repeated to himself. That was true. That was logical. That was completely insufficient.

His hands were shaking. Not the small tremor of adrenaline. The kind of shaking that felt like his own body had become an animal separate from his control. He pressed his hands on the table. The wood was real. That was good. Real was something he could hold onto.

"It's okay," Pieter said. "You're okay. We made it."

*We made it*, Aisen repeated. Made it. Made it past the guards. Made it past the dogs. Made it past the moment where everything should have collapsed and did not.

But something had collapsed.

His breath came faster. His vision was narrowing. The room was becoming smaller, the walls closer. The safe house was called a safe house but it was a room. A small room. With only one exit. And that exit could be sealed. And if it was sealed, he would be trapped, and the guards would find him, and there would be no chain fast enough, no calculation precise enough, no edge sharp enough to survive what would come next.

He was going to die. Not now, but eventually. Everyone died. The successful ones died later, but they still died. And between now and then was an infinite expanse of waiting and watching and the constant knowledge that someone, somewhere, was trying to kill him, and failure was not a lesson, failure was permanent.

He could not breathe.

His hands pressed harder on the table. The wood was real. The pressure was real. He was still in the room. That was still true. But he could not access the breath that came before the panic. He could only access the panic itself.

"Aisen." Pieter's hand on his shoulder. "Look at me."

Aisen looked. Pieter's face was close. Pieter was real. That was true.

"Your breath," Pieter said. "Follow mine. In. Hold. Out. Yes? In. Hold. Out."

Pieter was breathing deliberately. Exaggerating the motion. Showing him that breathing was possible. That the air still existed. That his lungs could still receive it.

Aisen tried. The breath caught. It was like trying to drink water that refused to go down.

"Again," Pieter said. "It will pass. Just breathe. Nothing is happening right now."

*Nothing is happening right now.* That was true. The guards were not in the room. The alarm had stopped hours ago. The danger had passed. He was safe.

But his body had not received that information.

His breath came faster. Faster. The world was contracting. His vision was bright at the edges and dark at the center. He was going to die. Not right now, but the knowledge that death was coming—that his body was failing him—that the walls were too close—

Pieter pulled him forward. Held him. Did not let him pull away.

"Breathe," Pieter said against his ear. "Breathe. I am here. You are safe. Breathe."

Slowly. Impossibly slowly. The breath came.

It was thin at first. Insufficient. But it was breath. He pulled another. Then another. His heart was still moving too fast. His hands were still shaking. But the world was no longer contracting.

It took a very long time for him to stop shaking. Longer still before he could move without feeling like his body was a thing happening to him rather than something he controlled.

When he could finally speak, his voice was barely audible.

"Thank you," he said.

"You do not need to thank me," Pieter said. They were still holding each other, in the way that soldiers learn to hold each other after fear. "This happens. It will happen again. It is not weakness."

"It feels like weakness," Aisen said.

"It is survival," Pieter corrected. "Your body is telling you that you made it through something impossible. Of course it is breaking down now. There is no one to fight anymore. There is no problem to solve. There is nothing but the aftermath, and your body is processing it the only way it knows how."

Aisen wanted to believe this. He wanted to accept that panic was a natural response, not a failure. He wanted to integrate this moment into his understanding of himself as a functional human being rather than a breaking machine.

He could not quite manage it.

But he breathed. And Pieter stayed. And eventually the night passed, and Aisen was still alive, which meant the panic had passed the way all things pass—not because they were solved, but because time moved forward regardless.

In the morning, Aisen would not speak of this. In the morning, he would file it away with all the other impossible things he carried.

But tonight, for a moment, he allowed himself to be held by someone who understood that survival sometimes meant breaking down.

And that was, perhaps, its own kind of survival.
