# THE PRICE OF KNOWING

Aisen noticed the pattern on a Tuesday.

It was not obvious—patterns that were obvious were meant to be seen. This one revealed itself only to someone practiced at looking for things larger than themselves. The kind of looking that had kept him alive when stronger people had died.

**The Pattern:**

First: A scribe at the Academy approached him asking about his power source. Casual question, wrapped in academic interest. "I'm studying Flux applications. Do you mind if I observe your technique?"

Second: A guard at the city garrison saluted him in the merchant district. Not remarkable. Except this guard had no reason to recognize him. Aisen had never been to the garrison.

Third: A merchant he had never met offered him a discount. "You look like someone who appreciates good value." Merchants offered discounts to repeat customers, not strangers.

Any one of these was forgettable. Together, they formed a picture: he was being mapped.

---

## The Analysis (Bastard's Lens)

Aisen sat alone in a borrowed room and let his mind work through what his instincts already knew.

**The Rule:** The heresy enforcement system operates through informant networks. Investigators don't act alone; they identify suspects, observe patterns, position assets to gather intelligence.

**The Weakness:** Informants need cover. They can't reveal themselves until they have enough data to move. Which means there's a window—a period where you know you're being watched but the watchers don't know you know.

**The Cost:** Staying silent means playing ignorant. Playing ignorant means he'll continue to be exposed to the investigation. Eventually, something will be found or fabricated. Staying silent buys time, but time has limits.

Speaking up—warning people he knows—means admitting he's been flagged. It accelerates the timeline. But it gives people a choice: flee, hide, prepare.

**Tell:** The question isn't whether to tell people he's hunted. The question is who gets to know, what do they get to do with that knowledge, and what does it cost them to know?

## The Encounter

He found Pieter at the market, doing what Pieter always did: running calculations on profit margins for trade goods, completely unaware that he was about to become collateral in a larger game.

"We need to walk," Aisen said quietly.

Pieter looked up from his ledger. He was financially brilliant in ways Aisen would never be, but he understood immediately what the tone meant: this wasn't casual.

They walked through the market, moving without pattern. Not heading anywhere specific. Just moving through crowds so that anyone tracking them couldn't predict where they'd be in five minutes.

"I'm being investigated," Aisen said. "Informant networks. I probably have three to four weeks before they move. I wanted you to know so you could decide if you wanted to continue being around me."

Pieter didn't speak for a long time.

Then: "What did you do?"

"Nothing yet. I exist. I'm an anomaly they don't understand."

"Am I part of the investigation?"

"Not yet. But I am. Which means my associations might be scrutinized."

Pieter stopped walking. Aisen stopped with him. They were at a fruit vendor's stall. Normal. Unremarkable.

"I'm staying," Pieter said. "Whatever comes next, I'm staying."

"You should leave the city."

"You're not leaving."

"I don't have anywhere to go."

"Neither do I," Pieter said simply. "So if you're staying, I'm staying. We figure it out together or we figure it out separately in the same city. Either way, I'm not running."

Aisen recognized this as courage, which was sometimes indistinguishable from foolishness.

"They might execute you," Aisen said.

"They might. Or they might not. Either way, I'm choosing now. You're choosing now. We make the choice together."

## The Spread

Word spread like water finding cracks. By the next week, everyone Aisen trusted knew: he was flagged. The heresy hunters had him in their sights.

Ren arrived at the safe house with her weapons already packed. "I'm ready when you are."

Tupac showed up with questions: "Can I help? Anything you need? Contacts, supplies, exit routes?"

Amara called a meeting and walked him through the network, the protocols, the way information moved through the city below the level of what most people could see.

And Aisen realized something he should have understood earlier: being hunted wasn't isolation. It was connection. The moment your danger became known, a certain kind of loyalty crystallized around you. People who decided that your risk mattered more than their safety.

He did not ask them to make this choice. But once they made it, he had to respect it.

---

## The Realization

The night before the planned meeting with Amara, Aisen sat with his chain and thought about what Harald had taught him years ago, teaching him to ask questions instead of accepting answers.

**What is the rule?** 

The rule was: heretics are hunted to extinction. Anomalies are threats. Threats are eliminated.

**What breaks it?**

What breaks it is a network of people deciding that one person's right to exist matters more than the system's need for certainty. A network of people choosing to protect instead of report.

**Who pays?**

The administrator would pay. Maybe the people around Aisen would pay. Maybe Aisen himself would pay. The cost would be distributed across however much the system decided to spend to maintain control.

**Who needs to know?**

Everyone Aisen touched needed to know, because they were all becoming part of this story. And stories belong to everyone in them.

His chain coiled itself in his hands. Five loops. Clean. Perfect.

By tomorrow, the network would be in motion. People he'd never met would be making decisions based on intelligence about him. Informants would be comparing his movements to charts and predictions. The machinery of heresy enforcement would be identifying him as a priority.

And he would have to decide: was he going to hide, or was he going to lean into the role the system had decided he would play?

The answer, he realized, had already been made. The moment the investigations started, the moment people decided to stand beside him, the moment the pattern became visible—he had already chosen. He wasn't running.

He was going to walk directly into their targeting and see if they had the courage to do what they thought they believed.

And if they didn't, if they blinked first, then maybe the system wasn't as absolute as everyone feared.

Maybe rules, like all things, could break.
