Three in the morning.

The city had a different sound at three in the morning. Not silence—silence was for places that no longer held people. But the sound of a city sleeping. The difference between noise and rhythm.

Aisen had given up on sleep at midnight, had pretended for two hours, and had finally accepted the futility of it. His mind was cataloging. His body was too attuned to danger to allow unconsciousness. So he walked.

The streets were empty. That was the point of three in the morning—the world was less populated. Fewer people to see him. Fewer variables. The clean geometry of a city with its people removed.

He passed a tavern where light still moved behind closed windows. Drinkers who had not yet accepted the need for sleep. He understood them. Understanding the weakness of others was sometimes easier than understanding his own.

A cat watched him from a rooftop. In the darkness, Aisen could barely see it—just the shape of attention, the awareness of something moving below. At three in the morning, animals and solitary humans held the city. Everyone else had surrendered to the need for rest.

He found himself at the market, even though the market was closed. The stalls were empty, the day's goods cleared or spoiled. He stood in the center of the empty square and thought about nothing in particular. That was the gift of three in the morning—the permission to be without productivity. At three in the morning, even thinking seemed optional.

A memory surfaced:

His father, waking him at three in the morning (could not have been, Aisen was eight, his father would have been alive, but the dream-logic of sleep-deprived memory accepted it).

"Come," his father had said. "I want to show you something."

They had walked to the river. It was spring. The water was high from melting snow. His father had pointed to the way the moonlight moved across the current—the pattern of light fragmenting and reforming, never the same twice, but always itself.

"That," his father had said, "is what the world is. Ice melts into water. Water moves over stone. Stone becomes soil. Soil grows things. Nothing stays. Everything changes. And that is not sad. That is just true."

Had that memory actually happened? Aisen could not be certain. But it felt true in a way that mattered more than factual accuracy.

He sat down on the edge of a stall. The wood was cold. That was real. His breath fogged in the air. That was real. The city was asleep around him, and he was awake, and at three in the morning, that seemed like an acceptable way to exist.

A figure passed on the opposite edge of the square. Also awake. Also moving through the city at the hour when the rules were relaxed. They did not acknowledge each other. At three in the morning, there was a treaty—the people who were awake did not look at each other, did not ask questions, did not inhabit the same world as those who slept.

It was an hour for ghosts and soldiers and the people who had forgotten how to rest.

Aisen was all three.

He sat until the city began to change. At around four, the first bakers started their ovens. The first deliveries began. The night was ending. The day was coming. By five, the city would be waking. By six, the rhythm would return.

But at three in the morning, for one perfect hour, the world had belonged to the people who were awake. And Aisen had not been alone, because solitude at three in the morning was not quite the same as solitude at noon. It was a shared vulnerability. A collective admission that sleep was not always available, and consciousness had to be worn like a garment whether you wanted it or not.

He walked back to his room as the city began to wake. By the time dawn broke, he would be exhausted enough to collapse into a few hours of unconsciousness. By the time the work day began, he would have enough of a buffer to function.

He had learned to survive on this rhythm. Day that required alertness. Night that required lying still. And the hours in between, when sleep refused and the city was kind enough to let him walk without being questioned.

At three in the morning, Aisen was not alone.

At dawn, he would be alone again.

But the hour between night and day was his—a space where the normal rules did not apply, and a man could simply walk, and think, and accept that some types of rest did not require sleep.
