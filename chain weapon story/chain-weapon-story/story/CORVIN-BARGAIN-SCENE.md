# CORVIN: THE BARGAIN SCENE

## A Dream of Voices and Prices

Corvin was dying in the cave when the voice found him.

Not the voice of the beast—that came later, with howling and the taste of blood. This voice came first, in the space between breathing and not breathing. It came from everywhere and nowhere, as if the darkness itself had learned to speak.

*"You called,"* it said. *"Not with words. With desperation. With the need to survive something that wants you dead. I heard you."*

Corvin's vision was fragmentary. The death dog's fangs had torn through his arm. His blood was spreading across the stone floor in dark pools that reflected nothing. The creature hovered above him, confused by its own hunger—not beast-hunger, but something more complex. Recognition, maybe. Or kinship.

*"What are you?"* Corvin managed to ask.

*"An old thing. A thing that was here before the settlements came. A thing that wants to be useful again. I can save you."*

*"Why would you?"*

The voice seemed to consider this.

*"Because you are like me—bound to something you did not choose. And because the princess you were guarding will die in three days if you are not alive to prevent it. The Vex garrison has decided she has become inconvenient. She is scheduled for a hunting accident. Your death makes this easier for them."*

Corvin's vision cleared slightly. That part he believed. He understood the mechanisms of noble courts. The princess had made an enemy—not intentionally, but through the simple fact of existing in a way her uncle found threatening.

*"Save me, then,"* Corvin said.

*"The price."*

*"I have nothing. No wealth. My sword is my only—"*

*"I do not want your sword."*

The voice drew closer. In his fever-state, Corvin thought he saw the shape of it—vast, three-dimensional, existing in too many directions at once. The death dog stopped its circling. It seemed to recognize the presence, almost to submit to it.

*"What do you want?"* Corvin asked.

*"An equivalent exchange. That is how power works in this world. The Weave demands balance."*

*"I don't have anything equivalent to my life."*

*"Everyone does. You are simply not thinking creatively."*

The voice spiraled closer. Corvin could smell it now—not unpleasant, like stone water and electricity.

*"Your service, then. I will save you. Fuse you with this creature. Make you whole. And you will serve me—not in any way you can imagine yet, but you will know when the time comes."*

*"That's a life sentence."*

*"Yes."*

*"Why would I accept that?"*

The voice paused. The death dog made a sound—not threatening, but almost gentle. A rumble that seemed to come from the earth itself.

*"Because the princess will die otherwise. And you will have to live knowing you could have saved her."*

Corvin's hand was slick with blood. He could feel his heartbeat weakening. The fever was taking hold. In another hour, maybe two, he would be gone.

*"I accept,"* he said.

*"The binding requires a price. Not service. Something immediate. Something that balances the save immediately, not in the future."*

*"What do you want?"*

*"What do you hold most precious?"*

Corvin did not think. The answer came without negotiation.

*"The princess."*

*"Yes. That."*

*"Take it."*

*"Not a metaphor, Corvin. Not her life. Not her existence. What you hold of her. What you carry that makes your heart accelerate when she enters a room. What makes you willing to die defending her even when it gains you nothing."*

Corvin understood. The knowledge came with a sharp, terrible clarity.

*"You want me to stop loving her."*

*"Not your love. Your *feeling* of it. I want the capacity for that particular connection severed. You will remember that you loved her. You will know intellectually that she is precious. But the heart-deep knowledge, the visceral certainty that she matters more than your own survival—that will be gone. The trade is fair. You live. The princess lives. The debt is paid by losing the one thing that makes you human in that regard."*

Corvin felt his pulse. So weak now. Like a bird's heartbeat.

*"And after? When the service is done? What happens then?"*

*"You will be something different. Not human. Not beast. Something that walks between, that carries the death dog's nature and your own. The feeling will not return. You will live without it. You will be very, very good at protecting things you do not love. That is useful."*

*"Forever?"*

*"Yes."*

Corvin opened his mouth to speak, but no words came. Instead, he felt it happen—a tearing sensation like cloth splitting, like something sharp being drawn out of his chest. It was not painful in the way the beast's fangs were painful. It was painful in the way amputation is painful: the awareness that something essential has been removed, and will not be returning.

He felt the moment it left him. The way his heart had always quickened when she entered a room—gone. The way his chest had clenched when he saw her in danger—gone. The reason he had wanted to protect her above all other things—transformed into duty, into job, into function.

The love and the feeling of loving remained, but disconnected. Like a beautiful painting he could observe but not enter.

*"It is done,"* the voice said.

The death dog moved forward. Corvin felt its teeth close around him—not to kill, but to hold. He felt the fusion beginning, felt the creature's body accepting his own, felt the boundaries between them becoming negotiable.

He woke up screaming his throat raw.

## Waking to a New Body

When Corvin's eyes opened, he was on stone. Real stone, not dream-stone. He could feel the texture of rock beneath his palms. The cave smelled like copper and animal.

The death dog—no longer separate from him—moved internally. He could feel it there, vast and full of hunger, but not hostile. Negotiating. They occupied the same space like two people in a very small room who have agreed to an uncomfortable truce.

His arm had stopped bleeding. The wounds were still there, but sealed somehow, the death dog's biology knitting with his own in ways he did not have words for. He looked down at his hands and saw the fur beginning to grow on the backs of them—not human, not beast, but something between.

He tried to feel grief. The voice had said he would know what was missing. But all he found was a hollow space where the panic used to live. He had loved the princess. He knew that. The knowledge sat in his mind like a fact from a book he had read: *The capital of Vex is Thenhold. The princess is precious. The rain falls downward.*

True facts with no emotional weight.

He stood—surprisingly easy, his new body knowing how to balance—and walked toward the cave entrance.

The sunlight was almost unbearable. The world had too many colors, too many edges. The death dog's senses were keener than his own, could see heat-traces in the air, could smell the staleness of yesterday's wind.

He walked down the mountainside toward the settlement. No one recognized him. He looked human enough at a glance, the fur hidden under his armor, but there was something off about the way he moved. The way his eyes reflected light when the sun caught them the right way.

Three days later, he found the princess.

She was being prepared for a hunting accident—a horse to be spooked, a fall arranged to look natural. Corvin moved with his new body's speed, intercepted the conspirators before they could move, made it clear that such plans would no longer be tolerated.

The princess looked at him with confusion and something like fear.

*"You're different,"* she said.

*"I was very nearly dead."*

*"Your eyes—"*

*"Are healthier now, I think."*

She studied him. He felt the old reflex trying to activate—the heart-quickening that told him she was worth protecting. But the reflex was broken. He protected her now because it was his purpose, not because it mattered to him.

This made him better at it, he discovered. He could think clearly around her. He could make decisions without emotion clouding them. He could be reliable in ways he had not been before.

The princess called him her hero. He corrected her: he was her function. But he said it quietly, and she did not press.

Years passed. He protected her. He watched her grow clever and fierce. He saw her consider him with affection and eventually, as time went on, with something like love.

And he felt nothing about this except understanding—this was how it had to be. He had paid the price. This was the consequence of survival.

The death dog inside him was never quite satisfied. It wanted to run, to hunt, to exist as itself. But they had reached an accommodation. In sleep, it could have its dreams. In life, they moved as one.

He was a very good protector. The princess was never threatened again.

And Corvin lived on, hollow and perfect, asking for nothing, expecting nothing, becoming a man made entirely of duty.

She whispered that she loved him once, before the war took him to the borders.

He understood the meaning of the words. He simply could not feel them.

*"I know,"* he said, and meant only the words.
