Wei watched the boy work.

His name was Kess, and he had been born in the lowlands to a metalworker and a textile merchant—nobody important by the old measures, which meant he would have never been taught here. The Academy that had trained Wei had been ruthless in its sorting: talent identified early, bloodlines examined, aptitudes tested, the majority discarded before they learned enough to be dangerous.

That Academy was dust now.

This one—the Reformed Academy that had risen from its ashes—was different. It still tested for aptitude, but it tested everyone who came through the doors. Kess had shown dual competency in both kinetics and chains, which was rare. Wei had accepted him into his cohort six months ago.

Now Wei stood at the edge of the practice yard and watched the boy move through a basic chain configuration: spiral bind, loop turn, extension snap. The movements were clumsy—hesitant, overcorrected. But the intention was there. The boy was thinking about what the chain wanted instead of forcing it to obey.

That had taken Wei forty years to learn.

"Again," Wei called. "But this time, feel the coil before you throw it. Don't snap. Invite."

Kess reeled the chain back and reset his stance. He was sweating—the sun was vicious today—but he did not ask for water or rest. That was either stubbornness or discipline, and Wei had learned long ago that the difference between them was time.

The boy moved. Chain flew. It caught the wooden post dead center, wrapped, locked. Not perfect, but better. The tell was in the wrist—less violence, more suggestion.

"Better," Wei said. Then: "Worse."

Kess looked at him, confused.

"The first time, you were fighting the chain," Wei continued. "This time, you are cooperating with it. But you are not yet listening to what it wants to do. There is a difference between those things. A conversation is different from a negotiation."

Kess nodded slowly. He was learning. Most of them did, eventually.

Wei had been the one to survive the old Academy. Not through brilliance—Wei was talented but not transcendent. He had survived through consistency, and careful choices, and the willingness to nod along with institutional cruelty while secretly documenting it. He had been the one who took notes. The one who survived long enough to testify when the Reformation came.

For that, he had been offered a choice: retire, and be spared the reconstruction. Or stay, and build something new.

He should have retired. Should have taken his compensation and lived quietly somewhere. But he had remembered Aisen—the boy who had come under his tutelage years ago, who had asked difficult questions and who had absorbed the principle that good teaching meant showing a student your own limitations.

Aisen had died for this. For the possibility of what Wei was now watching: a common boy, sweating in the sun, learning to speak to a chain as an equal instead of a tool.

"Come," Wei said when Kess had completed another cycle. "Sit."

They sat in the shadow of the pillars. Wei poured water from a ceramic jug—the small courtesies that mattered. The boy drank gratefully.

"You are wondering," Wei said, "if you are good enough."

Kess made a small noise of protest, but it was false.

"You will wonder this your entire life," Wei continued. "The question does not have an answer. Goodness is not a destination. It is a direction. You are moving. That is enough."

"Master Wei," Kess said carefully, "the other students talk about him. About the one who—" He stopped.

"Aisen."

"Yes. They say he was perfect. That he could do things no one else could. That he was—" Kess struggled for the word. "—special."

Wei smiled. It was a small, tired smile.

"Aisen was many things," Wei said. "He was observant. He was precise. He was braver than he believed himself to be. But he was not special. He was a person who had learned to see what others overlook. And he used that seeing to try to change things."

"Did he succeed?"

Wei thought about the Academy reforming. About the systems of oppression cracking open step by step. About the students like Kess now being taught instead of discarded. About the night Aisen had died, and the message that had come the next day: *The work continues.*

"Yes," Wei said. "And no. He died believing he had failed. But the failure was the cost of the change. That is what change requires. Someone has to be willing to break themselves against the wall. Aisen was willing. So the wall cracked."

Kess sat with that. He was young enough that he thought in straight lines—success and failure, achievement and loss. It would take him years to understand that they were the same thing, that the people who changed the world were the ones who could accept being broken by it.

"Do you think," Kess asked quietly, "that I could ever be as good as he was?"

Wei looked at the boy. At his dust-covered knees, his calloused hands, his earnest face still uncertain of its own worth. And Wei thought about the one thing he had learned from Aisen that he had never been taught in the old Academy—the principle of elevation.

"Be better," Wei said. "That's what he would have wanted."

Kess blinked. "Better? But he was—"

"He was a man who died believing that small edges were all anyone had. He was wrong. You have something he didn't. You have his example. You have the knowledge that the wall cracked because he was willing to break. And you have systems changed because others are now building on what he began." Wei stood, offering his hand to help the boy up. "Aisen looked at a chain and saw a weapon. You can look at it and see something else. Call that better. Call it what comes next."

They returned to the practice yard. The sun was beginning to lower, casting everything in orange light that made the world look like it was burning from within.

"Again," Wei said. "And this time, think about what you would tell the next person who comes after you. What insight would make their learning faster? What mistake would you want them to avoid? Teach the chain the way you would want to be taught."

Kess moved through the configuration again. He was not perfect. But he was thinking now about something larger than his own competence. He was thinking about legacy.

That was what Aisen had learned to do.

That was enough.
