Summary:

Kess tapped a rhythm on the wooden crate with a thumb that bore a permanent smear of ash. He had the air of someone who measured days by tasks: scrape pan, stack bread, fetch water. Tonight, his shoulders were set against the thought that he would always be the one to wipe the table while others left for the Academy.

"They told me I shouldn't bother applying," Kess said, voice small but edged. He stirred the stew with a long spoon as if to make the words settle into the pot. "Said I had no papers, no pedigree. My ma said it's because we don't own land."

Aisen, wiping a plate, looked at him with an expression that was careful and slow, like someone reading the grain of a board. "You could learn a trade," he suggested, because trade was the thing a tavern boy thought to offer as comfort.

Kess snorted. "Trade's for those who can apprentice. You need someone to take you in. Who takes in a boy with no coin, no kin, and an accent from the low-side? The Academy—they asked for letters and coins and recommendations. I had none."

He shifted his weight and glanced at the doorway where Thorne had been earlier, where rich travelers sometimes left crumbs of advice or a single silver coin behind. "My friend Joren—he tried the interviews. Came back with his head down. Said the judges asked what his family did and then smiled and said, 'We're looking for other qualities.' It's polite for 'you don't belong here.'"

Aisen considered this. The boy's observation felt like a small machine whirring to life inside him: how systems gatekeep and who they allow to cross thresholds.

Kess's hands were small but worked like a clock. "You got in," he said, not a question. "They said you had potential. It's not fair."

Aisen's face shifted. He had the coin in his pocket, warm from habit, and he thought of the strange, casual generosity of the visiting scholar who'd praised him—an act that looked like kindness and also like selection. "It wasn't a guarantee," Aisen said. "It was a chance. I don't know what I'll become there."

Kess's jaw set. "Chance is different when your chances depend on other people's names and money. You were lucky, the way some are lucky to be born in a place that matters. That luck stacks up."

There was no accusation in Kess's voice, only a fact that burned. The tavern had many small arguments that lasted the length of a cup of ale; this one felt like it might keep its heat for longer.

Father's eyes softened. He had the worn patience of someone who'd seen the world tilt for boys like Kess. "There's still work for you here, lad. Maybe not the Academy, but there's skill in what you do. A blacksmith will teach a lad for food sometimes. A caravan will take on a careful hand if you can keep credit. There's more than one way to hold a life."

Kess looked at Aisen then, and for a moment the resentment became something else: a wary hope wrapped in bitterness. "If I had papers—if my father had told anyone where he came from—maybe they'd see me different. But I don't. So I cook and I listen."

Aisen stepped closer and placed the plate in Kess's hands, the motion of a friend making a small, practical promise. "Teach me to flip a pan without burning it properly," Aisen said lightly, knowing that such small trades mattered more than lectures.

Kess smiled, the kind that cracked open a person and made them brighter for a second. "If you teach me to read the menu, I'll show you how to save the bones for stock."

The bargain was simple: skills traded like coins. The Academy loomed as a dream for some and a closed gate for others. For Kess, the future was a kitchen and small victories; for Aisen, an uncertain scholarship. Both men moved through the same room, rearranging what they could.

Even so, Kess's voice softened at one point, and Aisen heard, under the bitterness, a small inventory of hopes. "My sister—Olva—she's clever with knots. She could be a roper on a caravan if she had the chance. But she can't leave the little ones behind. Somebody's got to watch them. It's all tied together."

The boy's wish was simple and clear: a trade that could be turned into a ladder if only the ladder had a rung wide enough for their feet. Aisen thought of the strange scholar who had smiled at him and the small favor that had pushed open a door. He had not been handed a map; he had been handed a sliver of permission.

Kess scraped a spoon along the edge of the pot, the sound a small metronome to steady his thoughts. "Sometimes folks say it's character that matters, not coin," he said. "But character don't pay for a bed when the roof leaks. Character don't bribe a guard when the road is closed. Character needs a way in."

There was an honesty in his words that made Aisen feel both grateful and awkward. He could not pretend his own chance had been only merit; the world had shifted around him in ways that had nothing to do with worth. He felt the weight of that and wondered how many other small, careful people carried the same quiet injury.

For the rest of the shift, they worked side by side and traded small confidences. Kess told a story about an old woman who once taught him to spot the weather by the direction of birds; Aisen told of the scholar's pamphlet he had been allowed to read when the evening was slow. They taught one another the useful things: how to stitch a torn hem so it lasted a winter, how to hide a saved coin beneath the lining of a boot.

Before the night ended, Father called them both and laid out two slices of bread between them. It was an offer that needed no preface: bread shared was a conversation kept alive. Aisen, mindful of Kess's look, slipped an extra corner of bread into Kess's hand when he reached for his coin. The gesture was small and practical—less a concession than a recognition.

When Kess went out to stack the last chairs, he paused and looked at the door as if weighing whether to cross the threshold into a world that tended to shut neat things away. He turned back and, in a voice that managed to be both defiant and tired, said, "Promise me you'll write if it opens for you. Not all of us have a way to see that far."

Aisen nodded, the promise landing like a small pact. It was not a plan; it was a thread they could both clutch if the world tugged. He thought then how small acts—an extra crust, a taught trade, a letter carried—could stack into a difference over time.
