# SOLDIER PERSPECTIVES: THE COORDINATION BREAKDOWN

## Scene I: Ghost Captain (4th Party Soldier, Unknown Name)

The standing orders were simple: *Maintain perimeter. No one exits. No one enters.*

I had been given this order seventy-three times. By the end of today, I would understand why seventy-three mattered. Why the exact number was the only thing my commander cared about.

The blockade around Thornwood Hollow was perfect. Forty soldiers, positioned at equal distances, with coordinated signal flares ready. It was the kind of operation the 4th Party executed without failure.

Until the small man with the split-colored eyes started moving through our formations.

I did not see him cross the line. One moment, the perimeter was intact. The next, he was past it, moving with the kind of precision that had no physics. He was going backwards through our positions, leaving something behind.

A marker. A goal. A redefining of what "escape" meant.

When I reported it, my commander already knew. He was receiving reports from other soldiers about the same phenomenon.

*"The goal has changed,"* my commander said, very quietly. *"We're not stopping them anymore. We're enabling them. Some of us have already died helping them. We don't remember doing it. But they do."*

I wanted to argue that this was impossible. That 4th Party coordination never failed. That we were trained for this.

But I watched, over the next hours, as our blockade became a funnel. As the prisoners escaped through gaps we didn't remember creating. As the small man with the split eyes worked backward through time itself, setting up situations where we had no choice but to let them through.

By the end, I realized: I had been working against my own orders for hours and didn't know it.

The escape count reached 143. Then 156. Then it stabilized.

My commander looked at me with an expression I had never seen on a 4th Party officer's face.

Fear.

*"We stayed,"* he said. *"Most of us stayed and tried to stop them anyway. But the goal kept shifting. Every time we blocked one exit, we found we had opened another. It was as if our deaths were being used as hinges."*

I understood then: this was not military conflict. This was something else. Something the 4th Party had no framework for fighting.

## Scene II: The Formation That Broke (Garrison Soldier, Name: Horne)

Lightning doesn't follow tactical doctrine.

That's what I should have known. That's what years of magical theory should have taught me. But the academy had given us rules about how powers worked, and those rules presumed orderly opposition.

The young man with the lightning—Ryo—came through the center of our defensive formation and broke it the way you break a clay tablet.

Our tactical maneuvers assumed sequential positioning. He moved through five positions simultaneously, his electricity creating feedback loops that turned our synchronized movements into liability. The more we held formation, the more damage the lightning did.

By the time we retreated, half our unit was struggling with nerve damage. The kind that doesn't kill you but makes you question whether living is worth the tremor in your hands.

He didn't kill us, though. He could have. But instead, he moved past us, breaking our formations the way you'd break a child's toy.

I realized, watching him pass: he was teaching us something. Showing us that the formations the 4th Party prided themselves on—the coordinated movements that made us unstoppable—were actually fragile. Dependent on enemies being orderly and predictable.

A single person who didn't care about formations could shatter what a hundred of us had built.

## Scene III: The Invisible One (Garrison Sergeant, Name: Volk)

The thing about coordination is that it requires everyone to see the same picture. It requires perfect information.

That changed when the Kairos anomaly started moving through our perimeter.

I won't try to explain what happened because I genuine don't understand it. But here's what I know: I received an order to hold a position. I held it. Then at some point—unclear when—I received a different order. I held that position instead.

No one told me to move. But I was definitely in two places, and I definitely completed both assignments.

When I asked my commander what had happened, he said: *"You were doing what the anomaly wanted. But you were doing it because it was still technically an order. He just... changed the shape of the orders. Made them more achievable."*

*"So I was compromised?"*

*"No. You were made efficient. There's a difference. But the result is the same: instead of blocking one exit with certainty, you were moved six times in coordination with people you didn't see, creating six micro-perimeters that were all impossible to maintain simultaneously."*

I understood then: there was someone who had learned to coordinate our own coordination against us. To recognize that our strength—synchronized movement—was actually a liability if the goal kept shifting.

By the time we figured out what was happening, the Thornwood breach was complete.

Seventy-one people escaped because someone had learned to make invisibility into a kind of organization.

## Scene IV: The Doctor Who Couldn't Run (4th Party Commander, Name: Kess)

I was there to observe the final stand. The Covenant had sent me to catalog how the heretics fell, so that future confrontations could be optimized.

What I observed instead was the failure of everything I understood.

The young man named Aisen fought from the cave fortress with precision. He did not use large powers—or if he did, I didn't recognize them. Instead, he used observation.

He noticed that our soldiers were moving in particular patterns. He exploited the gaps. He created situations where our superior numbers became a liability instead of an asset.

But worse: people were choosing to let him win.

I watched one of my own soldiers—a perfectly trained, perfectly loyal officer—encounter the young man and then turn away instead of engaging. When I demanded an explanation, he said:

*"He offered me a choice. He said: you can fight me and die, or you can understand why your mission was flawed and help me instead."*

I had him executed for cowardice.

Then I watched another soldier make the same choice. And another. Until I realized: this was not cowardice. This was something else. Something my framework could not accommodate.

The young man was not just fighting. He was teaching. He was taking our soldiers and, in moments of combat, convincing them that their orders were based on false premises. That they could choose differently.

It was not magic. It was something more dangerous: it was persuasion delivered under pressure.

I understood then why the Covenant was so frightened of knowledge-users. Not because they could cast powerful spells. But because they could change the shape of reality inside people's heads. And once people's heads changed, armies meant nothing.

I reported to the Covenant: *The heretic won not through power but through reframing. He convinced soldiers that their war was unwinnable not because he was stronger, but because he was clearer about what was actually being asked of them.*

The Covenant's response was predictable: execute everyone he recruited. Execute their families. Execute the entire region if necessary.

But I understood what the Covenant did not: you cannot execute an idea once it's been made clear. You can execute people. But the clarity remains. The next generation will know what these soldiers knew.

And that would be the real problem.

## Scene V: The Rifleman Who Touched the Edge (Garrison Soldier, Name: Cole)

I shot at the young man during the cave defense. Got off three shots. All missed—not because my aim was bad but because he was moving in ways that made trajectories irrelevant.

But when my fourth shot was chambered, he stopped and looked at me. Actually looked. Made eye contact across a hundred yards of firelight and shadow.

And I understood something in that moment: he was tired.

Not defeated. Not even particularly wounded. But exhausted in the way that comes from carrying knowledge no one else can bear.

I lowered my weapon.

My commander screamed at me to fire. My sergeant grabbed my arm. But I was watching the young man's shoulders ease—imperceptibly, but definitely ease—as one less threat required his attention.

I chose that moment. Not because I was brave or disillusioned. But because I saw someone who was carrying something too heavy to carry alone, and I didn't want to be the one adding weight.

When the cave fortress fell, I was among the soldiers who was taken alive. The Covenant executed me anyway, three months later, during ideological purification.

But in those three months, I learned the method. The way of reframing problems. The way of small goals leading to large freedom.

And when I was executed, I did what Aisen had done: I carried the method in my mind until the last moment. I made sure my death was as useless to the system as I could possibly make it.

At least, I hoped that's what I did.

## Scene VI: The Coordination Officer Who Quit (4th Party Lieutenant, Name: Dex)

The most terrifying moment of my career was when I realized the Thornwood assault was failing.

Not that we were losing personnel. The 4th Party accepted personnel loss. But the coordination itself—the invisible network that made us dangerous—was being disrupted.

It started with impossible choices. Soldiers would report completing contradictory orders. Two different units would have executed the same mission simultaneously and separately, as if time had fractured.

Then I understood: time HAD fractured. There was someone bending the shape of moments, creating alternatives where missions could be completed impossibly.

I received a directive from above: contain the anomaly. Stop whatever was creating temporal disturbance.

I tried. The moment I focused on stopping the anomaly, I realized I didn't know what I was trying to stop. How do you fight someone who has altered the shape of events enough that you're not sure which events already happened and which haven't happened yet?

By the time I tried to withdraw and regroup, I realized that my withdrawal was already being used against the 4th Party. Every step I took to consolidate forces created gaps that the prisoner escapes were exploiting.

I made a decision: I quit. I walked away from the 4th Party.

The organization tried to stop me. But the young man with the split eyes—Kairos—had already arranged for my escape. I suspect he had arranged it long before I even decided to leave.

The method precedes the choice. The goal defines the options available.

I'm living in a settlement now, under a false name. I'm learning to set small, achievable goals. To understand that my whole career was built on organizing things that should not have been organized.

And I'm teaching others. Quietly. The way the method is always taught.
