# The Spy Boy

The boy was caught in the supply tent with a message already half-written. Aisen recognized the cipher immediately—Covenant military, level three, the kind that took hours to break and only a few key figures could generate.

The boy was fifteen. Maybe fourteen. His hands were shaking.

"Don't hurt him," Marcus said. It was not a request. Marcus was Aisen's senior soldier, sixteen years with the garrison, the kind of man who had probably seen worse things than a crying teenager. "He's a boy."

Aisen was twenty-three. By his own measurements, he was barely more than a boy himself. But he was also trained to see the problem before it became an atrocity.

"What's your name?" Aisen asked, keeping his voice gentle.

The boy's mouth opened and closed a few times before sound emerged. "Korrin. My name is Korrin."

"Were you alone out there, Korrin? Or are there more?"

"Alone. I was alone. I swear on my mother's—"

"Your mother," Aisen said. He kept the question simple. Direct. "Is she why you're working for the Covenant?"

The boy's shoulders collapsed. "They have her. They have her and my sister. They said if I transmitted messages, they wouldn't hurt them. They said I was just passing information. That no one would be hurt."

Aisen knew the calculation. The Covenant was good at this—taking ordinary people, threatening the people they loved, and turning them into tools. It was systematic. It was efficient. It was evil.

It was also almost impossible to fault an individual for.

"How many messages have you sent?" Aisen asked.

"Three. I don't know what was in them. They give me the text. I send it. That's all."

Marcus moved to grab the boy, but Aisen held up a hand. "What would the Covenant do to your mother if you were captured?"

The boy's face went white. He did not answer. He did not need to.

Aisen understood the geometry. The boy was leverage. The Covenant had invested in him because he was expendable and because his family provided motivation. If the boy was captured, the Covenant would assume the network was compromised. They would execute the hostages—Korrin's mother and sister—to clean up loose ends.

The kindest thing to do would be to execute Korrin now. Quick. Clean. The Covenant would never know.

The next kindest would be to release him with a warning to run, to hide, to find his family.

The stupidest would be to do exactly what Aisen found himself doing next.

"There's a town," Aisen said, "three days north. Fox-kin settlement. Merchants mostly. Tell them Aisen sent you. Tell them you need to find the Underground. They'll know what that means."

Korrin's eyes filled with tears. "I can go? You won't—"

"I'm giving you a map," Aisen said, and he drew one on the back of a supply manifest, marking the route, marking the settlement. "And I'm giving you this coin. It's not much, but it's enough to buy passage north. The Covenant might follow. The garrison might follow. But you'll have a head start, and in the north, there are people who help refugees."

"My mother," Korrin said. "We have to get my mother."

"The Covenant will be watching her," Aisen said. "If you go there, you'll die. If you make it to safety first, someday the war might end, and you might find her. But if you go now, you'll both die."

It was a lie. A kind lie. But a lie nonetheless. The war would not end soon. The Covenant would not release its leverage to a boy with a map and a coin.

Korrin was crying openly now, but he took the map. He took the coin. And he left the tent as Aisen held the flap open, letting him leave like a man with choices instead of a prisoner.

Marcus watched him go without saying a word.

Twelve hours later, a Covenant patrol found Korrin on the north road.

Aisen learned about it the next morning from a scout who'd been tracking refugee movement. The boy was dead—caught between crossfire, the scout said, trying to run. Aisen did not believe that. The Covenant did not leave witnesses. Korrin had been executed, probably carefully, probably made to look like an accident.

And his mother—the Covenant would assume he'd talked. An hour after Korrin died, a military detachment was sent to the labor camp where she was held. Aisen never learned exactly how many were executed, but the count the scout reported was nineteen dead, including one woman fitting his description.

"You gave him a chance," Marcus said that evening. They were drinking something that was probably not wine—it tasted like metal and regret.

"I gave him false hope," Aisen said. "I sent him north like I was saving him, like I was making a choice that mattered. All I did was extend his death by half a day."

"What else could you have done?"

"Executed him myself. Cleanly. It would have been the merciful thing."

"You're not a killer," Marcus said.

"Then I'm a coward," Aisen said. "I wanted to feel like I was saving someone, so I did something that felt like salvation without actually saving anything. The boy is dead. His mother is dead. And I get to carry the knowledge that I caused both through my refusal to be honest about the situation."

Marcus drank. "The war causes these things. Not us."

"Does it?" Aisen asked. "Or do we cause the war by making choices like this one, over and over? I could have killed a teenage spy today. That would have been an atrocity. Except I didn't kill him—I sent him north where he died anyway, along with anyone he was close to. How is that better? How is that not also an atrocity, just delayed and dressed up as mercy?"

They drank in silence after that.

A week later, Aisen requested a transfer out of the Border War. A month later, he got it. He never sent another teenage spy north with false hope. But he also never forgot the weight of the choice, the impossible geometry of it.

Some decisions had no right answer. Just the decision you made and the cost it extracted.

## Abercrombie Principle Applied

**No Right Answer**: Aisen's choices are:
1. Execute the boy (murder, atrocity, but merciful & guarantees quiet)
2. Release him (hope, but doom to death anyway)
3. Keep him prisoner (he'll be tortured; the Covenant will get nothing)

All options are terrible. Aisen chooses what feels merciful and is actually cruel.

**Protagonist Failure**: Aisen's compassion does not save anyone. It extends suffering. He makes himself feel better by pretending he's a savior when he's actually complicit.

**Complexity**: The war IS the problem, but so are Aisen's choices. The reader understands both truths simultaneously.

**Lasting Consequence**: This scene defines Aisen as someone who will live with guilt for acts of (failed) mercy. It shapes his philosophy going forward.
