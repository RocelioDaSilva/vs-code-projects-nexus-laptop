# The Dead Forest Market

The forest was dying.

Aisen could see it in the bone-white bark of the trees, in the gray soil beneath his boots, in the absence of underbrush and birdsong. A Flux pool had poisoned this place three years ago—one of the wild ruptures that happened when the Veil grew thin. The trees had lived for another season while the poison spread through the roots. Then they'd stopped growing. Then they'd stopped living.

Now the forest was a graveyard pretending to be landscape.

The beast was starving.

It was a common variety—a dire wolf, probably—though identifying species had become harder as the ecosystem collapsed. What mattered was that its ribs were visible through its matted fur, and its eyes had the desperate clarity of something that had not eaten in days. It had probably followed a refugee column out of desperation, then gotten trapped in this dead forest when the herds failed to materialize.

Aisen's job was simple: kill it before it attacked the village. The council had paid him twenty crowns—most of his operating budget for the month.

He approached carefully. The wolf saw him and did not run. That was the tell. A healthy predator runs. A desperate one stands and fights.

"I'm sorry," Aisen said, and meant it.

He was reaching for his chain when he heard the crying.

He stopped. The crying was human. A child, possibly. Coming from somewhere behind the dead trees, somewhere Aisen could not immediately see.

"Stay back!" a woman's voice shouted. "Stay back, I have a weapon!"

Aisen drew his hands away from his chain and called out, "I'm armed but I'm not a threat. Are you hurt?"

There was a long pause. Then a woman emerged from behind a stand of skeletal pines. She was thin—not sick-thin, but scrappy-thin, the kind of thin that came from stretching rations for weeks. She was carrying a makeshift spear, which trembled in her hands because she was barely strong enough to hold it.

Behind her, peering around her leg, was a child. Maybe eight.

"The wolf?" Aisen asked.

"Has been following us since yesterday," the woman said. "We were trying to reach the village, but the forest... the forest has nothing. And it's starving, and we're..." She did not finish. She did not need to.

Aisen understood. This was a refugee family. They had come from somewhere that was worse than this dead forest. Probably displaced by the Border War, or the ecological collapse, or both. They were crossing through to reach the settlements on the eastern side where food was still available.

And the wolf was the least of their problems.

"How long have you been walking?" Aisen asked.

"Seven days. We have two days of food left, if we ration." The woman's eyes were tracking the wolf. "If it takes us..."

"It won't," Aisen said. He did not know if that was true, but the certainty in his voice seemed to help. "Come with me to the village. I'll handle the wolf."

The walk took two hours. The woman's name was Senna. The child was her daughter, Mira. They came from a farming region south of the war, but the war had moved south too. The Covenant had taken resources, which meant less food. When less food meant starvation, refugees headed east.

"There are thousands like us," Senna said as they walked. "Just... walking. Looking for somewhere to stay."

Aisen already knew that. He had seen three refugee columns in the past month alone. He did not know how many had made it to the villages. He suspected the number was smaller than those who had started walking.

The village was small. Maybe two hundred people. They looked prosperous enough—buildings still stood, gardens existed. But there was no joy in it. Everyone moved with the kind of efficiency that came from watching resources carefully.

Aisen delivered Senna and Mira to the council elder, explained about the wolf. The elder—a man named Kavin who'd been decent enough in the past—listened with an expression that grew steadily more uncomfortable.

"We can't feed them," Kavin said flatly.

"They won't need feeding," Aisen said. "They can work. They're skilled, or they can learn to be."

"We look at our stores," Kavin said, "and we see enough for our people through the winter. Maybe enough if we're careful. One extra mouth means someone else goes hungry. Two extra mouths..."

"They'll work," Aisen repeated, but he could already see that it didn't matter. The math was too simple. Twenty crowns was what Aisen earned for service. Feeding two refugees might cost fifteen crowns in yearly resources, assuming they could work immediately. The village did not care that the math was actually beneficial. What they cared about was certainty, and they were certain they preferred their own people to stranger mouths.

"We can give them supplies for four more days," Kavin said. "Food and water. But then they need to move on."

Aisen thought about arguing. He thought about it for a long moment, and then he did not, because he understood that arguing would change nothing.

He walked back to find Senna. She was sitting with her daughter in the village center, looking exhausted and trying to hide it.

"They won't take us," she said. It was not a question.

"They'll give you supplies for four days," Aisen said. "That should get you to a larger city. The larger cities sometimes have more flexibility."

Senna laughed—a short, sharp sound with no humor in it. "Larger cities have larger populations competing for the same food. Do you know why we came to a small village? Because I thought resources would be less stretched. I thought on the edges of civilization, people might be kinder." She looked at her daughter. "She hasn't slept properly in three days. She cries when she thinks I'm not listening. And there's simply... no room for her here."

"I'll give you money," Aisen said, and he pulled out his remaining crowns—about twenty, his payment for killing the wolf plus a small reserve. "Take it. Add it to the village supplies. Have them buy you extra food instead of the supplies they mentioned."

"You can't solve this," Senna said, but she took the money anyway. "There are a thousand villages like this one. A thousand families like mine. And every village has the same conversation with every refugee family."

Aisen killed the wolf that evening. It was not difficult. The wolf was starving and weak. It stood and fought because it was too tired to run, and Aisen ended it with a chain spike through the skull. Quick. Clean.

He collected his twenty crowns from the council, but before he left the village, he found Kavin.

"I want to understand something," Aisen said. "The forest died from a Flux rupture, correct? That was not the village's fault. It happened because the Veil is thin and wild magic spills through. And now your food sources are compromised because your supply routes ran through that forest. You're in crisis not because you failed but because the world is failing."

"Yes," Kavin said carefully.

"And the refugees are starving for similar reasons—their homes are gone or their fields are gone or the war displaced them. They're in crisis because systems failed them. Not because they're weak or undeserving."

"Yes," Kavin said again.

"And yet," Aisen continued, "when one small family walks into your village asking for help, your response is to see them as the problem. To calculate whether their lives are worth the resources they'll consume. To choose your own certainty over their survival."

Kavin was quiet for a long time. "What would you have me do? Should I feed the refugees and watch my own people starve?"

"No," Aisen said. "I'm not suggesting you're wrong. I'm suggesting that you are all trapped. The ecological crisis created starvation. The ecological crisis drove refugees. The refugees now threaten the security of the remaining communities. And every person in this conversation is acting rationally within the constraints of scarcity. And yet everyone is suffering. That's not a moral failure on your part. That's a systemic failure and it has crushed you all."

"And?" Kavin asked.

"And I don't know the answer," Aisen said. "I just wanted you to understand that the choice you made wasn't between 'help refugees' and 'betray your people.' The real choice was already made for you when the Veil thinned and the forest died. Everything after that was just mathematics."

Aisen left the village that evening and never returned.

He heard later that Senna and her daughter had made it to a city. He never learned if they thrived or starved. He suspected both things happened to different people in different ways.

What he did know was that no one's moral failure had created that situation. The world itself had failed, and mercy had become a luxury that cost resources no one had to spare.

## Jemisin Principle Applied

**Systemic Roots of Conflict**: The wolf attack is not the actual problem. The problem is:
- Ecological collapse (Flux rupture killed forest)
- War displacement (created refugees)
- Resource scarcity (makes communities hostile)
- Class structures (villages prioritize own over stranger)

**Personal Intersects Systemic**: Aisen's individual kindness (giving money) cannot solve structural problems. The village's refusal (rationally justified by scarcity) is also right within its constraints.

**No Individual Solution**: The answer is not for villagers to be better people. It's for the system to change. That requires something beyond individual moral choice.

**Tragedy is Systemic**: Senna and her daughter suffer not because anyone chose to harm them, but because every rational actor within a broken system makes locally correct decisions that create globally catastrophic outcomes.
