# CASPIAN – FIRST STRANGER ENCOUNTER

The tavern at mid-morning was quiet in the way that establishments like the *Crossing* could be quiet—full of people, but the kind of full where everyone was absorbed in their own transactions, their own conversations, their own careful business. Smoke from the kitchen fires drifted through the common room, carrying the scent of bread and herbs.

Aisen was five years old and small enough that he could move between tables without drawing attention. He was supposed to be helping his father tend the common room—sweeping up spilled ale, collecting abandoned cups, the sort of work that children could do without requiring particular skill. In reality, he was watching people.

This was something he had been doing since he was very small: studying how people moved, how they spoke to each other, which words made them lean closer and which made them pull away. His father said it was a useful skill. His mother said it was unsettling. Both agreed that it was something he should keep doing if he wanted to understand how the world actually worked as opposed to how people said it worked.

A man came through the tavern doors in the middle of the morning. He was young, maybe twenty or so, dressed in the kind of clothes that suggested money but also suggested an unwillingness to spend that money on appearance. The cloak he wore was expensive wool, but it was also travel-stained and worn at the cuffs.

He ordered a drink from Marta in a voice that was quiet and precise: "The merchant's selection, if you have it. The one that comes from the northern orchards."

Marta had nodded without comment and produced a bottle that was definitely not commonly ordered. The drink was dark and aromatic—the kind of thing that required being able to afford specificity, to know what you wanted and trust that you'd be able to recognize it if you found it.

What Aisen noticed, though, was that the man didn't drink it immediately. He held it. He looked at it. He seemed to be waiting for something about the drink itself to tell him something.

When Marta moved away to attend to other customers, the man's attention shifted. He began watching the room the way Aisen was watching the room. Not hunting for something. Just observing. Understanding.

Aisen found himself fascinated by this activity—the idea that there was another person in the tavern whose job seemed to be what his job was, except higher-level, more intentional. He became aware of watching the man watching the room, which was a meta-layer of observation that he found intellectually satisfying even if he didn't entirely understand why.

The man caught him at it. His eyes shifted, and suddenly they had made contact. Aisen, who had been trained not to reveal observation, maintained his expression and went back to sweeping. But he was acutely aware that the man had identified him as someone who observed.

Later, when the tavern was in that between-time when the breakfast crowd had left and the lunch crowd hadn't yet arrived, the man approached him directly.

"You're watching people," the man said. He said it as a statement, not an accusation. This was important.

"I'm sweeping," Aisen replied, which was technically accurate.

"Yes. But you're also watching people." The man crouched down to be more or less at Aisen's eye level, which was considerate in a way that suggested he understood that height differentials created a particular kind of communication problem. "Can I ask you something?"

Aisen's training was to be cautious with strangers. But his observation suggested that this man, while unusual, was not dangerous in the way the people in the tavern who smelled like violence were dangerous. This was a different kind of unusual.

"Okay," Aisen said.

"What makes someone real?" the man asked.

It wasn't a question Aisen had any capacity to answer. He understood real in its most basic sense—the tavern was real, his father was real, the dust motes floating in the light were real. But the man was asking something more complicated than that, something about the nature of personhood that seemed impossible.

"I don't know," Aisen admitted.

"That's a good answer," the man said. He seemed genuinely pleased by Aisen's honesty. "Because the answer is complicated. But I'll tell you something: the way to become real is to be noticed. Not recognized, not understood. Just noticed. Seen. Acknowledged as existing."

He stood then, straightening up to his full height.

"Keep watching," the man said. "You're good at it. The world needs people who watch."

He returned to his table, drank his rare drink slowly, and left several hours later without saying another word to anyone. Aisen didn't see him again.

But the question lingered in his five-year-old mind: What makes someone real? The answer, he realized much later, was that becoming real was something that required external recognition. You couldn't make yourself real alone. You needed to be seen by other people, noticed by them, acknowledged as existing. The man had been telling him this in a kind way, had given him both a question and a hint toward its answer.

Years later, when Aisen was trying to understand what had happened to the people under the Covenant's system—how they had become less real, less present, less capable of acting as autonomous agents—he would remember this conversation. The system had worked by making people not see each other. By preventing acknowledgment. By creating structures where genuine human recognition was impossible.

And the solution, he understood then, wasn't to provide services or redistribute power or even to eliminate the Covenant entirely. The solution was simpler: make people visible to each other again. Let them be noticed. Allow them to be real in each other's eyes.

The man with the rare drink had been teaching him this from the beginning.

Years later, Aisen would realize that the man had been Caspian Vane, before circumstances had transformed him into something else entirely. But in that moment, at five years old, he was simply a stranger who had asked an impossible question and then left him with a koan that would take decades to fully understand.

Sometimes Aisen wondered if the stranger had known, that morning in the tavern, what Caspian would eventually become. If the question about reality had been a kind of gift—a way of telling his younger self that transformation was coming, that he would eventually need to understand how people became less real and how to make them real again.

But probably the man had just been a traveling scholar asking philosophical questions of children. Sometimes the simplest answer was the true one.

It just took Aisen a long time to believe that.
