Scene: The Abandoned Forge

The forge stank of old heat—smoke long gone but the memory of it clung to the stone. Aisen moved through the cold shadows with Amara, careful not to disturb the loose bricks. She kept the ledger folded in her leather palm like a live thing. The map fragment had been cinched into a seam of memory-metal; when Amara pried it loose the metal hissed like a waking thing.

"It was supposed to be a utensils order," Amara said quietly. Her eyes shone in the dark. "Then someone re-routed the manifest. Look—this line. 'calcined nodes.'"

The forge's center was a ring of scorched floor, concentric like a ritual. Little trenches ran from the ring into the earth; the trenches were filled with jagged slag and shattered flux beads. Aisen touched the ring and felt the aftertaste of cold humming—the sort of residual that made fingers itch.

He found the apprentice token stuck under a shifted stone: a child's copper coin with a teacher's sigil hammered into it. The token had a nick matching the missing apprentice's wrist scar—a private sign no one else would notice.

"They were testing something here," Aisen said. "Not a weapon—something worse. A way to pull or hold Flux."

Amara slid the map fragment under a candle and traced a faint set of coordinates and a crude marking—three little wedges like teeth, pointing northwest.

"If this is right," she said, "it's under—"

"The old marshworks," Aisen finished. He had sketched the marshworks when he'd been a boy. It was where the city had once buried bad things.

Amara folded the ledger back. "We need a copy, and we need more than paper. This is a redacted line item for a reason."

They left the forge with soot on their palms and a blank space in their chests where the apprentice's face had been. The city slept around them ignorant. The ring below the forge breathed hot and steady in the dark like a sleeping throat.

As they stepped out, Kaelen joined them with a small, crooked tendril curled in his hand—a bent sunflower root he'd coaxed earlier as a test. He braided it into the soft earth at the forge's lip and the root took like a living wire. "It will hold where a nail would slip," Kaelen said, watching Amara trace the map fragment.

Amara tapped the ledger and pointed north. "If the coordinates line up, that living anchor will be useful for fieldwork—conceals better than iron and throws no signature on a ledger." Aisen knelt and noted the way the root sat in the soil; there was a lesson in pragmatic growth here, not the soft poetry the Academy praised. He made a mental note to teach the others how to braid a living anchor and to ask Kaelen for a batch of bent sunflowers before they left.
