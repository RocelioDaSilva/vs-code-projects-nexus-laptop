# Aldric's Execution

Aisen was twelve when they hanged the priest.

The entire town was required to watch. His mother held his shoulder. His father stood silent beside them.

Aldric had been the town's healer. He had taught Aisen's mother about herbs. He had delivered Aisen himself, eight years before.

Now he stood on a platform with a rope around his neck.

"He taught forbidden knowledge," the Covenant official announced. His voice was clear. His hands were clean. "He revealed the nature of Flux to those without proper station. He committed heresy."

Aldric said nothing. He stood very still.

Aisen did not understand. Flux was Flux. Healing was healing. What knowledge was forbidden about saving a life?

He wanted to ask. He did not.

The official read from a scroll. The charges. The verdict. The sentence.

When he was finished, he nodded. The executioner stepped forward.

Aldric's eyes found Aisen's in the crowd. For just a moment, they locked. The healer's expression did not change. But Aisen saw something there—not fear, not acceptance.

*Apology.*

Aldric was apologizing for dying. For leaving. For the knowledge he could not pass on.

Then the rope tightened.

The crowd was quiet. A few people wept. Most watched without expression. They had learned, long ago, not to cry for heretics.

It was over quickly.

That night, Aisen's father took him aside. "Forget what you saw. Forget about Aldric. Forget about the questions you're asking."

"But Father—"

"Forget," his father said. His voice was flat. "Curiosity kills healers. Remember that."

Aisen remembered. He spent the next two years studying how not to ask questions. How to watch without seeing. How to survive.

It was a good education, in its way. It prepared him for everything that came after.

But he never forgot Aldric's eyes. He never forgot the apology in them.

Years later, when he held forbidden knowledge himself, he would think of that moment. He would understand that Aldric had not been guilty of heresy.

He had been guilty of caring. That was the real crime.
