Summary:

The interview room smelled of beeswax and old ink, a neat scent that suggested composure and measured judgment. The panel sat in an arc like quiet judges: three professors in dark coats, a single clerk with a ledger, and the Academy's standard lamp throwing a halo over the table. Deva smoothed her hands in her lap; her palms were warm from travel and her breath came a fraction fast. She had practiced the answers in the cart on the way in, listening to their rhythm like a merchant listening for a fair price.

She answered questions with facts and confidence, then with images—of river markets where traders bartered small wonders, of a mechanist's apprentice who taught her to think of cause and effect, of the tactile memory of weighing coin and measure. She spoke of wanting to study the mechanics of community, how knowledge might be made useful without being hoarded.

The first professor, Professor Alen, leaned forward and asked about her family's trade. Her father, Deva said calmly, made sails and mended nets. Her mother tended the ledger. Neither was a name the Academy recognized, and Deva saw the tiniest flicker along Alen's lip as if a private joke had passed behind his eyes.

"We look for certain markers of readiness," said the second, Mistress Yul. Her voice was cool and practiced. "Letters, recommendations, a history of scholarly mentorship. Not every place sends students prepared for our curriculum. Your region—it's pragmatic, is it not? Practical. How do you imagine translating that into abstract study?"

Deva answered with an example: a calculation she'd written about tide patterns and their effect on harbor trade. She described the equations, the observations she'd taken from the docks, the way merchants adjusted schedules by a margin of hours. She explained how a mathematical model could save a village from ruined grain shipments. The clerk's pen moved faster; Deva thought that meant interest.

Yet in the margins of the interview, small questions carved a different shape. "We must maintain standards," Professor Alen murmured at one point, like an oath. "Standards are why the Academy endures." It was a principle that sounded like law—neutral and inevitable. Deva could feel the room slide toward that word, as if 'standards' were a net thrown to catch inconvenient things.

When the panel asked about references, Deva spoke of a local mentor, a mechanist named Sora who'd shown her methods. The panel folded the name into their ledger as if they were weighing it. "We require proof of mentorship," Mistress Yul said. "A mentor recognized by the Academy, or someone of standing in our records. Who would vouch for you at that level?"

She told them the truth: that Sora had no pedigree and no letters, only trust. She spoke as if her pride were an offering; she would not trade sincerity for a forged recommendation. Her southern accent, relaxed vowels and a cadence shaped by harbor talk, sounded foreign in the panel's ears. It was not offensive; it was simply a reminder that she did not come from the cultivated circles meant to feed the Academy's ranks.

There was a question, after many others, about temperament. "We look for an ability to hold certainty under pressure," said Professor Alen. "We must know a mind will not break when doctrine is questioned. Does your upbringing prepare you for that?"

Deva told them about nights of bargaining under wind, about keeping ledgers straight when storms threatened, about watching a village coordinate emergency efforts. To her, the question was practical; to them, it slid toward something else—an assessment of whether she would defer appropriately, accept the Academy's structures without rancor.

When the clerk closed his ledger and slid it toward the head of the table, Deva felt the temperature change in the room. The lamp's light seemed to harden. "We have decided," Mistress Yul said with that practiced fairness, "that while you show promise, the Academy must be careful in maintaining its standards. We are declining your application at this time."

The words landed like a soft stone. They were measured, almost kind, but they had the weight to stop a future. Deva's throat tightened—not from surprise but from the smallness of the reason offered. Promises of possibility were replaced by a polite gate: standards, readiness, the need for recognized mentorship.

She left the building with her heart heavy and her hands empty. Outside, the market's smell hit her—fish and tar and the sharp bronze of coins—and for a moment she wanted to scream that standards had names, that the Academy's standards were shaped by those who had access to letters and favors and history. She wanted to shout that the Academy's 'standards' were a net woven by those who had already crossed the gate.

But instead she folded her chin and walked to the wharf where her father's stall waited. She wrote nothing in the moment; the clerk's ledger sat, an official sort of erasure. She thought of the people who had urged her to apply—the ones who had taught her to ask—and kept their faces like a small warmth inside her chest.

Later, that night, Deva opened her pocket and smoothed the edge of the leaflet the Academy gave to applicants: a polite seal, an inked rule. She traced the phrase about 'maintaining standards' until the letters blurred. She would not let the refusal be an end; the harbor taught repair and return. If the Academy's gate was closed, she would find another way to learn, or teach, or make knowledge travel.
