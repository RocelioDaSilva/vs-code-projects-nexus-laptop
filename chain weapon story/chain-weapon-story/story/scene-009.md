Summary:

Master Erich taught motion as if it were language—each swing a phrase, each stance a clause. He expected students to learn the grammar of balance and weight so they could improvise when the sentence of a fight bent unexpectedly. Lately, the sentences he was asked to teach were shorter, clipped into orders and repeated until reflex replaced reason.

In the training yard, Aisen moved with a nervous precision. Erich watched the boy's shoulders, the way he shifted weight when given a command. He adjusted Aisen's blade hand with a touch that was both correction and lesson: not only where to place the hand, but why that placement mattered. Understanding, he always thought, made a man safer than mere obedience.

The new drills arrived as directives from the Academy's administrative board: drills of compliance, synchronized footwork, responses measured to the second. They looked efficient on paper and terrifying in practice. Students would line up and perform sequences that left no room for deviation. The exercises trained reflexes but discouraged the small experiments a student might try when something unexpected broke the pattern.

Erich's left hand trembled sometimes—an old scar beneath the skin, a memory in muscle. He felt the tremor when he grew angry or when he held a student's gaze too long. It made him more careful in his demonstrations; he emphasized control, not show. He saw in Aisen a promising mind: an instinct for reading an opponent's weight rather than reacting to a cue. Erich prized that in students; such instincts could adapt, they could choose when to bend rules.

But the Academy's rhetoric shifted. Reports from the board insisted that new initiatives would "improve order and readiness." They came with phrases like "standardized behavior patterns" and "cohesive unit response." Erich smelled the same official language that accompanied military reforms and found it hard not to link it to a broader transfer of power. He had seen men trained as tools before; there was a slow corrosion when thought gave way to parade.

In private, Erich modified his teaching. He drilled Aisen in the new sequences because refusal would have consequences; he did so but layered them with comment, small breaks where he urged explanation over repetition. "Why does your foot drop here?" he'd ask quietly, forcing Aisen to name the reason. If the boy could name it, he could change it when necessary. The questions were a kind of smuggling—ideas carried beneath a compliant surface.

One afternoon, after the day's formal drills, Erich took Aisen aside. The yard was empty enough that their breaths showed like small clouds in the cool air. "You move like a practicing man," Erich said, correcting the angle of Aisen's forearm. "But don't let the patterns be all you have. A pattern is only useful until the shape of the world changes. Then you need judgment."

Aisen frowned. "They're making us do so many patterns, Master. If you don't follow them, the sergeants mark you down."

Erich's jaw tightened. The tremor came that afternoon when he rubbed the bruise on his hand. He thought of his own youth, trained in a troop that prized obedience, and how it had left him with memories that were harder to carry than any scar. "Then find the space inside them," he advised. "Learn them so you can bend them. Don't be a man who must wait to be told. Find the reason in the movement. That's what keeps you alive when orders fail."

There were consequences for his method. A report came that a member of the disciplinary board had questioned certain instructors for their tolerance of independent thought. Erich was called to a meeting where a thin man with careful hair spoke of "consistency across the curriculum" and "the need for conforming units." He spoke the language of metrics and readiness as if it were a moral law.

Erich listened and said nothing. He was a minor man in a great machine, but he was not without agency. He returned to the yard and continued to teach with quiet resistance—a question substituted where a command would have been, a corrective framed as an invitation to reason. With some students he was blunt; with others he was cunning, hiding lessons about judgment in the form of compliance.

When Aisen later left the Academy for reasons that would not be discussed in polite company, Erich felt both fear and a small relief. He knew that the boy would be watched, that the Academy's changes might shape him. But he also recognized that a person who had been taught to name reasons could navigate a world of ordered commands and still say 'no' when needed.

Erich did not often speak of his own compromises. He had a scrap of a letter folded in a ledger back home that he kept unread for reasons that had more to do with shame than sentiment. The tremor in his hand was not simply a physical thing; it was a ledger of choices. He had taught men to obey and had watched what obedience could do when misapplied.

So he taught in a way that carried double meaning. When he corrected a student's stance he would pause and ask why the student had chosen that stance, then insist they try an alternative and name the difference aloud. The exercises were small and could be framed as compliance, but the questions he asked made the students practice reasoning in the margin of obedience.

One evening after drills, Erich sent Aisen to fetch a practice partner and then placed two wooden poles on the ground close together. "Pretend the outer world insists on a step left or right," he said. "Now find a way to make that step useful. Change the shape of your answer without breaking the form."

Aisen frowned, then moved, and Erich watched the boy's shoulders set into the experiment. The younger man's eyes were honest and quick; he tried a small feint and then, when the instruction would have made him parry in a single rehearsed way, Aisen shifted his footing so that the pre-ordained response became a choice. Erich felt the tremor in his hand and thought, not for the first time, that to teach judgment was a kind of rebellion.

Rumors came in official memos: boards convened to reduce variance in teaching, faculty reviews that praised conformity. Erich's name appeared on one such list. He was summoned to a quiet room where a thin official spoke of metrics and the need for standardization. Erich listened without answering and, on the way back to the yard, he paused at the threshold to inhale the cold air as if it could fill him with courage.

He would not make a scene. He believed that small acts—an extra question, a withheld order, a note passed under a bench—could be the seed of something larger. So when he found a student being punished for a misread move, he would step in with a correction cloaked as advice; when a drill captain insisted on a cadence that made no sense in the mud, Erich would suggest a minor adjustment that preserved the cadence but gave an opening for thought.

His methods produced results that were not always visible. A few students learned to name reasons inside their bodies; they could anticipate the moment an order would fail. Others simply went through the motions and found themselves useful in the short term but brittle later. Erich kept a quiet list of those he thought might survive—those he had nudged toward judgment rather than mere repetition.

In his own small ways, he tried to protect Aisen's capacity to think. He asked questions that felt like kindnesses: what would you do if the command lost sense? who would you protect first? the answers were not always tidy, but they were practice for a life where orders might be unjust.
