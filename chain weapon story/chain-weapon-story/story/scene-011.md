The academy gardens held the last heat of the afternoon in their stone paths, releasing it slowly into the air the way a body releases a held breath. Castor had chosen this place because it was public—benches visible from the upper dormitory windows, the fountain audible from the refectory door—and because he needed the discipline of witnesses. If they were alone, he would tell her the truth. He was certain of this the way you are certain of falling: the direction was already determined, and only the surroundings kept the speed in check.

Marina arrived in the blue dress she wore on rest-days, the one with ink-stained cuffs from where she leaned on her sleeve during lectures. She was carrying two peaches from the kitchen—she always brought two, always assumed he hadn't eaten—and the sight of that assumption, that they were still the kind of people who brought each other fruit, put something behind his ribs that was worse than any physical wound he could name.

"You look terrible," she said, and kissed his cheek, and sat beside him.

He could feel the envelope in his jacket pocket. Not a real envelope—a spoken message, delivered by a junior proctor who had knocked on his door at dawn and said, with the perfect neutrality of someone who has practiced: *You have been selected for the Covenant's advanced placement program. acceptance is expected by close of day. Your family's reassessment has been suspended pending your cooperation.*

His family's reassessment. His mother, who sold lamp-oil in the border markets. His sister, who was nine and had just started showing trace-level flux sensitivity—which meant she would be tested, which meant she would be catalogued, which meant the Covenant would know exactly where to apply pressure if Castor was slow to comply.

"I need to talk to you," he said.

Marina looked at him the way she always looked at him: as though the world contained one genuine thing and she had found it sitting next to her on a bench. She bit into her peach. Juice ran down her wrist.

"Go ahead," she said.

He had practiced this. Three hours, walking the cloister in the grey hour before dawn, arranging sentences like a student arranges proof-steps in a logic examination. He would be cold. Convincing. He would say things that were true enough to hurt but not true enough to explain, and she would hate him, and the hatred would be a wall between her and the Covenant, because they only leveraged love.

"I think we should stop seeing each other."

She stopped chewing. The peach held in the air, half-bitten, juice on her chin.

"What?"

"I've been thinking about it for a while. I don't think this is working."

"You—" She set the peach down on the bench between them. Juice bled into the stone. "Castor, what are you talking about? We had dinner last night. You read me your sister's letter. You were fine."

"I was being polite."

The word landed like a slap. He watched it hit her—watched the understanding arrive not all at once but in stages, the way light leaves a room: first the edges go dark, then the middle, then the warmth.

"Polite," she repeated. As if testing whether the word still meant what she thought it meant.

"We're at the Academy," he said, and the lie came now, the practiced lie, the one he had assembled from true materials arranged in false order. "We're surrounded by talented people who are going to make important connections. I need to focus. I can't be—" He gestured vaguely, a gesture that was supposed to indicate *distracted* but which his hands performed as *drowning.* "I can't be carrying the weight of someone else's expectations."

"Weight," she said. And now her voice was changing—not louder, which would have been easier, but quieter, which was much worse. "Is that what I am? Weight?"

*You're the only thing I can't afford to carry because they'll use you to pull me under,* he thought. But what he said was: "I'm sorry, Marina. I've made my decision."

She searched his face. He could feel her reading him, and he knew—with a certainty that made his jaw ache from the effort of keeping it shut—that she was looking for the seam. The place where the lie was stitched to the truth. She was intelligent enough to find it. That was what terrified him.

So he did the cruelest thing he had ever done. He looked away.

Not dramatically. Not with the theatre of guilt. He simply turned his head toward the fountain and adopted the expression of someone who has already moved on—already left this conversation in his mind—and is only waiting for the formalities to conclude.

It worked. He felt the moment she stopped searching. The small collapse of something in the air between them, like a thread snapping that you hear more with your chest than your ears.

"Okay," Marina said. She stood up. She left the peaches on the bench—both of them, the bitten one and the whole one—and she walked away along the garden path with her arms at her sides and her ink-stained cuffs showing and her back very straight, the way people carry themselves when they are determined to reach a door before the crying starts.

Castor sat on the bench. The fountain ran. A bird landed on the path where Marina had walked, pecked at something, flew away. The peach bled juice through the stone like a small wound.

He took the whole peach—the one she had brought for him, the fruit of an assumption that they were fine—and held it in his hands and did not eat it. He sat there until the shadows covered the bench and the upper dormitory windows went dark one by one, and then he stood and walked to the Proctor's office and signed the form that accepted his placement in the Covenant's advanced program, and the junior proctor stamped it with the Academy seal and said, without looking up, "Your family will be notified of the suspension of reassessment proceedings. Congratulations on your selection."

He went to his room. He set the peach on his desk. It stayed there for three days until it started to rot, and he could not bring himself to throw it away, and finally his roommate disposed of it while he was in class.

Marina did not speak to him again. Not because she was angry—or not only because she was angry—but because she had looked at his face in the garden and seen something she could not name, and the not-naming of it was worse than the loss. She would tell friends, later, that he had changed. That something in the Academy had gotten to him. She was exactly right, in the way that people who are exactly right about the shape of a problem can still be exactly wrong about its cause.

Castor enrolled in the advanced program. He learned what the Covenant wanted him to learn. He became, by degrees, the thing that they had paid for—not through violence, not through ideology, but through the simple mechanics of the transaction: they had his family, and so they had him, and so Marina's peach rotted on his desk as the last artifact of a person he had chosen to stop being.

He thought about her often. This did not help.
