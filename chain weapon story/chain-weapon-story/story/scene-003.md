Summary:

Aldric's confession came with the rough honesty of a weary man who'd been given orders he could not fully swallow. He sat with his elbows on the bar, fingers wrapped around a clay mug that trembled only slightly. The captain's scar cut a thin white line above his left brow, a measure of the things he'd survived and had to live with.

He'd been the sort of man who measured truth by the weight of a hand on his shoulder. Tonight, his hand felt heavy. "They told me it's for the border," he said, voice hoarse. "Defense. Patrols. Keeping the pass clear."

But his eyes did not match the words; they looked like someone watching a map and finding unfamiliar marks. "They draft boys now, Aiden, lads with promise. They call it service. They give them a new uniform and a name on a list and they ship them off. The Covenant takes their names and sometimes their faces come back different or not at all."

Father set down a spoon like a man who didn't want to hurry the confession or change its map. "You mean conscription?" he asked, each syllable careful.

"Conscription, voluntary 'offerings', however you dress it up. The new orders come from high. People in clean collars who sign their names on pads that mean everything to some and nothing to others. You'll do as you're told, or you'll find yourself replaced."

Aisen, sweeping near the hearth, froze his motion with a broom in hand. The boy's face had the kind of focus Marta had seen only in too-rare moments—the look of someone attempting to translate adult syllables into a plan that fit inside his small chest.

"Keep him sharp," Aldric said suddenly, his eyes dropping to Aisen in a way that made the air tighten. "Teach him to keep his feet and his head. Teach him to read men. Teach him not to make enemies before he has to. There's a difference now between being clever and being noticed." He drained his mug and set it down hard.

Father's mouth tightened. "And if he gets taken?" he managed.

Aldric's laugh was a small, painful sound. "Then we do what we can. We drink. We hide what matters. We curse the sermon and promise the saints we'll not forget them."

"You've seen it—that they take names and never return?" Marta asked, the question barely a whisper.

He nodded once. "I've seen boys come back younger somehow. I've seen lads disappear from a field and their mothers silence themselves for a year. You get used to that kind of grief only if you have to. If you can avoid it, do. Keep your boy alive. Keep him small as long as you can if that's what will keep him home."

There was a hardness to his voice now—something like regret grinding with resignation. "I signed papers I didn't read. I was told it was for 'defense measures.' They showed me the names on the list and I did my job. I won't lie—there's a coin on the table and a whisper in the hall. I took my share because what else does a man do when his family needs bread?"

The tavern held its breath. Aisen's hand clenched the broom handle. For a child, the shape of promises is a hard thing to see; for Aldric, the heavy cost of those promises sat like a stone in his chest.

"Don't let him join nothing thoughtless," Aldric said, voice threading through the hush. "Teach him to ask why. Teach him to count who benefits when men leave. And when they come to the door with ribbons and a smile, you ask them where they learned their numbers." He gave a humorless smile that didn't reach his scar.

As he rose to go, his chair scraped the floor like a small indictment. He paused at the door, turned, and without ceremony said, "If you need a warning, I'll give it. If you need food—I'll find it. I won't pretend I can fix everything. I'm a poor captain with debts and a scar. But I will tell you what I know."

When the door closed behind him, the room exhaled. The ledger reopened, the spoon polished, the small work resumed; but the sound of his confession hung in the rafters like the smell of smoke—hard to name, harder to remove.
