He tried to weep and could not find the feeling.

The funeral was happening without him—Aisen had asked if he wanted to come, and Caspian had said no, and Aisen had not pushed. That was the gift Aisen gave: the acceptance of refusal. But Caspian could see the body from where he stood in the forest, a small shape wrapped in cloth, and he knew he should be grieving. He had lost a friend. A good man. Someone he had chosen to be with in the way only the living can choose.

But his chest would not crack. His eyes would not wet. The feeling was somewhere behind doors he could no longer open.

Amara found him in the afternoon, sitting by a stream watching his reflection. The reflection showed the same thing it had shown for three weeks: changes. The lines of gold in his eyes were brighter now. His skin had taken on a faint metallic sheen in direct light. His teeth were sharper. He was becoming.

"He's gone," Amara said. "The funeral is done. They placed the plaque in the stone above the ridge."

Caspian nodded. He understood the words. He could calculate the emotion that should accompany them. But the feeling itself was locked away, as distant as his human childhood.

"You're not sad," Amara said. Not a question.

"I should be," Caspian said. "I'm trying to be. But it's like opening a door and finding the room empty. The room where sadness used to live is just... gone."

Amara sat beside him. She was quiet for a long time. Then: "The heart. It's not your heart anymore. It's hers. Serafina's heart."

"I know," Caspian said. "She told me when she took it. She said it was a gift. That I would never have to grieve again. That I would be unburdened by the weight of loss."

"Do you believe her?"

"I don't know what I believe anymore," Caspian said. "I can remember grieving. I can remember how it felt when people died before. That heavy, breaking feeling. But I can't access it. It's like a story I read once but can't quite recall the details of."

He pressed his hand to his chest where the heart beat with its strange, irregular rhythm. Thump-thump. Pause. Thump. Thump-thump. It was not quite a human heartbeat. It was something else. A metronome of an alien thing.

"This is the real curse," Caspian said. "Not that I'm changing. Not that I have a non-human heart. The real curse is that I can see what I've lost and can't feel bad about losing it. I can understand that I should grieve and not be able to. It's like watching a play where I'm supposed to cry at a particular moment, but I can't remember any of my lines."

He looked at his reflection again. The gold lines in his eyes caught the sunlight and held it. He looked like money. Like value. Like something that could be traded for.

"I went to see him," Caspian said quietly. "After they wrapped the body. I wanted to feel something. I stood there and I remembered all the times we talked. All the moments we had. I remembered choosing to be his friend and him choosing to accept that friendship. And I waited for sadness. I waited for regret. I waited for that breaking sensation that means you've lost something precious."

He paused.

"It never came," he said. "I felt empty. I felt the absence of feeling. But I didn't feel the feeling that should fill that absence."

Amara reached over and checked his pulse. It was still irregular, still not quite right.

"The contract is working," she said. "Serafina is changing you progressively. Each day you'll feel less. Each day you'll become more like her."

"Is that what you wanted?" Caspian asked. "When you brought me to her?"

"No," Amara said. "I wanted you to survive. I wanted you to have a choice other than the one that would have killed you. But I made the choice for you, and this is the choice I made."

"You protected me," Caspian said, not quite a question.

"I did. And now you're becoming something I'm not sure how to protect from."

Caspian stood and looked back toward where the funeral was ending. He could see movement in the distance. Aisen, walking away from the grave. Kaelen following. Others he had chosen to call friends, all of them carrying the weight of a death that wasn't theirs but had become theirs because they were together.

And Caspian was not carrying it. Caspian was empty.

"Can I fix it?" he asked Amara. "Can I break the contract? Can I go back to being able to feel?"

Amara looked at him with something like pity. "No," she said. "Once Serafina has your heart, it stays hers. The change is progressive. In a year, you won't remember what sadness felt like. In five years, you won't remember what any human emotion felt like. You'll become something else entirely."

"Something better?" Caspian asked.

"Something else," Amara repeated. "Better and worse don't apply."

That night, Caspian tried to write about it. He had started keeping a journal, an attempt to document his transformation in case some part of him wanted to remember what he had been. But the words were hollow. He could write: *I feel nothing. I grieve nothing. I am changing.* But the words on the page were just shapes. They didn't carry weight. They didn't carry truth. They carried only the memory of what truth felt like.

He looked at the words and realized: this is what Serafina meant about unburdening him. She had not taken his grief. She had taken his capacity to grieve. She had taken the fact of emotion and replaced it with the knowledge of it—a thing you could think about but not feel.

When Aisen found him the next morning, Caspian was still writing. Line after line of the same sentence: *I should be sad. I should be sad. I should be sad.*

Aisen read it without comment.

"You didn't come to the funeral," Aisen said finally.

"I couldn't," Caspian said.

"Because you don't grieve," Aisen said, still not quite a question.

"Because I can't grieve," Caspian corrected. "There's a difference."

Aisen sat beside him. "Do you want to feel sad?"

"Yes," Caspian said. And it was true. He wanted it the way you want a place you've never been—intellectually, without the reality of desire. "But I can't."

"That will get worse," Aisen said.

"I know."

"And you're okay with it?"

Caspian put down his pen. "No," he said. "But okay is not an option anymore. Okay requires choices, and I've already made the only choice that mattered. Everything else is just consequence."

He looked at Aisen with eyes that had gold lines burning through them like scars.

"I'm becoming something that can't hurt," he said. "That's what Serafina promised. And she's keeping that promise. I can't hurt anymore because I can't feel anymore. I can't lose anymore because I can't want anymore. I'm being fixed."

"Fixed?" Aisen asked.

"Healed," Caspian said. "Protected. Take your pick of words. They all mean the same thing now. I'm becoming unburdened. And in a year, I won't even remember what the burden felt like."

Aisen stood and left without speaking. That was kindness too, in its way. The knowledge that some people cannot be comforted and the mercy of not trying.

Caspian returned to his journal. He wrote the same sentence ninety-seven times, and with each repetition, he could feel the truth of it slipping further away. By the hundredth time, the words were just shapes. By the hundred-fiftieth, they meant nothing.

He put down the pen. The heart in his chest beat its irregular rhythm. Thump-thump. Pause. Thump-thump. Gold and strange and not-his.

And Caspian, who could no longer grieve the loss of himself, simply sat in the darkness and waited to become someone new.
