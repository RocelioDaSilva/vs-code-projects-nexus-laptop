# ORIN: THE DRUNK CONFESSION

## Scene: The Campfire Unraveling

The fire had burned down to embers by the time Orin started talking. Aisen was repairing his chain, working methodically, asking no questions. This was what Orin needed—companionship without interrogation.

The wine had been warming them both. Orin had found a merchant's abandoned cask two days back. He drank from it slowly, letting the alcohol work into his bones.

"I was born angry," Orin said.

Aisen did not look up from his work.

"Not like normal. I was born already convinced that the world owed me something, and the world had no intention of paying. I could feel it from the start. From when I was five years old, watching other children get chosen for games, and I was always the fifth wheel. Not mean enough to reject, not good enough to want. Just... there."

Orin took another drink. His voice was slurring slightly, but his mind was clear. 

"I tried everything. I was helpful. I carried things. I cleaned. I cooked. I worked harder than anyone else. I was invisible. The more I tried, the less anyone saw me. It's like I was asking them to notice me, and by asking, I proved I wasn't worth noticing."

"So I stopped asking," Orin continued. "I stopped trying to be good. I became useful instead. I learned to fight. I learned to kill. I learned to be the thing you call on when you don't want to do something yourself. And suddenly—suddenly—people wanted me."

He laughed, but it was a hollow sound.

"They didn't love me. They didn't even like me. But they wanted me. They valued me. They would travel to find me, pay me, treat me well as long as I could do something for them. And I thought—God, I thought that was love. I thought if I could make myself needed enough, someone would eventually move from needing to caring."

Aisen's hands continued working the chain. The repetitive motion was soothing to watch.

"It never happened. I killed people. I solved problems. I fixed things that couldn't be fixed any other way. And when the job was done, I was dismissed. Sent away. Forgotten until the next time I was needed. I was a tool. Tools don't get loved. They get used and stored in the shed."

Orin's hands were shaking. Whether from alcohol or emotion, Aisen could not tell.

"The worst part is—the worst part is I *understood*. I understood why they didn't love me. Because I didn't love me. How could they love something I had already rejected? I was the first one to say I wasn't worthy of it. I proved it by accepting every debasement, every dismissal, every time someone treated me like I was just a sword with a mouth attached."

"One time," Orin said, his voice very quiet now, "I saved a village. Not from immediate danger, but from starvation. I hunted. I fought off bandits trying to steal their spring stores. I made it possible for them to survive the winter. And when the crisis was over, they tried to send me away without even a meal."

He looked at Aisen directly.

"The reason I stayed was not kindness. It was spite. I wanted to prove to myself that I was worth feeding. I think they recognized it. I think they fed me because they were afraid of me, not grateful to me."

Aisen set down the chain.

"Years later, that same village knew I was famous. I had become known in certain circles. They called me to save another problem. Different problem. And when I arrived, I recognized one of the women who had told me to leave years ago."

"She didn't recognize me at first. I was different by then—harder, more confident, carrying the weight of all the things I had done. But I recognized her."

"I asked her if she remembered the boy who hunted for them, who fought for them, who they had thrown out because he was not worth the cost of feeding."

"She remembered. And Aisen, she was *ashamed*. She felt guilt. She apologized."

Orin's voice broke slightly.

"And I felt nothing. No satisfaction. No validation. Just... empty. Because her guilt didn't give me back the years I had spent thinking I was worthless. It didn't change the fact that I had already decided she was right."

Orin drank again.

"That's when I understood: I will never be loved. Not because I'm unlovable. But because I stopped believing I was worth loving, and I made that disbelief so convincing that it became true. I became the thing I was afraid of. I became disposable."

"So now I accept my fate. I fight. I solve problems. I receive payment and dismissal in equal measure. I no longer even try to find the moments when payment might turn into connection. I know better."

Aisen picked up the chain again. He worked in silence for a long moment.

"You're wrong about something," Aisen said finally.

"Yeah? What's that?"

"You think you proved you were worthless by accepting debasement. But that's not what you proved. You proved you were willing to be useful even when debasement was the only payment offered. That's not worthlessness. That's just survival."

Orin wanted to argue, but something in Aisen's voice stopped him.

"The people who threw you out and later felt shame—they felt it because you were right. Because you *were* worth feeding. Because the cost wasn't actually that high, and they knew it. They just didn't care until you mattered to them outside their immediate crisis."

Aisen resumed his work.

"That doesn't mean you need to accept their love now. That doesn't mean you need anything from them. But it does mean that what you did for them—it meant something. You're not disposable. You're just... undervalued. By everyone but you."

Orin stared at the fire.

"If I stop being useful, what happens to me?"

"I don't know," Aisen said honestly. "But I'm willing to find out. If you want to stay when there's no immediate crisis. If you want to exist even when existence doesn't require you to solve something."

"Why would you offer that?"

"Because someone offered it to me once. And I haven't figured out how to refuse it yet."

Orin drank. The alcohol was starting to take deeper effect now. His eyes were getting heavy. But he was no longer shaking.

"I don't know how to be anything but useful anymore," he whispered.

"I don't know how to be anything but useful anymore," he whispered.

Aisen set the chain down and looked at him, not with instruction but with a kind of patient curiosity. "Then we'll figure it out slowly," he said. "Start with breakfast. Tell me something you like that isn't for anyone else. We'll practice small things. No requirements. No reward. Just... mornings."

Orin tried to laugh, failed, and then managed a tiny, incredulous smile. "Breakfast seems dangerous. I might be asked to cook for an army."

"Then cook for one person," Aisen answered.

Orin's laugh this time was softer. "Alright. One person."

As Orin drifted into sleep, he felt something he had only experienced a handful of times in his life.

It was not love. It was not even connection.

But it was something. And for the first time, he did not immediately decide it was worthless.
