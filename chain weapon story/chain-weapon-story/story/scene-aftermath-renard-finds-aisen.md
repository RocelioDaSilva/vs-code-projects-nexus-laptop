# The Aftermath

The pass was quiet.

Renard had expected to find bodies. He had expected to find soldiers looting, crows feasting. He had expected many things.

He had not expected to find Aisen alone.

The human was leaning against a broken wall of stone—his wall, the one he had made with his chain. The chain was scattered around him, links shattered, crystals cracked. His hands were frozen around a single remaining length.

His eyes were closed.

Renard stood still. He did not approach. He had seen death before. He had seen his mate's body, cold and stiff, an arrow in her chest. He had seen his brother's body, torn by wolves. He had seen too many.

But this one was different.

This one had chosen.

Renard knelt. He touched Aisen's wrist. No pulse. The skin was cold. He had been dead for hours.

Renard closed his eyes. He did not pray—his people did not pray to gods. They sang. But he had no song. His voice was empty.

He opened his eyes. He looked at Aisen's face. It was peaceful. Not happy—Aisen had never been happy. But peaceful.

*You were not supposed to be here.* Renard thought. *You were supposed to run.*

But Aisen had not run. He had stayed. He had fought. He had died.

Renard stood. He gathered the broken chain—the largest pieces, the ones that still held a faint resonance. He would take them to the shrine. He would sing the old songs. He would remember.

He lifted Aisen's body. It was lighter than he expected. The man had been starving himself. Saving rations for the villagers.

*Of course he had.*

Renard carried him down the pass. He did not look back. He did not need to. He would remember every step.
