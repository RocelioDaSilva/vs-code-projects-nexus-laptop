Harald walked in as if he had been summoned by the sound of a single, sharp bell. He had been walking many quiet streets that morning; that was what mentors did when they could not be in classrooms. He kept his pace even, not quick to the point of panic but quick enough to cross the distance with purpose.

He observed before he committed—this was a habit that had saved him from needless blows more than once. The crowd had folded itself around the trouble like a scattering of birds; gestures were frantic, faces wet, and the merchant Thorne's hands were trembling. Aisen's small shape stood near his father, a child with dirt on his cheek and something blue tucked in his small fist.

He stepped forward and did what needed doing, not loud but precise. He moved between a man and a path like the lay of a well-placed stone—steadying without dominating. When the largest thief tried to push past, Harald took his wrist in a manner that looked almost casual. The wrist did not break; the man felt the fact of being held. Harald's fingers closed in a grip that suggested comfort to those who understood the difference—firm, with intention.

"Hold the arm, watch the other," he said to the nearest guard and, when the guard hesitated, he gave the man a look that was as close to a teacher's reprimand as a soldier's order. The guard moved with the corrected energy of someone who had been trained to hold, not to crush.

Harald's eyes were the color of an old sword: grey, and when he fixed them on a thing they asked it if it had room for repentance. He looked at Aisen as if he were measuring wood for a frame. "Child," he said—there was no mockery in the word, only observation. "Is he hurt?"

Father answered in a voice that was raw with the aftertaste of adrenaline. Harald listened, and when the explanation ended he inclined his head once and the narrow lines at his eyes softened. He did not pat a child on the shoulder or make a show of anything; he assessed, and in assessment he found what to do next.

He bound the thief's wrist with a strip of rope, not to humiliate but to make the man incapable of sudden movement. He had no taste for cruelty; his work had always been to take the force out of force. He could break a bone if he wished—he had the strength—but he preferred to make the correct constraints that a man could remember and learn from. There was a dignity to a clean restraint.

After the market settled, when coins had been picked up and the crowd's pulse slowed, Harald said, quietly and without drama, "Lessons begin early in some places. Keep him close. Teach him to notice. Teach him redundancy—what to do when a hand finds you." He tapped his own wrist as if indicating a rhythm, a pattern.

Aisen watched him with the kind of attention that made small things larger. Harald's hands were not the quick, clever hands of a pickpocket; they were deliberate, patient instruments. That patience was a language. Aisen felt it like a surface under his feet and wanted, for a terrible, bright second, to be larger and still hold it.

Harald turned once, hand on the small thief's shoulder, and looked at the man like a man appraising timber. "Don't teach him to think himself a master of survival when he is only a student of luck," he said to the guard. "And make sure the merchant's report is taken."

He lowered his voice and addressed Father, who stood with the weight of a man who had been shoved into the teeth of the day. "He is quick-eyed. He will either be quick-hearted or quick-broken. If you can spare the time, and if you think the boy might listen, there's practice that will keep him from being only frightened by life."

Father looked at Aisen in a new way then, and Aisen felt that look land like a compass pin. The choice it asked for—allow him to practice, or shelter him—was larger than either of them in that moment. Harald did not press the answer. He only offered a steady presence and the possibility of direction.

As Harald departed, small and patient steps following the map he was used to, he left a footprint of calm that would hang over the market for days. People would tell the story in different ways—some would call it luck, some would call it coincidence—but for Aisen the shape of the hand that had stopped the thief and the sound of a man who measured himself before action would remain. It would press into him like a pattern he could imitate if he wished.

At the gate, Harald turned back once, the corner of his mouth lifting in something that might have been an offer or might have only been acknowledgment. "Learn to notice," he said, and then he was gone on the road, leaving behind the slow work of mending and counting.
