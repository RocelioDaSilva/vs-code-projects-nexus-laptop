# AISEN: THE PREPARATION WEEKS

## Quiet Reconnaissance

Aisen did not arrive at Thornwood by chance. The route had been walked by him in half-measures for months. He had been watching traffic patterns from the pass, noting supply timings, listening to how the garrison rotated its watch. He measured not just the enemy's strength but the impossible things—how long it took a fire to be noticed when started on a slope, which sentries took the longer route because they liked the smell of the north wind.

This was detective work, not heroics. He took notes in his head and then burned them. He formed mental maps and cross-checked two different days of rainfall to know which trails would be slippery in spring. He wasn't preparing for a single assault; he was preparing contingency after contingency.

## Supply Caches and Signals

Aisen placed caches of food and spare clothing for the refugees at intervals he hoped would match a night march. He did not leave himself notes. He left physical objects: a length of cloth tied to a branch, a stack of flat stones arranged in a small arrow, a pouch of salt buried under an oddly placed root. Each was simple, forgettable, and reliable.

He practiced the signals. One whistle meant "move now." Two whistles, spaced a certain way, meant "wait and stretch the line." The signals were not magic—they were habits. The point was to make the escape mechanistic so panic had fewer places to hide.

## Teaching in Fragments

He taught the method in fragments. To one woman he showed a single bodily maneuver that would reduce injury when carrying a child across a slope. To a retired teacher he explained a way to hide books in the geometry of a weaving pattern so that a policeman unfamiliar with looms would never notice them. To a boy he showed how to tie a boot so it would not come off in mud.

The lessons were small because small lessons do not attract attention. They were also effective because they were immediately useful. People could practice them the next day. They could keep them in their hands instead of in their heads where the Covenant might want to look.

## The Chain Check

Aisen worked on his chain. It had been his companion for years, and he had adapted techniques to use it quietly—binding, disarming, closing a gap. But he also knew a weapon could fail. It had a link that worried him. He tried to mend it once, twice. The metal would not take his repair cleanly.

He considered making a new chain. He considered inventing something he could better trust. In the end, he left the chain as it was and practiced contingency movements that did not rely on perfect equipment. If a link broke, he wanted to know how to keep the person defended for three breaths, five seconds, ten—however long it took someone else to make a choice.

## Recruiting Small Allies

Quietly, he cultivated allies without calling them that. He fed a garrison guard's child when the mother could not afford bread. He fixed a barrel for a merchant who later unwittingly carried messages for him. He kept the loyalty shallow enough that nothing would tie him publicly to those favors, but deep enough that those favors would be repaid when the moment came.

The most important of these alliances was with Renard. They met three times before the siege proper—once when Renard was still a soldier and the two shared water under a ruined wall, once when Aisen gave him a trick that saved his unit an embarrassing mistake, and once when they spoke frankly about what loyalty looked like.

Renard did not sign his name. He did not swear fealty. He simply promised to slow the garrison's response when Aisen's signal went out. That minimal promise became a hinge.

## Maps of Failure

Aisen drew maps not of victory but of failure—where the road would collapse, where a wagon might tip, where the rain would make a slope into mud. He knew that when you planned for failure, you reduce catastrophe.

He practiced walking paths with people carrying different loads and timed them. He ran exercises where a group would rehearse moving three useful people and two sacks through a narrow passage. They practiced in silence, with the kind of repetition that becomes habitual muscle memory.

## The Final Decisions

In the last week, he made final choices that were almost brutal in their simplicity. He chose the night they would walk, a night with the moon low enough to make shadows useful but high enough to keep the temperature tolerable. He chose three departure points instead of one. He chose the people who would move fastest at the front and those who would be placed at the back with a single instruction: "Do not fight to hold us in place. Bend when told to bend. Move when told to move." 

He told one person—an old healer—to watch the children and keep the story of why they left short. He set a baker with a single instruction: leave bread at marker two if you can. These were not orders so much as choreography.

## The Quiet Before

In the hour before the escape, the pass smelled of pine and cold metal. Aisen checked the caches twice. He hummed a small rhythm to himself to steady his hands. He slit his palm and smeared a drop of blood on the underside of the largest cache-stone—the kind of talisman he did not believe in but kept as a personal ritual.

He walked the pass one last time, touching a tree here, running his hand along a handhold there, making sure the path he had thought through so often matched the reality beneath his boots.

When the time came, he would not be heroic. He would be precise. He would do the thing that needed doing, then the next thing, then the small next thing after that. His life, in that moment, would be a string of small, exact movements.

And when the first whistle sounded, years of practical and private work would be tested. He had prepared not to win but to make a way. That was how he had always thought about the world.
