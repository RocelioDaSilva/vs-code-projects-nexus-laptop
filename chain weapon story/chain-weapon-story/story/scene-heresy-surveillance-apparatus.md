Master Wei kept a ledger that was never on any official inventory.

Aisen had seen glimpses of it twice by accident—once when a page had been left on Wei's desk, and once when Wei had closed it quickly enough that the gesture itself became a confession. The ledger was old paper, the kind that had been written over and then scraped clean and written over again. It was a palimpsest of suspicion.

'Come,' Wei said one evening, after advanced seminar. Aisen followed without question; Wei had the particular authority of someone who had taught enough to know exactly which students were dangerous and which were simply ambitious.

They walked to Wei's study, a narrow room that smelled of ink and something metallic—salt, perhaps, or the dust of old bindings. Wei closed the door and locked it with the kind of care that meant the gesture was significant.

'You are going to be watched,' Wei said without preamble. 'You already are being watched, in fact. I thought you should know this from someone who is not trying to trap you.'

Aisen felt the statement like a stone placed in still water. 'By whom?'

'By everyone. By no one in particular. That's how the system works.' Wei sat at his desk and opened the ledger. On the left page were names, some marked with simple notches, others with elaborate little marks that looked almost like a code. 'This is the Grey List. Students who are watched but not yet accused. Students who ask the right questions, who learn too quickly, who spend time with the wrong people.'

'How do they know who to watch?'

'Informants. Servants. Other students who want advancement.' Wei pointed to a name. 'This boy was on the Grey List for two years because he asked what would happen if the heresy laws were applied equally to nobles. He's gone now. Noble family bought him out.'

'Bought him out?'

'Removed him from the Academy before he could be formally accused. It's cleaner than execution.' Wei pointed to another name, then another. 'This one became an informant. This one left the country. This one—' he paused at a name written in different ink, circled multiple times, '—this one was executed three years ago.'

Aisen looked at the names and felt the shape of the system crystallize. It was not a system of rules; it was a system of observation. It was an apparatus designed to produce compliance through the threat of visibility.

'How do I get off the list?' Aisen asked.

'You don't. You're too intelligent, and intelligence is suspicious. What you do instead is learn to move within the system. You make allies among the watchers. You give them information they can trade upward so they're not in danger either.' Wei leaned back. 'You become useful rather than threatening.'

'How do I do that?'

Wei gestured vaguely. 'You've already started. You have a friend—Amara—who works for the intelligence apparatus. She will protect you if you give her reason to. She will report you if you become a liability. This is the nature of such arrangements.'

Aisen tried to process this. 'So I'm complicit in whatever she does?'

'You're alive,' Wei said flatly. 'Yes, that has a price. The question is whether the price is one you can live with.'

A servant entered then—a man Aisen had seen around the Academy for years but had never truly noticed. Wei handed him a coin without speaking, and the man left. The gesture was small and practiced.

'Even that was observed,' Wei said. 'There are people who note who I give coins to and how often. There are people who watch me watching you. The whole thing is a system of mirrors.'

'How do you bear it?' Aisen asked.

'By understanding the rules,' Wei said. 'The heresy laws exist because knowledge is power. If commoners learned what nobles can do, the power structure would collapse. So the system must be absolute, or it fails entirely. That means everyone must be watched, including the watchers. The system is not hypocritical—it is complete. It incorporates its own surveillance into the structure.'

He closed the ledger. 'You need to graduate quickly and quietly. You need to take what you learn and vanish into the mercenary economy where you can operate with less scrutiny. And you need to remember that every choice you make in that economy will ripple back to people you know.'

'You mean Mira,' Aisen said.

'I mean everyone. Your family is already marked because of you. Your association will carry consequence.'

The weight of that settled like a stone. Aisen had known abstractly that he was dangerous to people, but he had not understood it as a structural inevitability. He had thought it was a choice—that he could contain his danger through careful action. Now he understood that danger, once released into a system of observation, belonged to the system.

'There's a woman named Lor who works for the Satin Watch,' Wei continued. 'She will approach you sometime in the next months. She will offer you the chance to become an informant. You should agree. You should give her information that is true but not dangerous—things she could find elsewhere, things that are already known. This will protect you and will protect her.'

'How do you know this?'

'Because I told her to.'

Aisen looked at his teacher and felt the last of his innocence settle into the past. Wei was showing him that the system was inescapable because it was totalizing. The only survival strategy was complicity.

'Can we change it?' Aisen asked.

'Not from inside,' Wei said. 'Not while dependent on its structure. You can only change a system if you first remove yourself from it entirely.'

That stayed with him—the implication that freedom required exile. That you could not speak the truth inside the system; you could only speak it from outside, to people already willing to listen.

When Amara found him later that week and invited him to coffee, Aisen understood that Wei had arranged it. When Lor approached him the next month with her offer, Aisen accepted knowing he was accepting complicity. When he began to pass information upward through the apparatus, he understood that he was now part of the surveillance system he had hoped to resist.

It was not a betrayal because he had agreed to it. But it was a weight, and it was constant, and it would never fully lift.
