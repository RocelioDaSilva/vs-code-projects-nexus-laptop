They had been walking for three days.

The contract was half-finished—a merchant's shipment safely delivered to the federation checkpoint, payment scattered across their travel packs in coins that felt heavier than they should. Ahead, a village offered the promise of an inn. Behind them, the road had been empty all morning, which meant the bandits had moved on or found easier prey.

Aisen was tired. The kind of tired that sat in his bones.

Pieter was humming. He hummed when he was happy, the way other people might sing. It was out of tune, and he never completed a full melody, but the sound of it meant something had shifted in him—the weight had lightened.

"Tell me something," Pieter said as they crested a hill. The village was visible below them now, smoke rising from chimneys, the small shapes of people moving through streets. "Why did the merchant refuse to enter the tavern?"

Aisen did not answer. He knew the architecture of jokes. There would be a setup, a preamble, a reason to think about merchants and taverns. He would be expected to guess before the punchline, and when the punchline came, it would be bad. That was Pieter's gift: the ability to construct jokes so terrible they were almost profound in their uselessness.

"Because," Pieter continued, undeterred by the silence, "he was worried about the spirits."

Aisen waited. There was something coming.

"But the inn-keeper told him not to worry. The spirits were very reasonable. So the merchant asked how much he owed. And the inn-keeper said, 'The spirits don't accept payment. They accept—'" Pieter paused for effect, "—proposals."

It took Aisen a moment. When the meaning landed, he felt something he had not felt in months: the sharp, surprised impulse to laugh. It was not a response he had control over. His body simply did it.

A snort escaped his nose. Then a short, sharp bark of sound that was barely a laugh but close enough that Pieter's face transformed.

"Did you—did you just—" Pieter could barely speak through his own grinning.

"That was the worst joke I have ever heard," Aisen said.

"You laughed," Pieter said. It was a revelation in his voice. "I have spent four years trying to make you laugh, and that was what did it? That joke?"

"I did not laugh," Aisen said.

But he had. The evidence was still in his chest, a strange lightness that made his ribs feel hollow.

Pieter did a small, complicated dance move that was half-joy, half-stupidity. "The worst joke wins. The strategy works. I will now tell the worst jokes every day until you smile like a regular person."

"Please do not."

"I will do it," Pieter said. "Why did the telekinetic give up magic?"

"Do not."

"Because he lost his mind."

Despite himself, despite everything, Aisen felt the corner of his mouth threaten to curve. He pressed his lips together. The feeling was wrong—too light, too close to joy, which seemed frivolous when there were people dying in wars and children starving in camps.

But Pieter was walking beside him, humming again, waiting for the next joke to land or fail, and Aisen realized something he had not let himself consider in a very long time: the lightness was not frivolous. It was a reprieve. It was necessary.

They walked down the hill toward the village. Pieter told three more jokes. Each was worse than the last. By the time they reached the inn, Aisen had almost-smiled twice, which Pieter seemed to count as a complete victory.

Inside the inn, over food and cheap wine, Pieter ordered another round and said, "You are going to die doing this. The war. Fighting for people who will never know it was worth it."

Aisen did not argue. It was true.

"So while you have the time," Pieter continued, "let me tell you all the worst jokes. Let me be the person who reminds you that there is some lightness left. Some stupidity worth experiencing. Can you do that?"

Aisen picked up his wine glass. It was scratched, plain, one of a thousand identical glasses worn smooth by other hands. He drank from it slowly.

"All right," he said.

"All right?"

"Tell me the jokes," Aisen said. "All of them. The worst collection you have."

Pieter's face did something Aisen had never seen before—it cracked open into genuine joy. Not performance, not trying-hard. Just simple happiness at being heard.

"Why did the vampire become a lawyer?" Pieter started.

And Aisen, against all his better judgment, against everything his years of training had taught him about efficiency and survival, sat in a shabby inn in a village whose name he would never remember, and listened to his friend tell ridiculous jokes.

When he laughed again—a real laugh, not just a snort, but actual laughter that came from his belly—Pieter reached over and squeezed his shoulder.

"There you are," Pieter said quietly. "I was wondering where you went."

Aisen did not answer. But he let the warmth of it sit. He let himself be light for a moment. He let himself be, improbably and temporarily, happy.

It would not last. Tomorrow the war would still be waiting. But tonight, in a tavern that smelled like stale bread and cheap ale, with a friend who had spent years trying to reach the unreachable part of him, Aisen was laughing.

That was enough.

It was more than he had any right to expect.
