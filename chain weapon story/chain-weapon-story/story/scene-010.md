Summary:

Lin moved through the servant passages like water through narrow pipes—quiet, steady, and unnoticed. Her pale skin caught the dim light in a way that made other people look away; she had learned to let that happen. She cleaned offices and swept corridors, carrying crisp linens and empty trays while listening for the small noises that told a building's secrets.

That afternoon, when the Academy's upper halls were full of professors' footsteps and the soft clack of debate, Lin was clearing Master Halvor's office. She found the note by accident: a folded margin protruding from a ledger, tucked beneath a scrap of wrapping paper, the ink dark on the crease.

Her hands, meticulous by habit, unfolded it like a careful cook peeling a fruit. The handwriting was spidery and hurried; the note spoke of a ritual and used a phrase that made Lin's stomach tighten—'blood sanctification'—followed by a place and a time. It was not a page of doctrine, more an instruction: this is where it will be done, this is what is needed.

Lin's first instinct was to fold it back and put it where she had found it. She had seen many things she was not meant to see and had learned that survival often meant pretending ignorance. But the note did not read like a harmless memo; the edges of the paper had a faint powder on them, the sort of residue used in ceremonial bindings. The idea that such things might be practiced within the Academy made her fingers go cold.

She considered the room. Halvor's ledger lay open on his desk; a map showed marked sites along the river. Voices floated from the corridor—students debating, a scholar's laugh. No one in the hall had any reason to suspect an albino kitchen girl of mischief.

Lin hid the note in the hem of her apron and finished her tasks with the practiced care of someone who wished not to be noticed. Later, in the scullery where the steam and clatter made a small country of noise, she smoothed the paper on a wooden table and read it again by the muted light of a lantern. The words felt like a key; if it fit, she would find a lock.

She did not tell the steward. She did not tell the head cook. She thought of the people who would be most vulnerable if the Academy's rituals were more than rumor—the students who came from villages who believed in different rites, the servants who cleared rooms and might be used unawares. Her decisions, she knew, could cause trouble. But they could also save someone.

So Lin began to watch. She learned the professor's schedule, the time he took his midday coffee, the hours when his door remained open for a minute too long. She watched how certain students lingered in the corridors after lectures, their hands folded as if to hide anything they carried. She made small notes in a folded scrap, diagrams of the office and a list of names she heard in passing.

When she decided to move the note, she did so with the stealth of a girl who had practiced silence. She slid the paper into a hollow in a broom handle—a hiding place no one would think to check—and marked that broom with a nick only she could feel. That way, if Halvor searched his room, the original would be gone and the ledger would look complete. The note would be safe with her until she knew more.

It was, she told herself, the smallest kind of resistance: a piece of paper kept out of reach. Later, if the risk called for it, she might pass the information along to someone who could do more. For now, she did what the servants did best—observe, collect, and wait.

Lin didn't plan to be a spy. The world had a way of handing jobs to those who moved through its seams unnoticed, and she discovered a strange competence in that invisibility. Over a week she sketched the professor's habits in the margins of her mind: where he kept his quills, the times the shutters were left cracked, the apprentice who fetched his ink at dusk. She learned which students quietly carried extra cloaks and which ones wore the look of someone measured by other people's expectations.

At night, when the kitchen dwindled to a few coals and the pantry smelled of starch and lemon, Lin would warm her hands around a cup and read the note again, committing phrases to memory. Each line felt heavier the more she thought of it as evidence rather than rumor. She began to imagine how a single ritual, performed without witnesses, could shift what the Academy meant to those who didn't understand its named terms.

Her watch produced small prizes. A student with a limp always paused by Halvor's door when the professor left his ledger open; a janitor with a loose tongue mentioned, in passing, that Halvor took tea at the west window on the third day of market. Lin started a tiny map: a scrap with arrows and time stamps that she folded and refolded, hiding it inside a spoon box in the scullery.

She also learned restraint. When a cleaner's curiosity would have sent a kitchen girl to the steward with news, Lin held back. She had seen the way accusations could be swallowed by larger hands and turned into danger for the one who sounded the alarm. So she waited until she could pair a name with a pattern, a time with a place, until she had something that could be shown without looking like malice.

Finally, when she felt the note could be used, she did not run to the watch or the steward but to someone quieter: a young student who often lingered in the stacks—Aisen's sort of careful reader—or perhaps Master Tholen, who loved the smell of paper. She left a tiny token where he would find it: a chipped spoon with a nick she knew he admired. It was a slender gesture—an invitation and a test. If the student cared enough to ask, Lin would decide whether to reveal the broom-hollow or keep the paper tucked until a clearer need arose.

The choice to pass the secret forward was itself a kind of trust. Lin had no illusions about heroism; she knew the slow arithmetic of risk. But she also knew that silence could be complicity, and that some small interventions—hidden paper, a marked broom, a spoon left in the right hand—could be the difference between a private rite and a public harm.
