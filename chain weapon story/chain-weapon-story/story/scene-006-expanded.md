The tavern did not notice the absence all at once; it let itself fray in small, practical ways, as if each ordinary thing could afford the space to loosen. Plates were left where someone had eaten and lingered rather than carried away; the back door sighed on its hinges more often than it slammed; the kettle sang a note with no answer and then sank to a lower simmer. Marta kept her broom moving because movement made absence manageable, and because keeping the hearth tidy felt like the right kind of faith.

Thorne set a smooth, dark stone on the scarred counter as if it were a coin to be counted. He had the habit of leaving small things behind—pebbles rubbed by river water, a splinter of road-wood each worn to a private polish. Marta watched the stone catch the light and hold it like a pocket of promise.

'What do you call that for?' she asked, quieter than she meant to be.

'For the long roads,' Thorne said. 'For steady feet. For the sort of boy who forgets to look both ways.'

Aldric laughed, the sound coming out rough and unpracticed, like a throat that had gone too long between honest noises. He wiped his hands on a rag and smoothed the edge of a glass as if to steady himself.

'An academy,' he said. 'They take boys and teach 'em letters and histories and the ways of the world. If they like 'em, they swear 'em into something else. War, maybe. Service. Places where a man's name comes with a blade and a duty.'

Around them, the regulars added their own small threads to the day's story. Old Marta—no relation, only the name they treated like an anchor—told of Aisen's first winter, how he held a hollowed potato to a shivering child and insisted it be warm enough. She mimed tucking a portion of bread beneath a tunic and slipping it to a stray that had followed him home. Bill, the barkeep, laughed so easily that for a second the rafters seemed less empty; then the laugh cracked and his eyes got wet.

Laughter is often a way of stitching holes, Marta thought. People stitch the past with jokes so the holes look smaller. It worked sometimes, though not always. The rafters may look less empty by the hour, but the space where Aisen stood when he would sweep a crate aside to make room for a neighbor remained a place the eye wanted to find and could not.

She listened to the litany of small mercies and small cruelties from which the village made its map: how Aisen tended to quarrels with a patient tongue, how he carried embers for Mrs. Penk so she wouldn't bend, how he once climbed the rafters to fetch a cat and returned flushed with the nonsense of a child who had learned to laugh. Each memory was a pebble placed in a line toward whoever would be traveling Aisen's roads now.

'He learned to be careful here,' Marta said, surprising herself with how steady her voice felt. 'He learned to notice what others need. That's not nothing.'

Thorne rolled the stone between fingers that had the long grace of someone who had worked with tools all his life. 'They'll make of him what they will,' he said. 'But whatever the Academy teaches, it can't pluck out the parts that were grown here. You can't unlearn how to tie a child's shoe.'

Aldric's mouth softened. 'Maybe. But an institution shapes a man. Shapes his loyalties. We don't know if he'll bring what he learns back, or if the world will keep him.'

They spoke of change as if it were a storm that could be weathered with rope and patience. Marta thought of being in the middle of fires and helping manage embers—how a slip of attention could turn a warm hearth into a blaze. People spent much of their lives learning which embers needed poking and which needed leaving alone.

The practical talk came next; loss is a thing that makes lists. Who will fetch the herbs for market day? Who will mind the door when the west wind comes like a hound? Who will carry the extra load for old Mrs. Penk when her knees refuse? Marta assigned small duties in a voice that was both manager and mother: send Jan for the kettle on Tuesday, tell Mara to cover an extra shift, let Thorne keep the stone until Aisen writes or returns.

She made the lists the way a midwife marks contractions: not because it could halt what had begun, but because counting made the pain legible. People around her clasped the tasks like talismans.

As the night thinned and the last patron left, the tavern settled into a different kind of quiet. The coals in the hearth were a slow, contented glow. Marta wiped down the counter and paused to look at Aisen's cup, nicked and worn to a pale sheen at the rim. She set the wooden cup beside Thorne's stone.

Thorne caught her hand as she passed. 'For the road,' he said simply.

Marta nodded. The gesture felt like a bridge built of small things. The town had no banners to wave; it had a cup and a stone and a roster of shifts and a chorus of stories that followed Aisen like a trailing cloak. For the first time since he'd left, the emptiness felt less like a wound and more like a ground where something might be planted, if someone kept watch.

She walked the tavern once more before undoing the latch. Her broom paused at the corner where dust gathered in the crossbeam. She ran the brush across the beam until a faint line appeared and then, from a small box beneath the counter, she pulled a scrap of faded cloth—a remnant of a shirt, frayed at the edges. Marta tied the cloth into a rough knot and tucked it beneath the stone.

It was foolish, perhaps, to think a scrap of cloth could mean much to a man on a long road. But foolishness can be a kind of prayer. People needed ways to hold on while the world rearranged itself.

When she stepped outside, the air was colder, and the sound of the river nearby thinned the night. She looked at the road that ran past the tavern and thought of all the roads that bent and wove through the world. She imagined Aisen walking one, carrying small kindnesses like pockets of light.

A child from the lane—Tomkin, no more than seven—had lingered on the step. He shuffled forward, cheeks flushed, and set a small folded note beside the cup. The handwriting was clumsy, but the words were the same as any of theirs: 'Come back.'

Marta smiled in the way she sometimes did when a stitch came together. It was not everything. It would not protect anyone from the orders or the duties that might change Aisen. But for a moment, the village had wrapped itself in a simple, human thing: memory made small and kept warm.

As she latched the door and turned the key, Marta said into the quiet, not sure whether she spoke to the town or to herself, 'We'll keep the hearth.'

She meant more than the fire. She meant to keep the ways people were careful with one another; the lists, the small kindnesses, the monitoring of embers. In the months to come, those things might be what held them steady when larger storms came.
