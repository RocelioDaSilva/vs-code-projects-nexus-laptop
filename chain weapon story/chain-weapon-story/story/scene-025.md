The cave breathed. That was how Elder Meth always thought of it—not a dead space but a living one, its walls exhaling the slow mineral scent of water that had passed through limestone for centuries. The entrance was hidden behind a fall of creeper vine that the clan kept trimmed just enough to walk through, and the path down was worn smooth by generations of feet that had been taught, one by one, never to come here in daylight and never to come here alone.

Meth arrived first, as was his right and his burden. He was old enough that his knees complained on the descent but not so old that he needed a hand. His hair was white at the temples and bound with a leather cord dyed in the blue of the Celestial uplands, and his fingers—long, careful, stained at the tips with the ash of ceremonial inks—moved along the cave wall as he walked, reading the stone's temperature the way a doctor reads a pulse.

He set the circle. Four lanterns, low-burning, placed at the cardinal points of the cave's widest chamber. Between them, he laid a cloth of undyed wool—the meeting cloth, old enough that its edges were frayed into the soft, gray texture of age but still whole where it mattered. On the cloth he placed the clan's memory stone: a flat disc of jade, carved on one face with the lineage spiral and on the other worn smooth by the thumbs of every elder who had held a meeting in this chamber for three hundred years.

The others came in ones and twos, stepping through the vine curtain with the practiced silence of people who had been taught that arrival was not an announcement but a contribution. Elder Solenne came next—a woman whose age showed in her hands but not her eyes. She carried a bundle of dried herbs that she placed at the eastern lamp, their smoke intended to thin the air and open the throat for honest speech. Behind her, Keeper Yaen: the clan's records-bearer, a man whose face was mapped with the fine tattoo lines of the Celestial tradition, each line a memorized verse of the clan's history inked directly into his skin.

Eight adults settled into the circle. No children were present yet. That would come later, if the vote went a certain way.

Meth opened his palms above the memory stone. "We gather because the Covenant has sent another envoy to the valley. Three weeks ago, Envoy Darshan met with the regional governor and presented a proposal to standardize magical instruction across the Celestial territories."

Silence. The word "standardize" had a weight in this room that outsiders would not feel. To the Covenant, standardization meant efficiency—a common curriculum, common tests, common language for describing what magic did. To the clan, it meant the extinction of everything they had kept alive in caves like this one: the specific breathing techniques passed from elder to student in unbroken chains of personal instruction, the meditation forms that could not be written down because their power lay in the relationship between teacher and student, the tattoo-verses that encoded not just history but method.

Solenne spoke first. Her voice was measured, the way water moves over flat stone—steady, unhurried, carrying weight without rushing. "Darshan's proposal includes a clause requiring all magical instruction to be registered with the regional academy within six months. Unregistered techniques will be classified as unsanctioned."

"Unsanctioned." Yaen repeated the word like a man tasting something rotten. "Which means heretical, in the Covenant's dictionary."

"Not yet heretical," Meth said carefully. "Unsanctioned is a staging ground. It means our techniques will be documented, examined, and assessed for compatibility with the Covenant's framework. Those that pass will be absorbed. Those that fail will be restricted. Those that resist documentation will be classified."

"And classified means hunted," Solenne said.

Meth inclined his head. The progression was familiar to everyone in the room: registration, assessment, classification, restriction, enforcement. The Covenant did not begin with violence. It began with paperwork, and the paperwork created a framework within which violence became administrative rather than aggressive—a matter of compliance, not conquest.

A younger elder, Kharris, shifted on the cloth. He was broad-shouldered and spoke with the blunt efficiency of a man who had spent more years in the field than in ceremony. "What do we lose if we comply? Honestly. If we register our techniques—what changes?"

Meth took a slow breath. This was the question that tested every elder, because the honest answer was complicated and the simple answer was a lie. "If we register, we preserve the forms but lose the context. The Covenant will record the steps of a meditation but not the relationship between teacher and student that gives it meaning. They will document the breathing pattern but not the years of trust that allow the student to reach the depth where the pattern works. They will take the shell and call it the creature."

"So we lose the soul," Solenne said.

"We lose the transmission method. Which is the soul."

Yaen touched the lines on his face—an unconscious gesture, protective, the way a man touches a scar. "Every verse on my skin was given to me by my teacher's voice, in sequence, in relationship. If you write those verses in a book, they become information. They stop being teaching. And the Covenant will notice the difference—they will notice that the documented version doesn't produce the same results—and they will conclude that we are withholding the 'real' technique. Which we will be, because the real technique cannot be written."

The cave's breath moved across them—cold, damp, patient.

"So we resist," Kharris said. Not a question.

Meth raised a hand—not to silence him but to slow the room. "We resist, but we must decide what form resistance takes. Open defiance invites inspection. Inspection invites classification. Classification invites enforcement. We have seen this in the lowland clans. Three villages registered their water-singing techniques last year. Within four months, their best students had been recruited to the Academy, their teachers assessed and licensed, and their practice halls converted to registered instruction centers. The technique survives in name. The tradition is dead."

"Hidden teaching, then," Solenne said. "As we have done before."

"Hidden teaching is dangerous," Meth replied. "Not because the Covenant discovers it immediately—they rarely do immediately. It is dangerous because it requires us to choose which students we trust. And choosing means some children will be taught and others will not, and the ones who are not taught will wonder why, and some of them will be angry enough to report what they suspect."

The room held several breaths at once.

"We also need to decide about the girl," Yaen said quietly.

The girl was Rin. She was not in the room, but her name moved through the circle like a current. Rin, who was nine years old, who had the stillest hands anyone had seen in a child, who listened the way the cave listened—completely, without judgment, absorbing everything. Rin, whose mother had been a Keeper before she died and whose father served in the lowland garrison and sent letters that were carefully emptied of anything useful.

"She has the aptitude," Solenne said.

"She has more than aptitude," Yaen corrected. "She has the temperament. She remembers what she is told, and she does not repeat what she remembers. I tested her last month—gave her a verse and asked her to keep it for a week without speaking it. She kept it for ten days and then recited it perfectly, in her sleep, to an empty room. I was listening from the corridor."

"That is not a test of teaching," Kharris said. "That is a test of secrecy."

"In the world the Covenant is building," Meth said, "secrecy and teaching are the same skill."

The lamps flickered. Someone outside the cave—a sentry, one of three who watched the approaches—tapped a stone twice against the rock: the signal for "clear, no movement." The sound arrived like punctuation, a reminder that even this conversation required guards.

Meth looked at each face in the circle. "I propose that we begin training Rin in the hidden curriculum. Not the registered forms—those she can learn at the village school—but the transmission techniques, the memory verses, the meditation depths that require personal instruction. I propose that Yaen be her primary teacher, with Solenne as her second, and that the training begin in this cave, at night, with no written record."

"And if she is discovered?" Kharris asked.

"Then we will say she is learning folk songs from her grandmother's tradition. The Covenant does not yet classify folk songs."

"Yet," Solenne repeated.

"Yet," Meth agreed.

The vote was taken by stone. Each elder placed a hand on the memory stone; those who agreed pressed their thumb into the worn face, those who dissented placed their fingers on the edge. Meth counted by touch, his eyes closed, and when he opened them the count was seven to one in favor. Kharris had dissented—not from disagreement, Meth knew, but from the soldier's habit of voting against risk as a way of acknowledging it.

"It is decided." Meth lifted the stone and held it so the lantern light caught the spiral. "We train Rin. We continue hidden instruction for the three students already in progress. We register our surface techniques with the Covenant's envoy to maintain compliance, and we keep the deep practices here, in the dark, where they have lived for three centuries."

He paused, and the cave's breath filled the silence.

"One more thing. Rin will also be trained in observation and intelligence. Not because we wish to make her a spy—" he looked at Yaen, whose face had tightened—"but because the world she will enter requires her to be both a student and a keeper. She must carry the tradition, and she must survive carrying it. In this time, those are not separate skills."

Yaen touched his tattooed face again, and this time the gesture was deliberate—an acknowledgment, a transfer. "I will teach her the verses," he said. "And I will teach her when to be silent about them."

The meeting ended without ceremony. The lanterns were dimmed, the cloth folded, the memory stone returned to the pocket of Meth's coat where it lived between gatherings. The elders left as they had come: one by one, through the vine curtain, into the night that smelled of mountain and distance.

Meth waited last, his hand on the cave wall. The stone was cool and steady under his palm—old enough to outlast anyone's politics, patient enough to hold what was placed against it. He thought of Rin, asleep somewhere in the village, her hands still even in dreams. He thought of the Covenant's envoy, sleeping in clean sheets at the governor's house, his briefcase full of forms that would reduce three hundred years of transmitted knowledge to a line on a ledger.

He extinguished the last lamp and climbed the path by memory, his feet finding each worn step as they had been taught to find them—by feel, in the dark, trusting what had been passed to him by someone who had climbed the same path before.
