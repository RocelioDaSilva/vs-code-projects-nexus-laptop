# ALDRIC'S EXECUTION: The Formative Trauma

## Scene: Public Death of a Healer

The marketplace of Thornshire was crowded, though it was not market day. News traveled through the settlement in whispers—the kind of whispers that summon witnesses. They came: merchants, parents, children, goatherds. They came because the Covenant said they must attend. They came because the warning had to be seen to be believed.

Aisen was twelve.

He stood at the edge of the crowd, small enough to fit between adults, tall enough to see. Aldric had told him to stay home. Aldric had told him many things in the past week—all of them lies meant to be kind.

The platform had been built in the center of the square. It was crude wood, already stained. Aisen wondered how many people had stood where Aldric would stand. He wondered if that number mattered.

The soldiers came first. Four of them, moving in formation, their armor catching the autumn sun. They moved with the precision of men who had done this before. Behind them came the prisoner.

Aldric looked smaller on the platform. That was wrong. Aldric was not a small man. But the platform made everything small.

He wore no chains—the Covenant did not chain prisoners who were about to be erased. There was a mercy in that, Aisen would realize later. No weight to carry into whatever came next.

A man in gray robes—a Covenant inquisitor, Aisen would learn the title meant nothing and everything—stepped forward with a scroll. His voice carried across the square with the practiced clarity of someone who had rehearsed this speech many times.

*"Aldric of Thornshire, you stand convicted of unauthorized instruction in forbidden techniques. Specifically, the unauthorized teaching of the Healing Flux to three common persons, including one minor. As a result of this instruction, when the noble son Castellan Vex fell ill, the proper hierarchy of care was circumvented. A common healer was called. The boy lived—but this was an accident of outcome, not an outcome of proper procedure. You have violated the core principle of information restriction."*

Aisen's hands were numb. They had always been numb since Aldric's arrest.

The inquisitor continued: *"The sentence is Flux severance, followed by execution. May your dissolution serve as warning."*

The inquisitor stepped back.

One of the soldiers approached Aldric with a small bronze vessel. Inside it was something that made the air shimmer—Aisen could see the heat distortion even from the crowd. Void-touched water. Corruption waiting to unspool.

Aldric did not run. That surprised no one who knew him. Running was for people with other options.

The soldier tilted the vessel. The liquid poured onto Aldric's skin.

What happened next was not death—it was slower than death.

Aldric's scream came first, a sound that seemed to tear from a place deeper than his body. The liquid burned, but not with fire. Aisen understood, watching, that this was something different. The Flux that lived in Aldric—the subtle magic that let him heal, that he had used every day since childhood—was being severed from him. Violently. From the outside in.

His skin began to char, but not uniformly. The burning followed lines of light that only Aldric could see—the channels of magical resonance that ran through him. The Void-touched water was not destroying his flesh; it was destroying his connection to the Weave itself.

One of the merchants beside Aisen looked away. Another reached for her child. No one left.

This was the warning. The warning had to be witnessed.

Aldric's body convulsed. His hands opened and closed, grasping at air. The robes he wore began to smoke. The burning spread across his chest, his arms, his face.

And then—a moment of terrible clarity. Aldric's eyes found the crowd. They found Aisen.

For a single second, Aisen saw his mentor look at him. Not in accusation. Not in blame. But in recognition of what Aisen was witnessing. A knowledge passing between them that could not be spoken aloud.

*Live differently,* that look said. *Not like me.*

Aldric's body folded. His legs gave. He fell to his knees on the platform, still burning, still screaming—though the sound had become something less than human, a rawer frequency of agony that made Aisen's teeth ache.

The inquisitor waited, patient. This was procedure. The Flux severance had to run its course before the killing blow came. The Covenant believed in completeness.

It took five minutes. Aisen counted. One, two, three—mentally numbering each breath he took, each moment that Aldric continued to exist.

When the final soldier stepped forward with his sword, the mercy seemed almost obscene.

The blade entered between Aldric's shoulder blades. He was already half-burned, already broken. The sword was simply a period at the end of a sentence that had been too long.

Aldric stopped moving.

The body remained on the platform for the crowd to see. The Covenant representative stepped forward again and announced that anyone found teaching forbidden techniques would face the same fate. Anyone found *learning* forbidden techniques would face trial. Anyone who suspected their neighbor of either offense could report to the garrison.

The crowd began to disperse.

Aisen did not move until the merchant beside him gently touched his shoulder and suggested he leave. Then he walked. He does not remember the walk home. He does not remember if he wept. He remembers the feeling of Aldric's eyes finding him in the crowd, and the knowledge that came with it: *information is death, and I have taught you information.*

## Aftermath: Aisen's Thirteen Years of Hiding

That night, Aisen took every book Aldric had given him and burned them in the courtyard.

He memorized seven of them first. Not the techniques. The warnings. The cautions. The patterns of what the Covenant hunted.

He learned, in those burned pages, that Aldric was only one of four people executed that month for teaching forbidden knowledge. He learned that the estates of executed heretics were seized. He learned that anyone who had studied under them became suspect.

And he learned, over the next thirteen years, that survival meant becoming small. Not weak—small. Forgettable. A person whose knowledge was so distributed, so manifested in practical skill rather than theoretical understanding, that no one could prosecute him for knowing too much.

He learned to move through the world as a shadow. He learned that kindness was dangerous—because Aldric had shown kindness, and kindness had led him to teach, and teaching had led him to the platform.

He learned that the best way to honor Aldric was to be everything Aldric was not: guarded, careful, alone.

And he learned, understanding this, that he was becoming a different person than the boy who had stood in that crowd and watched his mentor burn.

The Covenant had taken Aldric's life. But Aisen had taken something else: his own capacity to trust easily, his own willingness to be known completely, his own belief that knowledge could be freely shared without cost.

In the years after Aldric died, Aisen sometimes wondered if the Covenant had achieved what they intended—not just the death of one healer, but the death of a thousand potential ones. Because every boy who watched a mentor burn learned the same lesson.

*Stay small. Stay hidden. Stay alone. And never, ever teach what you know.*

It was the perfect system. It did not require many executions. Just enough to remind everyone what happened to people who did not obey.

Years later, standing in the ruins of Thornwood Hollow, Aisen would understand that he had spent his entire life trying to prove the system wrong. And he would fail, because the system was too perfect, and he had learned his lessons too well.

But in that moment, at twelve years old, he simply learned to burn.
