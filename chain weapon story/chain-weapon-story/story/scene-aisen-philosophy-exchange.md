The child had survived three wars.

That was remarkable for a being so small. She was maybe seven years old, maybe younger. It was hard to tell with refugee children—hunger and trauma aged them in ways that had nothing to do with years.

She was sitting beside Aisen on a log, eating bread that Renard had given her. They were, for the moment, in a place between conflicts. Not safe, but not actively dangerous. Those moments were rare.

"Why do people fight?" she asked.

It was the question that destroyed most adults. It was the question that made philosophers and generals and priests struggle for hours and produce answers that satisfied no one. Aisen had learned long ago not to pretend he knew.

"Because they want different things," he said.

"But why can't they just... not?" The child looked at him with the simplicity of someone who had not yet learned that most problems could not be solved through obvious solutions.

"Because sometimes the different things are important enough," Aisen said. "And sometimes people are afraid of what will happen if they do not fight."

The child chewed her bread. "Is fighting justice?"

Aisen paused. This was the kind of question that required him to think about what he actually believed versus what sounded right.

"No," he said finally. "Fighting is sometimes necessary. But justice is different. Justice is... trying to make things right when they have been made wrong. But it is also... difficult. Because sometimes 'right' looks different depending on who is looking at it."

Renard, who had been listening from a distance, approached. In human form now, which the child had learned not to be afraid of. He sat down on the other side of the child.

"A fox-kin elder asked me once," Renard said, "what justice meant for us. My people were persecuted. Hunted. So I said justice meant freedom. But the humans said justice meant order. Do you know what justice actually was?"

The child shook her head.

"It was neither," Renard said. "It was change. Slow, difficult, incomplete change, where both sides lost something and both sides gained something, and no one was entirely satisfied. That is the only kind of justice that exists."

The child was quiet. She had probably seen enough injustice in her short life to know that Renard was telling the truth. The world was not fair. Fighting did not fix it. Growing up happened anyway.

"Do you think there will be another war?" she asked.

"Eventually," Aisen said. "Not tomorrow. Not because we have found justice, but because we have found something like... temporary balance. And balance is always temporary."

"Is that sad?" the child asked.

Aisen looked at Renard. Renard looked at the child. And for a moment, they seemed to be sharing the weight of knowing something that should not be required knowledge for someone so young.

"Yes," Aisen said. "It is sad. Because it means that people you love will suffer. And there is no guarantee that anything better will come. But it is also... it is also the reason that any of us tries at all. Because we know it is temporary. So we work to make the temporary as good as it can be."

The child finished her bread. She did not ask more questions. But she leaned against Aisen's shoulder, and Aisen let her, because that was the kind of communication that transcended all the complicated philosophy.

Sometimes justice was just someone being willing to sit beside you while you tried to understand a broken world.

Later, Renard said quietly, "You answered her better than I did."

"I do not think I answered at all," Aisen replied. "I think I just acknowledged that there are no good answers."

"That," Renard said, "is the answer."

And they sat in the space between conflicts, and the child slept between them, and for a moment, they were not fighting. They were not philosophers. They were just alive, in a world that was not fair, doing what small edges they could manage.

It would not last.

But for now, it was enough.
