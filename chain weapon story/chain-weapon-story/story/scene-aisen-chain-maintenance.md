The workbench was arranged the same way every time.

Chain segments spread left to right in order: the main body, the throwing chains, the binding chains, the tracking link, the backup links. Each segment occupied its own space, the way books occupy shelves. Beside the chain: cleaning cloth, abrasive stone, resonance dampener, small hammer, replacement links carved from the last of his father's iron.

The smell of the oil was familiar now—not pleasant, exactly, but necessary. The same oil his father had used on his tools. Aisen could close his eyes and know where he was by that smell alone.

He picked up the first segment and began.

Light touch on the cleaning cloth, working into each gap where the links met. The steel was dark, worn smooth by years of handling, pitted in places where corrosion had taken small bites. His father's spear had been the same way—a tool that bore the marks of use without shame. The best weapons were not pretty. They were honest.

Aisen ran the cloth along each link, removing the accumulation of the day: rust dust, salt from sweat, the small debris of living in a world that was constantly trying to break things down.

The second link bore a hairline crack. He had been waiting for it to worsen, watching it grow millimeter by millimeter. Tonight was the night to replace it. He measured twice, then went to the small case where his father's iron lived.

The replacement link was pre-shaped—his father had done the work years ago, anticipating the day when this particular link would fail. That was the kind of careful that Aisen had inherited, the kind that prepared for predictable failure. He fitted the new link into place, using the small hammer to set it flush with its neighbors. Each strike was precise, measured, part of a rhythm he had learned before he could read.

His father had taught him this when he was seven. They had sat at a workbench in the cottage, and his father had shown him how to hammer a nail without bending it. How to listen for the sound that meant it was seated properly. How to treat a tool with the respect it deserved.

That lesson had taken a year. Aisen had ruined seventeen nails learning it.

He tested the new link by working the chain through its motion. The movement was smooth. The replacement seated perfectly. His father would have approved.

Next, the resonance links. These required the dampener—a cloth soaked in a special oil that muffled the hum of Flux moving through the steel. Aisen worked carefully here, ensuring the resonance was even across all the tuned links. If one vibrated incorrectly, the whole device would be off-balance. The chain had to be calibrated like an instrument, each part in harmony with the others.

He had learned this at the Academy, from a master smith who had spent three hours teaching Aisen why precision mattered. The master had seemed disappointed at the end. "You understand intellectually," she had said. "But you do not *feel* it yet. You will, when you have almost died because a misaligned link cost you a tenth of a second."

Aisen had almost died three times since then. Each time, he had felt exact where the precision was required.

The work took two hours. By the end, his chain was clean, maintained, calibrated. It coiled on the workbench like a sleeping thing, ready to move at his command. There was no rust. No misalignment. No weakness.

He stood and stretched. His back protested—the work required him to lean forward, to concentrate, to keep his body still while his hands moved. The tension in his shoulders was familiar. It was the cost of caring for something that kept him alive.

He packed away the tools. Everything returned to its place. The workbench empty. The chain waiting in the darkness for tomorrow, when he would uncoil it and move with it and trust that his maintenance would serve him.

Before he set the chain aside, he ran his fingers along it one more time. The metal was cool under his fingertips. Smooth where it had been polished. Silent where it should be silent. He could feel his father's hands in the links his father had made, in the iron that his father had selected, in the care that had been built into the design from the beginning.

"Thank you," he said. It was something he said every maintenance night, a small ritual that nobody knew about but him.

He coiled the chain and set it beside the bed, the way he did every night. Within arm's reach. Accessible. Ready.

He lay down in the darkness and thought about nothing for a moment. Just the weight of the day settling out of his bones, the knowledge that his tools were cared for, the quiet satisfaction of work done correctly.

His father had written once: *The man who does not maintain his tools will find himself abandoned by them when he needs them most.*

Aisen maintained his tools. He maintained his body. He maintained his watch. He maintained everything that required maintaining, which was everything.

Tomorrow the war would continue. Decisions would need to be made. People would need to be saved or sacrificed, depending on which mathematics he chose to use.

But tonight, the chain was maintained. So he could sleep.

That was enough.
