He remembered his childhood as lonely.

That was the core fact around which he had organized his entire understanding of himself. The tavern, constantly filled with travelers. His mother distant. His father absent. Mira busy. Success measured in survival. Childhood was the incubator of his self-reliance.

That memory had shaped everything. It had made isolation feel normal. It had made emotional distance feel like wisdom.

So when Mira said it, he thought she was being kind.

"You were not lonely," she said. They were in the tavern, years after the war, years after he should have died.

"I was," Aisen said. "I remember it clearly."

"You remember what you needed to remember," Mira said. This was unusual. Mira did not do philosophy. "You were a child who needed to justify solitude. So you remembered the solitude and forgot the other parts."

"What other parts?" Aisen asked.

"The parts where I held you. The parts where Father tried, unsuccessfully, to explain things. The parts where guests at the tavern brought you presents and told you stories. The parts where you were not alone but you—" Mira stopped. "You were afraid. So you built a story about solitude to explain why you could not require things from people."

Aisen tried to access the memory. He could not. What he could access was the feeling of needing to be independent. The feeling that depending on anyone was weakness. But when he tried to remember the specific moments that had created that feeling...

They were not there.

Instead, there were ghosts. A memory of his mother's hand on his head (had that happened?). A memory of his father's laugh (real or constructed?). A memory of Mira singing while she worked (true, but how many times?).

"I built a false memory," he said.

"You built a necessary memory," Mira corrected. "And then you lived into it. You became the lonely boy you remembered being, because the boy who had been held and sung to could not survive what you needed to survive. So your mind gave you what you needed: a story that made isolation feel like choice rather than circumstance."

Aisen sat down. This was the kind of conversation that required a chair.

"What else am I remembering wrong?" he asked.

"Everything important," Mira said. She was not being cruel. This was just truth, stated firmly. "You remember Frostfang as your failure. You remember the war as your responsibility. You remember yourself as the unspecial person who made small differences. But you also remember these things in isolation. You do not remember that we were building something together. That the small differences were only visible because you were paying attention, and others were paying attention because you had showed them how."

"I was doing my work," Aisen said. "That is truth."

"It is truth," Mira agreed. "But it is also incomplete truth. The kind of truth a lonely person tells themselves. A person who needs to be alone so much that even their victories feel like proof of isolation."

Aisen could feel the architecture of his self-understanding beginning to crack. Not everywhere—some memories held. But the foundational narrative, the story he had told about who he was... it was not stable.

"How do I know which memories are real?" he asked.

"You test them," Mira said. "You look for what you needed to remember, and you ask if that need created the memory. You look for gaps where details should be but are not. You look for moments that feel emotionally true but factually inconsistent. And you accept that you have been both right and wrong. Both the lonely boy and the held boy. Both completely isolated and never truly alone."

"That is not comfortable," Aisen said.

"No," Mira agreed. "But it is true."

She left him in the empty tavern. He sat in the chair where he had believed he spent years in isolation. But now, looking at the space, he could remember other things: the way the light had changed with the seasons. The way certain customers had asked about him. The way his mother had set his plate at the table, even in silence. The way his father had left letters.

He had been wrong. Or rather, he had been incomplete. He had remembered solitude truly, but he had forgotten the connections that had existed around it. He had built a story of isolation because isolation explained his strength. But the strength had come from somewhere else entirely—from people who had held him while he learned to stand, and who had stepped back when he needed to.

He could not undo the false memory now. It had shaped him. It had made him who he was. Someone who believed in self-reliance. Someone who did not require things from others. Someone who could be broken and continue anyway.

But perhaps knowing it was false... perhaps that was its own kind of freedom.

Perhaps understanding that you are not as alone as you believed was not weakness. Perhaps it was just truth—late, incomplete, but true.

He sat in the tavern until evening. He did not cry. But he was quieter than he had been. He was carrying something different now: the knowledge that his self-understanding was fragile. That memory was not archive. That identity was not destiny.

And that somewhere inside him, a held child still existed, even though he had spent his life being a solitary one.

Both could be true.

Neither could be the whole truth.

And somehow, that was enough.
