# CORVIN'S LAST STAND (BEAST FORM)

## SETUP

The soldiers came in formation: six of them, trained, armored, moving like a single organism. They had seen Corvin in human form and thought they had killed him. But human form was a costume. It was the thing Corvin wore to pass among people he did not want to consume.

Now the costume was gone.

The transformation had taken less than five seconds. One moment Corvin was on his knees, bleeding. The next, the beast was there—all fangs and shadow and the kind of muscle that made the air itself look compressed.

The six soldiers froze. They had trained for this, supposedly. They had orders: kill the thing, do not hesitate, do not allow it to single out a target. Move as one.

They broke their formation anyway. Fear does that to training.

## PHASE 1: TESTING (Aborted)

There was no testing phase. The moment the beast was fully present, it moved.

Not to attack. To demonstrate.

One of the soldiers—the one who tried to run—was caught in three strides. The beast did not claw or bite. It simply grabbed him by the torso and hurled him. The man flew backward through the clearing like a stone from a catapult. He crashed into a tree twenty feet away. His armor caved inward. His screaming stopped.

The other five held their ground. Training reasserted itself. They advanced in tighter formation, swords raised.

That was the last mistake they made.

## PHASE 2: ESCALATION (Total Domination)

The beast did not lunge. It moved. One moment it was twenty feet away; the next, the air itself was being shoved aside by its mass. The claws—each one the length of a man's finger—came at the front line like falling trees.

The leftmost soldier raised his sword to block.

The claw—not a blade, but all momentum and leverage—came down and the sword shattered. All of it. Steel and hilt and hope. The claw continued through without slowing and hit the soldier's chest. His ribs caved inward. He was lifted off his feet and propelled backward. His body slid through the clearing, leaving a trail of blood. When he stopped, he did not move.

The remaining four soldiers turned as one to see their companion fall.

That fraction of distraction was enough.

The beast spun. Its tail—a thing like an anaconda made of muscle and shadow—swept across the clearing. Three of the soldiers were knocked down. Not killed, not yet. This was not mercy. It was the beast warming up, learning the space, understanding how many times it could swing before the trees were in the way.

The fourth soldier, the one who kept his feet, tried to attack. He drove his sword forward at the beast's flank—a good strike, a trained strike, a strike that should have worked.

The blade hit the beast's hide and stopped as if it had hit stone. The beast did not even notice. It simply turned its head and looked at the soldier with eyes that were somehow intelligent and utterly inhuman at once.

"Run," the beast said. The word came through fangs and growl, barely language at all. But the soldier understood.

He ran.

He made it three steps.

The beast caught him with one clawed hand around the torso. It pulled him back toward itself. The soldier's feet left the ground. The beast lifted him—really lifted him, all his weight and armor held in one hand like a doll—and then threw him into the treeline hard enough to snap branches.

The other three soldiers were climbing back to their feet. The one with the crushed ribs was groaning now, semi-conscious. The two who had been hit by the tail were getting organized, were trying to regroup, were trying to remember their training.

The beast walked toward them with no hurry. It did not need to rush. There was time.

## PHASE 3: REVERSAL (No Reversal Possible)

There was no reversal because the soldiers had never had the advantage.

One of them raised his sword with shaking hands. "Come on," he said to his companion. "Together."

They attacked simultaneously. It was the right move. Double attack from two angles. The sword from the left, the spear from the right.

The beast blocked the sword with its arm. The blade hit flesh and cracked. The spear came from the right—the beast turned its head slightly and the spearpoint hit its jaw and snapped.

Both soldiers fell back, breathing hard, wounds open where flesh had fractured but not bled. The beast was not bleeding. It was not even breathing hard.

"Please," one of them said. "Please, we were just following orders."

The beast did not speak. It did not need to. It simply moved forward and—

But something changed. In the moment before it struck, the beast's eyes flickered. For an instant, something else looked out from behind the teeth and the fury. Something human. Something that still remembered the name Corvin.

The beast hesitated.

That hesitation was everything.

One of the soldiers—the one on the right—ran. Not away. At the beast. A suicide charge, a last desperate act. He drove his sword forward with all his remaining strength.

The beast batted the sword aside. The soldier crashed into its chest and bounced backward. It would have been a killing blow if the beast wanted it to be.

But Corvin, for one flashing second, had pulled back.

The damage was already done.

## PHASE 4: RESOLUTION

The beast shook its head as if clearing water. The human part was retreating. The animal part, sated and proven, was rising to the surface again.

One soldier was unconscious. One was barely moving. Two were still on their feet, barely.

The beast turned toward them and took one step.

"No," Corvin's voice came from behind the beast's face. Just one word. Just enough.

The beast stopped.

The soldiers ran. All of them who could move. They abandoned their wounded and ran into the forest, crashing through brush, their armor clanging, making no attempt at quiet. They just needed to survive.

The beast watched them go. For a long moment, it simply stood in the clearing, shoulders heaving, blood on its claws, surrounded by the bodies of men it had destroyed.

Then it sat. The massive form folded down like a cathedral collapsing. The beast made a sound—not quite a roar, not quite a cry. Something between grief and relief.

On the ground beside the beast's foot was a soldier's ring. A wedding band, fallen during the chaos. The beast stared at it for a long time.

When Corvin transformed back, it was slow, painful, a reversion he had to fight for moment by moment. His human body emerged like a man climbing out of water. He was naked, bleeding from a dozen places where the beast had bled through to his human skin. He was shaking.

He picked up the ring and looked at it. No name. No inscription. Just a man who would not be coming home.

Corvin held it for a moment, then placed it gently on a stone. A marker. The only monument he could give.

When he stood, he was no longer invincible. He was simply tired. And he was thinking about the moment when the beast had almost stopped, when the human part had held the animal back for just a second.

That second was the only thing keeping him alive.

## ANALYSIS

**Corvin's Style: Brute Strength (Beast Form)**

**Prose characteristics used:**
- Long, heavy build-up sentences: "The claw—not a blade, but all momentum and leverage—came down..."
- Heavy verbs: shattered, propelled, swept, crashed
- Sound words that emphasize impact: clanging, snapping, cracking
- Delayed payoff: describe the wind-up, then the impact, then the consequence
- No wasted motion in the prose

**Phase structure:**
- Phase 1: Aborted testing (the beast does not test; it demonstrates)
- Phase 2: Escalation/Domination (long, the single strongest phase)
- Phase 3: Reversal attempt (fails because the soldiers never had an advantage)
- Phase 4: Resolution with moral complexity (Corvin stops the beast)

**The Bastard's Lens applied to soldiers' perspective:**
- Rule: The beast is stronger than coordinated humans
- Weakness: Corvin's humanity pulls the beast back
- Cost: The beast tires Corvin; transformation is painful
- Tell: The moment when the beast's eyes flicker and something human looks out

**Why the soldiers lost:**
- They fought direct (brute strength) against the beast (overwhelming brute strength + supernatural power)
- They should have used strategy or environment
- Should have have retreated from the first casualty instead of advancing
- Against unstoppable force, the only counter is to not engage at all

**Counterplay not demonstrated:**
- To beat Brute Strength, you need Finesse or Strategy
- The soldiers relied on neither (they relied on training and numbers)
- They should have used the forest more (environmental strategy)
- They should have left earlier (strategic retreat)

**Cost of battle:**
- Corvin: bleeding internally, exhausted, moral weight of what the beast did
- Soldiers: two dead, one severely wounded, three escaping but traumatized
- The moment when Corvin stopped the beast is the most expensive part—it cost him control, cost him certainty about whether he's human or monster

**Character revealed:**
- Corvin is not a killer by nature; he is one by circumstance
- The beast is not mindless; it can be held back (barely) by the human part
- The true horror is not the power; it is the loss of control
- Corvin survives by maintaining the thinnest thread of humanity

**Key writing principle demonstrated:**
In a brute-strength fight, the prose should feel like an avalanche. Unstoppable, inevitable, heavy. Each impact is described fully. There is no finesse, no precision—just overwhelming force deployed without mercy. The only moment of complexity comes from the internal struggle (Corvin vs. the beast), which is where the real dramatic tension lies.

The soldiers never had a chance. The story is not about them. It is about Corvin's internal fight to remain himself.
