# THE SECONDS BETWEEN

Amara had exactly sixteen minutes before the net closed.

That's what Vera had told her, whispered across a marketplace while pretending to discuss fabric prices. "The 4th Party informants just moved. They're coordinating sweeps. Sixteen minutes before they reach the warehouse."

Sixteen minutes meant she could not save everything. Meant she could not save everyone.

The warehouse had four exits. Three were compromised—Vera had runners watching them, confirming they had informants posted. Only one exit was clear, and it led through a food market and then across three rooftops that were narrow enough to require momentum and small enough to be difficult to track.

In the office, Vera was destroying papers. Everything that mentioned names, operations, locations. She was burning the archive of the network's last three years.

"How many people are still in the building?" Amara asked.

"Seven. Plus the administrator who got flagged. Nine total."

Amara had seventeen locations mapped out in her mind. Seven safe houses, each one stocked for this moment. Each one capable of holding three to five people. She could save six or seven people with coordination. That meant choosing.

The mathematics were simple and impossible.

**Administrator**: Dead weight. But he knew too much. If 4th Party got him and enhanced interrogation worked, they'd have names. He had to be moved regardless.

**Vera**: Irreplaceable. She had built the communication network that kept everyone alive.

**Kato**: Contact at the garrison. Combat trained. Useful later.

**Lens**: Courier. Could move information between locations.

**The twins**: Young, newly recruited. Least valuable to the network operationally. Most valuable as humans with entire lives ahead.

**Moran**: Healer. Could treat people if things went wrong with escapes.

**Shen**: Historian. Maintained records when the building survived. Dead weight.

Amara made the decision in approximately three seconds, which was exactly how long this calculation deserved.

"Vera," she said, "contact the safe houses. Tell them to activate emergency protocols. Seven people incoming, not nine. I need Kato, yourself, the twins, Lens, and the administrator."

Vera didn't ask who was being left behind.

She went to the communication station and started sending out coded messages. Within thirty seconds, the network would begin absorbing people into shadow locations. The building itself would be burned—destroyed well enough that it looked like they were defending and lost, not evacuating cleanly.

---

## The Choice

"What about Moran?" Vera asked without looking up from the communication desk.

"Moran stays."

"Moran will be arrested."

"Yes," Amara said, "but Moran is our oldest asset. They've been with us for eleven years. The only archive of Moran's knowledge is in Moran's head. If 4th Party gets Moran under enhanced interrogation, they get everything. Everyone. All of us."

"So you're sacrificing Moran to protect the network."

"I'm sacrificing Moran to protect Moren's family," Amara said. "Moran has a sister in the lower districts. If Moran gets taken and the network survives without Moran, the sister survives. If the network collapses because Moran talked, the sister's associated with a destroyed movement. That's worse."

Vera made a noise that might have been acknowledgment or might have been disapproval. "What about Shen?"

"Shen is a historian. Shen's knowledge is historical, not operational. If Shen is arrested and talks, they give 4th Party context but not active threats. Shen is replaceable. And Shen has no family, which means Shen's capture doesn't cascade."

"You've already made these calculations," Vera said. It wasn't a question.

"Three weeks ago. The moment I realized we were being triangulated, I started running these scenarios. This is the outcome that saves the most people and destabilizes the network minimally."

---

## The Execution

Kato was waiting at the secondary office with the twins. They were nervous but trained. Amara gave them the route: through the market, rooftop escape, rendezvous at location Seven.

"The building is going to burn," Amara told them. "Not immediately. Give us thirty minutes to clear the rooftops. Then Vera triggers the accelerants. It will look like we fought and lost. It will look like a defeat. That's what we want 4th Party to think."

The twins nodded. They were young enough to think confidence was enough. They were old enough to know it wasn't.

"If you're stopped before the rooftops, you have minimal injury potential. If you're stopped on the rooftops, jump. The drop is maybe fifteen feet. It will hurt. You'll survive."

"And if we're stopped after the rooftops?" one of the twins asked—Sora, the younger one. She was nineteen.

"Then you run for location Seven and pray you get there first."

---

## The Movement

Amara took the administrator through the kitchen exit. The marketplace beyond was deliberately chaotic—controlled chaos by design. Vendors and customers, enough movement that one more person passing through would be noise in the system.

She kept the administrator's head down but not so far down that it looked like guilt. Confidence looked like innocence. Made innocence look like you belonged.

It worked for exactly two blocks.

Then she saw the informant. Same silver insignia that Vera had spotted earlier. Positioned at a fabric vendor's stall. Watching the flow of people.

Amara did not change direction. She did not run. She simply pulled the administrator into a side alley with the kind of casual possessiveness that meant they were together, not suspect.

"We're burned," she said quietly.

"Do we run?" the administrator asked.

"Wait here. Count to sixty. Then walk south until you hit the lower marketstalls. There's a wine merchant. Buy something. Any drink. Then head to the garrison district and find a guard you don't know. Ask for 'Kato.' Tell Kato 'the birds are flying.'"

"He won't believe me."

"He will. Kato is ours. Go."

The administrator went. He would probably get arrested within a day. But if he reached Kato first, Kato would get word to Vera, and they'd make arrangements.

And if he got arrested without reaching Kato, he would be trial and execution and another martyr to the network.

Either way, he mattered. Either way, his capture would be one less person than 4th Party anticipated, which meant one more piece of the network survived.

---

## The Aftermath

Amara didn't go to location Seven immediately. She had insurance: a third location that was not on the network's books. A space that existed outside the architecture, held by someone who wasn't part of the movement but had agreed to shelter a single person in emergencies.

She used it rarely, saving it for moments like this—when the network was exposed and people had to disappear without trying to reconnect. Disconnection for safety.

She stayed in that third location for three days, watching as the burning building made news and 4th Party investigated and tried to understand the scope of what had existed. She watched as Moran was arrested and kept waiting for confirmation that Moran had held under interrogation.

The confirmation never came. Moran had talked. The network had lost contact. Which meant Moran was either dead or had reached some accommodation with 4th Party. Either way, Moran was gone.

Six people were saved. Moran was lost. The administrator was left in motion, hoping to reach sanctuary.

It was not a victory. But it was not a collapse.

Very few operations in resistance work produced victory. The goal was simply to survive another cycle, to keep the network breathing, to ensure that when it was all over—if it ever ended—there would be someone left to say what had happened.

Amara would be that person. Because that was the job. That was always the job. To survive so that survival meant something.

She waited in the third location until it was safe to move again. Then she walked into the city that was still hunting her, still hunting the network, still running the machinery of purity that required people like Amara to exist in opposition.

The work continued. It always continued.

Until it didn't.
