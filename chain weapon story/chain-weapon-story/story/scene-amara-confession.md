Scene: The Sixth Lane — Confession

They sat on low crates in the alley where the city's light thinned. The market's hum was a distant reading; here Amara's face was sharper than Aisen had ever seen it, all angles of trade and ledger and calculus.

"You delayed the courier," Aisen said. He didn't ask for a denial—he'd read her hands the way she read manifests.

Amara shut her eyes once. "I delayed it, yes."

"A soldier died because of it. A whole patrol walked into—"

"—a trap we couldn't stop if the wrong people got the route," she finished. Her hand clenched the ledger until the leather complained. "You think I kept it from you to save a coin? I kept it because the other option was a city burned. The information would have let them carve a corridor through the refugees—children, Aisen. I did the arithmetic. Smaller loss now, greater loss later."

He wanted to say she had no right. He wanted to say she had no right to make that decision. Instead he felt the ledger's weight in her fist and the way her jaw worked—how her mouth tightened over a cost she had already paid.

"We trusted you with routes because you were better at reading noise than any of us," he said. "But trust and judgment are not the same thing."

Amara's laugh was short. "I know. I lost an informant yesterday. If I hadn't rerouted him he would have alerted them; instead he moved and lived. He owes me a debt I can't collect."

"Did you tell him?" Aisen asked.

"No. Would it have helped? He'd have demanded we move more. That would have widened the window. I kept him out of the loop. I told one family the wrong road. They left alive. Maybe I made the right call. Maybe I'm a butcher. I don't know." She swallowed and looked at him as if asking permission.

He thought of the apprentice token they'd found in the forge, of the boy's face in the ledger. He thought of the teleporter who'd died to a string and the merchant who sold pre-looped cords. He thought of tradeoffs stacked like coins.

"What happens now?" he asked.

"Now we decide how honest we are with each other," she said. "And if we can't forgive, we replace me. But I will keep you from a massacre if I can, even if it makes me hated."

Aisen held out his hand. It felt like a test. He chose to take it. "You keep doing that. But not alone."

She hooked her fingers around his palm like a ledger line and let a single, small sound out—an exhausted agreement.

They made another promise before they left: the work would not be solitary. Aisen would run a short practical session—how to set a loop and where to place a living anchor; Amara would run a mapping workshop on trade signatures and routing quirks. Varro would be asked to spar with deliberate noise to learn how to adapt when predictions failed.

It was the smallest of reforms: teach one, learn one. It would keep the group from feeling like a single engine with dead weight behind it. It would also mean, Aisen thought as they stood, that if one of them failed, the others might still know enough to keep the rest alive.
