The woman had no teeth.

That was the first thing Aisen noticed when she approached him on the road—a widow, probably, or a woman who had lost her family in one of the wars, the way older women often did. She was carrying bread.

"You look hungry," she said. Her accent was from the western territories, but softened by age and distance. She had been wandering for a while.

"I have money," Aisen said. It was the safest response. Money made transactions clear. Money kept relationships simple.

"This is not for sale," the woman said. She held out the bread. It was wrapped in cloth, still warm. "It is for eating."

Aisen did not take it. He had learned a long time ago that gifts were traps. The bread would come with expectation. With the weight of owing something. With the suggestion that he was the kind of person who needed to be helped, when the truth was more complicated and more bitter than that.

"I cannot accept it," he said.

The woman looked at him the way his mother used to look at him—with the patience of someone who had learned that some people refused kindness the way other people refused water. Not from lack of need, but from something deeper. Something broken.

"You can," she said. "You simply will not. That is different."

She set the bread down on a stone beside him, still wrapped, still warm. The smell of it was incredible—yeast and salt and hours of work. Someone had made this. Someone had given care to it.

"I saved a girl once," the woman said, sitting down on a different stone, one that was clearly meant for her rather than proximity to him. "Maybe your age now. Maybe younger. She was running from soldiers. I gave her food and shelter for three days. She left without thanking me."

Aisen did not respond. This was a story setup. There would be a moral.

"I never saw her again," the woman continued. "I do not know if she survived. But twenty years later, a young man came to my door. He was a teacher, he said. He had learned to read and write and to think. He said a woman had given him shelter once, and another woman—his mother—had raised him to be kind because of it. He wanted to thank me."

She looked at Aisen directly now. "It was not the girl. It was her child. And he thanked me for saving his mother, even though his mother never thanked me."

"Why are you telling me this?" Aisen asked.

"Because you are looking at that bread like it is a debt I am collecting," the woman said. "And I am telling you it is not. It is just bread. I made too much for myself, and you are hungry, and in this world there are few enough opportunities to be useful to someone. When one appears, I take it."

She stood up with the effort of someone whose knees no longer worked properly. "Eat the bread. Do not eat it. That is your choice. But do not refuse it because you are afraid of owing me something. You do not owe me anything. The bread does not cost you anything. It does not create a debt. It is just bread, offered because it was possible to offer it."

Aisen watched her walk away. She moved slowly, painfully, like someone who had learned to live in her own broken body the way other people learned to live in houses. She did not look back.

He sat for a long time without moving. The bread was still warm. He could smell it. Every instinct told him to leave it, to refuse the transaction, to move on without acceptance. But the woman's words kept returning: *It does not cost you anything.*

Finally, he picked it up.

The cloth was soft—woven carefully, the kind of thing that mattered to someone who had learned that small care could be an act of resistance against a world that did not care. He unwrapped the bread.

Inside was a loaf, dense and dark, studded with seeds. Real work went into this. Real time, real ingredients, real intention.

He broke off a piece and ate it.

The taste was simple: salt, yeast, the slight sweetness of good grain. Nothing fancy. Just bread, competently made, by someone who had taken care with it.

He finished half the loaf before stopping, wrapping the rest carefully in the cloth. He would eat it later. He would think about the woman, and her story about the girl who had run from soldiers, and the child she had raised who had returned to say thank you decades later.

He would wonder if the woman had given that girl the bread expecting nothing, or if she had known, somehow, that debt and gift were not the same thing, and that some acts of care could exist without requiring payment or gratitude or anything except the knowledge that they had happened.

He would try to understand, and fail, and accept the failure.

And he would carry the experience of someone offering him something with genuine kindness—no hook, no attachment, no catch—the way he carried everything else. As data. As proof that such things existed. As a moment when the world had been gentler than he expected.

That night, he did something he almost never did. He asked someone his own age, a young man in the tavern where he was staying, if he knew the old woman's name.

"Petra," the man said. "She has been here for maybe twenty years, maybe longer. Nobody knows where she is from. She just... appeared. Helps people sometimes. Never asks for payment. It is strange."

"Yes," Aisen said. "It is."

He did not say more. But he thought about the bread, and the old woman's patience, and the possibility that kindness existed in the world not because it was profitable or strategic, but because some people had decided that any opportunity to help was worth taking, regardless of whether that help was appreciated.

He finished the bread the next morning. It was still good. And as he ate it, he did not feel indebted. He felt, for a moment, less alone.

That was all the old woman had offered. And it was, surprisingly, enough.
