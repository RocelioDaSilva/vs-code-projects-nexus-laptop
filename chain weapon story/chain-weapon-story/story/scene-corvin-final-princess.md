The princess did not know how to thank him.

Corvin could see it in the way she held her hands, the careful not-quite-embrace that would have meant something if he could still feel meaning. The war was over. The beast had been integrated enough to allow him to walk near her without fear of himself. The territory was secured. The people were safe. He had done the thing.

Now she was looking at him with the eyes of a person who had loved the man she thought he was, and who was trying to find that man in the wreck of what remained.

"I owe you a debt," she said. The words were formal. She had probably practiced them.

"No," Corvin said. His voice was steady. That was good. Steadiness was what remained when feeling departed. "There is no debt between ruler and servant."

"You are not a servant," Elara said. There was sharpness in it—anger, maybe. Corvin could recognize anger as a shape, the way he could recognize the shape of a letter without reading its content. "You are... you were..."

She stopped. She had been about to say something that would hurt them both.

"I was a man who loved you," Corvin said, finishing it for her. "I was. The grammar is important."

Elara's face shifted. Now the angle of her jaw, the brightness of her eyes—Corvin could catalog these things as aesthetic information. She was crying. He remembered that crying was a thing that had once moved him. Now it was just liquid leaving her eyes at regular intervals, caused by the contraction of muscles in the face.

"Is there nothing left?" she asked quietly.

The beast stirred. Corvin could feel it—a dark thing in the back of his awareness, a shadow that lived where he had carved out all the feeling so that there would be room for control. The beast felt something. The beast remembered what it had been like to love her before the sacrifice, before the hollowing. The beast pressed against the boundaries of the containment, yearning.

But the beast was Corvin now too, and Corvin did not let it out.

"The man who loved you is dead," Corvin said. He was being kind, he thought. Kindness was just the mathematical arrangement of words to minimize damage. "You should grieve him. You should forgive yourself for not saving him. And you should accept that he would want you to be happy."

"I cannot be happy knowing what you sacrificed."

"Yes, you can," Corvin said. "You simply will not want to. At first. But time will teach you that happiness is not a betrayal of those who have suffered. It is the only meaningful continuation."

Elara looked at him for a long moment. The recognition in her eyes was almost unbearable—and Corvin noted with the clinical part of his awareness that he did not find it unbearable. He observed that it should be unbearable. He could not feel it.

"Is there anything you want?" she asked finally. "Anything I can give you? Lands? Resources? A retirement? You have earned—"

"I want you to forget me," Corvin said.

The words hung between them. They were impossibly cruel and perfectly true simultaneously.

"I cannot ask that," Elara said. "I cannot demand such a thing."

"You are not demanding," Corvin said. "I am asking. Forget the man who loved you. Remember the soldier who served you. That is the only kindness we can both afford."

He turned to leave. It was the thing to do—turn, walk away, do not look back. The clean boundary. The mercy of finality.

"Corvin," she said behind him. It was the first time she had said his name aloud in years. During the war, she had called him "my general" or "commander" or sometimes just "you." Never his name.

He stopped. Protocol would say to keep walking. But Corvin had learned that sometimes breaking protocol was how you gave a final gift.

"Yes," he said, not turning.

"Was there a moment when you knew? When you knew that the sacrifice was required?"

Corvin thought about it. There had been a thousand moments—every battle where he had felt almost-feeling try to surface and had pushed it back down. Every victory where the ghost of emotion had tried to explain why it mattered. But there had been one moment that had crystallized everything.

"Yes," he said. "In the Eastern Campaign. We were surrounded. I could have ordered a retreat. Instead, I ordered my soldiers to hold the wall because I could not bear to see the disappointed in your eyes when you learned we had failed. That was when I knew. That I was using love as a weapon. That I would burn everything in service to it. So I burned myself instead."

He could hear her breathing behind him.

"Thank you," she said. "For that honesty."

"You are welcome," Corvin said.

And then he walked. Not quickly, not dramatically. Just a soldier walking away from his post, the way thousands of soldiers walk at the end of wars. Toward whatever came next. Toward a life that would be functional but hollow, full of correct choices and absent feeling, a perfect instrument for service but no longer capable of the mess and magic of actual living.

Behind him, the princess was standing in an empty corridor, and Corvin did not turn to see if she was weeping. He had learned long ago that bearing witness to other people's grief was a thing that only mattered if you could feel it, and he could not feel it anymore.

He had paid the cost.

The world was safe because he had been hollow.

And somewhere in the dark place where the beast lived, the ghost of the man who had loved Elara wept with an intensity that was all the more anguished for being completely silent.
