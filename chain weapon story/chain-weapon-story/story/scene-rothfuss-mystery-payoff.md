# The String in the Teleporter's Shoulder

The mage was already dead when Aisen found him.

He lay in the grass at the boundary of the camp, body still cooling, eyes wide and fixed on something that was not the sky. There was a lot of blood, but the wound was strange for a battlefield death. It was precise. Deliberate. Almost surgical in its specificity.

Something had been driven through the mage's shoulder and out through the back of his neck. Not a sword. Swords left ragged wounds. This was a thin perforation, like something had punched through tissue with minimal resistance.

Aisen knelt beside the body and began cataloging details the way Harald had taught him: time of death (recent, maybe an hour), position of body (fallen forward, suggesting the wound came from behind), evidence of struggle (none—the mage had been ambushed).

But it was the thing's embedded in the wound that caught Aisen's attention.

Fishing line.

Ordinary fishing line. The kind merchants used, or children used to tie packages. It was embedded in the wound tract, pulled taut, buried in the cartilage and muscle so deeply it would have taken catastrophic force to drive it through a living body.

Aisen looked around the camp perimeter. The body was positioned exactly at the boundary where the sentries changed rotation. Where the sentry line was thinnest, for exactly ninety seconds while one guard broke away to check the water supply.

Someone had known the schedule. Someone had used that ninety-second window. Someone had used nothing but fishing line and mage-killing force.

Aisen pulled on the fishing line with tweezers. It emerged, blood-soaked and limp, with a small iron weight still attached to one end. The weight was the size of a fingernail but had been shaped with precision—a slim shaft with small barbs.

It was the kind of thing Aisen had never seen before.

It was also the kind of thing that made sense if you understood the one constraint on teleportation: the spell required a stable destination. A mage could not teleport into an area if the destination was blocked or unstable. A mage could not teleport through solid matter.

But a mage could teleport into an area that had been rigged with an ultra-thin trap. A trap so thin the mage's senses would not detect it. A trap that would pierce the mage's shoulder as the teleportation completed, yanking the weight in one direction while the mage's momentum carried them in another.

It would tear. It would bleed. It would probably kill.

Aisen pocketed the weight and the fishing line. He did not report it. He did not tell the commander. He simply kept the item and waited.

### PAYOFF - Act III, Thornwood Final Stand (Age 23)

Five years later, Aisen was facing the fourth teleporter.

The Covenant had learned. They sent mages in groups now. Two, sometimes three at once. It was harder to kill them solo. But Aisen had learned something too.

He had spent five years waiting for an opponent worth the preparation. He had spent five years thinking about what he had found in that dead mage's shoulder. About the weight. About the fishing line. About the simple physics of what worked against teleportation.

Aisen had strung the gateway with the thinnest fishing line he could find—the kind used for embroidery, nearly invisible in dim light. He had attached small iron weights at intervals along the line, each one positioned at the exact height where a teleporting mage's shoulder would arrive.

He had set the trap at the command tent entry. Where the Covenant would know to teleport in. Where the mage's arrival would be predictable.

The first teleporter arrived in a shimmer of displaced air.

The fishing line caught them across the collarbone.

The weight yanked downward. The mage's shoulder dis-articulated with an audible crack. They screamed—a sound of surprise and agony that echoed across the camp.

The second teleporter arrived simultaneously, materializing next to the first.

Aisen was already moving.

He did not try to fight them. Instead, he drove the chain through the second mage's chest while they were still disoriented by the companion's scream. It was cruel. It was efficient. It was the culmination of five years of patience.

The first mage, one-armed and screaming, tried to teleport again. Aisen had accounted for that too. He had positioned his own body in the exit corridor the mage would instinctively choose. Teleportation normally avoided living targets. But with a damaged shoulder and only one usable arm, precision would be impossible.

The mage appeared directly inside Aisen's circle of chains.

What followed was not a fight. It was slaughter.

---

Later, after the camp had been secured and the dead counted, Aisen sat alone with the dead mage and the fishing line trap.

He extracted the fishing line carefully. The weight at the center was still intact, still bloodied. He would keep this one too.

Harald found him sitting there, turning the weight over in his hands.

"That's what killed the mage five years ago," Harald said. It was not a question.

"Yes," Aisen said.

"You've been planning this for five years."

"Yes."

"Waiting for another teleporter so you could test your theory."

"Yes."

Harald sat down beside him. "That's the kind of patience that scares people, Aisen. That's the kind of planning that makes you dangerous."

"I'm already dangerous," Aisen said.

"Not the same kind of dangerous. If you're practiced with a sword, people understand that. If you can throw your chain well, people accept that. But if you're the kind of person who keeps a fishing line for five years waiting for a chance to test a theory..." Harald trailed off. "That's not training. That's something else."

Aisen did not argue. He simply kept turning the weight in his hands, feeling the shape of five years of patience, feeling the shape of a plan that had worked perfectly, feeling the weight of having become the kind of person who could wait five years and then kill someone using the knowledge he had gained.

## Rothfuss Principle Applied

**Evocative and Sensory**: 
- The "ultra-thin trap" and "fishing line" are described through physical sensation
- Subtle details: blood-soaked line, barbs, iron weight
- The mage's pain is a specific, audible sound

**Layered Mystery**:
- First incident: Aisen finds dead mage, cannot explain the weapon
- Five years of silent observation and planning
- Payoff: The mystery resolves when Aisen uses the same technique

**Patient Worldbuilding**: The reader learns teleportation rules through contradiction (what doesn't work becomes clear only when Aisen exploits it).

**Details That Matter**: The fishing line is remembered because it's specific and strange. When it reappears, the reader has been waiting five years (in-story) for the answer.

This demonstrates that Rothfuss-style mystery works best when the reader sees something unexplained early, then forgets about it, then has the revelation strike them as inevitable in retrospect.
