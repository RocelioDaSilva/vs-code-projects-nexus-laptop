Scene: The Thread

Aisen saw the corpse before the patrol did: on his own, breathing his night's smoke into the cold dawn, he noticed the angle of it. The body's forearm lay twisted over the lip of the road; a thin line of pale thread disappeared into the sleeve and reappeared taut, like a harp string, looped against the bone.

The patrol called it bad luck and moved on. Aisen walked closer. Up close, the thread didn't look like the sort cobblers favored; it had a tight, industrial twist and a tiny brass seal jammed where it looped. Someone had stamped the loop with a maker's mark and slipped it onto the path like a trap.

He unthreaded the seal and slipped it into his palm. The brass was warm from the body's blood and colder from the morning. He could have left it. Instead he slid the spool from his belt and tied a half-hitch, feeling for the slightest difference that would tell him whether it had been set by chance.

Rin crouched beside him and hissed. "You don't want that. It looks... wrong."

"Everything looks wrong up close," Aisen said. "Which is useful." He tucked the sealed loop into his pack.

At the market later, when he fidgeted with the spool, a merchant with hands like a hawk watched him. The merchant smiled too quickly.

"Need a better line for fishin'?" the man said.

Aisen let his fingers toy with the cord on his lap, careful to look casual. That merchant's thumb had a crescent scar, like someone who'd worked rope for years. He watched the man's fingers as if noticing them for the first time and remembered the seal in his palm.

He didn't buy the line. He paid for a dwarf-crumb of bread and left the market with his spool and a new suspicion.

Later that night he set a loop in a window, an idle experiment. He expected nothing. A teleporter's arrival flared like a struck lamp; a shape collapsed in the glass, a cry shredded into the room. The string had snared across the threshold and cut as the volumes tried to exchange.

He had not killed anyone. He had learned a rule.

When he folded his spool back into his pack he wrote the rule into his hands with the same decisive ink as a tally mark: teleports swap space; anything that spans departure and arrival tears the traveler. He did not call it justice or luck. He called it a tool.

He did not yet know the trade that would turn that tool into profit for someone else. He would learn soon.

Later, on a rain-slick rooftop, he taught Rin how to place a loop so it would catch a teleporter without leaving a tangle that a patrolling hand could notice. Rin, who had spent her life moving like a shadow, taught him how to step without announcing weight and how to pick a knot that would hold under sudden stress yet come free with a single practiced pull.

It was not a lesson of teacher and pupil so much as two people swapping small, vital secrets. Rin learned to think of traps as mechanical conversations—tiny pressures, angles, textures—rather than blunt killers. Aisen learned a quieter way to move through the world he pried apart. The spool in his pack became less a solitary tool than the start of a shared vocabulary.
