He was in the pass again.

The ice was not white. It was the blue-white of something that had been frozen so long it had become glass. The wind was not cold—cold was a word for human sensation. This was the absence of warmth at such scale that the distinction between air and death had become semantic.

The wyrm was above him.

It was not the same as it had been during the day. During the day, it had been a thing of scale and weight and the simple mathematics of being too large to fight. In the dream, it was awareness. It was hunger. It was the understanding that ice could think and want and that Aisen was too small to be more than a mote of dust in its attention.

He tried to move. His legs would not respond. The ice had frozen him—not physically, but as a state of being. He was conscious inside paralysis. He could see the wyrm. He could feel it seeing him.

The boy was crying.

That was the detail that would not change. The boy—Korren, his name was Korren, Aisen's mind supplied, though they had never met, though Aisen did not know if the boy was real—was lying in a ditch with snow already covering him. His eyes were open. He was crying without sound.

"I cannot reach him," Aisen said. His voice was not his own. It was the voice of someone who had already accepted failure.

The wyrm descended.

It did not move the way flying things moved. It descended the way inevitability descended—not fast or slow, but inexorable. The wings were not wings. They were the folding of space. The breath was not breath. It was the collapse of everything that was not ice.

The boy stopped crying.

---

Aisen woke without screaming.

That was the thing about the nightmares now—he had learned to suppress the scream. It had taken weeks of practice. Wake up. Recognize the dream. Do not make noise. Return to breathing. That was all.

The room was dark. That was correct. The building was silent. That was correct. He was not in the Frostfang Pass. That was the only thing that mattered.

He sat up slowly. His shirt was soaked with sweat. His hands were shaking. These were automatic responses that his body had learned. He did not try to stop them. Stopping them required energy he could not spare.

He counted his breaths. In for four. Hold for five. Out for six. That was the meditation the healer had taught him. In. Hold. Out. It was the rhythm of a heartbeat, external and therefore controllable.

After a time—he did not measure it—his hands stopped shaking. His breath steadied. He was awake enough to function.

He did not try to sleep again. Sleep was more danger than rest was worth. Instead, he sat by the window and watched the city's darkness. Somewhere out there, people were sleeping peacefully. Somewhere out there, the boy—if he was real—was also sleeping, or not sleeping, or was frozen in ice that would never melt.

The dream did not tell him. Dreams were useless that way. They created the feeling without the context. They gave him the knowledge that he had failed without the memory of success to balance it.

By dawn, the dream would be gone. He would remember it the way he remembered stories someone else had told him—with intellectual knowledge but not emotional reality. The fear would recede. The image of the boy would fade.

But tonight, in this moment, they were real. The failure was real. The weight was real.

He did not move from the window until dawn. He did not sleep. He did not dream again.

When the sun rose, the nightmare released its hold, and Aisen stood and began the work of the day as if the night had never happened.

That was survival too: the ability to contain the horror of the subconscious and continue functioning in the morning.

It was not a skill they taught at the Academy.

But it was the only skill that mattered.
