Pela's grandson would not stop crying.

She held him close, her old hands rough against his small back, and she did not shush him. What was the point? The soldiers knew where they were. Everyone knew where they were. The refugee column was a wound moving across the countryside—five thousand people, maybe six, all going nowhere, all waiting to see which army decided they were in the way.

Around her, others tried to sleep on the frozen ground. Children curled against mothers. Mothers curled around fathers. The living arranged themselves like a prayer that did not expect to be answered.

Pela had seen three wars. In the first, she had been young enough to run. In the second, she had run with a child on her back. In this one, she could barely walk. Her daughter pushed a cart that held everything they still owned—a pot, a blanket, a dress that had belonged to Pela's mother. The important things, supposedly. The things worth carrying.

Her grandson understood nothing. He was four. He only knew that the world had been warm and now it was cold, that people had fed him and now there was not enough, that there had been a home and now there was not.

"Hush," she whispered, though she did not believe in hushing. "Hush, little bird. We will rest soon."

"When?" he asked. The question that killed her every time. When would they rest? When would they go home? When would the world be warm again?

"Soon," she said, because that was the only lie that mattered anymore.

A man passed their section of the camp—one of the soldiers, or not a soldier. She was not sure anymore. He was young, moved with the grace of someone who was not hungry. He stopped and looked at the child.

"How old?" he asked.

Pela did not answer. Strange soldiers with strange questions meant strangers with intentions.

"Four," her daughter said, stepping forward. "Just four. He's sick from the cold, not from plague. Not danger to you."

The man nodded. He did not seem convinced, but he did seem... something else. Not unkind. That was dangerous in its own way. Kindness from a stranger meant they had decided whether you lived, and the decision had been made.

He gave her daughter his own blanket.

"For the child," he said. Then he walked on.

Pela watched him disappear into the darkness. She did not know his name. She would probably never see him again. But she would remember him, the way she remembered all the strangers who had made small decisions that kept her alive. The woman who had given her bread from her own ration. The soldier who had looked away when she stole water. The young man with the chain weapon and the careful eyes who had chosen not to see her when the officers came looking for people to question.

These small mercies were all that stood between survival and dissolution.

Her grandson cried less under the extra blanket. But he still cried, and Pela held him, and she thought about home—the orchard where she had lived for sixty years, the stone fence her husband had built with his own hands, the way the light came through the window in the morning.

All of it was gone now. The armies had moved through and made camps and torn everything down for firewood or spite.

Pela would not go back. She had already decided this, though she had not told anyone. When they reached the refugee city, when they were processed and assigned to a settlement, she would simply stop. She would lie down and close her eyes and that would be the end. Not a suffering end. Just an end. She had earned that much.

Her grandson fell asleep finally, and Pela rocked him gently. Around them, the camp settled into the false peace of exhaustion. Above, the stars looked down with the same indifference they always had. They did not judge or grieve or judge. They simply shone.

In her pocket, Pela carried a single seed from the orchard tree. She did not know why she still carried it. She had already decided not to plant it. But she held it anyway, the way her grandson held the edge of her dress.

A small thing. A reminder that something had grown once.

Tomorrow they would walk further. The next day, further still. And eventually, they would reach a place where they could stop, where someone would tell them they were safe, where they could pretend there was somewhere to go from here.

But tonight, in the frozen dark, with her grandson sleeping in her arms, Pela allowed herself one small hope. It was not big or grand. It was just the hope that tomorrow his cry would be quieter. That eventually, he would stop crying altogether. That he would grow up and forget this place and this cold and this waiting.

That was all a grandmother could ask anymore. That the boy would forget.
