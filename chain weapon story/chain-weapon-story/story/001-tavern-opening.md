The tavern smelled like boiled barley and bent spoons; it smelled like warm mouths and the slow, sticky sweetness of stew that had been ladled through the same wood for as long as Aisen could remember.

He sat on the low bench with his knees pulled to the grain, a small heap of legs and elbows that fit between a man's chair and the hearth. Father was a silhouette of elbows and patient hands behind the counter, a thumb that could pass a coin from one palm to another without thinking. Aisen knew the sound of that thumb better than the sound of his own name.

"Stay near the tankard," Father said, not looking up. His voice was the kind that folded itself into the room and did not startle the rafters. Marta—Marta with her laugh like a windchime that had lost a bell—moved between tables, carrying a tray that hissed when a spill fell into it. She reached down and patted Aisen's head the way she always did, leaving the faint smell of lemon soap in her wake.

She set a coin on the table in front of him—a small, nicked coin that caught the light in a way real coins did, the kind that felt like salt and a promise. "For your lessons," she said, and the words were as big as a chair to a child. "Don't eat it all at once."

Aisen clasped the coin in both hands, an animal careful of a bone. Coins were the size of the sky in that moment; they were histories, they were the sum of mornings, they were the razor-edge of whether a loaf would be bought that week. Father rapped his palm against the counter, cupped a bowl, and told Marta something that made her bark a laugh and roll her eyes. The two of them sounded like people who stitched the city together with small, steady stitches.

Aisen watched fingers. That was his first lesson. Fingers could tell you how old a man was by the calluses, how hungry by the steadiness of the grip, whether he had been at sea or at a forge. Father kept a patch on his palm where the knuckle had been ground by a rope long ago; Marta's nails always had a thin layer of flour under them from kneading when the town was busy.

Outside, a cart rattled and someone sang a wordless thing that moved like a question. Aisen pressed his ear to the corner of the table and saw the shadow of a crate being carried past the window. He could see the shape of a too-heavy thing, a corner of dark cloth frayed at the edge. He did not know then that merchants left not only goods but stories in the hollows of crates—little scraps of cloth that would come alive later. He only knew it had a smell like oil and old rope.

"Don't squint so," Father said, leaning down so his face filled Aisen's world for a second. "You'll see better with both eyes open."

Aisen tried with both eyes and found that the tavern had a weight to it he could hold. He liked the way Father hummed when he worked, liked the crease Father made by the bridge of his nose when thinking about the day's accounts. He liked the way Father would smooth his hair—which always stood a little up in the back—because the smoothing was the same action as saying he was safe.

The coin warm in Aisen's fist, he sampled the room for small things. A man's boot scuff; the hollow clink of a spoon against a bowl; someone telling a story in the corner in low, beautiful bits that left the room smelling like smoke. Marta came back and leaned over him. She touched his cheek with a thumb that smelled of lemon and yeast, and for a moment Aisen thought her thumb could fix anything.

She dropped a scrap of bread into his lap and winked. "You keep your eyes open," she said, "and you'll notice the important bits."

He nodded solemnly, as if the coin were a treaty to himself. And as the day folded into the late light that slanted through the window—gold across dust motes, like something pressed—Aisen picked at the edge of a scrap of blue cloth left on the windowsill. He tugged it free and held the fiber between finger and thumb, watching light pass through it like water. The cloth had a small, dark grease stain at its corner, and a whisper of metal—if you held it just so, you could think the cloth had the memory of a chain.

That small glint was the kind of thing he liked to keep: a pebble, an odd knot, a treasure that was not for showing anyone. He pocketed the cloth without telling Father. Pocketing was a quiet, important crime for a child, and Aisen liked the feeling of something secret and small and true tucked against his belly.

He did not know then that the glint would be the first thing that would not fit into the way his days were stitched. He did not know that a hand would be faster than the thumb that had given him the coin, that a man’s foot could make the tavern go very quiet, or that the little rope of threads in his pocket would mean something heavy later. For now, the trade of fingers and coins and kindness and the way Marta's laugh bounced against the rafters was enough.

He put his face into his father's sleeve, breathed the safe smell of him, and decided he would remember the shape of that sound forever.
