## Part I: The Contract

The handler was a man who had learned to feel nothing at his desk. He sat in a room that was neither warm nor cold, under light that was neither bright nor dim, and opened a ledger that contained the prices of human bodies.

"Aisen," the handler said, reading the name without inflection. "Age twenty-two. Commoner. No significant connections. Works as an informant for the Federation."

The assassin waited. The handler was the kind of thorough that turned killing into accounting.

"Payment: One hundred gold crowns. Standard contract. He will be in the city market on the fourth of Autumn, approximately midday. He will be purchasing supplies. You will have a ten-minute window to create an accident. Something that looks like misfortune. A collapsing shelf. A fall from a height. A poisoned drink. The client does not care, only that it appears coincidental."

The assassin nodded. They had been trained to nod instead of speak. Speech was inefficient.

"One thing," the handler added. They never added things without reason. "The client is very specific. The death must look natural, or like bad luck. If it looks like an assassination, the contract is void and you will not be paid. Worse, you will be found."

The assassin understood. This was a man with resources. A man who could afford to be meticulous.

"The target will be alone," the handler continued. "He trusts few people. He carries a chain weapon—unusual, probably custom. He may be armed with magic. You will need to be fast."

The assassin had been fast for fifteen years. They had killed forty-seven people. Forty-seven times, they had made the world smaller by one body. They had learned to feel nothing about it.

"Go," the handler said.

## Part II: The Market

Aisen was thinking about vegetables.

Specifically, he was thinking about the fact that the refugee camps needed more vegetables, and that the winter stores were depleted, and that the farmers in the interior were hoarding surplus because prices were high. He needed to find black-market access to someone who had stock. Or he needed to convince someone to sell at loss for the camps.

Neither was likely.

He navigated the market by habit—the same path he always took, past the same stalls, buying the same items. Potatoes. Dried fish. A loaf of bread. He was not paying attention to the people around him. He was thinking about supply chains and logistics and the slow starvation of people in the camps.

The street was crowded. That was good—crowds meant dispersal of attention, meant no one could track one person's movements easily. The assassin was in the crowd. Aisen did not notice.

The assassin had three options:
1. Poison in his drink (there was a tavern ahead)
2. A staged fall (there was a market overhang nearby, strategically unstable)
3. A misdirected path into a prepared location

The assassin chose the overhang. It was clean. It was fast. The victim would be buried under produce. It would look like negligence, like the stall owner had built badly. It would look like bad luck.

The assassin began to steer toward the overhang, positioning themselves to coincide with Aisen's path. They had killed forty-seven people. Forty-eight would be no different.

## Part III: The Moment Before

Aisen felt it a half-second too late.

The sense of attention. The specific weight of someone's gaze. It was a skill he had learned—the ability to feel when he was being watched. It had saved him three times. It would have saved him now, except he was tired. His attention was fragmented across supply routes and refugee camps and the endless mathematics of insufficient aid.

He turned.

He saw the assassin. He saw the intention. He saw the overhang above him shifting—imperceptibly, but shifting. He understood, in the way that his analytical mind could process and execute simultaneously: *The stall is about to collapse. I am meant to be crushed beneath it.*

His body moved before his conscious thought could slow it down. Chain already uncoiling, already moving. One loop to catch the falling beam. One loop to anchor to the stable structure. His whole body pivoting, diving, rolling into the gap.

The overhang fell where he had been standing three seconds before.

The crowd screamed. The assassin was already moving—protocols demanded dispersal. The failure was absolute. The target had noticed. The target had moved. The clean accident had become a murder attempt.

The handler would not pay.

The assassin disappears into the crowd carrying the knowledge of failure.

## Part IV: The Aftermath (Aisen's Perspective)

Aisen lay on his back under the fallen structure, half-protected by the angle of his chain, surrounded by splintered wood and scattered vegetables. His heart was moving at a speed that made thought difficult.

That had been an assassination. Professional grade. The kind where someone had been paid money to end him.

Who? The obvious answer was Heresy, but they usually came with more obvious violence. The Federation? No—they needed him. The beast-kin? No motive. Someone he had wronged during his informant work?

There were too many possibilities. There were never enough certainties.

A woman approached, helping him stand. "Are you hurt?"

"No," Aisen said. He was already cataloging damage: the chain was intact, no links broken. His wrist was strained but functional. His pride was intact because pride was a luxury he could not afford.

"That was lucky," the woman said. "That stall—someone built it terribly wrong."

"Yes," Aisen agreed, though he did not believe it was luck.

He looked around the market, at the crowd that was already beginning to disperse, at the way attention was already moving on to the next thing. Somewhere in that crowd, the person who had tried to kill him was walking away, moving toward their next job or their next failure.

Aisen filed the information. He would need to change his patterns. He would need to tell someone—but tell who? The less people knew about an assassination attempt, the safer he was. Information was danger.

He picked up his scattered supplies—the vegetables, the fish, the bread—and continued through the market as if nothing had happened. Because that was the only way to survive: you did not acknowledge the attempt. You did not show the fear. You simply integrated the knowledge that someone was trying to kill you into the already-heavy load of things you were carrying.

By evening, he would have figured out which contact had sold his routine. By tomorrow, he would have changed his route. By the end of the week, he would have planted false information about his location to misdirect whoever had hired the assassin.

But right now, in the market, surrounded by people buying vegetables for dinner, Aisen simply gathered what he needed and walked forward.

Lucky did not exist. But survival did.

And survival required the understanding that someone, somewhere, wanted him dead.

It was not new information.

But it was heavier today than it had been yesterday.
