He had started the letter seventeen times.

The paper was expensive—Pieter's supply, stolen from the Academy stores. The ink was black. The quill was borrowed from a merchant's desk. All the tools to say something final were in place. Aisen's hands were not cooperating.

*Dear Mira,*

He had written that much. Then stopped. *Dear Mira* was what you wrote when something came afterwards that justified the word "Dear." When you had gratitude to express, or news to share, or a reason to expect a response.

He crumpled the paper. Started again.

*I am alive.*

That was true. Verifiable. A statement of fact without claim. He set down the quill and read it. The words looked cold. True, but cold. He wanted to crumple this one too, but the supply was limited.

Outside, the tavern was shuttering for the night. Aisen could hear the small sounds of it—the bolts drawn, the window frames closed, the final voices of the serving staff moving toward bed. The tavern had a rhythm to it, the way a heartbeat had rhythm. He had lived in that rhythm for years. Now years since then felt like months, or maybe seconds. Time had become negotiable.

He tried again.

*I think about the kitchen.*

He wrote faster now, before the thought could scatter.

*I think about you teaching me to knead bread. I was twelve. My hands were too small and too stiff. You showed me the way—let the dough push back, meet it halfway. Said that bread was like anything else: if you fought it, it would fight you. If you learned to listen to it, it would become what it wanted to be.*

He paused. This was true. This was the kind of true that hurt because he was already someone who had learned to fight instead of listen.

*I learned to fight instead. I am sorry.* 

He set the quill down again. The ink was drying. He would have to start over.

Actually, no. He would keep the crossed-out letters. They were more honest.

*I learned to fight instead. I am sorry. I think that was the choice that mattered. The moment I stopped listening to the dough and started forcing it into shapes. You tried to teach me differently. I did not listen.*

He could picture her reaction if she read this. Mira did not do sentiment. She would read his apology and set the letter down and go about her work. But he knew her well enough to know that the letter would be folded and kept, maybe in an inside pocket, maybe in the space between the pages of an old book.

He wrote more. Fast now, the way words came when you stopped thinking about them:

*I cannot come home. Even if I run now, the war will find me, or the things I have seen will find me, or I will find them. I am tangled up in too many ends. If I came home, I would be a rope unspooling, and you would be caught at the end of it.*

*Sometimes I think about what would happen if I came back. If the war ended tomorrow. If I simply walked away. I think you would not ask questions. You would make food. You would let the rhythm of the kitchen be enough. And I would try to pretend to be small again. But I cannot.*

He set down the quill. The next thought was the important one, and he did not know how to write it without sounding like something someone else had written. Like a lesson or a moral. He tried anyway.

*I think I came to understand what you were teaching me. The bread—it was never about bread. It was about respect. About learning that the world is not your tool, and people are not puzzles to solve. About meeting things halfway. But by the time I understood, I had already become someone who forgot those lessons. Someone who sees everything as a problem requiring a solution.*

*I do not know if you will read this. I do not know if I will send it. But I wanted you to know that you were right. About the bread. About everything.*

*I will try to come home if I can. If I cannot, please do not wait.*

He set down the quill. The letter was three pages now. It had taken an hour. His hands were shaking—not from fear, but from the effort of holding in all the things he could not say. Could not explain. Could not fix.

He read it through once. The words were adequate. They were honest. They were useless because they explained nothing and changed nothing and could not possibly repair the year he had missed, the weight he had brought home and then carried away again into the war.

Aisen folded the letter. He folded it small, three times, until it was no bigger than his palm. He had a choice now: seal it or burn it.

The fire was warm. It would be quick.

He held the letter over the flame without moving. The edge of the paper began to brown. He could smell the ink singeing. The moment of no-return was approaching.

He pulled his hand back.

He put the letter in a book—a volume of old poetry he had taken from the Academy, useless to him but beautiful to look at sometimes. He slid it between the pages near the end. If Mira ever found it, it would be by accident. If he ever came home, he would find it before she did and burn it then.

For now, it existed in the space between his thoughts and her knowledge. Unresolved. Honest. Impossible.

He lay down on the bed. Tomorrow there was a contract—a merchant caravan to the border, protection from bandits, payment in silver. Dangerous work. The kind of work where people died.

He might die tomorrow. Or the day after. Or the war might finally end and he might walk home and find the letter and burn it before showing it to her.

For now, the letter lived in the book, and he lived in the war, and Mira lived in the tavern waiting for a letter that might never come.

It was not enough. But it was honest.

That would have to be sufficient.
