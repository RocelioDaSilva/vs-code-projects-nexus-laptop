# Epilogue — The Wandering Stag, Years Later

The plaque was small and unadorned, nailed to the beam above the bar: *Aisen Korv — Kept the North. Remembered by those he kept.* People read it and walked on. People who had known the man read it and paused. The Wandering Stag had changed in details—new varnish on the counter, a different pot over the hearth, a child's face at the window—but it had kept its rhythm: stories and stew and people needing one another in small, consistent ways.

Mira ran the place with the steady confidence of someone who had been given responsibility by force of circumstance. She had inherited her father's economy and his stubborn gentleness; she also had his temper in a narrow, useful cut. She kept a space behind the bar for those with heavy things in their hands, and she took the time to set the ale down with a hand that said everything need not be explained.

People came to the tavern with purposes. One summer a young man with callused fingers and a far look in his eyes came by asking after stories of the chain. He was a student at the Common Stave and had heard the name in lectures. Mira served him stew and then handed him a small, wrapped parcel.

Inside was a link of chain polished to a soft rust. "Keep it," Mira said. "Don't make it something it's not. It's for the boy who thinks he needs proof."

The Academy changed. Not overnight, but in accumulative edits. Master Wei published a short manual in the Common Stave curriculum titled *Gradients and Leverage: Practical Applications for Low‑Power Weaves*. The appendix included a short section—unnamed as a dedication—on the use of memory‑metal links as local constructs for tactical defense. It spoke plainly about small pushes, the cost of tethers, and the ethics of rescue as a discipline rather than spectacle.

In the Common Stave's yard, a handful of students practiced the chain under a watchful instructor with hands that had once trembled over notes. They called it, among themselves and with the mischievous affection of those who learn by doing, "Aisen's Method." It was taught not as hero worship but as a curriculum: how to set a spring trap, how to make a coil hold a roof long enough for a rescue, how to measure MU cost against human life.

Pieter came through the tavern sometimes; he was older and more careful about the kinds of risks he took with other people's money. He still argued about whether the academy had been right to hold a field trial at Frostfang; Mira let him. Amara sent a letter once from a post no map could find easily; it contained a pressed leaf and a single line: *We keep him by doing good work.*

The boy named Aiden—after Aisen—grew up threading rope and learning how to count the sound of footsteps on different boards. He did not become a warrior; he became the sort of man who made sure the village knew its exits. On certain nights he would climb the tavern roof and run his hands along the sign's carving and think of a man who had been ordinary and then not once.

At the Common Stave, instructors taught a ritual of remembrance that was practical: a small hour of the year set aside to check gear, to teach the fundamentals of knot and anchor, to remind students that usefulness is not a glamorous thing. They wound the broken link into a display case and placed it in a glass box, not as relic but as reminder: tools wear out and people do too. Keep both tended.

Some men who had been his enemies changed their faces in public; some hid their discomfort with more money and quieter houses. The Heartland sent a small delegation one year that gave a formal nod and then left without a banquet. The world, in its slow social gravity, rearranged itself around the absence.

Mira kept a slot on the bar for a small carved stag. It was worn and the wood had been oiled into soft detail. People asked about it sometimes and she would tell the story—the theft in the tavern, the rope, the cleric's hands—because stories told plainly are useful. They teach people what to do when they see unknown things: stop, help, and then remember.

On the anniversary they set one extra plate at the end of the bar. The tavern kept a moment of silence that was simple and not quite solemn. A mason's hand hammered a small nail into the plaque and someone told a joke so bad it made everyone laugh. They balanced grief and humor the way beds balance comfort and necessity.

The chain's style lived on not as legend but as discipline. In the Common Stave library a small card hung near the shelving that read: *The point of a life is not that it be heralded; it is that it make other lives easier to live.* Students copied the line into margins and then threw themselves into work with the odd fervor that follows a clear instruction.

Years later, a traveling student visited the Wandering Stag with a uniform patched and a scroll tucked in his belt. He set the scroll on the bar, and Mira read the neat sentences. Master Wei's school had recommended a minor medalion for the Common Stave students who taught the chain methods to non‑patron villages; it was modest but the first public recognition of the method.

There was no monument of empire for Aisen. There was only the wooden stag in a pocket and a plaque above a bar and the knowledge that if a person learned to make the small pushes and to spare the little energy for another's life then, in a modest arithmetic of kindness, more people kept walking.
