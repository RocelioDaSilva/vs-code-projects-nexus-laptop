The chain lay across his lap like a sleeping thing.

Aisen sat on the edge of the pier, feet dangling above dark water that reflected nothing—no moon, no stars, only the suggestion of depth. The chain was cold. His father's chain. The weight of it was exactly what it had always been, and yet it felt heavier now, as if grief had physics and could press down through steel and into bone.

He had not come to the water to drown. He had come to consider it.

This was not noble. Heroes in stories did not sit in mud-soaked clothes contemplating the mercy of drowning. But Aisen had never been interested in being a hero, and he was running out of reasons to be anything else.

The telegram had been brief: **Father deceased. Letters enclosed. Come home or don't.** Those were his mother's words—not hostile, not forgiving, simply reporting fact. His mother reported facts the way other people breathed. She did not waste breath on emotion.

He had not gone home. Instead, he had received the packet of letters—seven of them, dated across the years, each folded with the precision of a man who had learned that care in small things sometimes prevented care in large ones. The most recent was dated three weeks before his father's death, which meant his father had known. Not why, perhaps. But that he was going.

In the last letter, his father had written: *I do not know how to tell you this without it sounding like forgiveness, which I do not believe in. I only believe in honest work and trying to see the world before it is too late. I was not always good at this. I hope you will be better.*

That was all.

Aisen had read it seventeen times. (He had counted. Aisen counted everything.) Each time, the words felt different. Each time, they landed in a different place and bled into a different failure.

The war had been running for three years. Aisen had prevented three assassinations, disrupted two spy networks, guided refugees through two border crossings, been shot twice (once through the shoulder, once in the thigh), and had been commended once by a general whose name he barely remembered. He had done work. Good work, perhaps. Honest work, certainly. The sort of work his father might have approved of.

And his father was dead anyway.

That was the hinge-point, the thing that would not turn in his mind. He had chosen this path—the small edges, the quiet precision, the belief that incremental progress mattered. He had built his life on the assumption that if he was careful enough, precise enough, good enough, it would matter. Not to him. To them. To someone.

But his father had died while Aisen was three hundred miles away documenting evidence of supply corruption. His father had died while Aisen was making small differences.

What if he could not afford small differences? What if the cost was everything, and the return was nothing?

He thought about leaving. It was not a new thought, but it had weight today. He could fold the chain, pack the letters, and walk home. He could help his mother. She would not thank him—that was not her way—but she would need help, and he could provide it. He could be useful in a way that was visible. He could sleep in his own bed. He could stop documenting deaths in ledgers and start living a life instead.

The thought was so vivid he could taste it. A cottage. A trade—something simple. Metalwork, perhaps. He had always had hands that could move with intention. He could make things instead of preventing their destruction. He could be small in a place where small was enough.

His father would have liked that. *Honest work.* What was more honest than a man who made shoes or mended gates?

But he knew—and this was the thought that would not let him sleep—he knew that his mother had written *come home or don't* for a reason. She knew him. She knew that if he came home, he would spend the rest of his life knowing that he had abandoned others to come to her. She was giving him permission to choose the war over her grief because she could not bear to be the reason he became someone who looked back with regret.

That was love, Aisen thought. Not the rescuing kind, not the kind that appeared in stories. Not the kind his father had shown him through tutorials and letters. The love that said: *I will live with your absence if it means you don't have to live with your guilt.*

The chain was still cold in his lap.

It was possible—likely, even—that his father would have approved of Aisen leaving the war. It was also possible that his father would have understood why Aisen could not. His father had been a man of small choices, but they had always required him to stay. To finish the work. To not abandon the half-done thing.

Aisen had inherited that, as much as he had inherited the chain.

He pulled the chain into his chest, letting it coil against his sternum like something alive. The links pressed against his ribs. He could feel his heartbeat through the steel.

He would go back to the war. He would not go home. He would carry the letters and the chain and the weight of his father's death the way he carried everything else: carefully, precisely, with the knowledge that some things could never be set down.

But he would also stop. For one moment, he would stop and acknowledge that this choice had a cost. That he was choosing the war over his mother's grief. That his father had made it possible for him to make this choice, and that was the whole of his father's gift—not forgiveness, as he had said, but the clarity to see what the choice required and the courage to ask his son to make it anyway.

Aisen stood. The chain fell away from his lap and coiled around his arm like it had found a resting place. The water below reflected nothing, so he did not see himself in it. He turned away from the pier and walked back toward the camp, where there were reports to file and refugees waiting and work still undone.

He did not look back.
