The prisoners were tied to stone pillars—three of them, maybe four. It was hard to tell if one of them was still breathing.

Aisen watched from the gallery above, hidden in shadow, his hand moving across paper in quick, coded shorthand. Documentation. This was what Amara paid him for now—not combat, but witnessing. Writing down the names of men and women who committed atrocities so that someone, somewhere, might one day hold them accountable.

He did not believe that day would come. But he wrote anyway.

Lady Cassandra Vex stood in the center of the chamber in robes of deep blue, her hands moving through gestures that were not quite violent and not quite graceful. They were pure command. Pure will imposed on flesh.

"Speak," she said to the first prisoner.

The man did not speak. His jaw was locked.

Cassandra smiled. She raised her hand and the man's arm broke. Not by blow, but by the power itself—the bones twisted under their own weight, reduced to angles that human bones could not hold.

He screamed.

"The route," Cassandra said. "Where does your resistance hide? Where do they move supplies?"

The man screamed and screamed. Aisen wrote: *Bone manipulation. Selective application. Victims unable to resist.*

"Where?"

The man gave her a name. A false name, Aisen suspected. Cassandra did not seem to care. She released the pressure and the arm fell, useless and wrong.

"Next," she said.

The second prisoner was younger—maybe sixteen, maybe younger. Female. Her eyes were already broken.

"I don't know anything," the girl said before Cassandra even asked. "Please. I don't know anything."

"Neither do these," Cassandra said, gesturing at the prisoners. "But I ask anyway, because asking is the nature of my work. You understand?"

The girl did not understand, but she opened her mouth anyway. Cassandra was already moving. The technique this time was different—not destructive, but paralyzing. The girl's body went rigid. Her eyes wide. Unable to move, unable to cry out, locked in her own muscle.

Aisen wrote: *Nervous system interruption. Subject conscious but immobilized. Duration unknown. Potential for permanent damage.*

Cassandra held it for thirty seconds. Then released. The girl gasped and wept and tried to curl into herself, but her body was struggling to remember how to work properly. Cassandra moved on.

The third prisoner was silent throughout. When Cassandra approached, he spat at her feet.

She did not respond to the insult. Instead, she reached out and touched his forehead.

What happened next was not violent in a conventional sense. The man simply began to change. His skin took on a grey cast. His hair whitened. His body collapsed inward as if time was being applied selectively to him alone.

"Accelerated aging," Cassandra said conversationally. "Fascinating work, really. The mages who developed it said it could be controlled, reverse-applied for healing. But it's not easy to reverse. Usually the body just continues aging until it gives out."

She released. The prisoner was ancient now, maybe eighty though he had been thirty minutes ago. He was barely breathing.

"Please," he whispered. "Please."

"I know," Cassandra said gently. "It's painful. But you shouldn't have helped the rebels. This is what choosing the wrong side looks like."

Aisen did not move. He did not breathe. He wrote: *Accelerated temporal decay. Applied selectively. Subject still conscious. Technique appears to be irreversible. Subject aging at estimated rate: 1 year per minute. Death expected within hours if not reversed by original caster.*

The name of this technique was forbidden knowledge. It was on the heresy list. Nobility was not supposed to know it, much less use it casually in a basement chamber like a merchant uses a scale to weigh grain.

Cassandra would not be punished. Aisen knew this with perfect clarity. She was a noble. This chamber was not a crime scene—it was a tactical interrogation center. The prisoners would be listed as casualties of war, if they were listed at all.

But Aisen was writing it down. Aisen was making a record.

"You may go," Cassandra said to the girl and the man with the broken arm. "Tell your people what you saw. Tell them I have many techniques. Tell them that resistance is a choice that has consequences."

They fled, limping and weeping, into the darkness above the stairs.

Only the aged prisoner remained, gasping, dying at an accelerated pace.

Cassandra looked up—directly at the gallery where Aisen was hidden. For an instant, their eyes met.

She knew he was there. She had always known.

He held her gaze. "Am I supposed to run?" he asked, stepping from shadow into the pale lantern light.

"No," Cassandra said. "I'm supposed to let you write it down. I'm supposed to let you carry this back to your network. I'm supposed to let them know that I am capable of this and willing to use it. Fear is the point."

"And the prisoners?"

"The prisoners are leverage. You are insurance."

Aisen descended the stairs slowly. He did not run. He walked past Cassandra and the dying man and out into the night. Behind him, he heard the prisoner's final ragged breath. Heard it cut short. Heard whatever Cassandra did to end it quickly, as a mercy for a useful corpse.

When he reached Amara's safe house, she was waiting for him.

"Did you see?" she asked.

"Everything," Aisen said. "I have everything."

"Then we are done here," Amara said. "We have what we need to prove that the noble houses are using forbidden techniques. This is leverage. This is the thing we need to begin the move against them."

"She knew I was watching," Aisen said.

"Yes," Amara said. "Because the point is not actually to catch her using forbidden magic. The point is to make you a witness. To make you understand what is happening in this war. To make you complicit in the knowledge and therefore committed to the action that comes next."

Aisen understood. He had been used deliberately. He had been sent to document a crime not because documentation would stop it, but because witnessing would bind him to the obligation of response.

He looked at his notes. The careful shorthand, the scientific language, the distance he had maintained by writing it down as if it were theoretical.

But none of that changed what he had seen. Three people, tortured in a basement, using techniques that were supposed to be impossible to possess. One was dead. One was aged to death. One would carry the scars forever.

And Cassandra Vex had let him watch specifically so he would carry the story outward. Fear as currency. Atrocity as negotiation.

"What now?" Aisen asked.

"Now," Amara said, "you become a believer in the revolution. Now you understand that the world cannot be fixed by rules. It can only be fixed by breaking them first."

Aisen did not sleep that night. He did not eat or rest. He simply sat with his documentation and watched the words blur as his eyes moved across them. Four people. Four different techniques. Four different violations of the sacred laws.

And the clear understanding: the Sacred Laws only protected people when there was someone willing to enforce them.

In this war, no one was enforcing anything. There was only the strong doing whatever they needed to do, and the weak being done unto.

Aisen was still the weak in this equation. He was not strong enough to stop Cassandra. He was not powerful enough to change the nature of the war.

All he could do was write it down. All he could do was carry the story forward.

All he could do was let the knowledge corrupt him the way Cassandra had intended.
