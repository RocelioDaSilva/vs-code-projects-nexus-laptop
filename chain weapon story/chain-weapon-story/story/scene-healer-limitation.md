The soldier had been bleeding for seven minutes when they brought him to the healing tent.

Naia did not know his name. There was no time for names. There was only the map of blood and damage that spread across his body like a language written in the wrong alphabet—fracture here, rupture here, the slow pooling of a severed artery. She had trained for six years to read that language. She had broken the first three rules of her bloodline to learn it.

She could not read this one.

"Hold him," she said to the people who had brought him. They did not move. They were soldiers, and soldiers knew better than to watch a healer work. They knew that watching made it real in a different way. They left.

Naia placed her hands on the wound.

The Flux came when she called it—that was the first rule, the one her grandmother had taught her with nothing but a glance and a shared bloodline. The Flux knew her. It flowed where she directed. It understood the shape of healing the way water understands the shape of a river.

But it did not understand this.

The soldier's Flux was leaving him. She could feel it—a slow, steady departure, like a guest who has decided the party is over and is moving toward the door. She could sense it in his cells, in his bones, in the spreading cold that no amount of her heat could counteract. His body had already made a decision. Already begun its return to the elements.

She pushed harder. She pulled Flux from her own reserves—her grandmother would have called her foolish for this, but foolishness was another name for urgency. She poured energy into tissue repair, into stemming the bleeding, into convincing the soldier's own biology to hold on.

For a moment, it worked. The bleeding slowed. The wound began to close. The soldier gasped like a man breaking the surface of water.

"It's working," he whispered. "I can—I feel—"

But Naia felt it too: the shift. The moment when the Flux she had forced into his body was not enough, when his own internal reserves proved insufficient, when the loss had been too much for recovery. The door had opened too wide. The guest was not returning.

She tried everything she had learned. She called on blood-bonding—illegal, forbidden, a breaking of the first rule. She reached into his cells and tried to rebuild them from nothing. She borrowed Flux from the boy watching at the edge of the tent—Aisen, she thought his name was—and when he was empty, she tried to pull from herself again.

Nothing held.

The soldier stopped gasping. He stopped trying. His Flux dispersed into the air like a sigh. Within seconds, he was cooling. Within minutes, he was inert. Within another minute, he was something else entirely—a body, a thing, a problem that would need to be moved and disposed of.

Naia sat back on her heels. Her hands were shaking. Seven minutes of perfect work, and it had meant nothing. Seven minutes of effort against a door that should not have opened.

"You did everything you could," Aisen said. He was standing closer now. She had not heard him move.

"I failed," Naia said.

"You lost a battle with death. That is not the same as failure."

Naia looked at him. The boy was young, older than some of the soldiers but younger than most of the officers. He had the bearing of someone who had learned to observe without judgment. She did not like it.

"The healing texts say that death is the absence of Flux," she said flatly. "If that is true, then bringing Flux back should prevent it. I brought Flux. I brought all of it I could. And he died anyway."

"Because death is not the absence of Flux," said Master Wei. He had entered while they were speaking. The old trainer was like that—appearing in places he was needed without seeming to need invitation. "Death is the Flux's decision to leave. Once the body has decided the cost is too high, once the Flux has committed to dispersing back into the general field, no amount of external Flux will convince it to stay. The decision has to come from within. And that decision is final."

Naia felt something settle in her chest. Not comfort, exactly. But a kind of understanding. She had been fighting the wrong battle. She had been trying to prevent a departure that had already been decided.

"So we cannot resurrect," she said.

"You can heal," Wei replied. "But only if the Flux still has the will to stay. Once it has decided to leave, there is no calling it back. The person is gone."

Wei looked at Aisen, and something passed between them—an acknowledgment, a shared understanding.

"This is important," Wei continued, turning back to Naia. "You will be a healer for many years. You will lose people despite your best efforts. You will learn to accept that some deaths cannot be prevented. But you will also learn that trying—even when trying fails—is the boundary between healing and loss. The moment you stop trying is the moment death becomes inevitable."

He placed a hand on her shoulder, and to her surprise, Naia found herself weeping. Not from failure, but from the strange relief of having a boundary drawn. She could work within it now. She could fail within it, and know that her failure was not the same as the soldier's choice.

Aisen was still watching, still observing. He did not offer comfort. Instead, he said: "Thank you for trying. That matters more than the outcome."

And then he left, the way he left most conversations—without fanfare, without waiting for response, understanding that he had said the thing that needed to be said and that staying longer would only complicate it.

Naia looked at the body.

"Once the Flux leaves," she whispered, continuing Wei's lesson to herself, "they are gone. No coming back. No resurrection. Only the work we do before that moment, only the people we save while they are still choosing to stay."

She began the work of preparing the body for removal. Her hands had stopped shaking. Her mind was clear again.

She would fail again. She knew that now. She would watch people die despite her best efforts. But she would also understand—truly understand—that some lessons could only be learned through loss, and that the mark of a true healer was not preventing death, but knowing when to let it come gently, knowing when to try desperately, and knowing the difference between those two moments.

The soldier's Flux was long gone now, scattered back into the general field, no part of it claiming individuality anymore. But he would be remembered by the person who had tried to save him. That was a kind of resurrection—not of the body, but of the choice. Of the moment when someone had loved him enough to break every rule trying to keep him alive.

Once the Flux departs, they are gone.

Never to return.

But while they choose to stay, everything is possible.
