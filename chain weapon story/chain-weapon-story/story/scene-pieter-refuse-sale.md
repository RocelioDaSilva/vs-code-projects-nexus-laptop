His father's ledger was a language Pieter had learned at age seven: the lean of numbers, the weight of profit, the small, cold arithmetic that made a man into a vessel for accumulation. Every entry was a promise that had been kept and a person who had been counted.

The merchant who came to the warehouse that afternoon smelled of silk and cruelty—not the brutish kind that announces itself, but the refined variety that moves through a room like a guest. He came with a simple request: a shipment of forged blades, the kind that looked well-made but would shatter in the third real use. He wanted them sent to a village in the lowlands and he wanted them labeled with confidence.

'The commoners won't know the difference,' the man said, setting a purse on the table as if the weight of gold could be an argument. 'And if one breaks in a quarrel, well—they have no recourse. No one hears their complaints.'

Pieter's father had trained him to see such commissions as straightforward trades. A merchant buys, a merchant sells; the use of the thing is not the merchant's concern. His father had said this a hundred times in a hundred ways: conscience is a luxury that makes a man poor.

But Pieter had been to the village. He had gone with Aisen to help move refugees after a border skirmish, and he had seen the hands of people who had nothing to trade but their labor. He had watched a woman hold a blade—a good blade—and understand it as the difference between eating and starving. He had felt the weight of that moment in his own palms.

He looked at the gold purse and then at the forged blades stacked in neat rows like teeth.

'No,' he said.

His father came down from the office sometime later, drawn by the commotion. He moved like a man who had never heard the word 'no' used in his own warehouse and was curious about its shape. He studied Pieter with the dispassion of someone examining inventory.

'These are profitable,' his father said, not asking.

'They're poison,' Pieter replied.

His father picked up one of the blades and tested its weight. 'They're metal. They will do what metal does. That they are not perfect metal is not our concern.'

'The men who use them will die.'

'Men die,' his father said. 'It is not our business whether they die well or poorly. Our business is the trade.'

Pieter felt something crack inside him—not dramatically, but like ice thinning under spring sun. He had spent his entire life in the shadow of this arithmetic, and for the first time he understood that remaining silent was itself a choice. It was a choice to let the gold become heavier than the hands that would hold the blade.

'I won't pack them,' Pieter said.

His father set the blade down with care. He looked at his son in the way a man looks at a piece of property that has devalued. 'You will pack them, or you will not come home.'

Pieter felt the weight of that ultimatum like a stone. He had been raised to believe that the warehouse was the only home that mattered—that outside its walls was dissolution and irrelevance. But standing in front of his father, he realized the warehouse had never been a home. It had been a cage dressed up in the language of legacy.

He picked up the purse of gold and set it on the floor. Then he began, methodically, to break the forged blades. One by one, he brought them down against the table's edge. The sound was small and repetitive and true. His father watched without moving, as if watching a man commit suicide.

When the last blade was bent beyond use, Pieter looked at his father and saw, for perhaps the first time, a man rather than an authority. He saw someone who had chosen the ledger over every other thing and had built such a perfect cage around himself that he could not even recognize it.

'I'm going with Aisen,' Pieter said quietly. 'I'm going to find something worth trading for.'

He walked out of the warehouse in the afternoon light and did not look back. His father did not call out; there was nothing to call him back with, nothing that outweighed the small, human decision to refuse.

Later, when Aisen asked him where he'd come from and why he looked as if he'd walked through fire, Pieter said simply: 'I stopped being a merchant.'

It was not grand. It was only a refusal, a moment when gold ceased to be heavier than conscience. But in that moment, Pieter discovered something his father had never found: the particular lightness that comes from choosing the harder thing.
