# ORIN: THE WANDERER

## Years After Thornwood

Orin did not stay in any settlement. That had never been his pattern, and Aisen's death—far from changing it—only reinforced the principle: he was useful in motion. He was valuable in the spaces between.

He became a ghost story. A rumor. *A man came through here. He solved a problem that seemed unsolvable. He didn't ask for payment. He just... left.*

In a garrison town north of the border, a captain was struggling with a tactical problem. The 4th Party had cut supply lines. Soldiers were going hungry. The situation was untenable, but any attempt at supply runs increased casualties.

Orin appeared at the gates one evening. He listened to the problem. He asked three questions. Then he said: "You're thinking about this wrong. You don't need to break the blockade. You need to encourage desertion in the 4th Party line. Enough soldiers leaving means the blockade becomes logistically impossible to maintain. And you can encourage desertion without violence, simply by making the choice clear and achievable."

He explained the method—the one Aisen had taught him, the one Aisen had practiced. Set small, achievable goals. Make visible change. Show that the alternative is possible.

The captain implemented it. Within a month, the blockade collapsed. Not through military victory, but through the simple fact that keeping it was harder than abandoning it.

Orin left before the captain could offer him a reward.

## The Recognition

In a farming village near the coast, Orin encountered a young boy working in the fields. The boy had a clubfoot—a liability in most physical labor, a marker of uselessness in cultures that valued only strength.

Orin watched the boy compensate for his disability through cleverness and precision. He watched other workers overlook the boy's contributions because they were too focused on what he couldn't do.

Orin sat down beside him during the midday rest.

"You're undervalued," Orin said simply.

The boy looked at him with confusion. "I'm useless."

"No. You're inconvenient. That's different. Useless things get discarded. Inconvenient things get worked around, and most people aren't creative enough to work around them effectively. But you're creative. So you're not actually inconvenient. You're invisible."

"That's not better."

"Isn't it? Because when you're invisible, no one is trying to change you. No one is pressuring you to be something you're not. You just have to learn to exist in the margins until someone smart enough recognizes what you actually are."

The boy was quiet for a long moment.

"Will anyone recognize it?"

"Maybe. Maybe not. But that's not actually your problem. Your problem is surviving until they do. And then your problem becomes deciding if you want to be recognized, or if you prefer the freedom of invisibility."

## The Method Spreads

Orin did not teach formally. He simply lived the principle. He appeared in places where the 4th Party had coordinated control, and he ask simple questions that revealed gaps in the coordination.

*"What would happen if the soldiers here simply refused to blockade, but not violently? If they just deserted quietly?"*

*"What would happen if every refugee took a different route, each person setting a small, achievable goal to reach safety?"*

*"What would happen if the heretic-hunters looked for something that didn't want to be found?"*

These questions created spaces. Small spaces. But small spaces could be expanded through persistence.

Slowly, the 4th Party's perfect coordination developed cracks. Not from military opposition, but from the simple fact that Aisen's method had been taught to people like Orin, and Orin was teaching it through living example.

## The Hollowness

Years of traveling alone, years of helping without connection, took their toll.

Orin began to notice that the pattern was familiar. He was useful. People appreciated him. But no one wanted to know him. No one asked him to stay. No one offered anything but gratitude and distance.

He was still being thrown out of the tavern. Just more politely.

One night, in a settlement he did not recognize, he found himself in a tavern—the very same tavern, perhaps, where he had once confessed to Aisen. A young person was sitting alone, untouched drink in front of them, staring at a wall.

Orin recognized the posture. He had worn it for decades.

He sat down across from the young person without asking permission.

"What are you thinking about?" Orin asked.

The young person looked up. "Why I exist, if nobody wants me to."

Orin retrieved the untouched drink and took a sip. It was terrible. "You exist for the same reason I do. Because the alternative is worse. And because somewhere, there might be one person worth existing for, even if you haven't met them yet."

"Is there?"

Orin considered the question, and for the first time in years, he gave the honest answer. "I don't know. But Aisen thought there was. And he died for it. So I'm going to assume he was right."

## The Final Teaching

Orin did eventually encounter someone who wanted more than his usefulness. It took forty years and happened in a place very far from where he had started.

It was a younger man, a refugee, someone who had learned Aisen's method and had become skilled at setting small, achievable goals—so skilled that he rarely failed, and his failures always taught him something.

This man asked Orin to stay. Not to solve a problem. Just to stay.

"Why?" Orin asked.

"Because I recognize something in you. You're someone who helped many people escape. And I want to help you escape something."

"What would that be?"

"The belief that you're only valuable when you're useful."

Orin wanted to leave. Every instinct told him to. But something in Aisen's legacy—something in the principle of being seen—made him stay.

They never became lovers or even close friends in the conventional sense. But they became something: two people who had been broken by systems that valued efficiency, learning that existence was its own justification.

Orin learned, late in life, that being useless was not the same as being worthless. That rest was not surrender. That being known was not the same as being appreciated.

---

## The End: Still Wandering

Orin did eventually leave the settlement. It was in his nature. But this time, when he left, someone knew where he was going. Someone would think of him and wonder. Someone would carry his memory as a person, not a tool.

He returned to wandering, but the wandering was different now. It was not an escape. It was a choice.

And in his wandering, he continued to teach the method without naming it as teaching. He asked questions. He listened. He helped people set small, achievable goals.

And slowly, the principle that Aisen had embodied—that understanding could solve what force could not—became less a rumor and more a way of being.

Not through grand gestures. Through small acts. Through people like Orin, who had learned that being invisible was not the same as not mattering.

He was still overlooked. Still dismissed. Still treated as useful rather than valuable.

But now Orin understood that this was not a personal failing. This was simply how societies worked. The brave thing was not to demand recognition, but to persist anyway.

Because somewhere, someone would need to hear the question he asked. Would need to learn the achievable goal. Would need to know that escape was possible.

And Orin would be there to show them.

Not for payment. Not for recognition.

Simply because Aisen had shown him how.
