He counted objects the way some men count prayers.

The chain at his throat had lost its polish in places where habit had made a pattern. He turned it between fingers callused by drafting and by work, and each link told a minor story: a scrape from a fall, a rounded edge where a child's hand had once trusted him, a dent that looked like a small moon. He had never been sentimental about metal. Metal was utility and weight. Yet tonight the chain felt like a cord to hold him to a world he might otherwise slide from.

He opened a small wooden box and took out three things: a folded letter, a carved stag no larger than his palm, and a thin strip of leather with the knot Harald had braided into it when they were young foolhardy things. His father's letter was the first: a page with the ink faint and the edges softened by being reread. The words were not grand—reminders about mending a collar, an aside about a market price—but they carried a steadiness that had always outranked eloquence.

Mira's carved stag had been whittled from a scrap left by the barn's joiner. She had given it to him with a blunt-faced smile and the simple instruction to keep steady, to 'remember the hills.' The stag's legs were short but confident; its eyes were two careful chips.

Harald's leather knot smelled faintly of oil and smoke. Harald had tied it into Aisen's wrist once when they'd been so young that their bravado had not yet learned discretion. He had said nothing, only clasped Aisen's palm and left the knot there.

Aisen set the things in a line across the table and let his hand hover over them. He thought of years like a series of small apprenticeships—how to stand, how to listen, how to keep the sick away from the mad. Each of these small objects was a lesson given back to him in a form that would not move. He smiled, and it was the size of a coin dropped in a stranger's pocket—private, immediate.

He remembered a night years before when he had made a small, impossible bargain for the life of someone he loved. In the memory, the room smelled of peat and vinegar and the man had been younger and far braver. He had lied then in service of a larger truth; tonight he thought of that lie as a rope he might one day need to climb. He thought of the weight of promises.

He ran a thumb along the stag's carved flank. The little groove where a blade had nicked it the year of the winter fair made him laugh softly; the sound was dry and quick. He imagined Mira somewhere sleeping with her hands curled, a small tidal movement in the quilt; he imagined Harald sharpening a spear and humming some old work-song.

Outside, men talked in the dark in low voices, planning movements, aligning themselves with a map drawn more by instincts than ink. The world had been reduced to angles and holdfasts and the calculus of who could be trusted. Aisen felt the irony like a flame: he had become, by degrees, someone who could be trusted to make a refuse of his own comfort for the safety of others.

He worked through it the way Harald had taught him, the way he worked through every impossible thing.

*What is the rule? The war continues. The 4th Party advances. People will die regardless of what I choose.*

*What breaks the rule? If I stand at Thornwood Hollow with this chain and these people, the 4th Party will break their advance. Three hundred people live to see tomorrow instead of zero. Not victory—just reprieve. Just the arithmetic of survival.*

*What is the cost? I don't come back. The chain doesn't come back. The people I could have protected in other futures don't exist because I chose this path instead. But that's always the choice: not between loss and no-loss, but between different losses.*

*What do I tell them? That I saw the rule and chose to break it in the direction that matters. That I know the cost. That I'm choosing it anyway, and they should know I chose it with my eyes open.*

He rose and fastened the chain at his throat. The simple motion grounded him in his body—metal against collarbone, a small pressure that translated into purpose. He put the leather knot into his pocket and then slid the stag into his palm. For a moment he held both and let their different weights settle.

There was no grand speech waiting for him. There was only the modest accumulation of fidelity: letters that said, 'come back,' carvings that said, 'remember,' knots that said, 'hold.' He folded the little things back into the box and closed it as if sealing a pact that required no herald.

He went to the door where his friends stood, the light from the lantern catching on the edge of his chain. He did not speak, because he had already chosen the sentence he needed to live with. He only met Mira's eyes and felt her lift the corners of her mouth a fraction. Harald's grip on his shoulder was brief and as solid as the wood of the spear. He smiled, the full kind that reached his eyes, and it was enough.

When he stepped out, it was with the feeling of all the small things he carried—those ordinary objects also served as ballast. They made the decision less like a single rupture and more like joining a line of men who did what had to be done. He straightened his shoulders and found, in that straightening, the last kind of courage that came not from certainty but from attachment.
