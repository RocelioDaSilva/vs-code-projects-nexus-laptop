# Tupac's Letter

Tupac sat in the library, his hands wrapped around a cup of cold tea.

He had been writing letters home since his first week at the Academy. Most of them were never sent.

This one, he began anyway.

*Mother,*

*I have learned to tie knots that can hold a man's weight. I have learned to read the wind. I have learned that lowlanders do not see the mountains as we do—to them, the peaks are obstacles, not ancestors. To them, the altitude is a hardship. To me, it is home.*

*But I am changing. I can feel it. My hands are softer. My lungs do not burn when I climb. I am forgetting the ceremonial songs. I am forgetting the names of the winds.*

*I will bring this knowledge home. The combat, the strategy, the foreign ways. But I fear it will not fit. I fear that when I return, I will be between two worlds, fully belonging to neither.*

*I miss you. I miss the peaks. I miss the air that remembers my ancestors.*

*Your son,*
*Tupac*

He folded the letter. He looked at his hands—callused from sword work, pale from years of northern winters.

He did not send the letter. He would deliver it himself. One day. When his studies were finished.

But he knew, even then, that the mother who read those words would not recognize the son who wrote them.
