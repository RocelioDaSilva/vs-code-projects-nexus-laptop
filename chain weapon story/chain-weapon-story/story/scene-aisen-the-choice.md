Lord Matthias smelled of lavender and certainty.

He sat across from Aisen in a tent that was too clean—canvas stretched tight, Persian rugs layered to muffle sound, wine glasses that caught candlelight like small, expensive catches. Aisen's boots left mud on the edge of a rug that probably cost more than his annual stipend. He did not apologize. Matthias did not seem to notice.

"You are wasted here," Matthias said, not unkindly. "Reporting to commoner officers, living on rations that would insult a dog. I've read your dispatches. The work is brilliant. But the context is all wrong."

Aisen said nothing. He had learned long ago that silence was a tool. People rushed to fill it.

"I can place you," Matthias continued. "Private counsel. You would report directly to House Matthias. The pay is three times what the military offers. You would have access to resources—archivists, scholars, informants across the kingdom. You could do the same work you're doing now, but with actual support. You would be able to prevent things instead of just documenting them afterwards."

It was seductive, framed exactly right. Aisen could see how it worked: the man did not promise power, only efficacy. Did not promise glory, only the ability to do something well.

"I appreciate the offer," Aisen said, and meant it.

"But you're going to refuse." Matthias smiled. It was the smile of a man who was used to people refusing him, and who had long ago learned to find it charming. "The principled young man. You have your own path."

"Yes."

"Why?"

Aisen considered the question. It would be easy to cite duty to the military, or loyalty to the cause. Matthias would understand those reasons. He could marshal them convincingly, and the conversation would end with Matthias nodding respectfully and moving on to his next acquisition.

But Aisen had learned something in the past month, since Frostfang. He had learned what it meant to be wrong at scale. He had learned what it felt like to make a choice that killed people who had nothing to do with his choice. And he had learned that the feeling of that failure was going to live in his chest for as long as he lived.

So he decided to be honest.

"Because if I work for you, I succeed on your terms," Aisen said. "If I fail, I fail in a way you approve of. If I refuse something you want me to do, the cost is my employment. You have a way of turning everything into a transaction. And I will start to think that way too. I will start to believe that the only kind of success that matters is the kind you recognize and reward. And one day, I will make a choice I think is right, and you will think it is wrong, and I will doubt myself because I have spent so long learning to doubt myself in your direction."

Matthias did not stop smiling, but something shifted in his eyes. A recalibration. Aisen had moved from interesting to dangerous.

"You think I would corrupt you," Matthias said.

"I think you would improve me," Aisen replied. "That's the problem. I would become better at the work, and worse at the thinking. I would know more, and understand less. And I would blame you for that, which would be unfair, because the choice to accept would be mine."

"So you choose to remain ineffective."

"I choose to remain honest."

Matthias poured himself more wine. He offered the bottle to Aisen, who declined. The silence stretched. This time, Matthias did not rush to fill it.

"Your father was a scholar," Matthias said finally. "Did you know that?"

"I knew he read."

"More than that. He was offered a position at House Matthias thirty years ago. I was younger then, newer to power. He turned me down too. Told me something very similar—that he wanted to be wrong on his own terms." Matthias took a sip. "I was furious with him for months."

"What changed?"

"I realized he was right. The men I kept—the brilliant ones who said yes—they were afraid of being wrong. So they only said yes to things they were certain about. Which meant they only worked on safe projects. Which meant I stopped getting brilliant work out of them. But your father..." Matthias smiled, and this time it was quieter, less calculated. "Your father did brilliant things because he didn't fear the cost. He could look at a problem and say, 'I might be wrong about this.' And then he would work anyway."

Aisen felt something shift in his chest. It was not forgiveness, exactly, but it was a kind of recognition.

"I'll make you an offer anyway," Matthias continued. "Not patronage. A retainer. You stay where you are, doing what you're doing, on your own terms. But you have access to my resources if you need them. No accountability to me, no obligation. You ask for something, I provide it or I don't, and you go on working regardless. In return, when you learn something important, you tell me. Not filtered through military channels, not shaped to someone else's narrative. Just the truth as you see it."

"That's still a transaction."

"Everything is a transaction," Matthias said. "Even refusal. You're paying with the knowledge that you might spend your life being less effective than you could have been. I'm paying with the knowledge that I might never hear the things you know. We are both choosing the cost we can live with."

Aisen thought about Frostfang. About the boy in the ditch. About the names he had written afterwards. About the feeling that even small edges might not be enough.

"I'll consider it," he said.

Matthias nodded. He did not push. That, more than anything, made Aisen believe the man's offer was genuine.

"One more thing," Matthias said as Aisen stood to leave. "Your father told me something, at the end. He said, 'I hope my son will be braver than I was.' I told him I doubted that was possible—that you seemed remarkably afraid already." Matthias smiled. "He said, 'Exactly. He's afraid *on his own terms*. That's all the bravery anyone gets.'"

Aisen left without responding. He walked back through the camp, past the fires and the wounded and the endless machinery of war, carrying that gift like a secret. His father had known what this moment would be. His father had known that Aisen would face the choice between effectiveness and autonomy.

And his father had told a powerful man to trust that Aisen would choose wrong.

That was love too.
