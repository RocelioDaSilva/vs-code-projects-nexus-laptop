Seraphine showed him the price written in symbols he did not recognize.

"This," she said, tracing a finger across the contract, "is the cost of the binding. You will feel nothing. No grief, no love, no attachment. You will be free."

Caspian read the words twice. The third time, he did not read them at all—he already knew what they said. He had been reading them for weeks, sleeping with the contract under his pillow, waking in the night with the knowledge that saying yes would solve everything.

All he had to do was let her take something.

"What will I lose?" he asked. He knew the answer. But he needed to hear it.

"A memory," Seraphine said. "Your choice which one. I will hollow it out. The moment will remain, but the feeling will be gone. You will remember being happy. You will not remember what happiness felt like."

She was standing close to him. Even now, even knowing what she was, he wanted to reach for her. That was the problem that the contract would solve. That wanting. That vulnerability. That feeling of being crushed under the weight of caring about someone who could unmake you with a word.

Caspian stood and walked to the window. The city below was moving—carts, people, the usual machinery of mortality. How many of them felt this? How many of them woke up every morning feeling like they were drowning?

"There is another option," Seraphine said behind him. "You could refuse. Leave. Find someone who loves you less. Less painful that way."

He knew she meant it. That was what made her dangerous—she was not cruel, only practical. She saw what needed to be done and did it. She saw that he was suffering and offered him the cleanest escape: make the price worth paying.

"I need to know," Caspian said, still looking at the city, "what memory?"

He had spent three days considering this. He had made lists. Moments with his parents (he was not close to them anyway). Moments with friends (replaceable). Moments of achievement (shallow, all of them).

There was one memory he kept returning to.

Aisen, two years ago, during a tournament. They had fought—properly fought, for points, in front of an audience. Aisen had beaten him, though barely. Afterwards, Aisen had approached him in the corridor and said something Caspian had not expected: *"You learned faster than I thought you would. Next time, maybe you win."*

It was not a compliment. It was a recognition. In that moment, Caspian had felt seen. Not as a threat, not as competition, but as a person who was learning. As someone who could improve.

He wanted to feel that again.

That was the problem. Wanting anything, wanting anyone, meant the possibility of loss. Seraphine had made that calculation very clear: care, and you will suffer when I take it from you. Or let me take the caring now, and suffer only once.

"The tournament," Caspian said. "When Aisen beat me. I want to give you that memory."

"The feeling of being recognized," Seraphine said. It was not a question.

"Yes."

He could hear her move. She came to stand beside him, close enough that he could feel her cold presence. In a moment, she would ask if he was sure. He was not. He never would be.

"One more thing," she said. "The contract requires that you actively choose it. You cannot simply accept passively. You must ask for it. You must want this."

Caspian felt something break open in his chest. Not hope—he had no hope. But something close to relief.

He turned to face her.

"I choose this," he said. "I want you to take the memory of being recognized. I want you to hollow it out. I want to remember that Aisen beat me, but I do not want to remember what it felt like to have him notice that I could learn."

As he spoke the words, he felt them carve away at something inside him. Not physical pain—nothing as simple as that. But a kind of amputation, preemptive and precise.

Seraphine smiled. It was not a cruel smile. That made it worse.

She reached up and pressed her palm to his forehead. Cold spread through his skull. He could feel the memory being located—the moment of standing in that corridor, the shock of Aisen's words, the bloom of something that felt like worth.

It was being erased. Not forgotten, exactly. The moment remained. He could see it clearly. But the feeling that had lived in it was being drawn out like water from a cup. He could see the outlines where warmth had been. But he could not feel the warmth itself.

When Seraphine pulled her hand away, Caspian felt lighter. Not better. Lighter, the way a corpse is lighter than a living thing.

"It is done," she said.

He tried to remember what the warmth had felt like. He could not. He could describe it—a brightness, a sense of mattering, a moment when he had felt like he was not drowning. But the actual feeling was gone, replaced by a clean, clinical understanding that such a feeling had once existed.

"How do you feel?" Seraphine asked.

"Empty," Caspian said. Then, correcting himself: "No. Not even empty. I remember being empty. I do not feel it."

She took his hand. It was strange to have her touch him now. It produced no feeling at all. No fear, no desire, no hope that she might care.

"This is the gift I am giving you," she said. "This emptiness. This clarity. You will never need anyone again. You will never be disappointed. You will never feel less because someone sees you as less."

Caspian looked at their hands joined together. Hers was pale, soft, with nails that were too long. His was warmer—still human, though that would change. He could observe how they looked together aesthetically. But he could not feel what it meant.

"When will the rest come?" he asked. "When will I stop feeling everything?"

"Slowly," Seraphine said. "With each contract you keep, with each promise you make, a bit more of you will hollow out. Eventually, you will be like me. Perfect. Free."

Caspian did not correct her. He did not say that this felt like dying, not freedom. Because he realized that objection itself was already fading. In a week, he would not be able to argue against this. In a month, he would not want to.

He was already becoming someone who had accepted the trade.

The worst part was that he could see it happening. He could observe his own dissolution the way he might observe a sunset. With interest, even. But without the feeling that would make him fight to stop it.

"Come," Seraphine said, leading him toward the bedroom. "There is much more to teach you. Much more to take."

Caspian followed because movement was easier than standing still. And as he followed, he tried one last time to remember what Aisen's recognition had felt like.

He could not.

The moment remained—a shape in his memory, an outline where something had lived. But the life itself was gone, carved out by his own choice, placed in the hands of someone who did not love him but was willing to accept the gift of his dissolution.

It was, he supposed, exactly as he had asked for.
