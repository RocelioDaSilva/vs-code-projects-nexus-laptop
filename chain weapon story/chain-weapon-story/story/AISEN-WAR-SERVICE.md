# AISEN'S WAR SERVICE: THE EXPOSED YEARS

## Part I: Conscription

The garrison commander stood in front of three hundred newly conscripted soldiers. Aisen was the youngest—sixteen, small, looking like he had never held a weapon in his life.

*"You have been selected to serve your region in the border conflict. Your service is compulsory. Your term is indefinite. You will obey orders. You will use your skills to defend against heretical incursion. Any Flux-user found among the enemy will be your priority target."*

Aisen was standing in the third row. His face gave nothing away. But internally, he was making calculations.

This was the trap. This was the moment Aldric had warned him about.

Flux-users were being conscripted into military service specifically because they were Flux-users. The Covenant could not risk them remaining hidden in settlements. So they drafted them, gave them uniforms, turned them into state property.

And any Flux-user who refused was automatically marked as heretical.

---

## Part II: The First Use of Forbidden Technique

It happened three weeks into the deployment.

A border garrison was being overwhelmed by a coordinated 4th Party assault. Soldiers were dying. The chain of command was collapsing. And a 4th Party lieutenant—coordinated, tactical, dangerous—was moving through the chaos with precision.

Aisen watched the lieutenant kill five soldiers who were, by all measurable standards, better trained than Aisen.

Something in him made a decision.

He used the tracking link—a forbidden technique Aldric had taught him. A resonance pulse sent through the Flux, creating a marker that only Aisen could perceive. It was subtle. It was also absolutely heretical.

Anyone with enough magical sensitivity to observe the technique would know: this young soldier just used a proscribed method.

But he had also just marked the 4th Party lieutenant so perfectly that capturing him alive became possible.

The lieutenant was subdued. Interrogated. The interrogation yielded intelligence that saved the garrison.

Captain Vex—the garrison commander—called Aisen in for questioning.

*"Where did you learn that technique?"* Vex asked.

Aisen had prepared for this moment for five years.

*"I figured it out. The theory was in a book I read before conscription. I adapted it intuitively."*

*"That's a heretical technique."*

*"Yes, sir. It's also useful for identifying enemy Flux-users. I would argue that the strategic benefit outweighs the doctrinal violation."*

Vex studied him. The calculation happening behind the commander's eyes was visible.

*"If you do that again without authorization, you will be arrested and executed as a heretic."*

*"Understood, sir."*

*"But do it again. We need that edge."*

## Part III: The Pattern

Over the next two years, Aisen became a specialist. He used forbidden techniques only when absolutely necessary. Always when they prevented something worse. Always with a plausible explanation.

The other soldiers began to notice. A few of them had Flux sensitivity. They recognized what Aisen was doing.

One soldier—a woman named Lyra who had learned basic binding techniques in a settlement market—approached him one night.

*"You know how to teach hiding, don't you?"* Lyra said.

*"I don't know what you mean."*

*"Yes, you do. You've been doing what we're all trying to do—exist as a Flux-user in a system designed to kill us. But you're doing it precisely. Carefully. You're teaching us through example."*

Aisen considered killing her to silence her. Then he considered the alternative: trusting her.

*"If I am teaching,"* Aisen said carefully, *"the first lesson is never speak about it aloud."*

*"Understood."*

*"The second lesson is: small techniques, used rarely, with plausible military application. The Covenant hunts knowledge that serves no purpose. But knowledge that wins battles? That saves lives? That's harder to prosecute."*

*"And the third lesson?"*

*"Never trust anyone completely. But trust the ones you choose carefully, and trust them completely."*

Lyra nodded. She would die two months later in an ambush—not killed as a heretic, but simply killed as a soldier. But in those two months, she learned enough to pass the knowledge to two other soldiers, who would pass it to others.

The method was spreading. Slowly. Quietly. But spreading.

## Part IV: The Heresy Investigation

Halfway through the second year, the Covenant sent an inquisitor to the garrison.

His name was Kess. He was old, experienced, and carried the kind of certainty that came from years of burning people for knowing too much.

*"We have evidence of heretical practice in this garrison,"* Kess announced at assembly. *"Prohibited techniques. Unauthorized knowledge. The Covenant will identify the guilty parties and execute them as warning."*

Aisen felt his pulse slow. The moment he had known would come was here.

The inquisitor's investigation was methodical. He was looking for obvious heretics—people who had demonstrated prohibited techniques, who had been caught with forbidden texts, who had made the mistake of being too open about their knowledge.

He found a junior soldier named Marcus, who had been showing off binding techniques to impress another recruit.

Marcus was arrested.

Taken to the garrison court.

Tried in front of a Covenant representative.

Convicted.

The execution was scheduled for three days later.

---

## Part V: The Standing Still

Aisen watched the execution from a distance. He did not attend formally—that would be too visible. But he positioned himself where he could observe.

Marcus was sixteen. Young. Frightened. He had learned binding techniques the way most soldiers learned languages: from other soldiers, in moments of relaxation, without understanding the full implications of what he knew.

The Covenant saw someone who had violated information hierarchy.

The Covenant saw a threat.

The execution was quick, but not clean. Marcus was burned by Void-touched fire while soldiers watched. The message was clear: *This is what happens. This is what we will do to you.*

Aisen did not move. Did not react. Did not allow his face to show anything.

Captain Vex, standing next to him, whispered: *"You did that."*

*"Did what, sir?"*

*"You could have prevented this. You know things he wanted to learn. You could have taught him how to hide."*

Aisen was quiet for a long moment.

*"If I had taught him, I would have been found as well. Two executions instead of one."*

*"And that would have bothered you?"*

*"Yes, sir. But not as much as the fact that your garrison needs Flux-users to survive. If we're all executed, you lose your advantage."*

Vex was quiet. Then, almost imperceptibly, he nodded.

*"Then don't get caught. And don't teach too many others. I need you functional, not burning on a pyre."*

The conversation was over. But Aisen understood: the captain knew what he was. The captain had made a calculation. The captain would protect him, as long as Aisen continued to be useful.

This was the arrangement that would define a decade of Aisen's life.

## Part VI: The Deepening Compromise

Year three brought new soldiers, new conflicts, new opportunities to use forbidden techniques.

Aisen became more calculating about it. Not less. He understood now that every time he used a prohibited method, he was walking a line between proving his value and exposing himself as a threat.

He became friends with Renard during this period—a soldier who had no Flux sensitivity but was observant and intelligent.

*"You're going to get caught eventually,"* Renard said one night. *"Everyone like you gets caught eventually."*

*"Not if I'm careful."*

*"No. Even if you're careful. Because the Covenant doesn't forgive by degree. They forgive by usefulness. And eventually, you won't be useful enough to save."*

Aisen had no response to this. Because Renard was right.

*"What will you do then?"* Renard asked.

*"Run. Or die. Whichever comes first."*

*"And what about everyone you've been teaching? Your method? Your way of hiding?"*

*"They'll have to survive without me. That's the arrangement."*

Renard nodded. He understood what Aisen had learned long ago: the only loyalty stronger than loyalty to a person is loyalty to a principle. And Aisen's principle was: teach the method, then let people implement it.

## Part VII: The War Deepens / Encounters with Other Rule-Breakers

Year four: Aisen was nineteen. The 4th Party's coordination was becoming visible. Soldiers reported déjà vu in certain battles. Repeated tactical patterns. As if the same people were dying over and over.

That was when Aisen first understood what Kairos was doing.

He never met Kairos in person during the war. But he heard stories: soldiers who had escaped certain death, who had lived through ambushes that should have killed them, who described time seeming to loop.

Aisen understood, with sudden clarity, that there was someone else—someone with a power he didn't recognize—who was also working to redefine battles. To create loopholes.

For the first time, Aisen felt less alone.

Year five: Aisen encountered Ryo during a rescue operation. The lightning user was being hunted by Covenant forces. Aisen helped him escape.

They didn't speak about it. But afterward, Ryo sought him out.

*"You know what I am,"* Ryo said, his two eyes—one gray, one blue—catching the firelight.

*"Yes. A Flux-user like me."*

*"More broken than most. I'm not sure if it's better or worse."*

*"Worse,"* Aisen said. *"But also more honest about the breaking."*

They shared a look of understanding. Not friendship yet. But recognition. Two people who had been fractured by systems and were learning to function with the fractures visible.

---

## Part VIII: The Moment of Understanding

Five years into his conscription, Aisen understood that the war was not going to end. It was designed not to end. As long as the heresy threat existed, the military could justify conscription, surveillance, control.

The 4th Party was not an enemy to be defeated. It was a framework that justified the system's perpetual operation.

And he—Aisen—was part of the machinery that kept it running.

He could walk away. He could refuse orders. He could declare himself a heretic and accept execution. But that would only remove one useful cog. The system would replace him.

So instead, he began his slow work: teaching people how to set small goals, how to hide what they knew, how to survive the machinery.

He began the process of spreading the method, understanding that true resistance was not dramatic but incremental.

And he understood that Aldric had died so that Aisen could teach this. So that the next generation could teach it. So that eventually, the system's machinery would grind to a halt not through force, but through the realization that its methods no longer worked.

It would take decades. It would require many deaths. But the alternative—direct confrontation—never worked.

So Aisen chose the long game. The patient work. The method of redefining problems.

He would carry this understanding until Thornwood Hollow. Then others would carry it forward.
