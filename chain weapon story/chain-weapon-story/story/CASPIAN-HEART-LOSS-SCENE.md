# CASPIAN: THE HEART'S EROSION

## Part I: The Discovery

Aisen found him bleeding in the library basement at midnight.

The Academy's lower levels were forbidden to most students. But Aisen had learned which stones around the foundation did not align properly, which gaps led into spaces the blueprints had forgotten. This was where he hid things he could not afford to have found.

This was where, apparently, someone else was also hiding.

The figure was human-shaped. Mostly. There was something wrong about the angle of the shadow he cast. The breathing was too shallow, as if the chest beneath was not quite sure how human lungs worked.

"You're injured," Aisen said.

The figure did not respond at first. Then: "I'm not injured. I'm feeding."

Aisen's chain was already in his hand. But he did not move.

"I can taste your blood from here," the voice continued. It was young, maybe Aisen's age, maybe younger. The tone was hollow. "I can tell you killed a rabbit two hours ago. I can smell the fear in your sweat. And I know—I know with absolute certainty—that if I move toward you, you will kill me without hesitation, and I will deserve it."

The figure looked up. Aisen's eyes adjusted. He saw a human face, pale with hunger, eyes that were not quite the right color. A vampire. An actual vampire, not theoretical, not academic.

"Why haven't you killed me?" the figure asked.

"Why haven't you killed me?" Aisen countered.

"Because I'm starving, and I don't trust myself. And because you came down here to hide, same as me. And because maybe—maybe there's someone else in this world who understands what it means to be a hunted thing that you didn't choose to be."

Aisen lowered the chain slightly.

"What's your name?"

"Caspian. I... nobody calls me that. I don't tell people. But you seem like someone who understands that names are dangerous."

"I'm Aisen."

Caspian laughed—a bitter, brittle sound. "Of course you are. Of course there's a name attached to the person who sees me. That makes you real, which means this moment is real, which means I can't erase it from my memory tomorrow."

## Part II: The Recognition

Over the following weeks, Aisen brought food to the basement—rabbits, birds, deliberately wounded game that satisfied whatever hunger drove Caspian without requiring him to hunt humans.

They did not speak much at first. Caspian was suspicious. Aisen was cautious. But gradually, something like understanding formed between them.

"Why do you bring me food?" Caspian asked one night.

"Because I was brought food once. By someone I didn't deserve to know. It changed something in me."

"What changed?"

"The knowledge that I didn't have to earn recognition to receive it. That someone could see me and choose to help anyway."

Caspian considered this. His hunger was less sharp when fed. His mind was clearer.

"I was born normal," Caspian said. "Here's the thing nobody tells you about becoming a vampire. It's not a single moment. You don't go to sleep human and wake up monster. You become a tool, slowly, through so many small compromises that by the time you realize what's happening, you can't remember which version of yourself was real."

Aisen listened. His mind was working in the way Harald had taught him—asking questions beneath the words.

*What is the rule? Caspian was offered something (eternal life, power, freedom from mortality). He accepted. That acceptance came with a cost, but he didn't know the cost until after he'd paid part of it.*

*What breaks the rule? If Caspian could remember being human—truly remember it, not as nostalgia but as present-tense reality—maybe he could choose something other than being a tool.*

"Tell me," Aisen said.

And Caspian told him about Seraphine, the vampire noble who found him and made him an offer: eternal life, freedom from mortality, power beyond measure. All he had to do was obey. All he had to do was stop asking questions.

"I said yes," Caspian whispered. "I said yes because I was sixteen and mortal and terrified, and she promised me I would never be vulnerable again. And she was right. I'm not vulnerable. I'm also not me anymore."

Aisen listened without judgment. This was what mattered, Caspian realized. Not having a solution, but having someone who didn't require him to have one.

"I'm going to ask you something," Aisen said. "And I want you to understand that I'm asking because I see you. Not the hunger. Not the vampire part. You."

"Okay."

"Does it hurt? The hunger?"

Caspian stared at him. No one had ever asked this. They asked if he was dangerous. They asked if he could be trusted. They asked what he was.

Nobody asked if it *hurt*.

"Every day," Caspian whispered. "It hurts every single day. It's like having a cavity in your soul. Like something essential is missing, and you can feel the absence the way you feel pain from a broken bone."

"Then that's what matters," Aisen said. "That you feel it. That you remember what was missing. The moment you stop feeling that is the moment you become what they wanted you to be."

## Part III: The Lightning Escape – Loss of the Human Heart

The moment changed when Ryo arrived at the Academy.

Aisen had warned Caspian about him: *"There's someone here who's broken like we're broken. Don't be afraid of him."*

Caspian had seen him on campus. The one with mismatched eyes. The one who moved like someone experiencing too many realities at once.

Then came the night when the 4th Party's agents tried to capture them both.

It started simply: a trap in the woods behind campus, coordinated hunters, Flux-weaving to bind their powers. But the trap was designed to separate them. Aisen was pulled one direction. Caspian was driven another.

That was when Ryo found him.

"Run," Ryo said, electricity crackling around him. "I'll hold them off."

"I don't know you," Caspian gasped.

"No. But Aisen does. And he told me you matter. So you're running."

Ryo transformed—the four constructs, the power that made reality bend. Lightning filled the forest. The 4th Party agents were thrown back.

For a moment, just a moment, Caspian saw the cost of that power. Ryo was bleeding from the nose. His body was convulsing with the strain of being split four ways.

And still he pushed, still he threw his power against impossible odds.

"Go!" Ryo screamed.

Caspian ran. But as he ran, he heard Ryo scream again—a scream of pain, of his pieces not quite reassembling, of the agony of being fractured and fractured and fractured again.

Caspian could have kept running. Should have. But he turned back.

What happened next, he would never fully understand. His hunger, his connection to his vampire nature, his desperation to help someone who had sacrificed for him—all of it came together in a moment of absolute instinct.

He fed on one of the 4th Party agents.

Not to the death—just enough to weaken them. Just enough to get them out of Ryo's way.

And in that moment, in that exchange of blood and sustenance and power, something broke in him that could never be repaired.

The vampire hunger was sated. But the human part of him—the part that remembered kindness, the part that Aisen had been slowly reawakening—it left him. It withdrew like a tide going out.

He could feel it leaving. He could feel the moment his human heart stopped beating and a second, vampiric heart took its place. Not metaphorically. Physically. He felt the change in his body, the reorganization of his cellular structure.

He became colder. The hunger became easier to ignore. The moral calculus became simpler: survival required sustenance, and sustenance had a cost, and that cost was no longer his problem.

By the time Aisen found him after the escape, Caspian could already feel the change completing itself.

"I had to feed," Caspian said flatly. "To survive. To help Ryo. I had to feed on them."

Aisen searched his face.

"And what did you lose?" Aisen asked.

Caspian tried to cry and discovered he couldn't. The reflex was there, but the emotional substance was gone.

"Everything that wasn't vampire," Caspian answered. "He saved us. And the cost of his saving us was my humanity. I can see the logic of it now with absolute clarity. And I don't care. That's what terrifies me. I understand the moral weight of what I've done, and I don't feel bad about it."

Aisen held him. For the first time, Caspian did not find comfort in the embrace.

He simply understood it as: human consolation for losses I can no longer feel.

## Part IV: The Learning – Aisen's Death

Years later, after the war, after Aisen's final stand, Caspian received the news in a place made of ice.

He had become the Ice Lord by then—a natural progression. His vampire powers had evolved into something that bent cold and time and crystallization. He had built himself a throne in a place that existed at the border of the Void Zones, where normal time barely functioned.

A messenger—a simple human who had climbed the glacier to reach him—delivered the message:

"Aisen died at Thornwood Hollow. He saved three hundred people. He was alone. He died fighting."

Caspian heard the words. He understood them intellectually. He could process the meaning, the implications, the historical significance.

He did not feel grief.

This was the true horror of what he had become. Not the hunger, not the power, but the inability to feel the proper weight of loss.

He tried to remember what grief felt like. He could recall the sensation theoretically—the impact on the chest, the obstruction in the throat, the darkness behind the eyes. It was like trying to remember the flavor of food after losing the sense of taste.

He remembered Aisen's voice in the basement library: "Does it hurt? The hunger?"

It had hurt then. He had felt it then. Aisen had seen him and asked about the pain because Aisen understood that pain was proof of humanity.

And Caspian had traded that pain for survival.

He wept then—not from grief, but from the understanding that he no longer had the capacity to grieve properly. Aisen deserved better. Aisen deserved someone who could cry for him, really cry, with the weight of a human heart behind it.

What Aisen got was Caspian, an hollow thing made of ice and vampire hunger, trying to perform grief like it was a dance he had memorized but no longer understood the purpose of.

But sitting in his ice throne, Caspian worked through it the way Aisen had taught him to work through things. Questions beneath questions.

*What is the rule? A human heart can feel. A vampire heart cannot. If you have a vampire heart, the human part of you is not just gone—it's a choice you made every day not to feed it, and eventually it dies of neglect.*

*What breaks the rule? The moment you stop choosing to feed the human part, it leaves. But before it leaves, there is a moment—just a moment—where you can call it back. That moment is grief, is pain, is the willingness to feel the weight of loss.*

*What is the cost? To keep the human heart alive, you have to accept that it will hurt every day. Aisen understood this. That's why he asked.*

*What do I tell? I tell myself that Aisen saw me. I tell myself that in that moment in the basement library, when he asked if the hunger hurt, he was asking me to choose. Choose the pain. Choose the human part. Choose to stay capable of grief. And I chose survival instead. That's what the Ice Lord is—the price of survival paid in full.*

Caspian understood, now, why Aisen had looked at him with such precision in that library. Not pity. Not judgment. Just the clarity of someone who had already done the calculation and decided that the cost of staying human was worth it, no matter how much it would eventually cost him to care about people he would outlive or lose or fail to save.

Caspian sat on his throne in the ice palace and wept without tears—the final cruel trick of becoming a vampire: you can remember the shape of emotions without ever feeling them again.

And he understood, finally, what price he had actually paid.

It wasn't his humanity. It was the ability to properly honor having had it.
