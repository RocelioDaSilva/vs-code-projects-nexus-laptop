The monastery smelled of beeswax and stone dust and something older than both—the accumulated patience of men who had poured their silence into walls for centuries until the silence became structural, load-bearing, as real as mortar.

Marten stood at the threshold and could not enter.

Not physically—the door was open, the brother at the gate had nodded him through without questions—but in the way a man cannot enter a clean room when the filth on him is not the kind that washes off. He stood with his pack on his shoulder and his boots leaving prints that were not quite mud, and he thought: *I am going to lie to these people, and then I am going to tell them the truth, and I do not know which will be worse.*

Brother Cassiel found him there forty minutes later, standing in the same spot, his boots having dried to the flagstones.

"You can come in," Cassiel said. He was a broad man with a face like a cliff that had been weathered into forgiveness—every line a place where severity had eroded into something gentler, and not entirely by choice.

"I need—" Marten started, and stopped. He had practiced this sentence for three days on the road, walking with the cadence of it, matching his footfalls to the rhythm: *I need sanctuary. I need absolution. I need someone to hear what I have done.* But the words would not come in order, and what emerged instead was: "Water. Can I have water?"

Cassiel brought him water in a clay cup. Marten drank it and then held the cup the way Lysine had held the broth bowl—as a small solid object in a world that had become liquid.

They sat in the cloister garden. Late afternoon; the shadow of the bell tower made a dark stroke across the herbs. Cassiel waited with the practiced patience of a man who has sat with broken people before and knows that silence is the only invitation they trust.

Marten set down the cup.

"I deserted," he said. "Twenty-three days ago. Third Frontier Regiment, Valdimere garrison at Thornstone Pass."

Cassiel's expression did not change. Marten had expected it to—had expected the flinch, the moral withdrawal, the tightening of the jaw that says *you are a different category of person now.* But Cassiel only nodded, once, slowly, the way a doctor nods when you describe the location of a pain.

"How far have you come?"

"Seventy leagues. Overland. I burned my insignia at the river crossing and changed my name twice." He paused. "My name is Marten. I'm done changing it."

"Are you injured?"

Marten looked down at his hands. The nail on his left index finger had turned black and would probably fall off. His right shoulder sat wrong—wrenched during a scramble down a scree slope in darkness—and there was a wound on his thigh he had stitched himself with fishing line that had become hot and angry in the way that suggested the infection was winning.

"Not from combat," he said. "I left before—" He stopped again. The sentence had a cliff edge in it that he could feel coming.

Cassiel waited.

"I was ordered to burn a village," Marten said. "Westhall. Seventy-three households, a market square, a school. The commander said the population was harboring Covenant sympathizers. The intelligence report said six individuals. The order was to burn the village to deny them shelter."

"Six people," Cassiel said.

"Six people. Seventy-three houses. I asked the lieutenant whether we would evacuate first, and he looked at me like I had asked whether the rain was wet."

The bell tower's shadow had moved. Somewhere in the monastery, a choir began practicing vespers—distant voices rising through stone, each note held with the precision of men who believe that God hears errors.

"We did it," Marten said. "I need you to understand that. I did not refuse. I did not object. I carried the torch the same as everyone else. The lieutenant paired us—one man to ignite, one to ensure no one left the house. I was the igniter. I set fire to—" He counted in his head, the way you count stairs in a building you can never enter again. "Seven houses. Of the ones I set, three were empty. Two had families who had already fled. Two—"

He stopped.

"Two were occupied," Cassiel said. Not a question.

"The second one had a woman and three children. She came to the window. She was holding a baby on one hip and had two others behind her and she looked at me—" His voice broke on *looked at me*, and what came out of the break was not weeping but something smaller and worse: a sound like a man swallowing a stone. "She looked at me the way you look at weather. Not with anger. Not with fear. She looked at me the way you look at something that cannot be bargained with. And I understood that she was right. I was weather. I was what had come for them, and there was nothing to say to it, and she turned from the window and went to gather her children, and my partner—"

"You don't have to—"

"My partner ensured no one left the house. That was the order. I set the fire. He held the door. The woman found the back window and got the children out, and they ran across the yard, and the lieutenant on the rooftop signaled that—" Marten was very still. "That it was acceptable. That the houses were the objective, not the people. But I know. I *know* what would have happened if she hadn't found the window. My partner's instructions did not include exceptions."

The vespers choir modulated into a minor key. The notes hung in the garden like pollen.

"After Westhall, the regiment received commendation. The lieutenant was promoted. I was given a weekend leave pass." Marten's voice had gone flat—the terrible flatness of someone who has told this story to himself so many times that the edges are worn smooth, the way coins wear smooth, and beneath the smoothness the metal is the same. "I used the leave pass to walk east. I did not stop."

"Twenty-three days," Cassiel said.

"Twenty-three days. I expected pursuit. There was none. I think—" He almost smiled, and it was the worst expression Marten's face had produced yet. "I think I was not important enough to chase."

Cassiel was quiet for a long time. The bell tower's shadow reached the herb bed, and the herbs released their scent into the cooling air, and the choir finished vespers, and the silence that followed was not empty but structural—the monastery's silence, the kind that bears weight.

"You're telling me this because you want absolution," Cassiel said.

"No." Marten looked at him. "I'm telling you because I want someone to know. Absolution is for people who stopped. I didn't stop. I set the fire. I set seven fires. The regiment set eighty-four. Westhall doesn't exist anymore—you can walk through it and there's nothing. Foundations and char. And the regiment moved on to the next assignment, and the intelligence report that justified Westhall turned out to be—" Another pause, this one heavier. "Partially accurate. Two of the six named individuals were in the village. Two. The other four had left a month before."

"Did you know that at the time?"

"No. I learned afterward. But I don't think it matters. If all six had been there—if the village had been full of Covenant agents—seventy-three houses for six people is not a tactic. It's an announcement. It says: *This is what we do. This is the cost of proximity.*"

Cassiel stood. He walked to the herb bed and picked a sprig of something—rosemary, by the scent—and rubbed it between his fingers. The gesture was domestic, almost absurd, and Marten understood it for what it was: a man touching something alive after hearing about death.

"Brother Cassiel," Marten said. "Harboring a deserter is a crime. If the garrison finds me here—"

"Then they find you here." Cassiel broke the rosemary sprig in half and dropped both halves into the herb bed. "This monastery has been here for four hundred years. It has harbored worse than deserters and survived worse than garrisons." He turned back. "Your wound needs cleaning. The infection will spread if you wait. Come inside."

Marten stood. His legs were stiff from sitting on stone, and his thigh burned where the fishing-line stitches pulled, and the black nail on his finger throbbed in the cooling air.

"I set fire to seven houses," he said.

"I heard you."

"Two of them were occupied."

"I heard you."

"The woman got her children out through the back window."

"Yes."

"If she hadn't—"

"Come inside, Marten."

He followed the brother through the cloister door and into the lamplight, and the monastery received him the way it received all things—without judgment, without pardon, with the bottomless structural patience of walls that had learned, over centuries, that holding things together is not the same as making them good.
