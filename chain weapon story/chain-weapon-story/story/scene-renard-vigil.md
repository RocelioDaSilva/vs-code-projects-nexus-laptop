# Renard's Vigil

The humans thought they were alone. They were wrong.

Renard had been watching them for three days. Their patrols, their meals, their arguments. He knew their faces. He knew which one snored. He knew which one sharpened his sword too often—the nervous one, the one who would break first.

None of them knew his name. None of them knew he was here.

He was old for a fox-kin. His muzzle was gray. His ears were torn—one from a wolf, one from a human. The human scar ached when it rained. It was not raining. It would rain tomorrow. He could smell it.

Behind him, the village slept. His people. His daughter's children. His mate's grave.

*She would have known what to do.* She had always known. She had been the one who believed in peace, in trust, in the lie that humans could change. She had died believing it. A hunter's arrow. A mistake, they said. An accident.

Renard did not believe in accidents.

He watched the campfires. He thought of the young human with the chain. Aisen. He had arrived two days ago, asking nothing, offering everything. He had looked at Renard—not with fear, not with pity, but with something Renard had not seen in years.

*Respect.*

"You are not like the others," Renard had said.

"I know," Aisen had replied. "That's why they want me dead."

Renard had almost smiled.

Now he touched the small wooden fox hidden in his pouch. Aisen had carved it. Given it to him without explanation. A gift. No debt, no contract, no expectation.

*He will die tomorrow.*

Renard knew this. He had seen the army. He had counted the soldiers. Aisen had counted them too, and he had not run. He had stayed.

*Why?*

The question was old. Renard had asked it a thousand times. Why did humans hunt them? Why did they fear what they did not understand? Why did they kill a healer who only wanted to help?

He had never found an answer.

But he thought, watching the campfires, that maybe the answer did not matter. Maybe what mattered was this: one human had stayed. One human had carved a fox. One human would die so that Renard's people could live.

*That is enough.*

He tucked the wooden fox back into his pouch. He stood. He walked back to the village. He did not look back at the campfires.

Tomorrow, he would fight. Tomorrow, he would lose a friend. But tonight, he would sleep.

His mate had believed in peace. He had not. But she had believed in him. That was enough.
