Scene: The Tribunal — When Knowledge Becomes Crime

Setting: A vaulted hall in Aldric’s Crossing, banners fluttering with patron sigils. The Academy holds a public tribunal to demonstrate the enforcement of information law.

Summary/Draft:

The hall smelled of wax and old paper. Rows of benches held students and townsfolk; magistrates sat high behind a carved dais, faces unreadable. In the center, an overturned table served as the accused’s stand — a place meant to humble and expose.

Aisen sits near the back, fingers curled around the edge of the bench. He remembers Aldric’s eyes from the platform, and his mouth is a dry seam.

The accused is a soft‑spoken scholar named Miren — a sympathetic figure who taught rune translation to the poor. The prosecution opens with a lecture about the sanctity of ordered knowledge: how stray techniques become contagions; how one misapplied pattern can topple a city. They read dry transcripts, play recorded demonstrations of improper techniques, and speak of precedence.

Miren’s defense is halting. She speaks of compassion and the moral obligation of knowledge. She tells a story of saving a child with an improvised binding—no ceremony, no sanction—just necessary action. The crowd murmurs.

The tribunal is not only about guilt; it is theatre. The magistrate asks leading questions designed to needle Miren into demonstrating a technique; the cameras (apprentice scribes with lens‑devices) are allowed only sanctioned views. When Miren refuses to reveal a chord she used to stabilize a patient, a scuffle breaks out at the edges of the gallery. Someone throws an accusation that she endangered a noble heir.

Aisen watches the ritualized mechanics of power: how the law speaks with a calm voice while violence is kept at hand. He realizes the tribunal is a trap: knowledge cannot survive being cleanly narrated; the act of explanation is itself dangerous. Miren is sentenced to a public discipline—severance of one small channel, public renunciation, and years of supervised labor.

Afterward, in the alley, a group of students trade whispers about loopholes. The tribunal achieved its purpose. The law did not need to kill everyone—only to teach the rule. People leave with a clearer idea of what knowledge will cost them.

Use: This scene demonstrates procedure, public spectacle, and stakes around teaching and information law. It also cements the Academy as a social machine where sympathy and legality collide.
