Summary:

Master Tholen's fingers remembered paper the way a clock remembers its gears. He could close his eyes and hear the cataloging tables shift: a reed-thin folio sliding under a brass clasp, a student's hurried footstep in the corridor, the tiny signature of the Covenant's seal pressed on a lending slip. The library was a map of small absences—blank spaces where a title should be—and Tholen traced those absences like a scholar tracing veins in a leaf.

It began with titles that did not go missing overnight so much as drifted away. "On Coastal Anchors and River Memory," a slim, salt-stained treatise on hydromantic artifacts, would be requested by a visiting lecturer and not returned. "Salt and the Shape of Time," one of the rare excavation journals, would leave with a professor and return with a note that the volume had been 'consumed' into departmental archives. Tholen noticed a pattern: books that described rituals, artifacts, and local histories—things that reconciled people's stories to the land—were vanishing more often than treatises on grammar or arithmetic.

He had a list, of course. It lived on the underside of his desk, a scrap of patched parchment that no visitor thought to look at. Each entry was precise: title, folio number, last known borrower, the small sign the borrower made on the ledger. He wrote the names with a steady hand—Professor Darik, the head of Antiquities; Mistress Rinn, who taught ceremonial practice; Master Halvor, whose lectures on taxes somehow also touched local lore. The list was not a flame of accusation; it was a cartography of concern.

Master Tholen treated the teachers gently. He was not a man of tantrum or confrontation. Instead he used the library's own mechanisms—duplicated catalogue entries, carefully timed shelving lists, marginal notes written in invisible ink that showed only under heat. When a volume did not return, he slid a duplicate to its original place, a clean copy bound for the stacks but bearing, in the margin, a tiny cipher that signaled to him the book's absence. The cipher was as harmless as an accent mark: two diagonal ticks near the head of the page—nothing that would catch a casual eye, but in Tholen's mind it was a way to keep track without making enemies.

He also learned to watch the lending slips. Professors were careless in ways students were not; they would scrawl initials or a date and tuck the slip into an inner pocket. Tholen would copy that scrawl onto a separate note—no one noticed when he commented on a heavy ledger, correcting a smudge or smoothing a corner. His memory for handwriting made the task easier. He could close his eyes and hear, in the scratch of ink, the same hesitation a student had when concealing a borrowed text.

Aisen interested him more than the absences did. The boy had a way of asking for obscure pamphlets as if they were ordinary things: a footnote to be followed, a hinge to be found. Aisen checked his books out and returned them in good order, seldom dog-eared, his fingers clean. Tholen noted that attention and shelved Aisen's requests under 'Careful Readers.' When Aisen's name appeared in the margins, Tholen would tuck a small paper—annotations he had saved over the years—beneath the binding: a line noting where a phrase might be read differently, a cross-reference to a long-forgotten excavation. He did this not to lead Aisen astray, but to protect the knowledge's shape from slow erasure.

The library's quiet methods extended into small, deliberate theatrics. When a professor came asking for "The Rituals of the Seaside Peoples," Tholen might produce a folio bound in plain cloth and, while the professor was distracted by an argument in the corridor, he would slip the original into a locked drawer. He did not keep the books for himself; instead he made witness copies—pages traced in fine ink, trimmed to fit the bindings of harmless volumes that could circulate while the originals waited in a safe place. He called these witness copies 'ledger echoes,' and they allowed stories to survive in a form that seemed to belong precisely where it was found.

One night—a thin moon, the stacks smelling of tallow—Tholen found a lending slip for "Salt and the Shape of Time" tucked inside a book of mathematical tables. The hand belonged to Professor Darik. The date was a week old. Tholen made his note, circled the folio number, and set a small mark in the margin that read, in his shorthand, 'watch.' He did not accuse; the act of watching, in his experience, could make even the most determined hand hesitate.

Tholen kept his ledger neat. He also kept a second book, smaller and private, where he copied the most telling lines from the missing texts—sentences that tied names to places, names to rituals, names to ownership. He labeled each quote with a place and a face: "Folio 11b—Anchors: mentions river-tradition near Tarovin—possibly suppressed by Halvor's notes." The ledger was a map of what had been and what might yet be reclaimed.

There was danger in such care. The Covenant, after all, had good reasons—at least official ones—to control certain knowledge. Some texts were misapplied in panics or twisted into rituals that cost lives. But Tholen believed that erasure was a different kind of harm. When history was folded away, people lost the small truths that made them accountable to one another. It was not only about facts; it was about memory—who got to say what had happened.

Before Aisen left for the Academy, Tholen placed a small, folded note between the pages of a book Aisen had borrowed. It was not a command nor a sermon. It was a pointer: look at folio 42b. Aisen would find, if he followed the hint, a passage on water rites and the tang of how communities anchored themselves to rivers. Tholen hoped the boy would carry it like a seed.
