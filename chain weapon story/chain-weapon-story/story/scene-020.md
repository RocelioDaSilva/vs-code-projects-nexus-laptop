The ledger did not lie, but it could be made to whisper. Yasmin had learned this from her mother, who kept the accounts for a salt merchant in the harbor quarter and who said that a clean ledger was like a clean knife—the dishonesty was not in the surface but in what it had cut.

She sat in the supply tent at the edge of the border garrison, her lamp low and her fingers moving down columns of figures that should have added up and didn't. The tent smelled of canvas and dried grain and the particular staleness of inventory that had been counted too many times. Outside, the garrison's night watch called to each other in the clipped, bored tones of men who expected nothing and feared everything.

The discrepancy was small: fourteen crates of medical salve listed as "dispatched to Forward Camp Seven" three weeks ago. Forward Camp Seven's quartermaster had signed the receipt. The crates should have been there. But Yasmin had visited Forward Camp Seven two days ago on a routine audit, and the quartermaster—a man named Urik with a scar across his palm and the careful politeness of someone who had learned to lie to inspectors—had shown her a supply room with eleven crates, not fourteen. When she asked about the three missing, he blinked and said there must be a counting error.

She had smiled and thanked him and left, because counting errors existed and supply masters who accused quartermasters of stealing medical salve in a border garrison tended to discover that their own records were suddenly incomplete.

Now, in the tent, she opened the secondary ledger—her private one, written in a shorthand she had invented at thirteen, a system of dots and half-letters that looked like idle doodling to anyone who did not know the key. She turned to the page where she had begun tracking irregularities six weeks ago.

Three crates of salve, missing. Two barrels of preserved grain, listed as spoiled and disposed of but never recorded in the waste log. A delivery of iron-tipped bolts diverted from the armory to a civilian transport at the river crossing—signed off by Corporal Dane, a man she had never seen handle anything heavier than a pen.

The pattern was not random. Random shortages scattered across categories. This was specific: medical supplies, preserved food, weapons. Things you would stockpile if you were building a cache somewhere outside the garrison's ledger.

Yasmin flattened her palm against the page and breathed. The air tasted of dust and the worry she kept folded at the back of her mind like a note she was not ready to read. She was seventeen. She had been given this post because she was good with numbers and quiet with opinions, and because the garrison's previous supply master had died of what was called a fever but which smelled, to those who noticed, like something administered in a cup of tea.

The thought made her close the ledger and hold it against her chest for a moment, the way a person holds a shield. She was not a soldier. She was not a spy. She was a girl who could count and who had been taught that counting was not neutral—that the person who controlled the numbers controlled the story.

She stood and crossed to the entry flap, listening. The camp's breathing was regular: the creak of tents in wind, a mule's complaint, the distant hiss of a cookfire dying. No footsteps near her tent. She returned to the desk and pulled out a second document: the transport manifest from the river crossing, stamped with Corporal Dane's seal.

The manifest listed the diverted bolts as "excess inventory, reassigned per Regional Order 14-C." She knew Regional Order 14-C. It authorized the transfer of surplus materials between garrisons during peacetime. This was not peacetime—the border was under active patrol—and the bolts were not surplus. They had been requisitioned two weeks prior by the armorer, who was still waiting for them.

Yasmin traced the seal with her fingernail. The wax was proper; the stamp was genuine. But the underlying order—where was it? She had requested a copy from the regional office and been told it would "arrive in due course." That phrase, she had learned, was the bureaucratic equivalent of a closed door.

She made two decisions. The first was small: she would visit the river crossing herself, on the pretext of verifying the transport register, and ask the merchant handlers what they had seen loaded onto the civilian boat. If the bolts had gone downriver, someone at the receiving end would know.

The second was larger and made her hands shake: she would copy the relevant pages from both ledgers—the official and her private one—and seal them in the waterproof pouch she kept in her boot. If anything happened to her, the numbers would survive. She had learned from her mother that the most dangerous thing in a corrupt system was not the lie but the evidence. Lies could be argued away. Numbers, properly documented, were silent witnesses that did not flinch under questioning.

She began to copy, her pen moving with the careful, unhurried strokes of someone who understood that speed was the enemy of accuracy. Each figure transferred exactly: quantities, dates, signatures, discrepancies. She noted the gap between Urik's signed receipt and her physical count. She noted the timing of the grain disposal—three days before the regional inspector's visit, when spoilage records would not be questioned.

Midway through the copy, a sound stopped her pen: footsteps, deliberate and steady, approaching the tent from the east side—the side that faced the officers' quarters, not the common path. She closed both ledgers, slid the private one under a loose plank in the desk's lower shelf, and placed the official one open to a page of routine inventories. By the time the tent flap moved, she was writing a routine note about grain rotation, her face composed and her pulse hard in her throat.

Corporal Dane entered. He was a narrow man with the sort of face that suggested careful decisions made slowly—a thin mouth, watchful eyes, and hands that always seemed to be holding something invisible. He smiled at her, and the smile was the kind that asked questions without speaking them.

"Working late, Supply Master?"

"Grain audit," she said, her voice steady. "The mildew count from Third Storage needs updating before the morning report."

He nodded, as if mildew were exactly the sort of thing that kept a supply master awake. His eyes moved across the desk—not quickly, but with the methodical interest of someone who counted things the way she did. She forced herself not to glance at the lower shelf.

"I wanted to ask about tomorrow's delivery," he said. "The medical shipment from Saren—has the manifest been filed?"

"Filed and logged. Three crates of field bandages, two of antiseptic salve, one of surgical instruments. Due at the river crossing by midmorning."

"Good." He paused, and in the pause she heard the sound of a man deciding whether to say more. "Make sure the log reflects what arrives, not what was expected. There have been… adjustments… to some recent shipments. Regional logistics, nothing unusual."

The word "adjustments" sat in the air between them like a blade laid on a table. It was not a threat. It was an instruction wrapped in courtesy—a reminder that ledgers could be shaped to accommodate reality, and that reality was what Corporal Dane said it was.

"Of course," Yasmin said, and her voice did not waver.

He left, and the tent returned to silence. Yasmin sat still for thirty seconds, counting her breaths the way her mother had taught her to count coins: steadily, without emotion, each one a fact. Then she pulled out the private ledger and added a new entry: *Dane visited. Mentioned "adjustments." Appeared to check desk contents visually. Timestamp: third bell after midnight.*

She finished the copies and sealed them in her boot pouch, pressing the waterproof flap until the wax held. Then she extinguished the lamp and lay on her cot in the dark, listening to the garrison breathe.

In the morning, she would visit the river crossing. She would ask the merchant Ilko—a man who owed her mother a favor from the salt years—to describe what had been loaded. She would compare his account to the manifest. And she would add the results to her private ledger, line by line, fact by fact, building a record that no one had asked for and that no one, she hoped, would think to look for in a supply master's boot.

Yasmin closed her eyes and counted the discrepancies from memory: three crates, two barrels, one bolt shipment, one dead supply master, and one corporal who smiled when he should have been sleeping. The numbers did not balance. That was, she understood, the point. Numbers that balanced were invisible. Numbers that didn't were evidence.

She fell asleep holding the ledger's shape in her mind like a map, and dreamed of columns that added up to a question she was not yet ready to answer aloud.
