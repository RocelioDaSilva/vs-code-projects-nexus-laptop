Rin had a habit of cataloguing small things that made the world legible: notches in a training post, the way a merchant folded a cloth, the time a child's laugh split like a bell. Today the market smelled of hot sugar and corded rope, and Rin moved through it like someone reading margins in a book.

He first saw the boy by the tavern—small, with a secret bulge in his pocket and a face like one used to making small bargains with the world. Father stood with the steady posture of someone who had worked many hours at the same counter. Rin's eyes caught the way the man watched the street and then the child, a soft beam like a tool.

Rin paused to watch. He had only recently come from the academy and was learning the vocabulary of people in their towns: where they kept kindness, where they kept their cold. Aisen looked like someone who had two parts in his life and kept the smaller part hidden so as not to lose it.

Rin stepped near as if the air between would reveal a page. The boy looked up with the quick, open face of a small animal startled into curiosity. "What's that you have?" Rin asked quietly, lowering his voice like a glove.

Aisen clutched the scrap of blue cloth and blinked. "Nothing."

Rin smiled the way a person uses as a key; it is the kind of smile that asks for a small exchange and offers nothing that will break. "You can tell me. I collect odd things. I trade for them sometimes."

Aisen considered the offer with the gravity of someone weighing a coin he did not know belonged to him yet. He fidgeted and then, in a small motion, showed the corner of the cloth. "It was on the windowsill. I like things that smell like other places."

Rin crouched a little to be nearer his eye level. "That's a good sort of thing. It means you notice. Notice is better than strength sometimes. Keep it safe."

Father watched the small exchange with the wary patience of someone who knows the calculation of strangers. "He keeps odd tokens," the man said, with a soft gruffness that contained gratitude. "They keep him out of my hair and out of trouble."

Rin nodded. "I am Rin. I just came to the academy. I write things down—little maps of people. If you ever want to trade notes, find me by the practice pole."

Aisen's eyes lit at the idea of maps. It was a thing he understood. He pressed the cloth into his fist, then eased it into Rin's small, offered hand for a second—an exchange as if to test whether the boy was real. Rin used no sharp tricks. He returned the cloth with a small, folded coin for Aisen to keep, a courtesy that said he was not a thief and did not want to be cruel.

The coin fit in Aisen's palm like a promise; the world grew a fraction warmer. Father began to speak, but Rin bowed his head politely and moved away with the kind of quiet that leaves the street untroubled. He turned back once to watch Aisen tuck the coin away and felt a slight, private spark of connection. Aisen would be a small thread he might one day pull; for now, the thread was only a curiosity.

As Rin made his way to the practice pole later, he wrote a single item in his notebook: "Tavern boy—Aisen—keeps tokens. Possible friend? Observe." It was an anchor. He did not know then that anchors could grow to become ropes, but he kept his pen ready.
