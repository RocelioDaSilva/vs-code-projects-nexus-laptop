He counted names the way some men counted breaths.

The table was a slab of rough pine, scarred where knives had been set down and cups had been knocked careless. A single guttering candle made the room small and movable, a world that could be held between one finger and one thumb. Aisen's hand hovered over the page and then moved, ink scratching like a small animal protesting. The quill trembled; his fingers trembled more.

He wrote: Tomas Hennet. Bel, the cooper. Marek Hollow. The ink bled into the grain of the paper as if it wanted to be swallowed whole. He did not speak the names aloud. Saying them into the air would be an offering, and he had none to give.

Outside, the rain on the eaves sounded like a closing door. The house smelled of wet wool and iron and the slow, steady stink of boiled bones; someone had left a kettle too long on the coals. He had been up through the night, through the hours when the dark presses so close that memory becomes a separate creature and begins to bite. He had watched smoke lift off the hill and had felt the shape of it settle in his chest like a stone.

He did not know how long he could keep writing. Time had become an arrangement of small tasks: count the dead, mend the torn, help slide mattresses into a corridor to make room, press cool cloths to fevered foreheads. Each recorded name was another small authority given to a hand that would not forget.

His hand wanted to stop. It wanted to rest on the table and feel the wood catch the padded weight of his palm. Instead he kept scratching, convinced that if he paused he would forget one whole face and that would be a different kind of death—one his guilt could not bear.

He thought of Frostfang as if it were a distant country, not a place on the map but a thing that had happened to weather. The sky had been the color of hammered tin; the wind had been full of glass. He thought of the boy who'd fallen in the ditch and how Aisen had tried to lift him and had been too slow by the measure of a heartbeat. A heartbeat was an ungodly thing to misplace.

He reached for the small, folded letter at the edge of the table. It was one of his father's—cheap card stock, the ink pressed in a neat, firm hand that somehow made the world less arbitrary. He unfolded it and flattened the creases, not to read but to feel. His father had written once about veering to save a stray dog and losing his boot in the mud. The letter had been sent months before the frost that had taken so much; it was nothing like the brutal geometry of the field. Its ordinariness cut him now.

Aisen pressed the pad of his thumb against the old ink until he made a faint smudge. The motion was small. It was the only kind of human thing he could still tolerate in the room: the smell of paper, the texture of linen, the imperfect loop of his father's g in 'good health.' He let his shoulders go. They did not stay down.

There was no prayer in him tonight, only the arithmetic of loss. He tucked the quill behind his ear—habit, old and useless—and stared at the wall. The whitewash had been slashed by someone with a temper; it made a map of fault lines. He watched a single hairline crack travel like frost across plaster.

He tried to name the feeling. Regret. Guilt. A sort of fierce, animal exhaustion that sat at the base of his throat and would not be fed. He thought of blame like an animal in a trap—how you could nurse it, how it could rot if left too long. The only thing that kept it from that was work: small, exact tasks that kept the mind busy enough to not invent worse things.

He paused, and the quill was quiet. For a breath, for two, nothing changed. He realized his hand had been moving for so long that he no longer knew where each name had come from: had he seen the man die, or had he heard the story of his death told later? Memory had folded over itself until the true chronology was a rumor.

His eyes stung. He could not tell whether the salt there was from the wind or from something deeper.

He stood slowly, the chair protesting, and crossed to the window. Rain had flattened the world into a smear of coal and ash. The road beyond the yard was half a dark ribbon and half a suggestion. He could feel the weight of the paper in his satchel—names stacked like stones.

He went back to the ledger and picked up the quill again, not to write names but to write underneath them the thing he could not speak aloud. He forced his hand to work through it the way Harald had taught him—to see what had broken underneath what was broken.

*What is the rule? The 4th Party came. We prepared. We practiced. We organized the evacuation.*

*What breaks the rule? You cannot prepare for winter coming all at once. You cannot teach people to run when they have children and elderly and sick. You cannot organize what breaks on contact with reality.*

*What is the cost? These names. This guilt. The knowledge that if I had chosen differently—deployed people faster, predicted the timing better, moved people earlier—some of them would be writing their own names down right now instead of leaving that burden to me.*

*What do I tell? That I did exactly what I could with what I knew, and it was not enough. That this failure belongs to me. That I'm carrying it forward so they don't have to.*

He looked at the names again, and they no longer floated like accusations. They settled like stones in a cairn—weight given shape, burden honored. He stood slowly, the chair protesting, and crossed to the window.

He did not close the ledger. He left it on the table with the ink still wet, as if the record needed time to set before he could be forgiven for having written it.

He walked out into the rain as if into a question, and the rain answered only with its own steady presence. It did not absolve him. It only made the world audible again, and for now that quiet brutality was an acceptable thing.
