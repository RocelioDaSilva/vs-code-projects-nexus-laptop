He did not cry at the funeral.

He did not cry when reading the telegram. He did not cry when receiving the letters, nor when reading them once, or twice, or seventeen times. He did not cry during the days that followed, when he moved through the motions of work with the precision of someone whose bones had been replaced with steel.

He cried alone, at night, on an unfamiliar bed in a rented room in a town whose name he had already forgotten.

It came without warning. He was lying on his back, staring at the ceiling, thinking about nothing in particular—just the blank, smooth surface of his mind when it was finally allowed to rest. And then the thought arrived, simple and devastating: *He will never see what I become.*

His father had written once about a stone wall he had been rebuilding. A wall that had collapsed in a storm three centuries prior and had been left untouched for all that time. He was rebuilding it carefully, replacing each stone, learning the language of how they fit together. He had written: *I will not see it finished. I may not even see this season's work completed. But someone will live in the shadow of it. That is enough.*

Aisen's father had believed in unfinished work. In the legacy of effort that would extend beyond his own life. He had written that to Aisen when Aisen was beginning his studies at the Academy—a message that seemed to say: *You may not see the end either. But you will be part of building the wall.*

Now the builder was dead, and Aisen was still building, alone, without the knowledge that his father had believed the work was worth doing.

The tears came without permission. His throat closed. His body convulsed with the effort of breathing around the constriction. He did not turn away from the ceiling. He let them fall sideways across his temples, wetting his hair, soaking into the pillow he had arranged beside him.

He did not sob. That would have required sound, and he could not make sound. He could only lie there, broken open by the knowledge that his father had died waiting to see what Aisen would build, and Aisen had been too busy building to write and tell him that the work was possible. That the wall was standing. That someone was listening.

His father would have understood the metaphor. Walls and chains, stone and steel—they were the same thing. They were structures built against collapse. They were how you left behind proof that you had been here, that you had tried, that you had done the work even when no one was watching.

Aisen's father had done that. And he had died without knowing that his son had understood it, finally.

The tears kept coming. They were hot and burning, tasting of salt and failure. His hands curled into fists, but he did not move them. He lay exactly as he had been—flat, exposed, open to the ceiling that saw nothing and nobody.

He did not know how long he lay there. Eventually the tears stopped. His breathing steadied. His body became something he could live inside again instead of something that was happening to him.

He turned over onto his side and looked at the darkness of the room.

"I understand," he whispered. "I understand now."

The room did not answer. There was no voice, no sign, no indication that the universe had heard him. But he had said it anyway. He had spoken the words into silence, and that was enough. That was the only kind of answer he was likely to get.

He got out of bed. He washed his face in the cold water from the basin, watching his own reflection blur and reform in the dark. His eyes were swollen. His face was blotched. He looked like someone who had been broken and was slowly, carefully, putting himself back together.

He dressed carefully. He arranged his chain with the precision it required. He filed away the experience of his own grief the way he filed away everything else—as data, as information, as proof that he was still capable of being human, even if he would never allow it again.

By the time dawn came, he was at his desk, writing a report about the supply corruption he had uncovered. His handwriting was steady. His analysis was precise. There was no trace in the words of what he had done in the dark.

But his hands remembered. His body remembered. The knowledge that he had broken, and that breaking had changed him, lived in his chest now like a quiet second heartbeat.

He would not tell anyone. He would not speak of it. But he would carry it, and it would make him careful in a way he had not been before. Because now he knew what would happen to the people he left behind. He had felt it.

And that feeling, terrifying and unwanted as it was, was the only legacy his father could give him now.

The only way his father could say: *You are building the right wall. Keep going.*
