He found it on a corpse that had been dead three days.

The soldier was young—maybe twenty, maybe less. It was hard to tell when the sun had done its work. But the hands that held the letter were small, and the letter itself was worn soft from being folded and refolded in a pocket close to the heart.

Aisen opened it carefully. The ink was faded, the paper fragile. It had been written a long time before the corpse was made.

*To my wife Mara:*

*I do not know how to tell you this. I am afraid. I am afraid of what happens if I speak the thought aloud, and I am afraid of what happens if I keep it silent. So I am writing it here, to you, to someone who knows me better than I know myself.*

*I am going to die here.*

*Not today. Perhaps not tomorrow. But before I see the fields again, before I see the cottage, before I see your face—I will be gone. I do not know this in the way you know the weather is coming. I know it in my bones. The way animals know a storm.*

*I have been here for six months. I have seen forty of my brothers fall. Forty. I can name most of them. Tomas had a daughter he had never seen. Bel talked about opening a mill. Marek wanted to marry but never asked the girl. I learned their dreams so I could remember them when they were gone.*

*Now I wait to be remembered.*

*If you read this, I want you to know: I was not brave. I was not noble. I was simply here, doing what I was told, trying not to think about the fact that the people I am killing are also doing what they were told, also trying not to think. We are all just men trying to go home. Some of us will make it. Most of us will not.*

*I want you to know that every morning I thought of your face. Every evening I thought of the way the light comes through the kitchen window when you bake. I held those things close so I would not lose them to the dark.*

*I want you to know that if I do not come home, I did not go willingly. I did not choose this. I was chosen. And I walked toward it the way a soldier walks toward anything—with the knowledge that his feet have no choice but to go where they are pointed.*

*I love you. I have always loved you. I love you in this moment, writing this, knowing.*

*If you receive this, burn it. Do not let our children read it. Let them believe their father was brave. It is a better story, and a better story is all a dead man can give anymore.*

*Your husband,*
*—*

The name was too worn to read. Aisen turned the letter over. On the back, a date. Six months ago. The month before this sector of the border fell.

He looked at the body again and saw the small hands, the wedding band still on the left ring finger, the patch sewn into the collar where a wife had repaired a tear.

This man had been right. He had known. And he had gone anyway.

Aisen folded the letter carefully and placed it in his satchel. Somewhere in this war, there was a woman named Mara. Somewhere, there was a cottage with a kitchen window that let light in a particular way. Somewhere, there were children who would grow up and ask why their father never came home.

Aisen could not answer that question. No one could.

He marked the location on his map. Later, when the war was over (if the war was ever over), he would try to find Mara. He would give her the letter. He would burn it if she asked him to.

But he would not forget. The soldier's last gift to his wife was that someone would remember. Someone would know that he had existed, that he had loved, that he had walked toward his death with open eyes.

Aisen stood and looked down the road toward the next village, the next position, the next body. How many more letters were there, written in the pockets of the dying, hoping to be found?

He did not count them. He did not want to know.

He just walked forward, carrying the dead man's final words, carrying the knowledge that somewhere, a woman was waiting for a letter that would bring no comfort.

That was the Border War. Not glorious. Not righteous. Just the small, terrible attrition of men waiting to go home.
