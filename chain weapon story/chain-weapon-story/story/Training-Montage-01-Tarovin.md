# MASTER TAROVIN – CHAIN SPEAR TRAINING MONTAGE

**WEEK ONE: THE WEIGHT**

The chain spear weighed less than Aisen expected for something meant to kill people. That was the first lesson. That was, in fact, the entire first week's lesson.

"Pick it up," Tarovin said. He was standing in the cleared space behind the tavern that the old man had claimed as his practice yard. The space was nothing remarkable—just dirt and some rocks and the kind of deliberate emptiness that suggested it had been chosen for being unobtrusive rather than impressive.

Aisen lifted the weapon. It was perhaps eight feet long when fully extended, with the spear head itself no more than two inches wide. The chain connecting the head to the shaft was fine—thin enough to be flexible, thick enough to survive the demands of combat. The weight, when he held it, was distributed such that it felt almost negligent in his hands.

"Heavier than you expected?" Tarovin asked.

"Lighter," Aisen said honestly.

"Because you're prepared for a massive object that will wreck your shoulders." Tarovin circled him slowly. "The chain spear doesn't work that way. Its strength is not in weight. Its strength is in structure. In the spaces between things. In movement."

Tarovin gestured, and Aisen followed his pointing finger. The weapon's balance point, it turned out, was almost exactly at the user's hands. This meant that weight below and above were nearly equal, creating a kind of equilibrium that could be used.

"Most heavy weapons are all weight forward," Tarovin explained. "They rely on momentum, on the force of impact. That's useful if you're fighting people who can't defend against it. But people at the Academy are trained to defend. So your weapon needs to work differently."

For the rest of that week, Aisen did nothing but hold the weapon. Standing with it. Sitting with it. Walking through the yard with it, learning how it shifted his balance, how it required constant micro-adjustments to maintain position. His shoulders ached. His wrists began developing new muscle groups that had never been exercised before.

By the end of the week, his body understood something his mind had only intellectually grasped: that weight was a variable. That the same object could feel light or heavy depending on how you held it. That mastering a weapon was partly technical skill and partly just becoming accustomed to carrying it, letting your body learn its language.

**WEEK TWO: THE MOTION**

"Swing it," Tarovin said.

Aisen swung it. The weapon moved through the air with a kind of fluid grace that surprised him. The chain permitted flexibility at the end, so the head of the spear traced a curve rather than a direct line.

"Again," Tarovin said. "But this time, pretend the spear head is a pencil and the ground is paper. You're drawing."

This seemed like a pointless metaphor. But as Aisen executed the motion again, imagining the spear tracing lines on earth, something shifted in his understanding. The weapon became less about force and more about intention. Less about hitting things and more about creating patterns.

"You're not strong enough to defeat most opponents through pure strength," Tarovin said. "You will never be strong enough. So you'll use something else. You'll use patterns. You'll use the fact that your weapon moves in ways most weapons can't move. You'll use the fact that chain permits flexibility that rigid structures don't."

For two weeks, they spent hours every evening doing motion-based training. Overhead strikes. Side sweeps. Thrusts that came from impossible angles. Complex arcs that traced and retraced the same space using different pathways.

Aisen's arms burned. His shoulders became corded with emerging muscle. But more importantly, his body began to internalize patterns, began to move in ways that felt natural and unforced.

By the end of week two, Tarovin had him perform a long chain of motions, approximately two minutes of continuous movement that incorporated every technique they'd practiced.

"Faster," Tarovin said toward the end of the first run-through.

Aisen tried to move faster. His form immediately deteriorated. The weapon began to feel uncontrolled.

"So not faster," Tarovin observed. "What you learned is that speed isn't the solution. Most fighters rely on speed because they rely on strength. When you can't use strength, you need a different solution."

"What solution?" Aisen asked.

"Precision. Efficiency. Making every motion count. A strike that lands exactly where it needs to land is more effective than ten strikes that land approximately the right location. This is the spear's advantage. It's long, which means you have distance advantage. It's supported by chain, which means you have flexibility advantage. The combination gives you precision advantage. You won't beat people through raw speed. You'll beat them through knowing exactly where your weapon needs to be and placing it there."

**WEEK THREE: THE TIMING**

Tarovin brought in a dummy—a wooden post wrapped in padding, suspended from a frame. It could swing slightly, simulating impact resistance.

"The difference between hitting with force and hitting with precision is understanding the moment," Tarovin said. "If you strike too early, you haven't fully accelerated. If you strike too late, you've already begun to decelerate. There's a specific moment when maximum energy is present. That's the moment you strike."

Aisen spent a week learning to identify that moment. At first, he relied on visual markers—the dummy at a particular angle, his weapon at a particular height. But gradually, he learned to feel it. The moment existed not in space but in time, and it was present briefly in every motion.

By the end of that week, every strike he threw was landing at optimum impact. Not because he was strong, but because his timing had become precise.

"This is what separates a mediocre fighter from a good fighter," Tarovin said. "Raw power matters for a small amount. Technique matters for more. But perfect timing matters most. A strike with perfect timing can compensate for inadequate power. Imperfect timing will lose to both superior power and superior technique."

**WEEK FOUR: THE DEFENSE**

"Now," Tarovin said, "someone is going to try to kill you."

He'd brought a quarterstaff—shorter, heavier, designed to block and parry. He moved with a kind of economical efficiency that suggested this was not his first time training students.

The first day of actual combat practice, Tarovin didn't try very hard. He moved slowly, telegraphing his strikes, giving Aisen time to react. But even at that reduced pace, Aisen found himself panicking. The weapon coming toward him at speed was fundamentally different from striking a stationary dummy. The need to defend, to move out of the way, to survive—that introduced an element of desperation that training didn't quite prepare you for.

He took a hit to the shoulder that hurt worse than he expected. Not an injury, carefully controlled, but enough to sting and remind him of consequence.

"What did you learn?" Tarovin asked.

"That someone defending themselves is a lot harder than a dummy," Aisen said.

"Correct. Also, you panicked. When confronted with an actual threat, you stopped thinking about technique and started thinking about survival. That's natural. But it's also a vulnerable state. You need to teach yourselves to think even when you're afraid."

They practiced defense for two weeks. Tarovin gradually increasing the speed and difficulty of attacks. Aisen learned that a chain-spear's primary defense was mobility—the ability to move the weapon to intercept incoming attacks while simultaneously opening it for counterattack.

Against a heavier weapon, Aisen's spear was more mobile. Against a faster opponent, the length advantage was more apparent. But against a skilled opponent using a different style of weapon, Aisen found himself constantly adapting, constantly learning new ways to position his defense.

**WEEK SIX: THE COMBINATION**

"Now," Tarovin said, "you're going to use everything you've learned simultaneously while someone tries to hurt you."

They sparred for an hour. Aisen's strikes were precise and timed. His defense was mobile and effective. But Tarovin was still faster, still stronger, still more practiced. Aisen took hits. He blocked most attacks. He landed several strikes that Tarovin acknowledged with nods.

At the end of that first session, Aisen was exhausted and battered.

"You're improving," Tarovin said. He showed no sign of exertion. "In approximately two years, you might be actually dangerous to someone your own age who hasn't trained significantly. In approximately five years, you might be genuinely competent against most fighters. In approximately ten years, you might be exceptional."

"That's a long time," Aisen said.

"Yes. It is. Combat mastery is not something that happens quickly. The people who claim otherwise are selling you something. What we're building is something that will be useful for the rest of your life. That doesn't happen in a few weeks."

**CONTINUOUS TRAINING: THE INTEGRATION**

Over the next months, as Aisen's formal schooling began and his life became more complicated, training with Tarovin became something constant. Not daily—the old man wasn't fanatic about it—but regularly enough that the weapon became as natural in his hands as his own limbs.

Tarovin introduced new techniques: the chain-hook, where you used the flexibility to snare an opponent's weapon; the sweep, where the weapon's length could be used to unbalance someone; the bind, where you engaged an opponent's defense and then shifted your weight to create new angles.

More importantly, Tarovin trained him in the philosophy underlying the weapon.

"Strength and speed are resources," Tarovin explained during one of their sessions. "Everyone has them. The Covenant's fighters are trained to use them efficiently. You will never beat them using strength and speed as your primary advantage. So your advantage is understanding. You need to understand the spaces where strength and speed don't apply. You need to understand the principles underlying combat and exploit those principles.

A person with superior strength can overwhelm you. A person with superior speed can get inside your defenses. But a person whose entire strategy is built on strength or speed is exploitable. They have predictable patterns. They have points where their own power becomes a liability. That's where you strike.

This is why I'm teaching you the chain spear specifically. It's not the best weapon. It's not the strongest. But it permits a style that's fundamentally different from how most people train. When you face someone expecting traditional combat, you'll use tactics they don't have defenses for."

By the time Aisen was fourteen, he'd been training for almost a year. He wasn't an expert. He wasn't exceptional. But he had something more valuable: a deep understanding of how he could use a weapon to compensate for lack of raw power. A knowledge that skill and precision could matter more than strength.

And he had learned something else: that Tarovin had been training him not just as a fighter, but as someone who understood that difference was strength. That being ordinary was an advantage if you used it correctly.

Months later, when the Academy tried to teach him their version of combat—based on power and magical enhancement—Aisen would find that he already understood something they hadn't yet: that the most dangerous fighter wasn't the strongest. It was the one who understood that advantage wasn't given, it was constructed.

Tarovin had given him a key. The Covenant had just given him the lock it would open.
