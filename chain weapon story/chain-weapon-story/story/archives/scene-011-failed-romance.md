# THE FAILED ROMANCE

The gardens behind the Academy dormitories had always been Marina's favorite place. In autumn, when the leaves turned bronze and gold, the pathways became a cathedral of light. Castor had memorized the way she moved through them—hands trailing along stone walls, eyes following the flight of sparrows, the kind of attention that suggested she was genuinely present in the world rather than just passing through it.

He had been in love with her for two years. He had never told her.

Today, he would end things that were never officially begun.

Marina looked beautiful in the afternoon light, which felt like an unnecessary cruelty. If she had looked haggard or tired, it might have been easier. Instead, she was wearing the blue dress he liked—not because he'd told her he liked it, but because she'd chosen it without knowing his preferences mattered to him. That kind of coincidental perfection existed in the world sometimes.

"You've been distant lately," Marina said. She was sitting on the stone bench near the fountain, looking at him with an expression that suggested she already knew what was coming. "I thought maybe you'd finally tell me what's going on."

Castor had prepared words for this. Clinical, careful words that would minimize the damage while providing a sort of truth. But standing in front of her, with the fountain running in the background and her eyes waiting to understand what he couldn't explain, the prepared words felt obscene.

"I can't," he said instead. "I can't be with you."

"Can't or won't?" Her voice was steady, but he could see the tension in her shoulders. "Those are different things, Castor."

They were. And she deserved the real one. But the real one—*I've been approached by representatives of the Covenant and given a choice between breaking up with you or watching them systematize leverage over you*—was impossible to say. Impossible to articulate without revealing the pressure he was under, which was somehow worse than the breaking itself.

"Both," he said, which was at least technically honest.

Marina looked away from him then, toward the fountain. The water fell in the same pattern it had fallen for a hundred years. Regular. Predictable. Unburdened by the requirements of choice that complicated human relationship.

"Is there someone else?" she asked.

"No."

"Then I don't understand." She turned back to face him, and he could see her working through the logic problem his behavior had created. "If you love me and there's no one else, then why would you end this? The only reason that makes sense is if you've been told to. If someone has made you."

She was too intelligent. That was part of why he had fallen for her—why he had spent two years in proximity to her, learning the particular cadence of her laughter, the way she bit her lower lip when she was concentrating, the manner in which she could make anyone feel like they were the most interesting person in any given room.

"I can't confirm or deny that," he said carefully.

"So I'm right." She stood up then, putting the bench between them. The physical distance seemed to matter suddenly—as if closeness was something she needed to defend against. "Someone has threatened you. Or threatened me. And you've chosen to handle it by—what? By pretending you don't care? By letting me think I imagined the connection?"

"I was trying to protect you."

"By lying to me." Her voice wasn't angry. That would have been easier. Instead, she sounded tired. "By treating me like I was too fragile to know the truth about my own life. By deciding unilaterally that I should be abandoned for my own good."

The accusation landed exactly where she'd intended. It was true. He had been paternalistic about it, had assumed she couldn't handle the weight of his situation, had taken agency away from her in the name of keeping her safe.

"If I tell you what's really happening," Castor said, "they'll have confirmation that they can use you to pressure me. Right now, you're just someone I know. If you become someone I've trusted with classified information, you become a liability."

"So the way to keep me safe is to break my heart and make me look like a fool in front of our friends." She was moving toward the garden's exit now, her posture rigid. "Congratulations, Castor. You've successfully humiliated me and isolated me simultaneously. The Covenant must be very pleased."

"Marina—"

"No." She held up a hand. "I don't want to hear whatever explanation you're about to offer. I'm not interested in being protected by someone who respects me too little to let me make my own informed choices. If you want to be involved with their system, that's your decision. But don't pretend it was for my sake."

She left him in the gardens. He stood by the fountain and watched her go, watching the specific way she moved away, knowing that he would spend years unable to unhear the break in her voice on that last sentence—the moment when hurt transformed into genuine contempt.

By evening, rumors had spread through the Academy's social circles. Castor had broken Marina's heart without cause. He was cruel, ambitious, willing to sacrifice genuine connection for some unknown agenda. By the following week, Marina had been reframed in the social narrative—not as someone who had wronged her, but as someone who had been wronged, which in the intricate politics of Academy social life was far more powerful. She had supporters. She had allies. She had community.

Castor, increasingly isolated, would spend the remaining years of his Academy training becoming exactly what the Covenant needed him to be: a high-performing and entirely hollow individual, motivated by fear and the knowledge that he had already lost everything that mattered before he was even truly aware that it could be lost.

The Covenant would eventually reward his loyalty by positioning him as a regional administrator. He would execute his duties with precision. He would attend parties where Marina was also present and carefully not look at her.

And seven years after breaking her heart in the gardens, Castor would receive orders to capture or kill a network of ordinary people who had become extraordinarily dangerous. He would recognize Marina's name in the list of network members and understand, in that moment, that the Covenant's system of control had finally reached its end point—the moment where even its most loyal servants realized that the system was now asking them to destroy everything they had destroyed in the name of preserving.

But that realization was still years away. In the garden, standing by the fountain, Castor was simply a young man who had made a choice and was only beginning to understand the cost of it.

He walked back to his dormitory alone, understanding for the first time that loneliness was not something that happened to you. It was something you chose when you decided that other people's safety mattered more than shared presence.

He would make that choice many more times before the world ended.
