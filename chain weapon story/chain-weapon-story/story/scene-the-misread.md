# The Misread

He had been tracking the merchant captain for three days.

The intelligence was solid: Vareth was trafficking in Flux-harvested children. The network had confirmed it through three independent sources. Aisen had seen the evidence himself—a ledger, bought from a drunken quartermaster, showing shipment dates, locations, quantities of "cargo" that bore no relation to stated trade routes.

Vareth was guilty.

So when the captain arrived at the border inn to negotiate passage through the territory, Aisen had set a trap. Not a killing trap—the network needed Vareth alive to testify, to break the trafficking ring's supply chain. A containment trap, designed to isolate him from his guards, force negotiation, preserve options.

Aisen had studied Vareth for three days. Watched him move. Listened to him speak. Applied the framework he had been teaching himself since childhood: observe the tells. Recognize the patterns that reveal what a person *actually* wants beneath what they say.

Vareth's tell was fear.

The captain was afraid. Aisen could see it in the way his hands trembled around his drink, the way his eyes flicked to the exits, the way he moved with the posture of someone expecting violence. Guilty conscience. The fear of a man who knew he had transgressed, who expected consequences.

Fear made people predictable. Fear made them fallible. Fear made them escape.

So Aisen positioned his network outside the exits, ensuring that Vareth would have exactly one route open: the trade corridor into the city district. The route Aisen would be waiting in. The place where Aisen could cut off retreat, negotiate terms, force the confession needed to dismantle the entire trafficking network.

It was a good plan.

It was absolutely sound.

It was based on reading a tell that was not what he thought it was.

## THE TRAP

Vareth left the inn at sunset.

Exactly as predicted. Through the trade corridor. Exactly as predicted. Aisen emerged from the shadows, chain in hand, blocking retreat.

"Vareth," he said. "We need to talk about the children."

The merchant captain's face went pale.

Then he laughed.

It was not the sound of guilt breaking. It was the sound of someone who had just been released from unbearable tension.

"The children," Vareth said, still laughing. "Oh gods, finally."

Aisen's mind stuttered.

"I have been trying to get someone to *find out*," Vareth continued. He was not reaching for a weapon. He was sinking to his knees. "I have been praying for someone to investigate, to discover, to *stop* what was happening in my own company."

Aisen did not lower his chain.

"Vareth—"

"I am not the trafficker," Vareth said, and now there were tears running down his face. "My own crew is. My quartermaster—have you met my quartermaster? He has been selling harvested children out of my cargo hold without my knowledge. Without my permission. I have been terrified for *eighteen months* that someone would find out and think it was *me*."

The trap suddenly looked very different.

"The ledger," Aisen said.

"I don't keep ledgers," Vareth said. "I keep records. And yes, someone altered them. Someone who knew my supply routes and knew I would be blamed if discovered. I have been trying to *catch* them. I have been cooperating with the authorities. I have been dying of fear that you—that *someone*—would assume I was guilty."

Aisen lowered his chain.

For a moment, he said nothing. Could not process the recalibration happening in real time.

He had read fear.

He had interpreted that fear as guilty conscience.

He had built an entire operational plan around a tell that actually meant the opposite of what he thought it meant.

The merchant captain was not a trafficker. He was a victim of his own company's corruption, afraid that he would be blamed for something he had actively been trying to stop.

The real criminals—the quartermaster, the crew members profiting from the harvest—were not here. Were not contained. Were probably preparing to flee now, alerted by whatever commotion they had sensed from their captain's arrest.

Aisen had just sabotaged his entire operation by misreading a single piece of human behavior.

## THE IMPROVISATION

"Vareth," Aisen said. "I need to see the actual records. The ones you kept, not the falsified ones."

Vareth nodded, wiped his face. "They're in my cabin. On the ship. In the harbor."

Aisen made a decision.

"Go back inside the inn. Tell your guards that I was testing their security. Tell them you hired me to verify their readiness. Make it convincing."

"They'll know you're lying as soon as—"

"They don't care," Aisen said. "They're paid to not care. Go. Now. Before the quartermaster realizes something has changed."

Vareth went.

Aisen ran.

The harbor was three blocks away. The ship was anchored close to shore—a merchant vessel, two-masted, bearing the Valdimere colors that Vareth had mentioned. Aisen reached it as sunset finished dying into full darkness.

He needed to be on that ship, in that cabin, before the quartermaster realized their captain had been intercepted.

He climbed the anchor chain.

The deck was minimal crew—four sailors, maybe five, scattered across the watch positions. Aisen moved through them like smoke, not visible, not engaging, just displaced from attention. He had learned this from Kairos's own teaching: sometimes the best way to move past people was to accept that they were not actually watching, and then prove that acceptance by never forcing them to look.

The captain's cabin was below deck.

Aisen found it by smell—merchant captains all kept their cabins the same way, with the distinct scent of wax and paper and ink that came from record-keeping. He was inside, searching by candlelight, when he heard the alarm.

Someone had realized something was wrong.

He had maybe two minutes before the crew was mobilized.

Aisen found the records. Vareth was meticulous—Aisen could see it immediately. The real ledgers showed exactly what Vareth had said: cargo counts that didn't match the falsified documents, transactions recorded with the names of the crew members who had been siphoning children out of the holds.

More proof than Aisen needed.

He burned them.

It was the only way to preserve them—to create an incident significant enough that the crew would panic, scatter, make mistakes in their flight. Aisen set the candle to the corner of the records, watched it catch, and moved.

He was out of the cabin and back up the companionway as the smoke alarm started.

The crew was shouting now, responding to the fire signal. Aisen moved through them in the opposite direction, toward the stern, toward the darkness of the harbor.

He jumped.

The water was cold. The harbor was busy enough that a man swimming in darkness, moving against the tide, could be invisible. Aisen let the current carry him, let the dock lights pass, let the crew's yelling fade.

By the time he reached shore, the merchant vessel was fully ablaze.

The crew was abandoning ship. The quartermaster would be among them, panicked, making decisions that desperate people made. Aisen watched from the shadows as the crew hit the dock, scattered, ran.

He memorized their faces. Every single one.

## THE RECKONING

Later, in a safe house across the city, Aisen wrote the network report.

*Vareth is not the trafficker. Vareth is the victim. The quartermaster and crew members (names attached) are the actual trafficking ring.'

*I misread the initial intelligence and nearly captured an innocent person. This was human error. My projection was wrong. My interpretation of tells was wrong. I proceeded on a false assumption and built operational security around it.*

*The records are destroyed, but the crew is panicked and fleeing south. They can be intercepted if the network moves immediately. They have approximately twelve hours before they reach the border.'

He did not include the feeling of it—the sick certainty that he had been wrong, that his carefully cultivated observation skills were not infallible, that the system he had built to read others could fail catastrophically.

But the network understood.

Two days later, the quartermaster and five crew members were intercepted attempting to cross the southern border with a wagon full of Flux-harvesting equipment. They confessed immediately, probably because escape felt more achievable than capture.

The trafficking ring was broken.

The children were freed.

But Aisen understood something new in the aftermath: that observation was not certain. That tells were interpretable, not deterministic. That a person could read the evidence correctly and still arrive at the wrong conclusion.

He had trusted his skill too much.

That trust had almost cost him an innocent man.

He was more careful, after that, about building backup verification into his observations. More willing to consider that his reading might be wrong. More humble about the limits of even careful attention.

His small edges were still sharp. But he had learned that they were not unbreakable.

And somehow, that knowledge made them more reliable, not less—because now he was prepared for the moment they would inevitably fail him.
