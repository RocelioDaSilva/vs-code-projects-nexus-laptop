The blood was warm.

That was strange. He had expected it to be cold, already alien to his body. But it was warm still, flowing with the last heat his chest could generate, moving through his ribs the way the chain had learned to move through his hands.

Renard was standing above him, saying something. The words were not important. Words were the tool of people who had time. Aisen did not have time, so the words became noise—water flowing past him without meaning.

He was thinking, instead, about the girl.

There had been a girl in one of the refugee camps, maybe three years ago. She had been learning to read using a book he had left behind—a military manual about supply chains, of all useless things. But it had words, and she was learning them, sounding them out with the serious concentration of someone for whom knowledge was survival.

She would never know that Aisen had killed the man who would have enslaved her.

She would never know it because she would never know Aisen had existed. She would grow up—assuming she survived the displacement, the winter, the thousand small cruelties a refugee child encountered—and she would be a person with skills learned from a manual she had found. She might think the knowledge materialized from nowhere. Or she might know it came from a person she would never meet, and that would be enough.

That knowledge—not glory. Not songs. Not statues or commemorations or any of the things that made powerful people think their lives mattered. Just the knowledge that a small thing had changed because Aisen had been willing to act. That a girl had learned to read. That a village had survived one more winter. That a chain had been wielded to protect instead of to enslave.

The pain was fading.

That was how you knew it was the end—not the dramatic pain of an action sequence, but the slow, cold departure of sensation. His legs were already gone. His stomach was a thing that happened to someone else. Soon his hands. Soon the part of him that could think would follow.

He thought about his father's letters. About the wall that would never be completed, built by a builder who would never see it finished. About the assumption that unfinished work could still matter. That the person who came after could add their stone, and the person after them could add another, and eventually a wall existed that no single person had built but everyone had contributed to.

That was how the world changed. Not through heroes. Not through the grand gestures and noble sacrifices that stories celebrated. But through people doing small, precise, unglamorous work. Leaving behind stones. Leaving behind knowledge. Leaving behind proof that they had existed and that they had tried.

Aisen had done that. He had lived and paid attention and made careful choices about power. He had protected people who would never know his name. He had killed people who would deserve it. He had refused patronage and walked his own way and chosen the consequences rather than the comfort.

And now he was dying, and the world would continue, and most people would forget him within a generation.

This was as it should be.

This was the only kind of success that mattered.

He looked up at Renard—or rather, he turned his eyes in the direction where Renard was standing, because looking required muscles that were already in the process of stopping work.

"They will not sing songs," he said. His voice was strange—distant, like it was coming from someone else. "They will not carve statues. But they will live."

Renard was crying. Aisen could see the tears, though he could not quite access the part of himself that understood what tears meant.

"That is enough," Aisen said.

And it was. It was more than enough. It was the whole point. The entire philosophy that had carried him from childhood to death—that ordinary things mattered. That small edges could change the geometry of catastrophe. That winning did not require being noticed, recognized, or celebrated. That a life was successful if it left behind it a world slightly less broken than the one you inherited.

The darkness was coming now.

He let it.

One final thought arrived before the light closed: *Thank you, Father. I understood the wall.*

And then Aisen, who had spent his life learning to see what others overlooked, closed his eyes for the last time.

He had finished his work. Someone else would continue it. That was all the immortality anyone got, and it was exactly the right amount.
