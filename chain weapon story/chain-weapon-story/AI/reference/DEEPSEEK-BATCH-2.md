Key beats — high-level
- Academy as social crucible: exercises, competitions, and networking shape military, diplomatic, and guild recruitment. The protagonist’s stated goal: become a competent adventurer, remain neutral, and protect the forgotten.
- Protagonist tactics: "Play the field" — learn from multiple halls, build cross-hall friendships (Amara, Pieter, Tupac, Ren), use unorthodox chain techniques and syncretic physics-based approaches.
- Mentors: Master Wei (theory/political cover), Captain Vane (resource/contract management), Griot Amadi (intelligence). Their subtle protection allows risky independence.

Noble caste systems & halls (compact)
- Heartland: rigid imperial/military nobility; privileges, political clout, views other nations as provincial.
- Island Fortress: samurai codes, duels, spirit‑binding training; value martial skill above all.
- Celestial: scholar‑gentry, libraries, laboratories; meritocratic veneer, strong on theory.
- Sahelian: merchant‑princes, griot information networks, trade diplomacy.
- Andean: communal ayllu leaders, shamans, rope‑engineering expertise.
- Federation: patrician oligarchy, mercantile power and contract leverage.
- Common Stave: counter‑culture of merit; protagonist originates here.

Social events (storyworthy beats)
- Welcoming Gala: status display; early snubs, first misrecognitions.
- Hall dinners: hall-specific rituals and politics (Heartland political dinners, Island tea ceremonies, Celestial symposia).
- Tournament of Champions: major reputation engine; public exposure and recruiter attention.
- Grand Masquerade: anonymity drives secret deals and scandals; protagonist uses anonymity for intel.
- Graduation Gala: final offers and ideological choices — protagonist refuses binding offers (keeps neutrality).

Intra‑academy politics & factions
- Factions: Imperial Circle (Heartland), Way of the Spirit (Island Fortress), Harmonious Path (Celestial), Silent Network (Sahelian), Andean Circle, Counting House (Federation), Common Stave League.
- Power plays: curriculum wars (military vs. electives), sponsorship system (strings attached), Frostfang aftermath (military oversight push defeated by cross‑hall testimony).

Mortals vs. Anomalies
- Anomaly types: regressors (timeline survivors), transmigrators (out‑of‑world tech/knowledge), reincarnators (ancient memory), chosen ones, Aelfar‑touched individuals.
- Perception & politics: fear, worship, exploitation; factions attempt to control or weaponize anomalies.
- Examples: Lucius Varro (hidden regressor, personal nemesis), a transmigrator with modern engineering, a reluctant chosen one, a reincarnated Aelfar spirit allied to the protagonist.

Mind‑games, sabotage, and moral economy
- Historical parallels inform tactics (Byzantine, Sengoku, Chinese court politics, griot editing of history, mercantile leverage).
- Sabotage toolkit: social exclusion, rumor campaigns, academic tampering, alliance manipulation, physical/magical traps, financial pressure, psychological operations.
- Case studies: Heartland quiet purge (success), Varro’s overreach (failure due to networked defense), Federation double‑play (strategic duplication), Celestial alchemist (failed revenge—caught).

Vulnerabilities of anomalies
- Regressors: overreliance on past scripts; isolation leads to blind spots.
- Transmigrators: cultural blindness, misapplied tech, system dependence.
- Reincarnators: nostalgic overconfidence, legal/ethical risk (flux pollution laws).
- Chosen ones: entitlement, manipulation by others, inflexibility.

Protagonist’s political/ethical arc
- Neutrality is strategic: allows cross‑border work but leaves him without full protection.
- Defensive tactics: misinformation (false journal), trap setting, financial and intelligence countermeasures via friends.
- Thematic: merit vs. privilege; ordinary skill + empathy vs. scripted advantage.

- Power system specifics to define: mana metrics, artifact creation & decay, non‑human magic paradigms, environmental mana ecology, clear counters for major schools.
