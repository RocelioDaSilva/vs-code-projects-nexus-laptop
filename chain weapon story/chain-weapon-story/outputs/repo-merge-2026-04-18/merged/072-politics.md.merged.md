# Andean Alliance — Politics & Governance

## Governance Structure: The Three-Band System

### Layer 1: Community Council (Local)
- Every community (50-500 people) has a rotating council
- Leadership rotates among qualified families (3-year terms, no consecutive terms)
- Decisions by consensus; unresolved disputes escalate to band council
- Handles: daily governance, resource allocation, mit'a scheduling, ayni disputes

### Layer 2: Band Council (Regional)
- Each band (Urqu/Peak, Qucha/Terrace, Yunka/Valley) has a Band Council
- Representatives selected by community councils
- Handles: inter-community disputes, band-level resource management, military coordination within band
- Band Speaker elected by Band Council (5-year term, renewable once)
- Band Speakers represent their band in the Inter-Band Council

### Layer 3: Inter-Band Council (National)
- Three Band Speakers + Yachaq Council representative + War Commander (advisory)
- Meets at Qhapaq Llaqta (capital, terrace altitude)
- Handles: foreign policy, inter-band resource disputes, military deployment, trade policy
- Decisions require consensus of all three Band Speakers

### Layer 4: Yachaq Council (Spiritual Authority)
- Council of senior shamans; advises Inter-Band Council on spiritual matters
- Led by Yachaq Mama (senior female shaman) or Yachaq Tayta (senior male shaman)
- Cannot hold political office; cannot vote in councils; cannot be overruled on spiritual matters
- **Power**: Spiritual veto — can declare a political decision "against the mountain's breath"
- **Limit**: Spiritual veto used rarely (perhaps once per decade); overuse would destroy credibility

## Political Factions

### 1. Traditionalists (Dominant)
**Base**: Peak communities, senior shamans, elder councils
**Position**: The old ways work. Isolation protects the Alliance. External engagement corrupts.
**Method**: Consensus blocking (any Band Speaker can prevent action); spiritual veto threat
**Strength**: Cultural legitimacy; control of sacred knowledge; peak communities' prestige
**Weakness**: Cannot address depletion crisis, food security, or modernization pressure through isolation alone
**Key figure**: Yachaq Mama Sisa (spiritual authority)

### 2. Moderate Reformers (Growing)
**Base**: Terrace communities, younger council members, mid-rank shamans
**Position**: Controlled modernization — accept limited external trade and technology while preserving core values. Mit'a reform to address labor shortage. Selective crystal trade expansion.
**Method**: Proposal through council system; build consensus across bands
**Strength**: Practical arguments; terrace band is the largest and most militarily important
**Weakness**: "Controlled modernization" is viewed by traditionalists as the first step toward Federation-style capitalism
**Key figure**: Speaker Inti Wayra (Inter-Band Council leader)

### 3. Valley Modernizers (Small but Strategic)
**Base**: Yunka Hatun trade port, valley merchants, young people with external exposure
**Position**: The Alliance must engage with the outside world or decline into irrelevance. Open trade, accept technology, reform the ayni system to allow personal accumulation.
**Method**: De facto modernization (adopting Federation trade practices without formal authorization); lobbying through valley Band Speaker
**Strength**: Economic argument (Alliance needs metal and grain); generational momentum
**Weakness**: Culturally marginal; easily dismissed as "corrupted by lowland air"
**Risk**: Most vulnerable to Covenant co-optation (Covenant offers exactly what modernizers want)

### 4. Isolationists (Small, Extreme)
**Base**: Remote peak communities, conservative shamans
**Position**: Seal the borders. End ALL external trade. Return to pre-contact self-sufficiency.
**Method**: Community-level withdrawal (refusing to participate in inter-band governance); spiritual arguments
**Strength**: Cultural purity argument; moral clarity
**Weakness**: Impossible to implement (Alliance needs metal imports); would cause starvation in drought years

## Key Political Tensions

### Consensus Paralysis
- All major decisions require consensus of three Band Speakers
- Any one Speaker can block action indefinitely
- Result: the Alliance reacts slowly to changing circumstances
- **Current paralysis**: Covenant trade proposal has been "under discussion" for 2 years; no consensus possible

### Altitude Representation
- Formally, all three bands have equal voice (one Speaker each)
- In practice, Peak band (15% of population) has equal weight to Valley band (45%)
- Valley modernizers argue for proportional representation; Peak traditionalists resist
- **Unresolved**: The representation question is too fundamental to raise without risking band conflict

### Succession Dispute
- Speaker Inti Wayra (terrace, moderate reformer) is approaching term limit
- No clear successor; reformers and traditionalists both positioning candidates
- If a traditionalist becomes Speaker: modernization halted, Covenant proposal rejected
- If a reformer becomes Speaker: accelerated change, internal cultural crisis

### Shamanic Authority vs. Political Authority
- Yachaq Council claims spiritual veto over political decisions
- Political leaders increasingly see spiritual veto as obstruction
- **Current flashpoint**: Yachaq Mama Sisa threatened spiritual veto over mining technology proposal
- If spiritual veto is used and overridden, the legitimacy of the entire system collapses

### Military Command
- War Commander Tupaq Sinchi has authority during military threat; advisory role in peacetime
- Increasing Valdimerian border pressure is creating "permanent threat" justification
- Some terrace warriors support expanded military authority (faster decisions, less consensus paralysis)
- **Risk**: Military authority expanding into peacetime governance = beginning of authoritarian shift

## Decision-Making Process

### Standard Process
1. Issue raised at community council level
2. Community councils discuss; representatives carry position to Band Council
3. Band Council debates; Band Speaker carries position to Inter-Band Council
4. Inter-Band Council seeks consensus of three Speakers
5. Yachaq Council provides spiritual guidance (non-binding unless veto invoked)
6. If consensus reached: decision communicated back down through councils
7. If no consensus: issue tabled; revisited at next meeting (quarterly)

### Emergency Process
1. War Commander identifies threat
2. Inter-Band Council convened immediately (emergency session)
3. War Commander presents; Speakers decide (consensus still required)
4. If immediate military action needed: War Commander can act with 2 of 3 Speakers' approval
5. Post-action review required within 30 days

### Timeline
- Routine decisions: 1-3 months (community → band → inter-band cycle)
- Significant policy: 6-18 months
- Constitutional change: Years (requires community-level consensus across all three bands)
- Military emergency: Hours to days (faster than political process, but still consensus-dependent)

# Celestial Kingdom — Politics & Power Structure

## Governance Hierarchy

### Layer 1: Monastic Council (Supreme Authority)
- 7 senior meditation masters; selection by peer recognition
- Veto power over ALL governance decisions
- Controls: spiritual policy, magical education, diplomatic relations, aerolith distribution
- Meets in Crystal Terrace Monastery (highest permanent settlement)
- **Effective head of state**: Council Chair (currently Tenzin Dorje)

### Layer 2: Lay Administrative Assembly (Civil Governance)
- 30 representatives from monastery-cities and rural districts
- Handles: taxation, infrastructure, dispute resolution, trade regulation
- **Constraint**: Requires Monastic Council approval for any policy affecting spiritual practice, education, or magic (which is nearly everything)
- Growing in assertiveness; increasingly frustrated by Council vetoes

### Layer 3: Sentinel Order Command (Military-Spiritual Defense)
- Commander + 5 regional captains
- Reports to both Council (spiritual discipline) and Assembly (material support)
- **Dual accountability**: Creates confusion during crises; neither authority takes full responsibility
- Operationally autonomous for border defense; strategic decisions require Council approval

### Layer 4: Local Governance (Monastery-Cities & Rural Districts)
- Each settlement has local council (mix of monastic and lay members)
- Day-to-day governance: water distribution, road maintenance, dispute mediation
- Limited authority; cannot contradict monastery directives
- Rural districts particularly neglected by central governance

## Political Factions

### 1. Traditionalists (Dominant)
**Base**: Senior monastics, conservative lay officials, older lay communities
**Position**: Maintain current system. Spiritual practice IS governance. Material problems are spiritual problems manifesting physically.
**Method**: Procedural blocking of reform proposals; invoking founding principles
**Strength**: Control Monastic Council; possess genuine spiritual authority
**Weakness**: Declining credibility as material problems worsen without spiritual resolution
**Key figure**: Grand Master Tenzin Dorje

### 2. Reformers (Growing)
**Base**: Junior monastics, lay officials, urban lay communities, returning diaspora
**Position**: Selective modernization. Maintain meditation tradition but allow economic development, foreign trade, and Sentinel reform.
**Method**: Assembly proposals, informal lobbying, building alternative coalitions
**Strength**: Represent majority of population (Tier 3-4); practical solutions
**Weakness**: No institutional power; Council can veto any reform
**Key figure**: Master Pema Lhamo, Administrator Sonam Tsering

### 3. Isolationists (Militant Wing)
**Base**: Remote peak communities, conservative Sentinels, traditionalist monastics
**Position**: Increase isolation. Close passes to foreigners. Expel foreign students. Cut trade.
**Method**: Civil disobedience (refusing to maintain trade-route infrastructure); political pressure
**Strength**: Simple, emotionally satisfying message; resonates with fear of contamination
**Weakness**: Economic suicide — Kingdom cannot survive without imported metal and supplementary grain
**Trigger**: Any external crisis increases their appeal

### 4. Pragmatists (Small, Strategic)
**Base**: Trade-route communities, Sentinel veterans, intelligence operatives
**Position**: Engage with outside world strategically. Build alliances. Prepare for Covenant threat.
**Method**: Quiet influence; providing intelligence that traditionalists can't ignore
**Strength**: Understand external threats better than any other faction
**Weakness**: Tiny; viewed as spiritually compromised for engaging with the material world
**Key figure**: Commander Karma Wangchuk; Rin (from abroad)

## Key Political Tensions

### Spiritual Authority vs. Material Governance
- The foundational conflict. Council claims spiritual authority over all domains.
- Assembly argues: "Deciding where to build a bridge is not a spiritual question."
- Council responds: "Everything is a spiritual question. A bridge built with wrong intention creates wrong karma."
- **Escalation path**: Assembly eventually acts without Council approval → constitutional crisis

### Development vs. Preservation
- Aerolith mining could fund Kingdom modernization but damages Flux-geological formations
- Trade route expansion would reduce food insecurity but increases foreign contact
- Infrastructure investment would improve lay life but diverts resources from monastic maintenance
- **Every development proposal** triggers the same debate: material benefit vs. spiritual cost

### Sentinel Sustainability
- Current force: ~1,400 of 2,000 positions filled; declining
- Proposed solutions (all blocked by Council):
  - Reduce meditation requirements during active deployment
  - Allow non-monastic recruits (lay fighters)
  - Accept foreign military assistance
  - Create specialized roles (not all Sentinels need both combat and meditation mastery)
- **Council position**: "A Sentinel who does not meditate is just a soldier. Soldiers are not what we need."
- **Karma's response** (private): "A Sentinel who meditates but cannot defend the pass is not what we need either."

## Covenant Political Strategy

### Current Approach
1. **Validate Council authority** → Monastics welcome Covenant's praise of meditation tradition
2. **Fund research programs** → Create material dependency without overt political demand
3. **Cultivate reformers** → Sonam Tsering receives "helpful trade contacts" from Covenant intermediaries
4. **Gather intelligence** → Scholar exchange students map capabilities
5. **Wait for crisis** → When external threat forces the Kingdom to engage, Covenant positions itself as indispensable ally

### Long-Term Goal
- Convert meditation techniques into standardized, transmissible curriculum
- Export under Covenant institutional control
- Kingdom becomes culturally subordinate — its primary asset owned and distributed by others
- No military invasion needed; the Kingdom surrenders its uniqueness through institutional dependency

## Political Decision-Making

### Process
1. Issue identified (by Assembly, Sentinels, or local council)
2. Assembly debates and proposes response
3. Proposal sent to Monastic Council for approval
4. Council deliberates (weeks to months; meditation-based decision-making is slow)
5. Council approves, modifies, or vetoes
6. If approved: Assembly implements
7. If vetoed: Assembly cannot proceed; may appeal once; appeal rarely succeeds

### Timeline
- Routine decisions: 2-4 weeks
- Significant policy: 2-6 months
- Constitutional change: Years (if ever)
- Emergency response: Council can act quickly (hours) but rarely does — emergencies are redefined as "tests of spiritual resilience"

### Failure Mode
External threats that require rapid response (military incursion, trade route seizure, epidemic) are processed through a governance system designed for contemplation. By the time the Council acts, the crisis has often resolved itself — usually badly.

# Mercantile Federation — Politics & Power Structure

## Governance Hierarchy

### Layer 1: The Federal Compact (Constitutional Authority)
- The Federation itself is a contract, signed 300 years ago by 14 cantons
- Defines: common defense, trade freedom, arbitration procedures, amendment process
- Amendment requires 10 of 14 cantons — effectively impossible for controversial changes
- Treated with religious reverence (annual Compact Day celebrations)

### Layer 2: Federal Assembly (Legislative)
- Each canton sends 2-5 delegates (proportional to population + trade volume)
- Meets quarterly; supermajority (10 of 14) required for most decisions
- Can propose but cannot compel; dissenting cantons can ignore federal decisions
- Enforcement depends on other cantons' willingness to sanction

### Layer 3: Arbitration Court (Judicial)
- 7 arbitrators from retired legal scholars; 10-year non-renewable terms
- Hears inter-canton disputes and contract cases with federal implications
- Decisions are magically enforced through contract-vault enchantments
- Most trusted institution — precisely because it has no political power beyond cases

### Layer 4: Canton Governance (Local)
- **Merchant Council** (5 cantons): Guild representatives govern
- **Patriciate** (3 cantons): Hereditary merchant families rule
- **Mixed Assembly** (4 cantons): Guild + citizen representatives
- **Direct Democracy** (2 cantons, smallest): All citizens vote

## Political Factions

### 1. Patriciate Conservatives (Dominant)
**Base**: Old money families, senior guild masters, Goldmark/financial cantons
**Position**: Maintain current system. Contract equality IS equality. The Compact works.
**Method**: Procedural blocking through supermajority requirement; financial leverage over dependent cantons
**Strength**: Control Federal Assembly through delegate selection; control banks; control contract-vault access
**Weakness**: Declining legitimacy as contract-bound crisis grows
**Key figure**: Patrician Aldric van der Berg

### 2. Federal Strengtheners (Growing)
**Base**: Naval cantons (Portmund, Eastwall), military-minded delegates, trade security advocates
**Position**: Federation needs stronger central authority. Defense, financial regulation, and trade policy should be federal, not cantonal.
**Method**: Assembly proposals, security argument ("Covenant threat requires unified response")
**Strength**: Security argument gains traction with each external threat
**Weakness**: Cantons jealously guard autonomy; "federal strengthening" sounds like "Valdimere with contracts"
**Key figure**: Assembly Speaker Maximilian Thrace

### 3. Guild Reformers (Small, Vocal)
**Base**: Progressive guild officers, urban professionals, some Tier 3 guild members
**Position**: Guild system needs modernization. Non-guild workers need representation. Life Contracts need reform.
**Method**: Canton-level legislative proposals, public advocacy, arbitration test cases
**Strength**: Morally compelling argument; growing public sympathy
**Weakness**: No federal power; blocked by patriciate at canton level
**Key figure**: Guildmaster Margarethe Stern

### 4. Abolitionists (Radical)
**Base**: Freeport community, progressive physicians, religious dissenters, some Tier 3 artisans
**Position**: Life Contracts must be abolished. Magically-binding servitude is slavery regardless of contract language.
**Method**: Direct action (harboring escaped contract-bound), legal challenges, public protest
**Strength**: Moral clarity; growing movement
**Weakness**: Abolishing Life Contracts requires changing the contract-enforcement mechanism — which threatens ALL contracts
**Risk**: If they succeed, the Federation's foundational principle (contract is sacred) is undermined
**Key figure**: Dr. Emilia Carsten

### 5. Canton Autonomists (Persistent)
**Base**: Smaller cantons, rural communities, anti-federal sentiment
**Position**: The Compact should be weakened, not strengthened. Cantons should govern themselves with minimal federal interference.
**Method**: Blocking federal proposals (3 cantons can prevent supermajority); withholding federal contributions
**Strength**: The Compact itself protects canton autonomy; they're defending the status quo
**Weakness**: Cannot address threats that require coordination (military, Covenant infiltration)

## Key Political Tensions

### Contract Exploitation vs. Contract Sanctity
- The system's moral crisis: contracts that are technically legal but substantively unjust
- Reformers argue: exploitative contracts violate the spirit of the Compact
- Conservatives argue: a contract freely signed is a contract; judging "spirit" is subjective
- The Arbitration Court is caught between legal consistency and moral reality
- **Outcome pending**: How this tension resolves determines the Federation's future

### Federal Authority vs. Canton Autonomy
- Every external crisis (pirates, Covenant, Valdimere threats) creates demand for federal action
- But federal action requires supermajority — achievable ~once per year
- Individual cantons act unilaterally when federal response is too slow (Portmund precedent)
- **Paradox**: The Federation's greatest strength (individual liberty) is its greatest weakness (collective paralysis)

### Military Coordination
- No standing federal army; defense by contracted canton militias and mercenaries
- Authorization for military action requires 6+ weeks of Assembly debate
- By the time authorization is granted, the threat has often resolved — usually badly
- **Reform proposal**: Federal rapid-response force (blocked by autonomists as federal overreach)

### Covenant Legal Infiltration
- The existential threat that the system cannot address
- Covenant agents operate legally; their contracts are impeccable; they're members of the guilds
- Expelling them requires breaking contracts — which undermines the entire system
- Investigating them requires proving contract fraud — but their contracts are genuine
- **The perfect trap**: The system protects the mechanism of its own subordination

## Political Decision-Making

### Process
1. Issue raised in Federal Assembly by canton delegate
2. Committee review (1-4 weeks)
3. Assembly debate (weeks to months)
4. Supermajority vote (requires 10 of 14 cantons)
5. If passed: cantons implement (each canton may interpret differently)
6. If failed: issue returns to committee or is abandoned
7. Arbitration Court involvement only for contract disputes, not policy

### Timeline
- Routine decisions: 2-6 weeks
- Significant policy: 3-12 months
- Constitutional amendment: Years to never
- Emergency response: 6+ weeks (by which time it's usually too late)

### Failure Mode
The system is designed for deliberation, not action. It handles peacetime commerce well (contracts, arbitration, trade). It fails at: military threats, systemic infiltration, structural reform, and anything that requires speed.

# Valdimere — Politics & Power Dynamics

## The Political Hierarchy (Practical Reality)

### Layer 1: The Imperial Court
- Emperor/empress wields absolute theoretical authority
- Cabinet of advisors (often competing factions)
- Imperial family members jockey for influence
- Court intrigue: assassinations, poisonings, strategic marriages common
- **Reality**: Strong emperor can govern; weak emperor = court runs itself

### Layer 2: Regional Governors
- Appointed by emperor; loyalty reinforced through magical auditing
- Tasked with: collecting taxes, maintaining order, reporting to crown
- Actual power: Enormous (can exempt allies, crush dissidents)
- Corruption level: Varies; most run side operations
- **Reality**: Governors de facto independent; emperor's remote authority paper-thin

### Layer 3: Military Command Structure
- Separate from civilian governance; reports to emperor
- Lodges run locally by commanders with significant autonomy
- Military vs. civilian governor conflicts common
- Military budgets consume 30-40% of state revenue

### Layer 4: Guild Leadership
- Master merchants and craft guild heads
- Limited direct political power; significant economic influence
- Negotiate with governors/crown over taxation
- Collective action possible but rare (guilds compete more than unite)

## Current Political Factions

### The Centralists
- **Goal**: Strengthen imperial authority over regions
- **Method**: Increase bureaucratic oversight, reduce governor autonomy
- **Power base**: Court nobility, elite administrators, young military officers
- **Weakness**: Opposition from entrenched power (governors, marcher lords)
- **Story role**: Centralists push for standardization that obscures Covenant

### The Regionalists
- **Goal**: Maintain/expand local autonomy
- **Method**: Resist imperial mandates, build independent power bases
- **Power base**: Governors, marcher lords, local merchants
- **Weakness**: No unified agenda; each region has different goals
- **Story role**: Regional power brokers can be bribed or blackmailed

### The Militarists
- **Goal**: Expand Valdimerian territory/influence through force
- **Method**: Increase military spending, justify through external threats
- **Power base**: Military command, young nobility seeking glory
- **Weakness**: Costly; fiscal unsustainability
- **Story role**: War hawks are easy Covenant recruitment (promise glory/resources)

### The Conservatives
- **Goal**: Maintain status quo; resist change
- **Method**: Bureaucratic obstruction, tradition emphasis
- **Power base**: Elderly nobility, established guild masters, scholars
- **Weakness**: Paralyzed by internal disputes; slow to act
- **Story role**: Conservatives enable Covenant by refusing to acknowledge threats

### The Reformers (Rare)
- **Goal**: Address corruption, inequality, inefficiency
- **Method**: Challenge existing structures
- **Power base**: Young officials, progressive guild masters, intellectuals
- **Weakness**: Isolated; easily crushed by established powers
- **Story role**: Potential allies to resistance; often targeted for silencing

## Key Political Tensions

### Imperial Authority vs. Regional Autonomy
- Crown wants centralized control; governors resist
- Compromise: Governors maintain autonomy if taxes flow smoothly
- Tension point: Economic crisis forces crown to tighten control

### Military vs. Civil Authority
- Military wants larger budget; civilians see waste
- Result: Competing for same tax base
- Covenant exploits: Exaggerates external threats to increase military budget

### Traditional Power vs. Economic Reality
- Nobility hold political power; wealth concentrating in merchant class
- Wealthy merchants resent lack of political voice
- Guild masters want seats at decision table
- System unsustainable; revolution brewing

### Innovation vs. Standardization
- Standardization = stability, predictability, control
- Innovation = risk, unpredictability, threat to established order
- Covenant benefits: Standardization makes them harder to detect

## Key Figures & Power Players

| Figure | Faction | Power Base | Vulnerability |
|--------|---------|-----------|---|
| Governor of [Heartland Region] | Regionalist | Control of capital region | Dependent on military support |
| Lord Commander of [Military Region] | Militarist | Military forces | Budget constraints; needs political support |
| High Master Merchant | Economic | Wealth + guild networks | Vulnerable to political pressure |
| Chief of Imperial Magistrates | Centralist | Legal authority | Dependent on emperor's support |
| Court Advisor [Hidden Covenant Agent] | Centralist | Emperor's ear | Exposed if discovered |

## Political Cycles & Patterns

### Election/Succession Events
- Emperor dies → 2-3 year struggle for succession
- New governor appointed → local nobles maneuver for influence
- Military commander replaced → competing factions reorganize

### Predictable Conflicts
- Spring: Tax collection crises (shortfalls become apparent)
- Summer: Military campaigns justify budget increases
- Fall: Harvest determines budget surplus/deficit
- Winter: Court intrigue (weather restricts external conflict)

### Tipping Points
- **Now**: Economic crisis brewing; tensions mounting
- **Within 5 years**: Fiscal collapse possible; war likely
- **10+ years**: System collapse or revolutionary transformation

## The Covenant's Political Edge

**Advantage**: Operates invisibly within existing structures

They don't *fight* political factions; they *work through* them:
- Finance centralists (increase standardization)
- Bribe regionalists (ensure their silence)
- Support militarists (justify wars Covenant orchestrates)
- Paralyze reformers (eliminate them strategically)
- Feed conservatives (make them feel comfortable, protect status quo)

Result: **All factions believe the system serves their interests while Covenant hollows it from inside.**

## Story Political Sequence

1. **Chapters 1-12 (Act I)**: Aisen observes politics from tavern perspective (governors, trade conflicts, corruption)
2. **Chapters 13-22 (Act II early)**: Academy politics (which faction controls the school?)
3. **Chapters 23-35 (Act II late)**: Resistance discovers political conspiracy (Covenant's coordination of factions)
4. **Chapters 36-43 (Act III)**: War breaks out (which faction declared it? Why?)
5. **Chapters 44-52 (Post-war)**: Survivors must rebuild political system (how do they avoid old patterns?)
