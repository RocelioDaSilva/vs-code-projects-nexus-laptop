The professor taught modern ritual theory and smiled as if scholarship alone could fix the world. Aisen noticed the small things: appointments made at odd hours, an insistence on certain books being removed from the shelf during closed hours. 

When Aisen followed a trail of receipts and quiet conversations, he found the thin seam between public teaching and private procurement. The professor requested ritual texts that had no obvious academic use and asked Tholen for copies with a casual tone that implied authority. 

It was a small discovery but dangerous: this faculty member was a node in something larger. Aisen cataloged details, not accusations—who took what, when, and where. He began leaving questions in the margin of his notebooks; the professor's pattern fit too well with the Covenants' earlier recruitment methods. 

The truth lurked in the quiet paperwork.

Professor Edrik Thane taught modern ritual theory on Tuesdays and Thursdays in the third-floor lecture hall of the academy's northern wing, and he was, by every metric the institution used to evaluate its faculty, unremarkable.

This was the first thing Aisen noticed about him—not an inconsistency but the conspicuous quality of its absence. In a faculty populated by specialists who wore their disciplines like plumage—Maren with her clinical precision, Vellan with his controlled theatricality, the combat instructors with their scarred knuckles and predatory awareness—Thane occupied a register so temperate, so precisely calibrated to the median, that he passed through the academy's social architecture the way water passes through sand: touching everything, adhering to nothing, leaving behind only the faintest evidence that he had been there. He was forty-seven or fifty-two or somewhere in between, the kind of age that eludes precise estimation not because the face gives contradictory signals but because it gives too few signals of any kind—well-maintained, cleanly shaven, the brown hair trimmed to a length that was neither fashionable nor unfashionable but simply appropriate, the institutional equivalent of a room kept to temperature. He wore the faculty's dark wool coat without modification, the collar neither raised against cold nor loosened for comfort but resting as it had been designed to rest, as though the garment had come to him already fitted to the contours of someone who needed no adjustments.

Aisen had been watching him for eleven days.

The notebook recorded the initial observation on the facing page of Naia's entry—not because the two were connected but because the notebook's chronology was the chronology of Aisen's attention, and his attention had moved from Naia to Thane the way a surveyor's eye moves from the broken ground to the structure responsible for the breaking: upward, toward the source. Three days after the healing practicum, a Tuesday, Aisen had been seated in the second row of the ritual theory lecture—a class he attended not for its content, which he could have absorbed from the course text in an evening, but for the proximity it provided to the faculty's operational patterns. Thane had been lecturing on the theoretical basis of binding circles—the geometry of containment, the mathematical relationships between circle radius and Flux density that determined whether a binding held or failed—and the lecture had been competent, organised, delivered with the measured pace of someone who had given the same material enough times to have smoothed it into a performance that required minimal cognitive effort. A good lecture. An adequate lecture. The kind of lecture that a student could attend for an entire term and remember nothing about the person who had delivered it.

Except.

At the forty-minute mark, a knock had interrupted the lecture. A young man in the undecorated grey of an administrative aide opened the door, spoke two sentences that Aisen could not hear from the second row, and left. Thane had excused himself—politely, with the practised ease of a man accustomed to the minor interruptions of institutional life—stepped into the corridor for ninety seconds, and returned. He resumed the lecture at the precise sentence where he had been interrupted. No hesitation, no consultation of notes, no orienting pause. The continuity was seamless.

And wrong.

Because in the ninety seconds of Thane's absence, Aisen had counted—a reflex now, like breathing, the automatic measurement of duration that his years in the tavern and on the street had installed in his nervous system as permanently as the ability to read a room's exits or calculate the weight distribution of a man about to throw a punch. Ninety seconds was nothing. But when Thane returned, his right hand carried the faint scent of seal wax—the particular tallow-and-resin formula used not by the academy's internal correspondence office but by the external courier service that handled communications between the institution and its governing bodies. Aisen knew the difference because Rin had described it to him six weeks ago, a detail extracted from her cataloguing of the academy's logistical infrastructure: internal correspondence used beeswax seals, warm and honeyed; external correspondence used tallow-resin seals, sharp and mineral, with a trace of pine pitch that clung to the fingers and the nose.

Thane had opened an external letter in ninety seconds. Read it. Resealed or disposed of it. Returned. And none of this was remarkable—faculty received external correspondence; it was not prohibited, not unusual, not suspicious in isolation. But the knock had not come during Thane's office hours. It had not come during the intervals between classes when mail was customarily distributed. It had come mid-lecture, from an administrative aide whose grey clothing bore no departmental insignia, which meant either the aide served the general administration or the aide served no department at all.

Aisen had written in the notebook that evening: *Thane. External correspondence. Mid-class delivery. Aide unmarked. Seal wax: tallow-resin, external service. Follow.*

---

Over the next eight days, the notebook's margins accumulated.

Monday. Thane entered the faculty office corridor at seven-twelve in the morning, forty-eight minutes before his earliest scheduled obligation. The corridor's eastern door required a faculty key; the western door, which opened onto the restricted archive wing, required a key that Aisen had seen issued to only three faculty members during his months of hallway observation. Thane used the western door. He emerged from the eastern door thirty-one minutes later, carrying nothing visible, the set of his coat unchanged, his pace the same as it had been entering. The archive wing's western door was typically used by the historiography department for access to sealed record collections—land surveys, taxation rolls, census documents from the pre-Covenant administrative period that the academy maintained as research materials. Aisen had not been able to determine what Thane filed or retrieved from those archives, but the visit fell on the same day as the previous week's visit, the same approximate time, a pattern assembling itself in the spaces between ordinary actions.

Wednesday. Fourth bell. Aisen was crossing the central quadrangle on his way to the library when he saw Thane on the eastern colonnade, walking beside a man he had never seen within the academy's walls. The man wore a travelling coat—road-worn, the hem muddied to mid-calf in the red clay that characterized the border road rather than the grey chalk soil of the local paths—and carried no insignia, no badge, no visible institutional affiliation. He was older than Thane by perhaps a decade, heavyset, with the bearing of someone accustomed to authority exercised outside of academic settings—the set of the shoulders, the economy of gesture, the way he occupied the colonnade as though the architecture were built around him rather than the reverse. They walked slowly. Thane's head was inclined toward the visitor at an angle consistent with attentive listening. The visitor spoke in a continuous, low register that did not carry across the quadrangle. At the colonnade's eastern terminus, where the covered walkway gave onto the steps leading to the administrative building, they stopped. The visitor placed his hand on Thane's shoulder—not a pat, not a casual touch, but a placement: deliberate, sustained for two seconds, the kind of contact that communicated authority to body rather than to language. Then the visitor descended the steps, crossed the lower courtyard, and disappeared through the postern gate that connected the academy's grounds to the road.

Aisen had remained in the quadrangle, seated on the stone bench beneath the elm, his open book providing the appearance of a student reading in the afternoon light while his eyes tracked Thane's face in the moment after the visitor departed. The expression was minute and involuntary—a compression of the lips that lasted less than a second before the faculty mask resettled into place, the unremarkable composure closing over whatever had surfaced like water closing over a dropped stone. It had been too brief to interpret with certainty. But Aisen had seen compressions like it before—in the faces of men who had received instructions they did not relish from people they could not refuse.

Thursday. Ritual theory lecture. Thane distributed the term's reading list—a standard document, the same texts listed in the course prospectus, with one addition. At the bottom, below the required material, a single supplementary reference: *Flux Saturation Thresholds in Institutional Populations: A Statistical Overview*. Aisen copied the title into the notebook. The text was not in the library's general catalogue. He checked that evening, moving through the card index with the patient thoroughness of habit, pulling every entry under *Flux* and every cross-reference under *saturation* and *threshold* and *institutional*. Nothing. The text existed, if it existed at all, in a collection that the library's public index did not include.

A text about breaking points. Distributed to students by a professor who visited restricted archives weekly and received external visitors who touched his shoulder with the grammar of command.

Friday. Aisen positioned himself in the third-floor corridor outside the faculty offices at the close of the instructional day, ostensibly waiting for a consultation with another professor whose office shared the hallway. The real consultation was with proximity. At five-eighteen, Thane exited his office, locked the door with the deliberate turn-and-check of someone conscious of the lock's function, and walked not toward the faculty residences on the western hill but toward the administrative building's ground floor—toward the wing that housed the Registrar's office and, behind it, accessible through a door that bore no label, the room that Rin had identified three months earlier as the communications nexus where outgoing correspondence was sealed, coded, and dispatched to destinations that did not appear on any routing document available to students or junior faculty.

He was inside for fourteen minutes. Aisen timed it against his pulse—sixty-eight beats per minute, his resting rate, nine hundred and fifty-two beats total, fourteen minutes exactly—because pulling out a timepiece in a corridor where he had no reason to be lingering would have been the kind of conspicuous precision that turned a boy waiting for a consultation into a boy conducting surveillance.

When Thane emerged, he walked past Aisen's position. Three feet of distance. Close enough that Aisen could smell the tallow-resin seal wax again—on his left hand this time, the fingers that would have pressed a seal into correspondence, the residue consistent with sending rather than receiving. And beneath the wax, fainter, the metallic tang of the coded ink that Rin had described from her own intelligence work: iron gall modified with a mineral additive that rendered it unreadable without the corresponding reagent, one of the Covenant's standard communication security measures.

Thane's eyes passed over Aisen the way they passed over the corridor walls, the window frames, the stone floor—a gaze that registered surfaces without penetrating them. Or so it appeared. Aisen held his position, his face angled toward the professor whose office he was ostensibly waiting for, his body turned thirty degrees away from Thane's line of travel, the practiced geometry of apparent disinterest that he had perfected in years of watching people who must not know they were being watched.

But in the half-second before Thane's gaze moved on, it paused. The pause was fractional, the duration of a blink, a hesitation so small that Aisen might have imagined it—except he did not imagine these things; his nervous system catalogued them with a fidelity that precluded invention. Thane's eyes touched Aisen's position and held for three-tenths of a second longer than the walls had received—a differential that would have been meaningless from anyone else but that, from a man whose entire presentation was calibrated to the median, constituted a deviation significant enough to register in the notebook that night as: *Thane. Corridor, 5:32pm. Possible notice. Three-tenths.*

Three-tenths of a second, and the temperature of the corridor dropped by degrees that had nothing to do with the evening air.

---

Saturday. The library.

Aisen had claimed a carrel in the southeast corner of the second floor—the position that afforded sightlines to both the main staircase and the restricted access corridor while remaining sufficiently recessed to be overlooked by anyone who was not specifically looking for a person positioned to observe without being observed. The carrel was occupying him for the fourth consecutive Saturday, long enough that the librarian on weekend duty had ceased to notice him, the way people cease to notice furniture after the first week.

He was reading the course text on binding theory—or appearing to read it, the pages turning at intervals calibrated to the reading speed of a diligent student, the physical performance of study masking the actual work, which was the notebook open beneath the text. Three pages now dedicated to Thane. Times, dates, locations, details. The sealed correspondence. The restricted archive visits—every Monday, between seven-twelve and seven-forty-three, through the western door. The external visitor—one documented instance, probable recurrence, pending confirmation. The supplementary reading list entry. The coded ink. The corridor pause.

Individually: unremarkable. Faculty conducted administrative business; faculty corresponded externally; faculty hosted visitors; faculty added readings. Each datum was innocent in isolation, the way each thread in a net is merely a string until you step back far enough to see the mesh.

Aisen did not step back. He leaned in. The notebook was not a net; it was an archive, and the archive's purpose was not interpretation but preservation—the gathering of facts in their raw, unprocessed form so that the pattern, when it emerged, would emerge from the evidence rather than from the investigator's desire to find one. He had learned this discipline from Harald's bookkeeping in the tavern years, where the daily receipts were entered without comment or analysis, and the story of the business—what sold, what didn't, what changed with the seasons—told itself through accumulated numbers rather than through the narrative imposed upon them.

The staircase produced footsteps. Aisen's pencil continued its apparent note-taking, the page-turn cadence unbroken, while his peripheral vision resolved the approaching figure into recognition: Thane. Faculty coat, unchanged. Pace, unhurried. Direction, not toward the general stacks but toward the restricted access corridor—the same corridor that led to the same archive wing he visited on Mondays, except today was Saturday and the restricted wing was officially closed on weekends.

He carried a key.

Not the faculty key—Aisen had memorised that shape, the brass ward-key with the institutional crest that opened every faculty door. This key was smaller, iron, without markings. He did not wear it on the faculty key ring. He produced it from an interior pocket with the shielded movement of a hand that was accustomed to concealing its contents—the motion performed below the line of the library tables, invisible to anyone not positioned, as Aisen was positioned, at an angle that turned concealment into display.

Thane entered the restricted corridor. The door closed behind him. The lock engaged with a sound that the library's ambient silence made audible from Aisen's carrel: the discrete, metallic intimacy of a mechanism consenting to the right key.

Forty seconds. Aisen closed the notebook. Closed the text. Rose from the carrel with the unhurried movements of a student concluding a study session. Walked to the staircase. Descended. Crossed the library's ground floor. Exited through the eastern door that opened onto the colonnade.

He did not follow Thane into the restricted corridor. The impulse was there—sharp, electric, the animal pull of the chase—but the calculus forbade it. A student in a closed restricted wing on a Saturday had no cover story. The risk-to-information ratio collapsed when the observed party occupied a controlled space with only one exit. And there was the three-tenths of a second in the corridor, the fractional pause of eyes that might or might not have noticed, and the catastrophic asymmetry of the investigation's stakes: if Aisen was wrong about Thane, the cost of discovery was embarrassment; if Aisen was right about Thane, the cost of discovery was the kind of consequence that visited students who came too close to the machinery that Maren and Vellan operated—not breaking, not the practicum's direct violence, but something administrative, invisible, a reclassification of his status within the institution that would close doors he had not yet found.

He walked to the elm in the quadrangle and sat.

The evening air carried the scent of the lime trees along the western wall—their late-season bloom releasing a sweetness that Aisen associated, against his will, with the academy's better qualities, the beauty of the grounds that existed alongside and contrary to the institution's other functions, the way a garden could grow in the courtyard of a prison without the flowers constituting either endorsement or resistance. An owl called from the chapel eaves, the first of the evening's voices, its cry carrying across the quadrangle in a clean arc that connected the institutional stone with the living dusk. Beneath the elm, the ground was cool, and Aisen sat with the notebook in his lap and wrote in the fading light, the pencil moving by feel as much as sight, the entry for the day's final observation: *Thane. Library restricted wing. Saturday, closed day. Iron key, no markings. Interior pocket. Duration TBD. Second archive visit this week. Pattern: Monday standard, Saturday supplemental. Possible regular intelligence cycle?*

He underlined the question mark. Then, beneath it, he wrote: *Advancement Program reading list entry—Flux Saturation Thresholds. Text not in general catalogue. Not in restricted catalogue either (checked via Rin's access). Text may not exist as publication. May be internal Covenant document distributed through faculty channels. If so: Thane has access to Covenant operational material.*

---

Rin found him at the elm. She materialised from the colonnade's shadow the way she always materialised—without threshold, without approach, one moment absent and the next present, as though the distance between her and any given point were a formality she declined to observe. The star-marks on her forearms pulsed faintly in the dusk, silver against skin, the low-frequency glow that indicated baseline vigilance rather than active operancy. She sat beside him on the bench without greeting, which was itself a communication—Rin did not omit pleasantries from forgetfulness but from efficiency, the social preamble excised when the content was urgent.

"The visitor," she said. "Wednesday. Red clay on the hem."

Aisen waited. Rin's intelligence deliveries had a structure: the anchoring detail first, to establish shared context; the new information second, delivered in the compressed form that her mind preferred and that Aisen had learned to decompress without asking her to slow down.

"His name is Aldren Voss. Third-tier Covenant administrative liaison, assigned to the Office of Institutional Compliance." She let the title settle. Third-tier was not high—not a decision-maker, not a leader—but the Office of Institutional Compliance was the body responsible for ensuring that the academy operated within the Covenant's policy framework. It was the mechanism through which the Covenant's priorities became the academy's practices. "He visits quarterly. Has done for at least three years—I found travel reimbursement records in the Registrar's filed expenses going back that far. The reimbursements are coded to a budget line called *Curricular Alignment Review*."

Curricular alignment. The words carried the antiseptic precision of institutional language—language designed to describe a function without revealing its content, the way a door labelled *Maintenance* could open onto anything from a broom closet to a monitoring station. Curricular alignment could mean reviewing syllabi for consistency. It could also mean ensuring that the Advancement Program's testing protocols—the breaking of Soren, the aging of Naia—conformed to whatever metrics the Covenant had established for the measurement of student limits.

"Thane is Voss's on-site contact," Rin continued. Her voice carried no inflection, the flat delivery of verified intelligence, but her hands—resting on her knees, the marks pulsing—were still in the way that Rin's hands became still when she was presenting something she considered significant. "I traced the external correspondence routing. The coded dispatches from the communications room—the ones you smelled the ink on—go to the Office of Institutional Compliance. Not to the academy's board. Not to the Covenant's educational directorate. To Compliance. Thane is filing reports with Voss's office on a biweekly cycle."

Biweekly. Mondays and supplemental Saturdays. The archive visits aligned—the regular gathering of data on Monday, the supplemental filing on Saturday, the intelligence cycle that Aisen had hypothesised in the notebook confirmed by Rin's independent channel.

"What is he reporting on?" Aisen asked, though the architecture of the answer was already visible in his mind, the way the shape of a building becomes visible in its scaffolding before the walls go up.

Rin looked at him. In the dusk, her eyes held the particular intensity that preceded the moments when she allowed certainty to enter her voice—Rin, who trafficked in probabilities and qualifications, who hedged her intelligence with caveats because precision demanded it, reached a threshold past which the caveats fell away and what remained was the clean line of verified fact. "Students," she said. "Performance data. Practicum outcomes. Breaking points."

The word hung between them like the owl's cry—clean, specific, carrying across the institutional stone.

"He is not an instructor who happens to correspond with the Covenant," Rin said. "He is a Covenant intelligence officer embedded in the faculty. His teaching position is cover. His function is monitoring and reporting. The Advancement Program doesn't just have faculty support—it has dedicated operational oversight. Thane is the node. Everything Maren does, everything Vellan does, the practicums, the testing—it goes through Thane's reports to Voss's office. He's the reason the programme runs consistently. Not because the faculty are individually cruel. Because a compliance officer is ensuring they follow protocol."

Aisen exhaled. The breath carried something he had not realised he was holding—not surprise, because the notebook's accumulated evidence had already described this shape, but the particular weight of confirmation, the moment when suspicion becomes knowledge and the world reorganises itself around the new fact the way water reorganises around a stone. The academy did not merely tolerate the Advancement Program. The academy did not merely look away while ambitious instructors pushed students to destruction. The academy was instrumented. Monitored. The breaking was not incidental to the institution; it was reported, reviewed, assessed by a bureaucracy whose operational language was *curricular alignment* and whose operational function was the systematic measurement of how much damage the students could absorb.

The elm's branches moved in the evening air. Somewhere in the faculty residences, a light came on—a yellow square in the dark stone, domestic, ordinary, the light of someone preparing for an evening that did not include the knowledge that one of their colleagues filed intelligence reports about the cost extracted from students in closed practicums.

Aisen opened the notebook. He turned to the page dedicated to Thane—the eleven days of accumulated observation, the times and dates and corridor measurements—and at the bottom, below the last entry, he wrote:

*Confirmed: Professor Edrik Thane, faculty member, modern ritual theory. Function: Covenant compliance officer, embedded. Reports to: Aldren Voss, Office of Institutional Compliance, third-tier liaison. Reporting cycle: biweekly. Content: student performance data, practicum outcomes, limit-testing results. Role: operational oversight of Advancement Program testing protocol. Status: active.*

He looked at the words. They were precise and complete and they described a man who woke each morning in the faculty residences, ate breakfast in the common hall, lectured competently on binding circles and Flux geometry, smiled at students who asked questions after class, and then walked to the communications room and filed reports that catalogued how far a healer could be pushed before her hair turned white and how much force a combat channeller could sustain before his hands began to shake.

And the worst part—the part that Aisen wrote last, in the small hand that required proximity to read, the script reserved for the observations that cut deepest—was that Thane was not a monster. Monsters were simple. Monsters could be identified by the horror they produced and the pleasure they took in it. Thane produced no horror and took no pleasure. He was a man performing a function within a system he believed was necessary—a system that monitored student limits because unmonitored limits led to uncontrolled outcomes, because the Covenant's institutional logic held that measurement was preferable to ignorance, that the cost of not knowing what students could survive was higher than the cost of finding out. He was, in his own understanding, a safeguard. A man who ensured that the testing was systematic rather than arbitrary, that the breaking followed protocol rather than whim, that Naia's aging and Soren's tremor were recorded and reviewed rather than lost to institutional amnesia.

He believed in the system. That was what made him dangerous—not his malice, which did not exist, but his competence in the service of a logic that had made the destruction of students into an administrative function, a budget line, a quarterly compliance review. The evil was not in the man. The evil was in the ordinary.

Aisen closed the notebook. The pencil went into the spine. The notebook went into the inner pocket, against the ribs, where Soren's pages and Naia's pages rested now alongside Thane's—the archive growing, the weight of evidence accumulating in the space over his heart.

"What now?" Rin asked. The question was not rhetorical. Rin did not ask rhetorical questions—she asked operational ones, questions whose answers shaped the next action, the next movement of the network she and Aisen were building in the institution's margins.

"We document," Aisen said. "Every report cycle. Every visit. Every dispatch. We build the record of what they're reporting and who they're reporting to, until the record is complete enough that it describes the entire chain—from the practicum floor to the Covenant's compliance office—without gaps."

"And then?"

The owl called again. The lime trees breathed their sweetness into the dark. The academy's lights multiplied in the faculty windows—squares of yellow warmth, the domestic geography of people going about their evenings, reading, eating, grading papers, filing intelligence reports with the same hand that marked student essays.

"Then we have something they can't reclassify," Aisen said. "Something they can't call an *overexertion event* or *self-harm*. We have the structure. Not an accusation—a blueprint."

He stood. The elm's shadow stretched across the quadrangle in the last of the light, reaching for the colonnade where Thane had walked with Voss three days ago, the professor's head inclined in attentive listening, the visitor's hand on his shoulder carrying the grammar of command. The academy's stone was cold beneath the evening sky, and the institution rose around Aisen as it always rose—massive, indifferent, built to outlast the individual lives it processed—and within its walls a man who was not a monster sat in his office and compiled the data of damage with the quiet diligence of a clerk recording the day's receipts.

Aisen walked toward the dormitory. The notebook pressed flat against his ribs, warm from the proximity of his body, its pages holding the first complete map of a chain that he now understood extended from the closed practicum rooms where students broke to the floors of bureaucrats who would never see a student's face. The chain was not hidden. It was not secret, not exactly—it was administrative, procedural, filed and coded and stamped and dispatched along channels that existed in plain sight for anyone who knew where to look and what to smell and how to count the seconds of a pause.

Three-tenths, and the whole shape of the thing visible in the gap.
