Summary:

Thorne kept his cloak wrapped tight about him although the tavern's hearth pushed warmth toward the tables. The leather of his travel satchel was soft where his hand rested on the strap; coins, receipts, and a little folded map pressed into the leather's belly. He ordered two ales at once—an old man's habit for showing business to those around him—and drank one slowly while he watched the road.

Merchants taught you certain ways of reading a room. Watch the way a man rests his elbow; watch what he hides in his boot; listen to the way he counts a purse. Thorne had learned that on roads where trust was currency, a false smile could cost you more than a crooked scale.

"You're taking the north route tomorrow?" Marta asked as she set another plate near his hand. The question was small and practical; she was the kind of person who asked because details mattered.

Thorne shook his head once. "No. Too many stoppages. Too many questions. I may go west, but not the pass. Too much talk about missing carts, and that's never good." He spoke low as a man with a ledger of consequences in his head.

At his elbow, Aisen fidgeted with a scrap of cloth, watching with the careful attention of someone born into a tavern. There was a way the boy leaned forward when conversation meant change. Thorne's eyes flicked to him for half a breath and then away, the recognition of a merchant who'd seen the way children could notice trade patterns.

"Disappeared?" Marta pressed. It was the word everyone feared but spoke in small doses. In the tavern world, 'disappeared' was shorthand for ruin: wagons that never returned, caravans that never reached market, families left without news or coin.

Thorne set his ale down as if that single action could steady the night. "Two merchants didn't leave the last town. Hotel keeper says they wiped their ledger and sent no one after them. Another caravan took a detour and swore they were asked to show papers by officers who weren't the usual ones. I heard a story about a patrol asking whether wagons came from the north and then taking a sample of cloth off a cart to 'test for contraband.'"

Marta's hands paused. The cutler in the corner glanced up from his drink. The tavern seemed to shrink a degree around the conversation; people pressed against the edge of silence.

Thorne kept speaking, the words gentle but precise—merchants were trained to be careful with rumors. "It's not the city watch. These men have ribbons on their sleeves. Different collars, cleaner boots. They ask specific questions about which markets you trade in and who pays for your permits. If you can't show the right stamp or the right name on a contract, they'll hold you. Sometimes they take goods for 'inspection,' and the goods never return."

He tapped his satchel as if to emphasize the fragility of what it contained. "I've been on this road twenty-three years. You learn to read not only the faces but the spaces between them. A patrol asking for papers isn't new. A patrol that asks to inspect the crate of salted pork and doesn't give it back—that's different."

The word 'Covenant' had weight around it. It was a name held with different emotions by different people: fear, respect, resignation. Thorne used it because that was the shorthand merchants used when they wanted to hint at a force that moved in the halls of power.

"You plan to avoid it?" Father said.

Thorne shrugged. "I alter my route, take a smaller toll road and sell for less at the next market. Less profit, less attention. Safer than a full cart stopped at the pass. A man learns to eat less spice when the road tastes sour."

He sat back and watched Aisen sweep under the table. "That boy keeps his eyes open," Thorne said quietly. "You should watch him, that one. A good observer is worth more than an extra hand on nights like these."

Marta's chin lifted. "He's steady."

"He looks like he'll be steady in things that matter—watching, learning. If he keeps his head, he'll make wisdom out of scraps. That's the thing you want most on a road that chews merchants up."

Thorne folded a thin sheet of paper from his ledger and slid it across the table—someone else's practice of paying in notes until the market opened. "If you want to move goods later, do it quietly. Keep receipts and names. Avoid any man who smiles too much and asks too many questions about where you trade."

He stood to leave, cloak drawn close. "I'll go the west road. If I don't return next market, you'll know I found better trade or worse trouble. Either way, keep the kettle on. I'll leave a coin if I can."

As he shouldered out into the night, the tavern door closing behind him with the small, decisive thud that meant someone had chosen a course. Marta watched his back until the lantern swallowed him and then turned to the ledger again. The math of the day resumed: coin in, coin out, promises to hold. For now, their best defense was the slow, stubborn work of staying open and knowing who passed through. But Thorne's shadow at the door touched the room like a warning: the road was narrowing, and where it narrowed, men who traded in silence prospered.
