# Andean Alliance — Class Hierarchy & Social Structure

## The Altitude System

Social structure in the Andean Alliance is inseparable from altitude. Where you live determines your role, your status, and your identity. The system is not a hierarchy in the Valdimerian or Federation sense — it's a vertical ecology where each band is theoretically essential and interdependent. In practice, altitude carries prestige.

### Three-Band System

| Band | Altitude | Population | Identity | Status |
|------|----------|-----------|----------|--------|
| **Urqu (Peak)** | Above 4,000m | ~15% | Crystal miners, high-altitude shamans, condor-keepers, astronomical observers | Highest prestige — proximity to sky = proximity to sacred |
| **Qucha (Terrace)** | 2,500–4,000m | ~40% | Herders, weavers, terrace farmers, mid-altitude artisans, most warriors | Core population — backbone of military and economy |
| **Yunka (Valley)** | Below 2,500m | ~45% | Agricultural workers, river fishers, traders, lowland artisans, port communities | Largest population, lowest prestige — proximity to outsiders = cultural contamination |

## Within-Band Relations

### Egalitarian Principle
Within each band, the Alliance practices genuine egalitarianism:
- No hereditary wealth accumulation (mit'a rotation prevents permanent advantage)
- Leadership rotates through qualified families (3-year terms)
- Resource sharing through ayni (reciprocal exchange) is mandatory
- Status comes from contribution, not birth

### Status Markers (Within-Band)
| Status | How Earned | Benefits | Limits |
|--------|-----------|----------|--------|
| **Community Elder** | Age + demonstrated wisdom + community consensus | Advisory role in band governance; respected voice | Cannot become permanent leader |
| **Shaman/Yachaq** | Spiritual calling + apprenticeship (10+ years) | Spiritual authority; immune from mit'a labor rotation | Cannot hold political office |
| **War Commander** | Military achievement + warrior council selection | Military authority during threat; peacetime advisory | Authority ends when threat ends |
| **Master Artisan** | Demonstrated excellence in craft + peer recognition | Teaching authority; resource priority for materials | Cannot exempt self from community obligations |

## Between-Band Relations

### The Altitude Hierarchy (Unacknowledged)
Officially, all three bands are equal. In practice:

**Peak communities** carry greatest prestige because:
- Crystal mining is sacred work (crystals = ancestral connection)
- High altitude = closer to sky spirits = spiritual superiority
- Smallest population = exclusive identity
- Condor-keeping is the most prestigious military role

**Terrace communities** are the functional core:
- Produce most warriors (defensive militia)
- Weaving tradition is the Alliance's most valued export
- Terrace farming feeds the nation
- Political center (capital Qhapaq Llaqta is at terrace altitude)

**Valley communities** carry lowest prestige:
- Contact with outsiders (Federation traders, Valdimerian diplomats) = cultural contamination
- Agricultural work is "simple" compared to crystal mining or weaving
- Yunka Hatun trade port is necessary but culturally suspect
- Modernization pressure comes from the valleys

### Inter-Band Tensions

| Tension | Between | Expression |
|---------|---------|-----------|
| Cultural purity vs. modernization | Peak/Terrace vs. Valley | Valley communities want trade access; Peak communities want isolation |
| Resource distribution | All bands | Peak claims more crystals than production justifies; Valley claims more food credit than labor warrants |
| Military deployment | Terrace warriors vs. Peak decision-makers | Terrace does the fighting; Peak makes the strategic decisions |
| Shamanic authority | Peak shamans vs. Valley healers | Peak claims all legitimate spiritual authority flows from altitude |

## Outsider Status

### Foreign Residents
- Almost none — the Alliance doesn't accept permanent foreign residents
- Temporary traders permitted at Yunka Hatun (valley trade port) only
- Diplomatic visitors hosted at Qhapaq Llaqta with strict movement restrictions
- No foreign ownership of land, resources, or permanent structures

### Returned Diaspora
- Alliance members who leave and return are viewed with suspicion
- Must undergo reintegration ceremony (purification, community reaffirmation)
- Never fully trusted again — "lowland air changes the lungs"
- Tupac's experience: returning from the Academy will mark him permanently

### Covenant Agents
- The few Covenant agents who have entered the Alliance operate through the Yunka trade port
- Disguised as Federation merchants; offer crystal analysis and mining tools
- Detected by shamans approximately 60% of the time
- Cultural isolation is the Alliance's best defense against infiltration

## Mobility Rules

| Movement | Possibility | Method | Frequency |
|----------|------------|--------|-----------|
| Upward (Valley → Terrace) | Possible but rare | Marriage into terrace family + community acceptance + 5-year probation | Once per generation per family |
| Upward (Terrace → Peak) | Very rare | Shamanic calling confirmed by Yachaq council + lifelong commitment | Spiritual vocation only |
| Downward (Peak → Terrace) | Uncommon | Voluntary; loss of peak status permanent | Shamanic failure or personal choice |
| Downward (Terrace → Valley) | Common enough | Economic pressure, marriage, trade opportunity | Viewed as social decline |
| Lateral (within band) | Free | Community transfer requires host community acceptance | Regular during mit'a rotation |

## Key Structural Problem

**Egalitarianism masks altitude hierarchy.** The Alliance's genuine within-band equality obscures the between-band status system. Peak communities claim equal standing while exercising disproportionate spiritual and strategic authority. Valley communities claim equal standing while being treated as culturally inferior. The terrace band holds the system together through military service and economic productivity — but resents both peaks and valleys.

The Covenant exploits this by offering valley communities modernization (trade, technology, medicine) that peak communities refuse to allow — driving a wedge between bands.

# Celestial Kingdom — Class Hierarchy & Social Structure

## The Five Tiers

### Tier 1: Senior Monks & Meditation Masters (2%)
**Population**: ~2% | **Power**: Absolute (spiritual + political)

- Lifetime dedication to meditation. Documented spiritual-magical attainments.
- Members of the Monastic Council (supreme governing body)
- Govern through moral authority rather than bureaucratic control
- Live simply but want for nothing — communities provide everything

**Characteristics**:
- Decades of continuous meditation practice (40+ years)
- Direct perception of Flux field structure (Clear Light level)
- Minimal entropy generation; near-perfect magical efficiency
- Veto power over all governance decisions

**Notable Traits**:
- Serene to the point of detachment from material reality
- Genuinely believe spiritual practice solves material problems
- Cannot be bribed (no material desires) but can be manipulated through spiritual vanity
- Often unaware of ground-level suffering (isolation at extreme altitude)

### Tier 2: Monastics — Full-Time Monks & Nuns (8%)
**Population**: ~8% | **Power**: High (institutional)

- Full-time spiritual practitioners; training in meditation, magical technique, philosophy
- Teachers, healers, arbitrators, scribes
- Some focused purely on practice; others serve community functions
- Reside in monastery complexes at high altitude

**Characteristics**:
- 10-30 years of practice (Focused Attention to Open Awareness levels)
- Educated in classical texts, philosophy, and magical theory
- Celibate; renounce property and family obligations
- Respected throughout society; their word carries institutional weight

**Notable Traits**:
- Genuine spiritual commitment (most entered voluntarily)
- Growing frustration among junior monastics who see reform as necessary
- Some secretly sympathize with lay assembly's development agenda
- Rin was trained in this tier before being assigned to intelligence operations

**Story Connection**: Rin's guilt comes from leaving this tier — uncertain whether monks are right about spiritual patience or wrong about material action.

### Tier 3: Sentinel Warriors & Lay Officials (5%)
**Population**: ~5% | **Power**: Moderate (functional authority)

**Sentinel Warriors** (~2,000 active):
- Warrior-monks: military service + spiritual practice (dual-path)
- Defend mountain passes, patrol borders, protect caravans
- Report to both Monastic Council (spiritual discipline) and Lay Assembly (material support)
- Burnout rate: 30% leave within 10 years (dual demands unsustainable)

**Lay Officials**:
- Civil administrators handling taxation, infrastructure, dispute resolution
- Respected but considered spiritually lesser than monastics
- Interface between monastic governance and lay communities
- Growing political awareness; increasingly aligned with reform agenda

**Characteristics**:
- Both groups caught between spiritual ideals and practical demands
- Sentinels trained in meditation-enhanced combat (calm-state time dilation)
- Lay officials increasingly frustrated by monastic veto on development

**Story Connection**: Sentinel burnout mirrors the broader failure of the spiritual bypass — demanding both spiritual perfection and material effectiveness from the same people.

### Tier 4: Lay Artisans, Farmers & Herders (80%)
**Population**: ~80% | **Power**: Minimal (individually); significant (collectively)

- Agricultural workers, herders, craftspeople, builders
- The productive base — "the hands of the mountain"
- Grow food, raise livestock (yak, mountain goat), build structures, weave textiles
- Many attend monastery teachings part-time but cannot commit fully

**Characteristics**:
- Hard physical labor at extreme altitude (3,000-5,000m)
- Cultural pride in practical work; respect for monastics mixed with growing resentment
- Limited political representation through Lay Assembly
- Heavily dependent on monastic distribution of resources

**Notable Traits**:
- Youth exodus accelerating (15% decline in 18-30 cohort over two generations)
- See monastery wealth (aerolith trade, foreign teaching fees) not reaching communities
- Increasingly willing to trade with outsiders despite monastic disapproval
- "We build the terraces; they sit on them and meditate"

### Tier 5: Foreign Visitors & Diaspora (5%)
**Population**: ~5% | **Power**: None (dependent on sponsorship)

- Traders, refugees, scholars from other nations
- Cannot hold property; must have a monastic sponsor
- Maximum stay without special permission: one season
- Some have lived in Kingdom for decades under perpetual "temporary" status

**Characteristics**:
- Tolerated but watched; spiritual contamination feared
- Valuable as trade intermediaries and knowledge carriers
- No political representation; appeals go through monastic sponsors
- Vulnerable to expulsion at any time

**Notable Traits**:
- Diaspora Celestials (those who left and returned) occupy awkward position
- Covenant agents enter through this tier (posing as scholars, traders)
- Some foreign monks genuinely devoted to Path of Clear Mind; still excluded from governance

## Mobility & Class Crossing

### Ascending
- **Tier 4→2**: Any person may enter monastic life (requires community release from labor obligations + monastery acceptance). Meritocratic in theory.
- **Tier 2→1**: Recognition by peers based on demonstrated spiritual attainment. No formal promotion — other masters simply acknowledge you as master.
- **Tier 5→4**: Community membership after 3-year residence + community vote. Rare but possible.
- **Tier 3→2**: Sentinels may retire into monastic life; lay officials rarely transition.

**Barriers**:
- Labor obligations (communities may refuse to release productive workers)
- Altitude adaptation (lowland visitors struggle physically at monastery elevations)
- Cultural fluency (foreign monks must learn language, customs, meditation techniques from scratch)
- Gender: technically equal access; practically, female monastics underrepresented in Council

### Descending
- Monastics who break vows return to Tier 4 (no shame; "the mountain needs all kinds of service")
- Sentinels who burn out return to community life (accepted but pitied)
- Foreign visitors expelled for violations; cannot appeal
- Masters do not descend — they simply become "retired masters" with continued respect

## Characteristic Attitudes by Tier

| Tier | To Tier Above | To Tier Below | To Authority |
|------|----------|------------|---|
| 1 | N/A | Compassionate detachment | Spiritual will; above material politics |
| 2 | Reverent; aspiring | Pastoral care; teaching | Obedient to Council; growing frustration |
| 3 | Respectful; professionally distant | Protective (Sentinels); bureaucratic (officials) | Caught between two masters |
| 4 | Mixed: respect + resentment | Competitive (survival) | Deferential publicly; critical privately |
| 5 | Cautious deference | N/A (already at bottom) | Dependent; anxious |

## Key Structural Problem

**Spiritual meritocracy masks material inequality**: The system appears open (anyone can become a monk → anyone can become a master) but functionally, Tier 4 communities cannot spare productive workers for decades of meditation. The path to spiritual authority requires material freedom that the productive base doesn't have. Result: governance by those most detached from material reality.

**The Rin Problem**: The most talented individuals leave (Rin, youth exodus) because the system offers them only one path: decades of meditation before any agency. The Celestial Kingdom is losing its future because its hierarchy measures value in patience, not action.

# Mercantile Federation — Class Hierarchy & Social Structure

## The Five Tiers

### Tier 1: Patriciate & Senior Guild Masters (3%)
**Population**: ~3% | **Power**: Absolute (economic + political)

- Old money families whose names appear on the Federal Compact (300 years ago)
- Control the largest trading houses, contract-vault access, and Arbitration Court appointments
- Hereditary wealth reinforced by interlocking contracts (marriage alliances, trade monopolies, property chains)
- Access to restricted magical infrastructure (contract-vault enchantments, banking magic)

**Characteristics**:
- Everything is contractual — family relationships, inheritance, political alliances
- Educated in private academies; fluent in contract law, multiple trade languages, magical theory
- Assume contract-literacy is universal (it isn't; this assumption enables exploitation)
- Intermarriage between patriciate families maintains concentrated wealth

**Notable Traits**:
- Genuinely believe the contract system is fair ("everyone signs freely")
- Cannot conceive of structural inequality because contracts are technically voluntary
- See contract-bound persons as having made poor choices, not as victims of system design
- Some privately acknowledge the system's cruelty; few act on that acknowledgment

### Tier 2: Guild Officers & Professional Class (12%)
**Population**: ~12% | **Power**: High (within guild domain)

- Guild masters, licensed arbitrators, senior merchants, physicians, engineers
- Economically comfortable; politically influential within their canton
- Own businesses or hold positions of institutional authority
- Access to guild-controlled magical services (infrastructure, banking, maritime)

**Characteristics**:
- Career advancement through guild hierarchy (apprentice → journeyman → master → guild officer)
- Political representation in canton governance and Federal Assembly
- Contract-literate; can negotiate effectively with patriciate
- Some serve as Arbitration Court judges (10-year terms)

**Notable Traits**:
- Genuinely meritocratic within guild system (skill matters)
- Blind to how guild membership requirements exclude Tiers 4-5
- Often resentful of patriciate privilege but dependent on patriciate trade networks
- The most politically active tier; drives cantonal policy

**Story Connection**: Pieter van der Berg's family operates at this tier. Federation merchant-prince's son who sees the system's contradictions from inside the guild establishment.

### Tier 3: Guild Members & Artisans (35%)
**Population**: ~35% | **Power**: Moderate (collective)

- Skilled workers organized into guilds; each guild has its own charter contract
- Economic security comes from guild membership; guild provides work, training, basic protections
- Pay guild dues (10-15% of earnings); receive access to contracts, tools, facilities
- Political representation only through guild (no individual civic standing)

**Characteristics**:
- Apprenticeship-based career path (3-7 years unpaid → journeyman → master attempt)
- Guild membership is the primary source of social identity
- Limited mobility between guilds (contracts restrict cross-training)
- Most productive economic tier; generates majority of commercial value

**Notable Traits**:
- Strong guild solidarity; mutual support within profession
- Suspicious of non-guild workers (who undercut regulated prices)
- Resentful of guild officers who enforce rules but don't do productive work
- The tier most likely to organize reform movements (collective bargaining experience)

### Tier 4: Laborers & Unaffiliated (40%)
**Population**: ~40% | **Power**: Minimal

- Workers not organized into guilds: manual laborers, seasonal workers, sailors, servants, dock workers
- No contract protections beyond basic citizenship (minimal rights)
- Free Ports attract many: employment without guild restrictions, but also without guild protections
- Employment is precarious; contracts are short-term, unfavorable, and non-negotiable

**Characteristics**:
- No political representation (guild-based governance excludes them)
- Most vulnerable to contract exploitation (can't afford legal counsel; can't read complex contracts)
- Mobile (no guild ties; follow work opportunities between cantons)
- Largest population tier but politically invisible

**Notable Traits**:
- Growing resentment of guild system ("we do the work; they get the protections")
- Freeport subculture: independent, creative, dangerous — the Federation's innovation engine
- Increasingly vulnerable to Covenant recruitment (offered guild-equivalent protections through Covenant membership)
- If mobilized politically, represent the Federation's largest revolutionary force

### Tier 5: Contract-Bound & Refugees (10%)
**Population**: ~10% | **Power**: Negative (legally subordinated)

**Contract-Bound** (~7%):
- People who signed exploitative contracts and are now legally obligated to serve
- Path: incur debt → default → court adjudicates → Life Contract imposed → bound to creditor
- Magically enforced: cannot leave, cannot refuse work, cannot negotiate terms
- NOT called slavery; called "contractual obligation." Functionally identical.
- Debt terms ensure repayment is nearly impossible; most Life Contracts are permanent

**Refugees** (~3%):
- Non-citizens without contracts; no legal standing
- Vulnerable to exploitation: any contract they sign binds them, but no one is obligated to offer fair terms
- Many sign Life Contracts out of desperation (food and shelter in exchange for permanent servitude)
- Path to citizenship requires guild membership or property — both require existing wealth

**Characteristics**:
- No political standing; cannot petition, vote, or access Arbitration Court
- Invisible to governance system (contracts track persons; uncontracted persons are invisible)
- Create underground mutual-aid networks for survival
- Children of contract-bound inherit parents' contractual obligations (generational bondage)

**Notable Traits**:
- The system's moral failure made visible
- Abolitionist movements growing among Tiers 2-3 (contract-bound crisis threatens systemic legitimacy)
- Covenant exploits: offers contract-bound freedom through Covenant membership (exchanging one bondage for another)

## Mobility & Class Crossing

### Ascending
- **Tier 4→3**: Guild apprenticeship (competitive entry; sponsors required)
- **Tier 3→2**: Demonstrated mastery + guild election to officer position
- **Tier 2→1**: Marriage into patriciate family or exceptional wealth accumulation (decades)
- **Tier 5→4**: Pay off Life Contract debt OR escape to canton that doesn't enforce (rare; risky)

**Barriers**:
- Guild entry fees (price out Tier 4 applicants)
- Contract literacy (Tier 4-5 cannot navigate complex agreements)
- Patriciate networks (advancement above Tier 2 requires social access)
- Magical enforcement (Tier 5 contracts are physically binding; escape is painful)

### Descending
- **Tier 1→2**: Financial ruin (rare; old money insulated by contract chains)
- **Any tier→5**: Debt default → Life Contract adjudication
- Speed: Descending can happen in a single bad contract. Ascending takes years/generations.

## Characteristic Attitudes by Tier

| Tier | To Tier Above | To Tier Below | To Authority |
|------|----------|------------|---|
| 1 | N/A | Paternalistic; contractual duty | The system IS authority; they define it |
| 2 | Deferential; seeking patronage | Professional regard | Supports system; benefits from it |
| 3 | Loyal to guild leadership | Competitive (labor market) | Resentful compliance; reform-minded |
| 4 | Invisible; excluded | Sympathetic (shared precarity) | Hostile; unrepresented |
| 5 | Invisible; avoided | N/A | Trapped; cannot engage |

## Key Structural Problem

**Legal equality masks structural inequality**: "Before the contract, all parties are equal" — but the party who drafts the contract has enormous advantage. Patriciate contract-lawyers write agreements that technically comply with law but extractively exploit less-sophisticated parties. The system is self-reinforcing: wealth buys contract literacy, contract literacy extracts more wealth.

**The Covenant's perfect entry**: They operate within the system's rules. Their contracts are impeccable. Their terms are technically fair. They infiltrate not by breaking the system but by being better at it than anyone else.

# Shukei — Class Hierarchy & Social Structure

## The Five Tiers

### Tier 1: Nobility (Shogun, Daimyo, High-Ranked Samurai)
**Population**: ~1% | **Power**: Absolute

- **Shogun**: Supreme authority; chosen from daimyo families
- **Daimyo**: Regional lords; control territory and vassals
- **Senior samurai**: Generals, advisors, administrators

**Characteristics**:
- Hereditary titles; birth determines fate
- Extensive martial training from childhood
- Access to restricted magical/spiritual knowledge
- Answer only to superiors; command absolute obedience

**Notable Traits**:
- Deep sense of honor (dishonor = suicide obligation)
- Contempt for commercialism
- Attachment to traditions (often rigid)
- Loyalty more important than competence

### Tier 2: Samurai & Military Aristocracy
**Population**: ~5% | **Power**: High (locally)

- Professional warriors
- Administrators (magistrates, garrison commanders)
- Master-warrior teachers
- Trusted advisors to daimyo

**Characteristics**:
- Lifelong martial training; identity fused with weapon
- Serve specific daimyo; loyalty fierce
- Code of honor (bushido or equivalent)
- Career advancement through demonstrated excellence

**Notable Traits**:
- Willing to die for honor/leader
- Potential for disagreement with daimyo (creates conflicts)
- Some secretly question hierarchy (but cannot voice doubts)
- Attracted to military service as pathway to meaning

**Story Connection**: Some resistance members might be samurai questioning authority or dishonored samurai (ronin).

### Tier 3: Commoners (Farmers, Merchants, Artisans)
**Population**: ~60% | **Power**: Minimal

**Farmers** (~75% of commoners):
- Grow rice and vegetables
- Own no land (tenant farmers)
- Owe percentage of harvest to daimyo
- Restricted movement; rural bound

**Merchants** (~15% of commoners):
- Wealthy but socially degraded (bushido culture despises commerce)
- Own businesses; trade goods
- Resent lower social status despite wealth
- Often become moneylenders (exploiting power imbalance)

**Artisans** (~10% of commoners):
- Craft specialists (blacksmiths, carpenters, etc.)
- Guild-like organization; knowledge restricted
- Higher respect than merchants; still below samurai

**Characteristics - All Commoners**:
- Maximum lifespan: Bound to role; no escape path
- Minimal education (except apprenticeships)
- Subject to samurai violence without recourse
- Culturally lower than warriors despite potential wealth

**Notable Traits**:
- Merchants accumulating wealth become resentful of hierarchy
- Farmers desperate to improve conditions
- Artisans take pride in craft (sometimes inspires resistance)

### Tier 4: Outcasts (Eta, Hinin, Unregistered)
**Population**: ~8% | **Power**: Negative

- **Eta** (leather workers, butchers, grave-keepers): Ceremonial impurity from butchering
- **Hinin** (entertainers, prostitutes, day laborers): Social impurity from non-traditional work
- Unregistered persons: No legal status; fugitives

**Characteristics**:
- Legally permitted to exist but socially invisible
- Restricted housing, food, clothing
- Forbidden from entering temples/sacred spaces
- Can be killed with minimal consequences (technically illegal but ignored)

**Notable Traits**:
- Develop tight-knit communities for mutual protection
- Preserve forbidden knowledge and traditions
- Often involved in intelligence networks (invisible = can spy)
- Source of revolutionary potential (nothing to lose)

### Tier 5: Ronin (Masterless Samurai)
**Population**: ~1% | **Power**: Unpredictable (high but illegitimate)

- Samurai without daimyo (discharged, dishonored, or wandering)
- Skilled warriors without institutional support
- Often become mercenaries, bandits, or wanderers
- Status ambiguous: Respected for skill, despised for disloyalty

**Characteristics**:
- Highly trained; dangerous potential
- Free from hierarchy (also means no loyalty/obligation)
- Reputation-focused (individual honor separate from family/daimyo)
- Often recruited for illegal operations (deniable assets)

**Notable Traits**:
- Idealistic about honor code (often stricter than institutional samurai)
- Resentful of those who enjoy privilege without earning it
- Potential allies to resistance (skilled + unbound)
- Vulnerable to Covenant recruitment (offer purpose, wealth, belonging)

## Mobility & Class Crossing

### Ascending
- **From Tier 3→2**: Rare; military talent might be noticed; apprenticed to samurai as servant
- **From Tier 4→3**: Nearly impossible; generational change required
- **Within Tier 2**: Possible through military achievement and patronage
- **From any tier to Tier 1**: Virtually impossible (birth requirement)

**Barriers**:
- Birth (overrides everything)
- Samurai examinations (if permitted to try)
- Daimyo patronage (if noticed as useful)
- Generational passage (family status slowly improving)

### Descending
- Swift and permanent
- Samurai disgrace = suicide obligation or ronin status = social death
- Family shame lasts generations
- Tier 4 cannot descend further

## Characteristic Attitudes by Tier

| Tier | To Tier Above | To Tier Below | To Authority |
|------|---------|-----------|---|
| 1 | N/A | Superior beings; tools for use | Obligation to tradition |
| 2 | Loyal; subservient; aspiring | Protective/paternalistic | Absolute obedience to daimyo |
| 3 (farmer) | Respectful fear | Competitive (economic) | Resentful obedience |
| 3 (merchant) | Resentful deference | Generous; seeking status | Irritated compliance |
| 4 | Invisible; avoided | Competing for survival | Avoidant hostile |
| 5 | Mixed (respect+resentment) | Pragmatic; fellow outsiders | Complicated (independence vs. purpose-seeking) |

## Key Structural Problem

**Rigidity creates fragility**: System designed to resist change. When external threats appear (Covenant infiltration, ecological crisis, foreign pressure), rigid hierarchy cannot adapt. Solution becomes either: 1) More emphasis on hierarchy (Covenant manipulates this) or 2) System collapse.

Ronin and outcast populations, disadvantaged by rigidity, become revolutionary potential.

# Therryn — Class Hierarchy

## The Examination Paradox

Therryn's class system is the most dangerous kind: one that genuinely believes itself to be fair. Because the examination system theoretically allows anyone to rise, the system's failures are attributed to individual inadequacy rather than structural bias. A peasant who fails the exam "simply wasn't smart enough." The fact that the peasant couldn't afford ten years of exam preparation is invisible within the system's logic.

## Tier Structure

### Tier 1: Imperial House & Grand Scholars (1%)
- **Emperor/Empress**: Hereditary. Legitimacy comes from being "the most learned family" — a fiction maintained through private tutors and ghost-written publications.
- **Grand Council Members**: The twelve highest-ranked examination graduates who administer the empire. Typically 50-70 years old. They've spent their lives in scholarship and governance.
- **Imperial Scholars**: Small group (~50 people) who hold permanent positions at the Palace Academy. They advise, research, and preserve the most advanced knowledge.

**Daily reality**: Luxurious but constrained. Every word is scrutinized. Political maneuvering disguised as scholarly debate. A Grand Council member who loses an argument in open forum may lose their position.

### Tier 2: Scholar-Officials & Senior Gentry (5%)
- **Provincial Governors**: Examination-passed officials. Comfortable income, significant local power, but rotated every 3-5 years.
- **Ministry Officials**: Capital-based administrators. Reliable careers with gradual advancement.
- **Senior Gentry**: Landed families that have produced scholar-officials for multiple generations. Not automatically powerful, but their children have massive educational advantages.

**Daily reality**: Comfortable homes with private libraries. Children educated from birth with private tutors. Social life revolves around scholarly gatherings, publication parties, and debate evenings.

### Tier 3: Merchants, Junior Scholars & Professionals (15%)
- **Merchant Houses**: Wealthy commercial families. Fund libraries, examination scholarships, and public works. Buy political influence through patronage because they cannot earn it through examination.
- **Junior Scholars**: Passed provincial exam but not metropolitan. Work as teachers, minor administrators, scribes, engineers. The largest class of "educated poor" — high status, low income.
- **Professionals**: Physicians, engineers, architects, master craftspeople. Respected for skill but not for scholarship.

**Daily reality**: Merchants live better than Tier 2 but have less social respect. Junior scholars live in modest lodgings surrounded by books they can barely afford. The contrast creates bitterness.

### Tier 4: Commoners (70%)
- **Farmers**: The backbone of economy. Respected in rhetoric ("the hands that feed the nation"), ignored in policy.
- **Artisans**: Skilled but non-scholarly. Their work is necessary but their knowledge isn't valued by the examination system.
- **Laborers**: Unskilled workers. Construction, mining, carrying, cleaning. Functionally invisible.
- **Soldiers**: Professional military. Respected during war, forgotten during peace. No examination path for enlisted.

**Daily reality**: Hard work, modest comfort, near-zero social mobility for most. Their children might attend a public examination school, but the quality doesn't compare to private tutoring.

### Tier 5: Diaspora & Failed Scholars (9%)
- **Failed examination candidates**: Those who sat the exam multiple times and failed permanently. Social death in a society that defines worth by intellectual achievement. Many become alcoholics, drifters, or ironically competent private-sector administrators.
- **Diaspora communities**: Immigrants, refugees, foreign merchants. No examination tradition = no recognized competence. They do the work Therryn citizens won't: dangerous labor, frontier settlement, mercenary service.
- **Criminals**: Thieves, smugglers, black market operators. Many are failed scholars who turned bitterness into commerce.

**Daily reality**: The failed scholar is the most tragic figure in Therryn. They internalized the system's values, failed by the system's standards, and have no alternative framework for self-worth.

## Mobility Mechanics

### Upward Paths
1. **Examination success**: The legitimate path. Requires 5-15 years of preparation, access to texts, competent teaching, and financial support during study. Most realistic path: Tier 4 → Tier 3 through provincial exam. Generational: Tier 4 family → Tier 3 parent → Tier 2 child.
2. **Mercantile wealth**: Merchants can buy social proximity but not official rank. They fund scholars who advocate for their interests. Over generations, merchant families may produce scholar-officials through education investment.
3. **Military distinction**: During wartime, field officers can earn examination exemptions through exceptional service. Rare, controversial, resented by examination graduates.

### Downward Paths
1. **Examination failure**: A scholar-official's child who fails the exam drops from Tier 2 to Tier 3 or lower. This is devastating precisely because the system claims to be fair — there's no one to blame but yourself.
2. **Political disgrace**: Losing a scholarly argument, being caught in plagiarism, or backing the wrong faction in council. Demotion is swift and public.
3. **Provincial decline**: When a family's land fails or trade routes shift, the money for education evaporates. Within two generations, a Tier 2 family can become Tier 4.

## Class Interaction Patterns

### Scholar-Merchant Dynamic
The central class tension. Merchants have money; scholars have authority. Each needs the other; each despises the other.
- **Merchants** view scholars as impractical theorists who'd starve without merchant funding.
- **Scholars** view merchants as culturally ignorant money-counters who buy respectability they haven't earned.
- **In practice**: They intermarry. Merchant daughters marry junior scholars (money + status exchange). This produces families that are wealthy AND educated — and increasingly dominate both spheres.

### Scholar-Commoner Dynamic
- **Scholars** believe commoners have the same opportunity (examinations are open). Their failure to rise proves their limitations.
- **Commoners** know the examinations aren't accessible. But they've internalized the system enough to believe their limitations are personal, not structural.
- **Result**: Less overt resentment than Valdimere's class system. More internalized shame. This makes Therryn's inequality harder to organize against.

### The Failed Scholar
- Neither commoner (they're educated) nor scholar (they failed). 
- Other scholars pity them. Commoners resent them (educated but useless).
- They're the recruitment pool for every revolutionary movement, criminal organization, and heterodox philosophy.
- **Covenant exploitation**: Failed scholars are prime recruits. They have knowledge, resentment, and nothing to lose.

# Umara — Class Structure

## The Invisible Hierarchy

Umara has no formal class system. Ask any Umaran and they'll tell you their society is egalitarian. They're sincere — and they're wrong. Informal hierarchies exist in every human community. Umara's achievement is making them flexible and permeable. Its failure is making them invisible, which means they can't be challenged through formal mechanisms.

## Status Markers (Not Tiers)

Rather than fixed tiers, Umaran status is a composite of multiple overlapping measures:

### 1. Kinship Network Density
- **High status**: Connected to multiple families across multiple communities. Can call on favors from people spanning hundreds of miles.
- **Medium status**: Well-connected within one community, some external ties.
- **Low status**: Few connections. New arrivals, orphans, refugees, outcasts.
- **Reality**: In a system where relationships are power, those born into dense networks start life with enormous advantage. A griot's child meets more people by age 10 than a farmer's child meets by age 30.

### 2. Obligation Balance
- **Net giver**: Someone who consistently gives more than they receive. Highest social prestige. A merchant prince who has distributed surplus for decades has enormous social capital.
- **Balanced**: Most people. Give and receive roughly equally over time.
- **Net taker**: Someone who receives more than they give. Not shamed (everyone needs help sometimes) but tracked. Persistent net-taking erodes standing.
- **Reality**: Those who start with more can give more. Obligation balance "proves" merit but actually measures starting resources.

### 3. Griot Recognition
- If a griot tells your story at a gathering, you exist in the cultural record. Your deeds matter. Your ancestors matter.
- If no griot tells your story, you are culturally invisible — not persecuted, but unrecognized.
- **How griots choose**: They tell stories that serve communal purpose — heroes, cautionary tales, foundational myths. They don't tell boring stories. A person who lives quietly may be morally excellent and culturally invisible.
- **Power implication**: Griots decide who is remembered. This is enormous power unaccountable to any vote or examination.

### 4. Spiritual Standing
- Those perceived as having deep relationship with the land, seasons, and ancestors have elevated spiritual status.
- Spiritual leaders don't seek status (theoretically). It's recognized by the community.
- **Reality**: Spiritual charisma can't be faked easily but it can be performed. Ambitious individuals learn spiritual signification early.

### 5. Age
- Older = wiser (default assumption). Elders speak first, are consulted first, are deferred to first.
- **Reality**: Many elders are wise. Some are rigid, cruel, or demented. But challenging an elder's authority is culturally catastrophic — even when they're wrong.

## Functional Hierarchies That Emerge

Despite formal egalitarianism, consistent patterns emerge:

**Top**: Senior griots + elder council heads + established merchant princes + senior spiritual leaders
→ These people make decisions. Everyone else participates.

**Upper-middle**: Established family heads with extensive networks, successful traders, recognized warriors, experienced healers
→ These people influence decisions through lobbying and relationship pressure.

**Middle**: Average community members with stable families and moderate networks
→ These people consent to decisions and perform the labor that sustains them.

**Lower**: Recent arrivals, small-network individuals, those recovering from crisis
→ These people receive community support but have limited voice.

**Bottom**: Exiles, obligation-defaulters, those who violated sacred relationships
→ These people exist outside the system. Not persecuted (usually) but not protected.

## Mobility Patterns

### Upward
- **Griot recognition**: Being noticed by a griot and having your story told elevates you. This can happen at any age, from any starting position.
- **Marriage/adoption**: Marrying into a well-connected family expands your network overnight. Adoption by a patron does the same.
- **Crisis heroism**: Drought, war, flood — emergencies create opportunities for demonstrated excellence. The farmer who organizes food distribution during famine becomes a leader.
- **Trade success**: Building a successful trade route creates obligation networks that translate to political influence.

### Downward
- **Obligation default**: Failing to meet obligations repeatedly. Communities track this through gossip (the informal enforcement mechanism).
- **Community separation**: Physical distance from your network erodes it. Refugees and migrants lose status proportional to distance from home.
- **Griot condemnation**: A griot who tells your story as a cautionary tale effectively brands you. Hard to recover from.
- **Elder displeasure**: Angering the elder council. Usually recoverable through penance and relationship repair, but repeated offenses lead to marginal status.

## The Refugee Problem

Umara's greatest structural challenge: what to do with people who have no networks.

- **Traditional response**: Adopt them. Build connections. Give them time.
- **Current pressure**: Too many refugees arriving too fast from Valdimere's frontier collapse and Shukei's internal conflicts.
- **System strain**: The adoption process works for individuals and small groups. It breaks when thousands arrive simultaneously. Communities lack the relationship bandwidth.
- **Dangerous outcome**: Refugee camps become permanent settlements outside the obligation network. A permanent underclass develops — the first time in Umaran history that structural poverty exists without a mechanism for integration.
- **Covenant exploitation**: Offer refugees the integration the Umaran system is failing to provide. Build loyalty among the displaced. Turn Umara's most vulnerable population into Covenant assets.

# Valdimere — Class Hierarchy & Social Structure

## The Five Tiers

### Tier 1: Imperial Administration & Court Nobility
**Population**: ~2% | **Power**: Absolute

- Emperor/Empress and immediate family
- Court advisors and cabinet ministers
- Imperial mages and strategic planners
- Regional governors
- Ancient noble houses with hereditary titles

**Characteristics**:
- Everything documented and recorded in triplicate
- Education in imperial academies; fluent in bureaucratic processes
- Access to restricted magical knowledge
- Ability to authorize exceptions to laws
- Intermarriage between families maintains power

**Notable Traits**:
- Assume their word is law
- See ordinariness as threat
- Control historical narratives through record-keeping
- Often ignorant of ground-level reality (insulated by bureaucracy)

### Tier 2: Regional Governors & Military Officers
**Population**: ~3% | **Power**: High (locally absolute)

- Provincial administrators
- Military lodge commanders
- Chief magistrates and law enforcers
- Wealthy merchants with noble patronage

**Characteristics**:
- Execute imperial will; careers depend on compliance
- Control taxation and resource allocation
- Command local military forces
- Report directly to imperial court; under constant surveillance
- Significant autonomy so long as they meet quotas

**Notable Traits**:
- Caught between imperial demands and local reality
- Often resentful of arbitrary orders from distant court
- Some run side operations (corruption) for personal wealth
- Strategic about protecting local interests while appearing loyal

**Story Connection**: Border regions have governors competing with marcher lords over control; Caspian may negotiate with these figures.

### Tier 3: Urban Merchants, Tradesman Guilds, Military Lodge Warriors
**Population**: ~20% | **Power**: Moderate (collective bargaining)

**Urban Merchants**:
- Charter holders; licensed to trade
- Pay taxes in exchange for monopoly protection
- Operate within strict regulations
- Wealthy but not politically powerful individually
- Collective voice through merchant councils

**Tradesman Guilds**:
- Apprenticeship-based knowledge transmission
- Control who learns which trades
- Effectively gatekeep skilled labor
- Powerful when unified; often fractured

**Military Lodge Warriors**:
- Professional soldiers trained in state-sanctioned combat
- Telekinesis taught systematically
- Loyalty to lodge/empire rather than individual leaders
- Career advancement through demonstrated competence
- Forms backbone of imperial military

**Story Connection**: Aisen's father and Harald may have military lodge training. This tier produces the "common soldier" who follows orders unquestioningly.

### Tier 4: Rural Peasants, Frontier Settlers, Skilled Laborers
**Population**: ~65% | **Power**: Minimal (individually)

- Agricultural workers (majority)
- Frontier settlers and miners
- Construction and infrastructure workers
- Servants and household staff
- Unskilled laborers of all types

**Characteristics**:
- Bound to land/lord through legal contracts
- Taxes extracted as percentage of harvest or labor
- Minimal literacy (bureaucracy not designed for their education)
- Limited mobility (travel requires documentation)
- Subject to conscription during wartime

**Notable Traits**:
- Most vulnerable to Flux corruption (live on frontier)
- First to suffer food shortages
- Least able to appeal unjust taxation
- Desperate enough to join resistance (if fed/paid)

**Story Connection**: Tarovin and lower-class resistance members come from this tier. They experience Covenant corruption directly.

### Tier 5: Religious Minorities, Outcasts, Unregistered Populations
**Population**: ~10% | **Power**: Negative (actively oppressed)

- Practitioners of non-sanctioned religions
- Ethnic/cultural minorities
- Unregistered persons (no documentation)
- Former criminals
- Political exiles and refugees

**Characteristics**:
- No legal status; can be arrested/expelled on whim
- Pay taxes but receive no protections
- Often used as scapegoats for society's problems
- Create shadow networks for mutual protection
- Preserve forbidden knowledge and practices

**Notable Traits**:
- Cannot formally organize (illegal)
- Operate through family networks, diaspora connections
- Sometimes employed as spies/assassins (deniable assets)
- Develop alternative magical traditions (unregistered)

**Story Connection**: Underground resistance often recruits from this tier. They have least to lose by rebelling.

## Mobility & Class Crossing

### Ascending
- **From Tier 4→3**: Military service or apprenticeship (rare but possible)
- **From Tier 3→2**: Exceptional competence + patronage from nobility
- **From Tier 2→1**: Marriage into noble family or imperial appointment
- **From Tier 5**: Nearly impossible (legal status barrier)

**Barriers**:
- Documentation requirements (tier 5 particularly blocked)
- Guild membership (merchant tier gatekeeps)
- Noble birth advantage (system designed for inherited titles)
- Patronage necessity (cannot ascend without powerful sponsor)

### Descending
- Quick: Lose noble patronage, fail to meet quotas, commit crime, lose guild membership
- Tier 1 rarely falls (power protects them)
- Tier 5 cannot fall further (already at bottom)

## Characteristic Attitudes by Tier

| Tier | To Tier Above | To Tier Below | To Authority |
|------|----------|------------|---|
| 1 | N/A | Superior; sees them as tools | Imperial will; above all |
| 2 | Loyal; dependent | Paternalistic; superior | Complicated (local vs. imperial) |
| 3 | Deferential; seeking patronage | Pragmatic; employment | Resentful; following rules unwillingly |
| 4 | Respectful fear | Competitive (survival) | Fearful obedience |
| 5 | Invisible; avoided | Competing for scraps | Avoidant; hostile |

## Key Structural Problem

**Bureaucratic inertia**: System designed to prevent change. When reality (Flux corruption, ecological crisis) contradicts official records, bureaucracy denies reality rather than updating records. This creates gap between documented world and actual world—perfect place for Covenant to hide.
