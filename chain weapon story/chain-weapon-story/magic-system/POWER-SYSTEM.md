# Power System — Flux & Weave: Practical Rules (Working Draft)

Purpose: Turn the Flux–Weave framework into actionable mechanics writers can use in scenes. This file defines resource models, spell classes, failure modes, counters, and a short set of spell "cards" to drop into scenes.

See: [FLUX-WEAVE-PHYSICS-FRAMEWORK.md](FLUX-WEAVE-PHYSICS-FRAMEWORK.md) for the underlying theory.

---

## 1) Overview

- The Weave = the background physics (conservation, causality, thermodynamics).
- The Flux = the manipulable force; using it requires information and an attuned conduit.
- Writers: treat Flux like an engineering system with clear inputs/outputs and observable failure modes.

## 2) Resource Model (how magic is paid for)

- Ambient Flux: the environment's available Flux density (high near ley nodes, low in Void Zones).
- Conduit / Attunement: each caster has a limited reservoir and an efficiency curve (how well they convert ambient Flux into controlled effects).
- Information Cost: complex, high-precision effects require precise informational states in the caster's mind. Higher informational complexity raises entropy cost.
- Entropy Payment: when a spell reduces local entropy it must discharge entropy elsewhere (aging, bodily damage, environmental corruption).

Practical rule for scenes: describe the *visible* tradeoff (hand tremor, gray hair at the temple, nausea, a ring of dead grass) rather than abstract numbers.

## 3) Spell Taxonomy (classes and behavior)

- Manipulation (Telekinesis)
  - Purpose: apply precise forces, multi-point control, torque.
  - Cost: low–medium informational cost; scales with number of control points.
  - Failure Mode: cognitive overload → backlash (tremors, blurred boundary sense), interrupted hold.
  - Counters: shock / strong impact, anti-telekinesis crystals, loss of anchor (chain severed).

- Translocation (Teleportation / Swap)
  - Purpose: move a body/object by exchanging two volumes of space.
  - Cost: very high information cost (molecular/positional data).
  - Failure Mode: spanning objects create paradoxes (shearing); partial reconstitution causes injury; misalignment.
  - Counters: physical anchors (loops, thread), arrival-point disruption, masking arrival coordinates.

- Structural Memory (Memory‑Metal & State Locks)
  - Purpose: store a target physical state and toggle it (e.g., chain link profile, armor rigidity).
  - Cost: moderate informational cost; sensitive to particulate contamination and thermal extremes.
  - Failure Mode: jamming (foreign particles), thermal brittleness or stuck states.
  - Counters: dust pouches, temperature shocks, targeted abrasives.

- Biomancy / Growth (Plant & Organic Manipulation)
  - Purpose: guide growth or repattern living tissue.
  - Cost: low instantaneous cost; high time/attention investment.
  - Failure Mode: stress misapplication → malformed growth, unpredictable direction.
  - Counters: environmental stressors, grafting misdirection.

- Flux Corruption / Affliction
  - Purpose: abuse Flux to create corrupted entities or amplify biological effects.
  - Cost: nonlinear and dangerous; produces Flux residue (pollution) and attracts anti-Flux responses.
  - Failure Mode: backlash that spawns voidlike phenomena or draws predatory anomalies.
  - Counters: silver (material sensitivity), sanctified alloys, dampening relics.

- Information Magic (Prediction, Regression)
  - Purpose: gain knowledge outside normal causal flow (regressors, prophecy).
  - Cost: extreme; violates causality and yields entropic/paradoxical consequences over time.
  - Failure Mode: memory erosion, cognitive splitting, reality correction.
  - Counters: containment, forced cognitive re-synchronization, social exposure (force timeline divergence).

## 4) Mechanics & Constraints (for use in scenes)

- Range and Decay: fine control decays with distance; high-precision control only in close range (2–7m practical).
- Mass Limit: affecting unconnected heavy mass requires either more Flux or else a mechanical advantage (leverage, torque, connecting objects to caster).
- Multi-point Load: each additional control point multiplies cognitive load; keep this explicit in narration.
- Disruption & Interruption: shocks, impacts, magical dampeners, or trauma will sever delicate manipulations.
- Duration vs. Intensity tradeoff: sustained low‑intensity holds are cheaper than sudden, high‑intensity bursts of the same total effect.

## 5) Common Counters (practical checklist)

- Anchors: thin loops, thread, or anything that can span origin and destination (teleport counters).
- Particulates: sand/dust to jam memory-metal, or to foul a state lock.
- Temperature shock: freeze or overheat memory-alloys to make them brittle or immobile.
- Material sensitivity: silver, certain alloys, or salts that cause pain or break flux binding.
- Proximity/environment tagging: throw tags into an area rather than at a target ("splash" tagging).
- Texture exploitation: rough vs. smooth surfaces (garrote counters, grips).

## 6) Accident Discovery Pattern (how new counters appear)

1. Failure occurs in a usable, observable way.
2. Witnesses ask "why" and reconstruct conditions.
3. Principle is isolated and tested in small trials.
4. Technique is adapted into repeatable tactics.

Use this pattern when introducing a new counter: show the accident once, then show Aisen test and refine it.

## 7) Sample Spell Cards (drop into scenes)

- Telekinesis — Impulse Push
  - Effect: single microsecond impulse that nudges a weapon by a few degrees.
  - Cost: low (mental strain), consequence if overused: arm tremor, headache.
  - Failure: missed timing → wasted Flux; backlash if caster struck mid-push.

- Teleportation — Short Swap
  - Effect: exchange two small volumes (person-sized) within sight.
  - Cost: very high information payment; requires a clear origin and target volume.
  - Counter: any object spanning the swap (string loop, thread in hair) will be sheared or pull through; partial reconstitution if anchor resists.

- Memory‑Lock — Link Toggle
  - Effect: force a target link/gear to adopt or release a stored state.
  - Cost: moderate; sensitive to fine contamination.
  - Counter: throw abrasive dust into joints, freeze link to render toggle brittle.

- Tagging Link — Splash Tag
  - Effect: throw a weak tag into an area; it clings to anything passing through.
  - Cost: low; advantage: indirect tagging without line-of-sight.
  - Counter: environment cleansing; remove tagged items quickly.

## 8) Integration: Chain Weapon & Power System

- Chain acts as a physical interface that reduces telekinetic cost by providing mechanical anchors.
- Memory‑metal links are natural pairings with state-lock magic; the chain's four modes (loosen/tighten/connect/disconnect) map to different Flux demands.
- In scenes, show the chain reducing required information complexity by acting as a proxy.

## 9) Writer Checklist

- Introduce each new counter as an observed accident or explained limitation before relying on it in major beats.
- Always show an immediate, physical cost to complex magic (narrative shorthand: tremor, scar, sudden exhaustion).
- Keep the Flux rules consistent: if teleportation swaps volumes, never later treat it as literal movement without addressing the swap.
