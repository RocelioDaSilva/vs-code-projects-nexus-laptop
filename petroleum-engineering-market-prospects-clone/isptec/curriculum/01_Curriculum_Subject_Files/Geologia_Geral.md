## What You Learned

Rock cycle (igneous, sedimentary, metamorphic), plate tectonics, stratigraphy, structural geology (faults, folds, unconformities), sedimentary environments (fluvial, deltaic, marine, turbidite), basic paleontology, and geological time scale. You learned to "read" the Earth's history from rocks.

## Where This Subject Is Used in Industry

- **Exploration:** Geologists identify petroleum systems: source rock (where oil forms), reservoir rock (where oil accumulates), seal rock (what traps it), and the structural/stratigraphic trap geometry. Without understanding geology, you cannot find oil.
- **Reservoir Characterization:** Knowing that Angola's deepwater reservoirs are **turbidite sandstones** (deep-sea fan deposits) tells the reservoir engineer about expected porosity (20â€“30%), permeability (100â€“2,000 mD), and lateral continuity. This directly affects well placement and production forecasts.
- **Drilling:** Formation tops (where one rock type changes to another) are identified while drilling using cuttings analysis and log correlation. The mud logger and wellsite geologist use geological knowledge to call formation tops and identify shows (hydrocarbons).
- **Well Correlation:** Matching geological layers between wells using wireline logs requires understanding stratigraphy and sedimentary environments.

## Angola-Specific Context

Angola's offshore geology is world-class for petroleum:
- **Cretaceous source rocks** in the Aptian salt basin generated the oil
- **Post-salt turbidite sandstones** (Oligoceneâ€“Miocene) are the main reservoirs on Blocks 15, 17, and 32
- **Pre-salt carbonates** (Aptian) are emerging targets (similar to Brazil's pre-salt discoveries)
- **Salt tectonics** create structural traps â€” salt diapirs and associated faults

Understanding this geological framework is essential whether you end up in drilling (you need to know what formations you are drilling through) or production (you need to understand the reservoir architecture).
