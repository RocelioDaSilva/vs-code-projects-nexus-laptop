# FIFO Life Decoded — What Tseluca Actually Looks Like

> Before you choose a path, you need to understand what you're actually choosing. This file is the unfiltered reality of offshore rotational work: the schedules, the vessels, the money, the isolation, the relationships, the career capital, and the reasons people either love it for 20 years or leave after two.

---

## What FIFO Means

**FIFO = Fly In, Fly Out.** You fly to an offshore installation, work for a fixed period, then fly back. Your job is in two modes: ON and OFF. When you're on, you work. When you're off, you're completely free. The company covers everything while you're on: accommodation, food, transport, safety gear, and medical care.

In Angola's offshore sector, the common rotation schedules are:

| Schedule | On Days | Off Days | Common In |
|---|---|---|---|
| **28/28** | 28 days offshore | 28 days home | Most FPSO operations, some drillships |
| **21/21** | 21 days offshore | 21 days home | Some Tier 2 service company contracts |
| **14/14** | 14 days offshore | 14 days home | Shorter-duration projects, construction vessels |
| **28/14** | 28 days offshore | 14 days home | Some drilling contracts (less common, not preferred) |
| **Project-based** | Variable weeks | Variable off | Subsea installation vessels |

The 28/28 rotation is the gold standard. You work 28 days, go home for 28 days, then return. On an annual basis, you work **182 days and have 183 days free**. Your effective working time is just under half the year — but your salary is calculated as a full-time salary plus offshore premiums.

## The Vessels: Where You'll Actually Live

### The FPSO (Floating Production, Storage and Offloading)

The FPSO is where most production and reservoir engineers spend their rotations. It is a converted or purpose-built ship, typically 250–350 meters long, permanently moored over the reservoir. Angola has 12+ active FPSOs including:

- **Girassol** (Block 17, TotalEnergies) — 220,000 bbl/day capacity
- **Dalia** (Block 17, TotalEnergies) — 240,000 bbl/day
- **Kaombo Norte / Sul** (Block 32, TotalEnergies) — twin FPSOs
- **Ngoma** (Block 15, Azule Energy) — former Chevron Benguela-Belize
- **PSVM** (Block 31, bp) — ultra-deepwater, 4 FPSOs in one project
- **Greater Plutonio** (Block 18, bp)
- **Kizomba A/B/C** (Block 15, Azule Energy)

**What life is like on an FPSO:**
- **Crew size:** 100–250 people depending on the vessel
- **Your cabin:** A private or shared 2-person cabin. Smaller than a university dorm room. Has a bed, desk, small wardrobe, TV. Air conditioned.
- **Meals:** Full canteen open 24/7. Breakfast, lunch, dinner with hot meals. Most vessels have decent variety. Quality varies by operator — TotalEnergies vessels have a reputation for good food; some contractor vessels less so.
- **Leisure:** Gym, TV lounge, sometimes a small cinema room, internet access (limited bandwidth). No alcohol.
- **Internet:** 2–10 Mbps shared across all crew. Enough for WhatsApp, emails, basic browsing. Not enough for video streaming comfortably.
- **Noise:** Constant. Gas compressors, pumps, generators. You get used to it in 3–4 days. Earplugs available.
- **Smell:** Hydrocarbon smell in some areas. Safety showers and eyewash stations everywhere.
- **Movement:** FPSOs move with the swell. In calm weather: imperceptible. In rough weather (uncommon in Angola's 2–6m swells): noticeable but manageable. Seasickness is rare for most people after the first day.

### The Drillship

Where drilling engineers spend rotations. A drillship looks like a large ship with a derrick in the middle. It moves from well location to well location. Key difference from FPSO: the drillship is a working industrial machine.

**What life is like on a drillship:**
- **Crew size:** 100–180 people (rig crew + service company engineers)
- **Noise level:** Extremely high on the rig floor and near the drill floor. Quieter in the accommodation block.
- **Work pace:** More intense than FPSO. Drilling never stops. Day tour and night tour — 12-hour shifts with no variation.
- **Your role:** If you're a field engineer (SLB, Halliburton, Baker Hughes), you operate your service company's tools, write daily reports, interface with the operator's drilling supervisor (Company Man).
- **Location changes:** Drillships move. You may start a rotation on Block 15 and end it on Block 32. GPS trackers on your phone will show you're 150 km offshore.
- **Exercise:** Most drillships have a small gym. Some have a basketball court on deck.

### The Construction / Installation Vessel (CSV / DSV)

Where subsea engineers work during installation campaigns. These are purpose-built ships for laying pipelines, installing subsea trees, and performing diving or ROV operations.

**Key vessels in Angola:**
- Saipem's fleet (S7000, Castorone, Constellation)
- Subsea 7's vessels (Seven Waves, Seven Oceans)
- TechnipFMC's vessels (Deep Energy, others)

**What life is like on a CSV:**
- **Project-based rotations:** You're on for the duration of a campaign (a few weeks to several months), then off
- **Dynamic environment:** Vessel moves constantly for each lift or installation. You feel the weather more than on an FPSO
- **Crew mix:** Very international. You'll work with Brazilians, French, Norwegians, Nigerians
- **Pressure:** Project deadlines are hard. Offshore day rates for a construction vessel are $300,000–600,000/day — every delay costs money

## The Money: What You Actually Get Paid

Here is the full compensation picture for a Tier 2 service company field engineer on 28/28 rotation (entry level, Angola):

| Component | Amount | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Base salary** | $2,500–3,500/month | Paid every month, on and off rotation |
| **Offshore day rate premium** | $50–150/day x 28 days | Extra for each day spent offshore |
| **Offshore allowance (total)** | $1,400–4,200/month (on rotation months) | On top of base |
| **Free accommodation offshore** | $0 (covered) | Would cost $1,500–2,500/month in Luanda |
| **Free food offshore** | $0 (covered) | Would cost $400–700/month |
| **Flights Luanda ↔ offshore** | $0 (covered) | Charter helicopter or fixed-wing |
| **Annual effective compensation** | $45,000–75,000 | Base + premiums + saved living costs |

For context: an equivalent-experience onshore engineer at an Angolan company earns roughly **$8,000–18,000/year** (in Kwanzas). The offshore premium is not marginal — it is **3–5x the onshore equivalent**.

**The FIFO financial model:**
When you're offshore, you spend almost nothing. Food and housing are paid. When you return to Luanda with 28 days off, your full salary covers life AND lets you save or invest. Engineers who manage this well typically save 40–60% of their income in the first 3–5 offshore years.

## A Typical Day: 28/28 Drilling Rotation (Field Engineer, SLB)

**Day 1 of rotation:**
- 05:30 — Wake up in Luanda. Check email for mobilization brief.
- 07:00 — Report to Luanda base (SONILS or SLB base). Pick up equipment, PPE, survival suit.
- 08:00 — Security and documentation check.
- 09:00 — Helicopter to rig. 45–90 minute flight. Safety briefing on the helicopter.
- 10:30 — Land on helideck. Report to medic for offshore check-in. Get cabin assignment.
- 11:00 — Safety induction (fire exits, muster stations, lifeboat assignment). Even if you've been there before, mandatory.
- 12:00 — First meeting with outgoing engineer (your relief). 2-hour handover: what's been happening, where the well is, any issues.
- 14:00 — Your shift starts. You now own the service.

**Day 2–28 (typical day, night tour 18:00–06:00):**
- 17:30 — Wake up. Eat dinner in canteen.
- 18:00 — Shift start. Get handover from day-tour colleague.
- 18:30 — Review overnight drilling program with Company Man. What's the plan for the next 12 hours?
- 19:00 — Set up monitoring station. Multiple screens showing real-time drilling data.
- 20:00 — Connection. Drill pipe added. You supervise correct torque procedures.
- 21:00 — Drill bit on bottom. Monitor ROP, WOB, torque, ECD.
- 00:00 — Casing point approaching. Notify Company Man. Run logs? Decision meeting.
- 02:00 — Wiper trip (short pipe trip to clean the wellbore). Monitor fill. Record data.
- 04:00 — Back to drilling. Update daily drilling report.
- 05:30 — Morning handover report written. Give to day-tour colleague at 06:00.
- 06:00 — Eat breakfast. Go to cabin. Sleep.

**Day 29 — Demobilization:**
- 08:00 — Handover to incoming engineer. Thorough technical handover.
- 10:00 — Helicopter back to Luanda.
- 12:00 — Back home. 28 days free.

## The Reality of Offshore Life: Honest Assessment

### What Is Great

**1. Pure technical immersion.** You work with the equipment. You make the decisions. There is no buffer between you and the consequence. Engineers who do 3 years offshore are technically 3–5 years ahead of office engineers. The learning rate is exceptional.

**2. International network.** Your cabin neighbor is Norwegian. The Company Man is Australian. The drilling supervisor is Brazilian. In one 28-day rotation you can build 10+ genuine professional relationships that last for a career.

**3. Financial acceleration.** In 3–4 offshore years, an Angolan engineer can save enough to buy property, start a business, or fund an MSc abroad. The offshore years are a financial springboard.

**4. Clarity.** No office politics. No email chains about meetings. You work, you sleep, you work. The problem is concrete. The success is measurable.

**5. Free time.** 182 free days a year. That's more than most people take on holiday in a lifetime, concentrated into chunks long enough to actually do things — travel, build a business, study for an MSc, be present with family.

### What Is Hard

**1. Separation.** 28 days is a long time away from people you love. Relationships suffer. Some marriages don't survive offshore careers. This is real and must be confronted honestly, not avoided.

**2. Monotony.** Day 14 of a 28-day rotation looks exactly like Day 2. The routine is tight. The physical space is small. Boredom and cabin fever are real for some people.

**3. Sleep disruption.** Shift rotation (night tour vs day tour) messes with your body. Some people adapt in days; others struggle for years.

**4. Physical demands.** Industrial environments. Heavy equipment. Noise. Confined spaces. Chemical exposure. Offshore safety training exists because real accidents happen. The risk is managed but not zero.

**5. Career visibility.** When you're offshore, you're invisible in Luanda. You miss networking events, industry conferences, promotions discussions. Managing your onshore presence and career momentum from offshore requires deliberate effort.

**6. Mental health.** Isolation, repetition, and distance create conditions for depression and anxiety in some people. The industry has improved its awareness of this, but support systems are still developing. Know your mental health baseline before you commit.

## Who Thrives Offshore

The engineers who love offshore rotational life and build long careers in it tend to share these traits:

- **Comfortable with routine.** They can find satisfaction in mastery and repetition.
- **Technically self-motivated.** They study on their off days. They read company manuals on rotation. They ask the experienced engineers questions.
- **Socially adaptive.** They can build professional friendships quickly with people very different from them.
- **Financially disciplined.** They treat offshore income as a wealth-building tool, not just higher spending power.
- **Clear about personal relationships.** Their partners and family know the deal, have agreed to it, and the relationship is strong enough to handle the schedule.

---

## Who Should Think Carefully

- People who feel they need daily human connection to function well
- People with young children and partners who can't handle the absence
- People who get seasick easily (this can improve with medication and adaptation, but on a drillship in bad weather it's unpleasant)
- People who are deeply uncomfortable with industrial risk environments

None of these are disqualifiers. But they are real factors to weigh.

## Your First Offshore Trip: What to Expect

1. **Before you go:** You will complete a BOSIET/HUET course that includes actual underwater helicopter escape practice — being dunked upside down in a pool. It is intense. It is also excellent. You will feel genuinely prepared.
2. **The helicopter:** Smaller than a commercial aircraft. Loud. Vibrates. 45–90 minutes. Spectacular views of the ocean and (on approach) the installation. Most people find it exciting.
3. **Landing:** The helideck crew guide you in. You put your luggage in a net. You go into the accommodation block for check-in and safety induction.
4. **The smell:** Hydrocarbons. Some find it nauseating; most adapt within hours.
5. **Night 1:** Sleep is hard. Noise, movement, unfamiliar environment. By night 3, most people sleep fine.
6. **Week 1:** Information overload. Every system is new. Every person is new. Take notes. Ask questions. Don't pretend you know what you don't.
7. **Week 2:** Settling in. You start to feel competent. The routine becomes comfortable.
8. **Week 3–4:** You're productive. You're contributing. You're learning specific things.
9. **Demob day (Day 28):** The helicopter home feels excellent. You feel accomplished and exhausted.
