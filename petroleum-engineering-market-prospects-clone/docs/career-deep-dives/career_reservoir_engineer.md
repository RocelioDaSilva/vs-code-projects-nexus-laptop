## Angola Reservoir Engineering Context

Angola's deepwater turbidite and pre-salt carbonate reservoirs are among the most technically complex in the world. Reservoir engineers in Angola manage billion-dollar subsurface assets — every well placement, waterflood timing, and recovery factor decision directly affects national revenue.

### Angola-Specific Reservoir Challenges

- **Turbidite reservoir complexity:** Angola's Block 17 reservoirs are stacked channel-levee systems with poor lateral connectivity — history matching is extremely difficult
- **Pre-salt uncertainty:** Block 20 (Cameia) and Block 15/06 pre-salt carbonates have limited analogue data globally; reservoir models are highly uncertain
- **Waterflood optimization:** Mature blocks (0, 15, 17 early fields) are in late-life waterflood; maximizing sweep efficiency is the primary optimization lever
- **Recovery factor targets:** Typical Angola deepwater RF: 25–35%. Improving by even 1% on a 2-billion-bbl field = $200M+ value
- **Simulation model scale:** Block 17 full-field models have 5–10 million cells, running on HPC clusters; requires strong computational skills
- **PRMS reserves compliance:** All Angola reserves are reported under PRMS (not SEC rules); reservoir engineers must master PRMS 2P/3P classification

## 1. What Does a Reservoir Engineer Actually Do Day-to-Day?

A reservoir engineer predicts and optimizes how much oil and gas can be economically recovered from subsurface formations. It's the most analytically demanding discipline in petroleum engineering.

- **Morning**: Review pressure data, production plots, and waterflood performance. Check simulation run outputs that ran overnight.
- **Mid-day**: Build or update geological/simulation models (Petrel RE, Eclipse, CMG). Run sensitivity analyses — "What if we inject water at 10,000 bbl/d vs. 15,000?".
- **Afternoon**: Meet with geoscientists to integrate new seismic/log data. Update volumetric estimates. Work on the Field Development Plan (FDP) with the multidisciplinary team.
- **Evening**: Run material balance calculations, prepare reserves estimates for quarterly SEC/PRMS reporting, develop CAPEX justification for new infill wells.

**Key distinction:** Reservoir engineers spend 80%+ of their time in an office. Offshore visits are occasional (well tests, production reviews), not routine rotations.

## 5. Common Interview Questions

### Technical
1. Derive the material balance equation for an undersaturated oil reservoir above bubble point.
2. Explain the difference between OOIP, STOIIP, and EUR. How do you estimate each?
3. A reservoir is producing at 8,000 bopd with 30% water cut. Walk through your waterflood optimization approach.
4. What are the differences between black oil, compositional, and thermal simulation models? When do you use each?
5. Explain Buckley-Leverett theory and fractional flow. Draw the fractional flow curve.
6. How do you history-match a reservoir simulation model? What parameters do you adjust and why?
7. Describe the PRMS reserves classification framework (Proved, Probable, Possible, Contingent).
8. What is relative permeability and how do you measure it? How sensitive are production forecasts to kr curves?
9. Compare pressure decline analysis vs. rate transient analysis (RTA) for unconventional reservoirs.
10. Explain the physics behind capillary pressure and its role in transition zones.

### Behavioral
11. Describe a time your simulation model didn't match historical production. How did you diagnose it?
12. How do you communicate uncertainty in reserves estimates to management?
13. Tell me about a cross-functional project where your reservoir analysis changed the development plan.
14. How do you handle disagreements with geologists on geological models?

## 6. Portfolio Projects That Impress

1. **Full Reservoir Simulation Study** — Build a 3D model using Volve data (Eclipse/CMG/OPM). History match, forecast, propose development scenarios. Document in a technical report.
2. **Material Balance Analysis** — Apply MBAL or Excel-based material balance to a real dataset. Estimate OOIP, drive mechanism, recovery factor.
3. **Decline Curve Analysis vs. Simulation Comparison** — Compare DCA estimates (Arps) with simulation forecasts. Discuss when each approach is appropriate.
4. **Python-based PVT Correlations** — Code Standing, Vasquez-Beggs, Lee correlations. Build a PVT tool.
5. **Waterflood Optimization Model** — Use fractional flow theory to optimize injection patterns. Compare with simulation results.
6. **Enhanced Oil Recovery Screening** — Apply EOR screening criteria (Taber's) to a real reservoir. Recommend the best EOR method with economic justification.

## 12. 5-Year and 10-Year Outlook

### 5-Year (2026–2031)
- **High demand.** Operators need REs for FDP reviews, reserves reporting, and mature field optimization.
- **ML/AI integration** — Proxy models, ensemble history matching, and automated sensitivity analysis becoming standard.
- **CCS modeling** — CO₂ storage capacity estimation uses reservoir engineering fundamentals. Growing demand.
- **Digital twins** — Full-field real-time simulation models connected to production data.

### 10-Year (2026–2036)
- **CCS and hydrogen storage** become major career pathways using identical reservoir modelling skills.
- **Geothermal reservoir engineering** — Modelling heat extraction from subsurface formations.
- **Cloud-based simulation** — HPC reservoir simulations move to cloud (AWS, Azure), democratizing access.
- **Role evolution** — More data science, less manual model building. Engineers who combine domain knowledge + coding thrive.

## 14. In-Demand Sub-Specializations

1. **Reservoir Simulation & Modeling** — The core skill. Engineers who can build, calibrate, and forecast reliably are always in demand.
2. **Reserves Estimation (PRMS/SEC)** — Directly ties to company valuation. High-stakes, high-reward.
3. **Enhanced Oil Recovery (EOR)** — Chemical flood, gas injection, thermal recovery. Critical as fields mature.
4. **CCS / Underground Storage Modeling** — Energy transition driver. Same skills, new application.
5. **Unconventional Reservoirs** — Shale gas/tight oil (Permian, Vaca Muerta). Rate transient analysis, SRV modeling.
6. **Well Testing / Pressure Transient Analysis** — Niche but well-paid specialty.
7. **Geostatistics / Uncertainty Quantification** — Advanced modeling, experimental design, MCMC methods.

## 15. Academia to Industry Transition

1. **MSc is almost required.** Unlike other PE disciplines, reservoir engineering strongly prefers MSc+ candidates.
2. **Publish at SPE.** Even a student paper at ATCE shows analytical depth.
3. **Learn Eclipse or CMG.** If your university doesn't have licenses, use OPM Flow (open-source).
4. **Intern at an operator.** Shell, [TotalEnergies](../../data/company-directory/totalenergies.md), ExxonMobil reservoir teams offer structured internships.
5. **Thesis on simulation** — History matching with ML, EOR screening, CO₂ storage modeling.
6. **Network through SCRF, EAGE, SPE.** Reservoir engineering is a smaller community — word-of-mouth matters.

## 16. Critical Soft Skills

- **Analytical thinking** — You solve partial differential equations for a living. Comfort with abstraction is essential.
- **Uncertainty communication** — Reserves are probabilistic (P10/P50/P90). Executives want certainty; you must explain ranges.
- **Cross-disciplinary integration** — You sit between geologists, petrophysicists, and production engineers. You synthesize.
- **Technical writing** — FDP documents, reserves reports, simulation study reports. Extremely writing-heavy.
- **Presentation skills** — You present reserves estimates to boards, regulators, investors. Must be concise and defensible.
- **Patience with long timescales** — Simulation studies take months. Waterflood results take years.

## 22. Common Mistakes

1. **Relying only on simulation.** Material balance, decline curves, and analytical methods must complement simulation — not replace them.
2. **Over-fitting history matches.** A model that perfectly matches history but has wrong geology is useless for prediction.
3. **Ignoring geological input.** Reservoir engineers who dismiss geological models build wrong simulations.
4. **Not learning to code.** Python scripting for pre/post-processing simulation data is now table stakes.
5. **Staying in one basin.** Carbonate (ME), sandstone (North Sea), unconventional (Permian) — diversity makes you versatile.
6. **Neglecting reserves standards.** If you can't do PRMS-compliant reserves estimates, you're not a complete RE.

## 25. Emerging Technologies

- **AI/ML-assisted history matching** — Ensemble methods (ES-MDA), deep learning proxy models reduce calibration time from months to days.
- **Cloud HPC simulation** — Run 1000+ scenarios on AWS/Azure instead of waiting for local cluster time.
- **Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs)** — Hybrid ML + physics for faster reservoir forecasting.
- **Multi-scale simulation** — Pore-scale → core-scale → field-scale integration.
- **Open-source simulators (OPM)** — Democratizing reservoir simulation, reducing license costs.
- **Digital subsurface twins** — Continuous model updating with real-time production data.
- **CO₂ storage modeling tools** — Specialized simulators for CCS (CMG GEM, Eclipse CCS module).
