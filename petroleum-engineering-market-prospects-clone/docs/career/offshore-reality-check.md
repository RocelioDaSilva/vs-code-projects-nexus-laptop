[Offshore reality](../../isptec/guide/06_Offshore_Reality.md) check — What your first rotation will actually be like

**Purpose:** Cut through the corporate recruiting video. You need to know what it's *actually* like to step onto an offshore rig in Angola. This guide is raw and unfiltered. Read it before you sign that contract.

---

## Part 1: Pre-Mobilization — The Medical & Safety Gauntlet

### SONILS Base, Luanda. 05:15 AM

You've had 4 hours of sleep. Your mother made you promise to call every day. You won't—the Wi-Fi is garbage. You pull up to the [SONILS](../../data/company-directory/sonangol.md) gate on Avenida 4 de Fevereiro in a taxi, nervous and excited. This is it. Your first real offshore job.

**The Breathalyzer Test**
The first thing you do is blow into a machine. If it beeps for *anything* except toothpaste remnants, you are off the manifest. You will be sent home. Your name will be mud with the offshore installation manager (OIM) before you've even seen the ocean. Many engineers have been rejected at this point.

**The Medical Check**
A nurse checks your blood pressure, temperature, and weight. If your BP is elevated (you're nervous—of course it is), she may ask you to sit for 10 minutes and recheck. Blood pressure above 160/100 and you're off the manifest. Diabetes, heart conditions, bad knees—all disqualifying. The offshore medical is no joke.

You'll be weighed in your survival suit. This suit smells like 500 other sweaty people's fear and industrial rubber. The suit is neon orange with reflective strips. Wear it with dignity.

**The Safety Briefing Video**
You'll watch a 20-minute video about offshore safety. Abandon procedures. Lifeboat operations. Emergency beacons. You'll ignore most of it. Don't. When that helicopter ditches in the Congo River mouth—a swirling, debris-filled estuary with a 5-knot current and zero visibility—you have exactly **8 seconds** to find your reference point and deploy your Emergency Breathing System (EBS). The [HUET](../glossary.md) training at CFA Luanda will kick in automatically if you paid attention.

## Part 2: The Helicopter Ride — 2 Hours Over the Atlantic

The helicopter is a **Sikorsky S-76**. It's loud. Deafening. You sit in a jump seat, harness locked, your bag between your feet. The rotor noise means no conversation. You've got your earpiece (comms only), and you stare out the window.

The Angolan coast disappears. Blue water turns to blue haze. Then you see it: the brown plume of the **Congo River** pushing 60 kilometers out into the Atlantic. Silt, plastic bottles, floating logs. That's your reference point if you ditch. You don't want to ditch in that plume.

The helicopter descends. Your ears pop. You see the **helideck** of the rig or [FPSO](../glossary.md) getting larger. It's a steel octagon with a yellow circle painted on it. That circle is your target. The pilot hovers, steadies, and drops you onto the deck.

**The Touchdown**
Rotor still screaming. You unbuckle, grab your bag, and the **Helicopter Landing Officer (HLO)** screams at you to run forward, not backward. Don't walk near the tail rotor. Don't look back. Touch the yellow line on the other side of the helideck. When you do, the helicopter lifts off and banks away.

You just moved from one world into another. Welcome to Block 17. Your heart is pounding. Your boss is standing 20 meters away, waiting to meet you.

## Part 3: Your First 12-Hour Shift (Drilling Focus)

### The Doghouse: Handover at 06:00

You're in the **Drilling Supervisor's office** —the "Doghouse" — a small room covered in printed well schematics, mud tank gauges, and real-time drill string weight displays. The night Company Man looks grey. He's been up for 12 hours managing a well that didn't cooperate.

He points at the drill string weight log. *"We had a pack-off at 03:00. Lost annular pressure. She's held steady for the last 2 hours. But keep an eye on the ECD (Equivalent Circulating Density). If weight in starts to exceed weight out by more than 5 ppg, call me immediately. We might need to go dynamic."*

You have no idea what half of that means yet. You nod. He leaves. Your shift starts now.

### The Rig Floor: 06:30–12:00

**Round 1: The Shaker Room**
Welcome to sensory hell. It's 45°C (113°F). The humidity is 100% from diesel steam. The smell of **Oil-Based Mud (OBM)** is like hot asphalt mixed with diesel and a chemical sweetener. It coats your mouth.

You're here to look at **cuttings**—tiny rock chips coming up through the mud. Are they uniform? Angular (good) or rounded (bad—suggests wellbore cavings)? Is the color changing? Are there traces of **[H2S](../glossary.md)** in the air monitor? That rotten egg smell? If you smell it *at all*, you back away slowly and upwind, not downwind.

Your Angolan **deckhand** (Floorhand) pokes you and points at a sample. *"Cimento. Watch this color change."* You see the cutting bed change from grey to reddish-brown. You've just entered a new rock formation. Your brain is working at 200% capacity.

**Round 2: The [Mud Logging](../skills/07-mudlogging.md) Unit**
Air-conditioned. Blessed relief. Here, the **Mud Logger** (often a Scottish veteran or a trained Angolan guy) watches gas chromatographs and real-time sensor data. You look at the **Total Gas** reading on the screen. The baseline is green. If it spikes, you hit hydrocarbons. A sustained high reading means you're in the reservoir.

*"That's what we came for,"* the Mud Logger says matter-of-factly, pointing at a sustained gas spike on the chart. You've just witnessed a wildcat exploratory well hitting the sweet spot. The room goes quiet for a moment. Everyone understands the money that just happened.

**Round 3: Back to the Drilling Office**
You stare at the logs: **ROP (Rate of Penetration)**, **WOB (Weight on Bit)**, **Torque**, **Pump Pressure**, **Standpipe Temperature**. The ROP just dropped from 20 m/hr to 2 m/hr. The torque is spiking. You know, in your gut, you just hit a hard stringer or the bit is dulling.

Your supervisor walks in. *"What do you see?"*

You point at the screen. *"Torque's up, ROP's down. Either a hard formation or bit dulling?"*

He nods. *"Could be either. But look at the weight on bit—it's been steady. If it was formation hardness, the bit would climb. This is a dulled bit. We're going to trip out and change it out in about 3 hours."*

**Tripping** is when you pull the entire drill string out of the hole to change the bit. It takes 4–6 hours. You just lost 6 hours of productive drilling time and USD 50,000. This is the reality of the rig: constant small decisions adding up to big money.

### The Galley: 12:00–13:00

The food is the best part of your day. It has to be. There's **Angolan Muamba de Galinha with Funge** (chicken stew with cassava mash) right next to **Fish and Chips** for the Brits and expats. Pasta. Fresh fruit: passion fruit mousse.

**Unwritten rule:** Do not talk about the well at the dinner table. The toolpusher is trying to forget that the BOP (Blowout Preventer) test is due tomorrow. Leave him alone. Eat your food. Shut up.

Your cabin mate, a [Halliburton](../../data/company-directory/halliburton.md) **cementing engineer** from Scotland, tells you about the **mud volcanoes** he's seen offshore Equatorial Guinea. You listen. You eat. You try to relax your shoulders, which have been clenched for 6 hours.

### The IADC Report: 13:00–18:00

This is your **main administrative task** as a junior engineer: the **IADC Daily Report** —a 20-page Excel nightmare that codes every 15 minutes of the last 24 hours.

You have to decide: Is this time **D1 (Drilling)**, **T1 (Tripping)**, **W (Waiting on Weather)**, or **NPT (Non-Productive Time)**?

This is political. If you code something as NPT because rig equipment broke, the OIM will want to know why you're making his rig look bad to the operator's office in Luanda. If you code it as "Drilling" when you were actually stuck for 2 hours, you're lying and skewing the performance metrics for the whole shift.

You'll sweat over this report. You'll ask your supervisor 5 times if you're coding it right. You'll get it wrong the first time. The senior Company Man will correct it, not unkindly, and tell you: *"Next time, ask before you're unsure. The metrics matter."*

### End of Shift: 18:00–19:00

You see the night shift Company Man walking into the Doghouse, fresh as a daisy. The **ROP** just dropped from 15 m/hr to 1 m/hr. The **Torque** is spiking again. You know, in your gut, you're about to get stuck.

You hand over: *"She's about to get ugly. Bit is definitely dulled. Recommend trip out within 2 hours. Good luck."*

You walk out of the office and the vibration of the rig floor follows you down the corridor. Your back aches. Your ears are ringing. You've been on your feet for 12 hours straight with 20 minutes for lunch.

## Part 4: Off-Shift Reality (The Other 12 Hours)

### The Cabin: 19:00–20:00

If you're lucky, you share a **2-man cabin**. If you're on a crowded [FPSO](../glossary.md), it's a **4-man**. If you're the new guy, you're on **hot-bunking**—you sleep in the same bed as the day-shift engineer. The mattress is still warm.

Strip the bed. Shower. Spray your underwear and socks with vinegar (it reduces bacteria, and the smell prevents mold). Your body is coated in **OBM** (oil-based mud). It doesn't wash off easily. It takes 2 showers.

You collapse onto the bunk. The sound you've heard for 12 hours underground—the **thump-thump-thump** of the mud pumps—continues through the night. You sleep to this rhythm. After 3 days, silence is actually harder to sleep in.

### Connectivity & The Angolan WhatsApp Mafia: 20:00–22:00

You check your phone. **RigNet** is the Wi-Fi. It's slow. It's shared by 150 rig staff. You get bursts of speed: 2 Mbps if you're lucky. Video calls are impossible.

Your mother has called 3 times. Your girlfriend has sent a message: *"Call me?"* You compose a message on WhatsApp: *"All good, just landed. Network is terrible but will call on rest day. Love you."* It takes 8 minutes to send because of RigNet overload.

You check LinkedIn. One of your classmates just got hired as a Field Engineer at [Baker Hughes](../../data/company-directory/bakerhughes.md). You feel a pang of jealousy for exactly 10 seconds, then you remember: you're offshore. You're making it happen.

Your cabin mate comes in. He's Scottish, worked offshore for 15 years. *"You'll get used to it,"* he says, reading your tired face. *"First week is the hardest. After 3 weeks, you'll wonder why you were nervous."*

You sleep.

## Part 5: The Rest Day

### What It Means

Angola rotations are typically **28/28** (28 days on, 28 days off) or **21/21**. Every 7 days on rotation, you get 1 **rest day**—a day where you don't work, but you're still offshore.

### Day 7 of Your First Rotation: A Rest Day

You wake up at 07:00 naturally, without an alarm. Your body is still on rig time, so you're already awake. You have breakfast alone—no 12-hour shift pressure. You eat slowly.

You spend the morning in your cabin. You video-call your family on the satellite phone (not RigNet; the sat phone works better). The call costs USD 2/minute, so you keep it to 10 minutes. Your mother tells you about home. You tell her you're fine. You are, mostly. You miss solid ground.

Around noon, you go to the **Recreation Room**. There's a TV showing football (soccer). Angola vs. Brazil. The room is packed with Angolan deckands and toolpushers. You sit quietly and watch. During half-time, someone cracks open a beer. You abstain (offshore rules: alcohol only in your cabin, after shift, and in moderation).

In the afternoon, you message your family again. You work out a bit: 20 push-ups, some stretching. You read a technical paper on well-bore stability. You work on your [IWCF](../glossary.md) refresh. Time moves slowly on a rest day.

By evening, you're ready to go back to work. Inactivity is actually harder than the grind.

## Part 6: The Reality Check—What Happens When Things Go Wrong

### Well Control Event (Kick)

You're in your 10th day. It's 23:00. You're in the Doghouse studying mud weight when the **[well control](../skills/01-well-control.md) alarm** goes off—a loud buzzer that every single person on the rig can hear.

The floor hands immediately close in the **Blowout Preventer (BOP)** at the seafloor. Your supervisor yells: *"We're securing the well! Nobody move until the OIM briefs us!"*

Your heart is in your throat. This is what [IWCF](../glossary.md) Well Control training is for. This is real.

The OIM briefs the team within 3 minutes: a kick was detected (pressure into the well from the formation), the BOP is closed, circulation is being established through a **choke** and **kill line**. The situation is under control.

But for the next 6 hours, you're in the **Drilling Supervisor's Office** watching pressure gauges, calculating **Kill Fluid Weight**, and preparing to execute a **Volumetric Method** or **Wait and Weight** procedure to safely kill the well without causing a blowout.

This is the hardest night of your rotation. You don't sleep. You do the calculations 5 times to make sure they're right. If you make a math error, you could kill someone.

By 06:00, the well is dead. No hydrocarbons flowed. The BOP worked. Everyone is safe. The OIM buys the crew coffee and says: *"Good job, lads. That's why we train."*

You sit on your bunk and shake for an hour.

## Part 7: Fatigue, Loneliness & Mental Health

### Week 3 of 4

You're tired. Bone-tired. It's not the physical work—you're young, you can handle physical work. It's the **cognitive load**. You're making decisions that cost money. You're learning every day. Your brain is always "on."

You miss home. You miss your girlfriend. You miss solid ground. Your cabin mate is a good guy, but you haven't had a conversation longer than 5 minutes that wasn't work-related in 3 weeks.

One evening, you sit on deck (off the hot zone) and watch the sun set over the Atlantic. The sky is pink and purple. A pod of dolphins passes the rig. For 10 minutes, you forget about mud weights and ROP and IADC reports. You just watch the ocean.

This is why you came.

### The Mental Health Reality

Offshore rotational work is **psychologically taxing**. The industry doesn't talk about it enough. Some engineers struggle with:
- **Isolation:** No real social life, no dating, limited privacy
- **Fatigue:** Sleeping to the rhythm of machinery, never fully rested
- **Pressure:** Decisions that affect safety and financial outturns
- **Separation:** Relationships suffer. Marriages break up. Children grow while you're away.

**How to handle it:**
- Build strong relationships with your cabin mates and fellow engineers
- Write in a journal every few days
- Maintain a video call schedule with family (same time, same day)
- Read, exercise, watch movies—hobbies matter
- Talk to your supervisor or the rig's medic if you're struggling mentally (it's not weakness; it's self-care)
- Plan your rest days in advance (e.g., "I'll call my girlfriend on Wednesday, gym on Thursday")
- Understand that homesickness peaks in weeks 2–3, then improves in week 4

## Part 8: The Win — Your First Well Completed

### Day 21: Well Spud to TD (Total Depth)

You've been offshore for 21 days. The well you helped drill is now complete at **3,500 meters depth**. The reservoir was logged, perforated, and tested. Production rates are **5,200 barrels of oil per day**—better than the forecast.

The OIM announces it over the PA system: *"Well XX-01 is secure and producible. Good work, everyone. See you on the next well."*

Your supervisor comes to your cabin and shakes your hand. *"You did good work. You asked the right questions. You learned fast. The operator's toolpusher specifically asked about you. Said you were attentive and didn't make excuses. Just wanted you to know."*

You feel something you haven't felt in weeks: **pride**.

You're part of something real. This well will produce oil for years. The revenue it generates will fund infrastructure in Angola. Your work mattered.

---

## Part 9: Final Thoughts

Offshore rotational work is **hard**. It's isolating. It's mentally and physically demanding. There will be moments where you regret it deeply.

But there will also be moments—watching the sun set over the Atlantic, getting positive feedback from an experienced engineer, solving a technical problem that saves the company money—where you feel genuinely alive and purposeful.

The engineers who succeed offshore are not the smartest, the strongest, or the most technically brilliant. They are the **resilient**. They are the ones who can handle discomfort, accept boredom, push through fatigue, and find meaning in the work.

If that's you, welcome. You'll do well here.

**If it's not you, it's okay to realize that now, before you're two weeks into a rotation with no way out. That's wisdom, not weakness.**

Now go mobilize.
