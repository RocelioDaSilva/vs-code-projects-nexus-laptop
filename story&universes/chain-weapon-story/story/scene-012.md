The letter arrived in a cream envelope with the Academy seal pressed in wax the color of old blood. Professor Aldwyn had received enough institutional correspondence to read the weight of an envelope before opening it—thin meant routine, medium meant administrative, and heavy meant someone had decided something about your life. This one was heavy.

He opened it at his desk, between the stack of ungraded exercises and the cold cup of tisane he had forgotten to drink. Outside, the courtyard bell marked the third hour of afternoon, and somewhere in the dormitory wing a student was practicing scales on a reed flute, badly.

*Dear Professor Aldwyn,*

*The Academic Standards Committee is pleased to inform you that your name has been forwarded for consideration as Deputy Chair of Curriculum Integration. This position carries an enhanced stipend, faculty housing in the East Court, and a seat on the Advisory Board for Magical Pedagogy.*

*A representative of the Committee will call on you at your convenience to discuss the terms of appointment.*

*With esteem,*
*The Office of the Provost*

He read it twice. Then he set it down and pressed his thumb against the seal, feeling the ridged wax, and thought about what the letter did not say.

The Advisory Board for Magical Pedagogy did not exist six months ago. He knew this because he had served on the Curriculum Committee for nine years and would have noticed its creation. It was new—and new committees, at the Academy, did not appear from consensus. They appeared when someone with power needed a structure that looked institutional.

Aldwyn was fifty-three years old. He had been at the Academy for twenty-two of those years. His salary covered his rooms, his wife's medications, his daughter's tuition at the preparatory school in the capital. It did not cover the preparatory school's quarterly increase, or the specialist who had recommended his wife see a nerve-thread healer in the spring, or the growing cost of lamp-oil since the border tensions began.

He was not poor. He was precisely solvent enough that the difference between his current position and the one described in the letter was the difference between worry and its absence.

The representative came the next morning. Aldwyn had expected a proctor or a senior colleague. Instead, a woman arrived in a grey coat with silver fastenings—not military, not quite academic, something between the two that conveyed authority without identifying its source.

"Professor Aldwyn. Thank you for seeing me."

"I don't recall being given a choice."

She smiled. It was a competent smile, neither warm nor cool, calibrated to communicate that she appreciated directness without being disarmed by it.

"The position is genuine," she said, settling into the chair across from his desk without waiting for invitation. "The stipend is real. The housing is available immediately—East Court, third floor, the suite overlooking the fountain. Your wife would find it more comfortable than your current arrangement."

She knew about his wife. Of course she knew about his wife. He felt the knowledge settle into the room like a third presence, quiet and patient.

"What does the position actually involve?"

"Curriculum review. You would work with your colleagues to ensure that magical instruction aligns with current best practices. Standardization of technique nomenclature. Assessment framework modernization. The kind of work you've advocated for in faculty meetings, if I understand your record correctly."

She did. He had argued for curriculum standardization for years—railed against the patchwork of regional traditions that left students with incompatible techniques and no common vocabulary. It was good work. Necessary work. And hearing his own arguments returned to him in this context made him feel the way a man feels when he discovers that the path he's been walking leads to a door he did not choose.

"And the Advisory Board?"

"Advises. Reviews proposed changes to magical pedagogy across the Academy's satellite programs. Provides recommendations to the Provost."

"Recommendations that become policy."

"Recommendations that inform policy. The Provost retains final authority."

Aldwyn looked at her. She was very good. Everything she said was true in the way a map is true—accurate in outline, silent about the terrain.

"Who funds the Board?" he asked.

"The Academy's institutional partners."

"Which are?"

"I'm not at liberty to disclose the full roster. Standard practice for multi-patron funding structures."

"I've served on committees for twenty-two years. We've always known our patrons."

She held his gaze. The silence lasted long enough for him to understand that this was the boundary—that everything before this silence was the landscape of the offer, and everything after it was the landscape of the conditions. She was giving him the courtesy of recognizing the border. Whether that made it better or worse, he could not decide.

"The Board's work benefits every student in this Academy," she said. "Your involvement would lend it credibility. Your colleagues respect you. Your publications are cited. You are, frankly, the kind of person whose name on a letterhead makes other names unnecessary."

"You're telling me I'm a stamp."

"I'm telling you that good people doing good work is how institutions improve. The alternative is that the position is filled by someone who cares less about students than you do."

This was the argument he could not escape. He had encountered it before—in faculty politics, in marriage, in every situation where the choice was not between good and bad but between imperfect and worse. If he refused, someone else would accept. Someone less careful. Someone who would not notice, or would not care, when the curriculum review began to look less like standardization and more like conformity.

He thought about his wife. The nerve-thread healer cost eighty silver per consultation, and she needed three.

He thought about his daughter. The preparatory school's reputation would open doors that his salary could not.

He thought about the student practicing scales on the reed flute, getting the fingering wrong on every third note, and he thought: *That student will learn or not learn regardless of who sits on this Board. But my daughter will eat or not eat based on whether I sign this letter.*

"I'll need to see the terms in writing," he said.

"Of course." The woman produced a second envelope—already prepared, already sealed—and set it on his desk beside the first. She had brought it with her. She had known he would ask. She had known he would agree.

"One more thing," she said, standing. "The Board meets monthly. Attendance is expected. And Professor—" She paused at the door with the courtesy of someone who has rehearsed their exits. "We appreciate discretion regarding the composition of the Board. It's simply easier to work without the noise of public scrutiny. I'm sure you understand."

He understood. That was the worst part. He understood perfectly.

She left. Aldwyn sat at his desk with two cream envelopes and a cold cup of tisane. The reed flute had gone quiet. The courtyard bell marked the hour, and he thought about how many small decisions, stacked in sequence, it takes to build a cage that looks from the inside like a career.

He signed the letter that evening, after dinner, after helping his wife to bed, after checking the balance ledger where he tracked their expenses in a column so narrow it looked like a spine. He signed it with the pen his daughter had given him for his fiftieth birthday—the one with the Academy crest on the cap—and he folded it into the return envelope and sealed it and set it on the hall table for the morning courier.

Then he went to bed and lay awake for three hours thinking about the word *discretion* and how it sounded, in the dark, exactly like the word *complicity*, and how the difference between them was a matter of lighting.
