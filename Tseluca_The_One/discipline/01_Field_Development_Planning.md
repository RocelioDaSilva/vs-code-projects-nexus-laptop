# Field Development Planning
## The Discipline That Connects Everything

---

## What Is Field Development Planning?

Field Development Planning (FDP) is the process of designing how an oil or gas discovery will be developed into a producing field. It is the highest-level technical role in upstream petroleum engineering — the place where every discipline meets.

An FDP answers these questions:
- How many wells do we need to drill, and where?
- What facilities do we need (FPSO, fixed platform, subsea tieback)?
- How much oil and gas will we produce, and over what time period?
- How much will it cost to build and operate?
- Is it economically viable given current oil prices?
- What are the risks, and how do we manage them?
- What does ANPG require for local content and regulatory approval?

The FDP document is submitted to the government (ANPG in Angola) for approval before the operator can develop the field. It is the most consequential engineering document in the industry.

---

## The FDP Process: Phase by Phase

Field development does not happen in one step. It follows a structured process from discovery to first oil.

### Phase 1: Appraisal
**Trigger:** A discovery well has been drilled and oil/gas found.

**What happens:**
- Appraisal wells are drilled to map the reservoir extent
- Seismic data is reprocessed and reinterpreted
- Core and fluid samples are taken
- A preliminary resource estimate is made (STOIIP, GIIP)

**PE role:** Reservoir engineers build the static geological model and calculate volumes. Drilling engineers plan the appraisal wells. Petrophysicists interpret the log data from the appraisal wells.

**Angola example:** When TotalEnergies drilled the Kaombo discovery on Block 32 in 2009–2012, they ran a 3-year appraisal campaign with 8 appraisal wells to understand the reservoir architecture before committing to development.

---

### Phase 2: Concept Selection (Pre-FEED)

**Trigger:** Appraisal confirms sufficient reserves to justify development.

**What happens:**
- Engineering teams evaluate multiple development concepts
- Concept A: FPSO with subsea wells vs Concept B: Floating storage with fixed platform vs Concept C: Tieback to existing infrastructure
- Each concept is evaluated for: technical feasibility, cost (CAPEX/OPEX), schedule, risk, and economics (NPV)
- The preferred concept is selected

**Key decision drivers:**
- Water depth → determines hull type (FPSO, semi-sub, TLP, SPAR)
- Reservoir fluid properties → determines topside processing requirements
- Distance to infrastructure → determines tieback vs standalone
- CAPEX budget → operator's financial position
- ANPG local content rules → favours solutions with Angolan content

**Angola example:** Kaombo selected a twin-FPSO solution (Kaombo Norte and Kaombo Sul) — each processing 115,000 bbl/day — because the field was too large for a single FPSO and spread too wide for a fixed platform. This was not obvious at the start. It was the result of concept selection engineering.

---

### Phase 3: FEED (Front End Engineering Design)

**Trigger:** Concept selected and approved by operator management and ANPG.

**What happens:**
- Detailed engineering begins for the selected concept
- Wells team finalises the drilling program (number of wells, trajectories, schedule)
- Subsurface team finalises the reservoir model and production forecast
- Facilities team designs the FPSO or platform topsides
- Subsea team designs the SURF (flowlines, risers, umbilicals, manifolds, trees)
- Cost estimates become more precise (±15–20% accuracy at FEED end)
- Final Investment Decision (FID) is made

**FID:** This is when the operator formally commits to spending the capital to develop the field. FEED ends with FID. After FID, construction and procurement begin.

**ANPG role:** ANPG reviews and approves the FDP before or at FID. Their concerns: local content compliance, environmental impact, royalty and tax structure, reservoir management requirements.

---

### Phase 4: Execution (Construction, Installation, Drilling)

**Trigger:** FID taken and contracts placed.

**What happens:**
- FPSO hull is built (often South Korea or China)
- Subsea equipment is manufactured (trees at TechnipFMC/OneSubsea; umbilicals at Nexans/Aker)
- SURF is installed (Subsea 7, Saipem, TechnipFMC vessels)
- Development wells are drilled (drilling campaign begins 1-2 years after FID)
- FPSO topsides commissioning occurs

**Timeline:** Typically 4–7 years from FID to First Oil for a major Angola deepwater project.

---

### Phase 5: Production and Optimisation

**Trigger:** First Oil achieved.

**What happens:**
- Production ramps up to plateau
- Reservoir management plan is executed (well interventions, pressure surveillance, injection)
- Production optimisation is ongoing (lift optimisation, FPSO debottlenecking)
- Field reviews and reserves updates occur annually
- Late-life management decisions made (infill drilling, EOR, tieback of nearby discoveries)

---

### Phase 6: Late Life and Abandonment (P&A)

**Trigger:** Economic limit reached or regulatory requirement.

**What happens:**
- Wells are plugged and abandoned (P&A) — cement barriers placed in the wellbore
- Subsea equipment is removed (or decommissioned in-place)
- FPSO is demobilised
- Site is restored

**Growing importance in Angola:** Blocks 0 (Cabinda) and Block 3 have been producing since the 1970s and 1980s. These fields are now entering late life. P&A engineering is a growing discipline in Angola.

## The FDP Document Structure

An FDP document typically contains the following sections. Understanding this structure tells you what each discipline contributes:

```
FDP TABLE OF CONTENTS

1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
   - Discovery summary
   - Development concept
   - Recoverable reserves (P1, P2)
   - First Oil date, plateau rate, field life
   - CAPEX and OPEX summary
   - NPV and economics

2. GEOLOGY AND GEOPHYSICS
   - Seismic interpretation
   - Structural maps
   - Stratigraphic model
   ← CONTRIBUTED BY: Geologists, Petrophysicists

3. RESERVOIR ENGINEERING
   - Static model (STOIIP, GIIP)
   - Dynamic model (production forecast)
   - Drive mechanism
   - Recovery factor and recovery mechanisms
   - Production profile (P10/P50/P90 cases)
   - Pressure maintenance strategy
   ← CONTRIBUTED BY: Reservoir Engineers, Dynamic Modellers

4. WELLS
   - Well count and phasing
   - Well trajectories and target coordinates
   - Casing program
   - Completion design (sand control, artificial lift)
   - Drilling schedule and rig requirements
   - Well cost per type
   ← CONTRIBUTED BY: Drilling Engineers, Completions Engineers

5. SUBSEA AND FACILITIES
   - FPSO design and capacity
   - Subsea architecture (trees, manifolds, flowlines, risers)
   - Umbilical design
   - Mooring system
   - Offloading system
   ← CONTRIBUTED BY: Subsea Engineers, Facilities Engineers, Process Engineers

6. PRODUCTION FORECAST
   - Oil/gas/water production profiles
   - FPSO utilisation rates
   - Production efficiency assumptions
   ← CONTRIBUTED BY: Reservoir + Production Engineers jointly

7. ECONOMICS
   - CAPEX estimate (breakdown by category)
   - OPEX estimate ($/bbl)
   - Production Sharing Agreement model
   - Cash flow model (year-by-year)
   - NPV (base case + sensitivity)
   - IRR
   - Breakeven oil price
   ← CONTRIBUTED BY: Petroleum Economists, Commercial Team

8. UNCERTAINTY AND RISK
   - Geological risk (reservoir quality, extent)
   - Technical risk (well delivery, FPSO performance)
   - Commercial risk (oil price, cost escalation)
   - P10/P50/P90 economic outcomes
   ← CONTRIBUTED BY: All disciplines, Risk Manager

9. HEALTH, SAFETY AND ENVIRONMENT
   - ESHIA (Environmental, Social, Health Impact Assessment)
   - Major hazard register
   - Emergency response plan
   ← CONTRIBUTED BY: HSE Engineers, Environmental specialists

10. LOCAL CONTENT AND REGULATORY
    - ANPG local content commitment
    - Angolan workforce development plan
    - Regulatory approvals timeline
    ← CONTRIBUTED BY: Local Content Manager, Legal, Regulatory
```

## Production Profile Design

The production forecast is the heart of the FDP. Everything else — facilities size, well count, economics — depends on it.

### How a Production Profile Is Built

**Step 1: Define the plateau rate.**
The plateau rate is the maximum sustained production rate. It is determined by:
- Reservoir deliverability (maximum rate wells can produce without excessive drawdown)
- FPSO processing capacity (separators, gas compression, water injection)
- Number of producer wells available

**Step 2: Design the plateau duration.**
Plateau duration depends on:
- Number of producer wells
- Rate of natural pressure decline
- Pressure support (water injection, gas injection, gas lift)

**Step 3: Design the decline.**
After plateau, production declines. Typical Angola deepwater decline rates:
- Exponential decline rate: 10–25%/year depending on drive mechanism and well count
- Hyperbolic decline common in strong water drive reservoirs (b = 0.3–0.8)

**Worked Example:**
A simplified Block 32 satellite development:
- 8 producer wells, plateau rate 30,000 bbl/day/well = 240,000 bbl/day total
- FPSO capacity: 250,000 bbl/day (matches plateau with margin)
- Plateau duration: 4 years (water injection maintains pressure)
- Decline after plateau: 15%/year exponential

Year 1: 240,000 bbl/day → Annual production: 87.6 MMbbl
Year 2: 240,000 bbl/day (plateau)
Year 3: 240,000 bbl/day (plateau)
Year 4: 240,000 bbl/day (plateau)
Year 5: 240,000 × (1 - 0.15) = 204,000 bbl/day
Year 6: 204,000 × (1 - 0.15) = 173,400 bbl/day
...

Total field life: 25 years. Cumulative production (P50): ~600 MMbbl.

This production profile then feeds into the economics model.

## Concept Selection: FPSO vs Platform vs Tieback

This is the most consequential decision in an FDP. The concept chosen determines: CAPEX magnitude, schedule, contractor base, local content opportunity, and ultimately economics.

### Decision Framework

**Water depth:**
- < 100m: Fixed platform (Cabinda Block 0 style)
- 100–500m: Tension leg platform (TLP) or fixed platform (at shallow end)
- 500–3,000m: FPSO → dominant concept in Angola
- > 3,000m: Possible but requires ultra-deepwater mooring design

**Reservoir size and production rate:**
- < 50 MMbbl recoverable: Likely a tieback to existing FPSO (economics don't support standalone)
- 50–200 MMbbl: Standalone small FPSO or charter FPSO
- > 200 MMbbl: New-build FPSO justified

**Distance to existing infrastructure:**
- < 30 km to existing FPSO: Tieback is strongly preferred (saves $500M–$1B CAPEX)
- 30–70 km: Tieback possible with flow assurance management (long tieback)
- > 70 km: Standalone development typically preferred

**Angola-specific consideration:**
- All Angola deepwater is 1,000–2,500m water depth → FPSO is always the production vessel
- The decision becomes: new-build FPSO vs charter FPSO vs tieback to existing FPSO

## Local Content in the FDP

ANPG requires operators to demonstrate how Angolan companies and workers will participate in the development. This is specified in the FDP.

**Typical local content commitments in an Angola FDP:**
- PAENAL (Porto Amboim) used for fabrication of subsea structures
- Sonamet (Lobito) used for pipeline/spool fabrication
- Angolan-flagged vessels used for supply boat operations where available
- Sonangol Logistics and service companies used for catering, cleaning, logistics
- Angolan national workforce targets: 50%+ by Year 5 of operations
- Angolan graduate training programs (e.g., TotalEnergies YGP)

Understanding local content requirements is important for any Angolan petroleum engineer. It is one of the reasons the ISPTEC degree carries weight — operators must hire locally.

## FDP Roles for ISPTEC Graduates

You will not lead an FDP as a fresh graduate. But you will contribute to one much sooner than you think.

**Entry-level FDP contributions:**
- Reservoir team: Run Eclipse/CMG scenarios for production forecast cases
- Drilling team: Update well cost estimates, check casing design against trajectory
- Economics team: Update the cash flow model with new cost data
- Subsurface team: Update the static model with new appraisal well data

**Mid-level FDP contributions (Year 5–10):**
- Lead a discipline chapter of the FDP document
- Coordinate between your discipline and adjacent teams
- Present your section to operator management and ANPG

**Senior FDP role (Year 10+):**
- FDP Project Manager or Lead
- Responsible for the entire document
- Interface with ANPG, partner IOCs, and senior management

The Field Development Planner is effectively the conductor of the petroleum engineering orchestra. Getting there requires depth in at least one discipline and breadth across most of them.

## Angola-Specific FDP Case Study: Kaombo

The Kaombo development on Block 32 is the largest project TotalEnergies has ever operated in Angola. Key facts you should know as an Angolan PE:

- **Discovery:** 2009–2012 (multiple wells)
- **FID:** 2014
- **Concept:** Twin FPSO (Norte and Sul), each with 15 production wells and 7 water injection wells
- **Total well count:** ~44 wells (producers + injectors)
- **CAPEX:** ~$16 billion
- **First Oil:** Kaombo Norte: July 2018, Kaombo Sul: February 2019
- **Peak production:** ~230,000 bbl/day combined
- **Contractor involvement:** TechnipFMC (trees and SURF), Saipem (heavy lift installation), SBM (FPSO conversion), PAENAL (local fabrication)
- **ANPG local content highlight:** PAENAL fabricated the manifolds and pipeline structures

This is not just history. Kaombo is currently producing, and the lessons from its development are being applied to the next generation of Angola deepwater projects.

## The One Document You Should Write Before You Graduate

For your Projecto Final (Projecto I or II), consider designing a simplified FDP for a fictitious Angola deepwater discovery. Use:
- A defined STOIIP (say 300 MMbbl)
- A concept selection exercise (FPSO tieback vs standalone)
- A production forecast (using Arps decline equations from ER I)
- A basic cash flow model (from Engenharia Económica)
- An ANPG local content analysis

This project will demonstrate more integrated petroleum engineering thinking than almost any other ISPTEC final project topic. It is also directly relevant to what operators do every day — making it an extremely powerful interview tool.
