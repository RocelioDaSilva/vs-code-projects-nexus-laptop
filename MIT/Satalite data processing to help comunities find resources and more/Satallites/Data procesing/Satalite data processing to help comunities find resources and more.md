# Satelites

## what are they

### 1. Natural Satellites

These are naturally occurring celestial bodies.

- **Example**: **Earth's Moon** is a natural satellite of the Earth. Other planets have them too, like Jupiter's moons (Ganymede, Europa) and Saturn's moons (Titan).
    

### 2. Artificial Satellites

These are human-made machines launched into orbit by rockets. When people say "satellites" today, they usually mean these. They are built for specific purposes.

|Type of Artificial Satellite|Primary Purpose|Common Uses|
|---|---|---|
|**Communication**|Transmit signals across long distances.|Television, radio, telephone, internet (e.g., AngoSat-2).|
|**Earth Observation**|Take pictures and collect data about our planet.|Weather forecasting, mapping, monitoring crops/forests, disaster response.|
|**Navigation**|Provide precise location and timing signals.|GPS for phones, cars, ships, and airplanes.|
|**Scientific**|Observe space or conduct experiments.|Space telescopes (like Hubble), research on microgravity.|

In short: they are **orbiting tools**. Natural ones are like moons, and artificial ones are like flying computers in space that help us communicate, observe, and navigate.
## how do they work 

Satellites work by balancing two key principles: **falling toward Earth** and **moving sideways so fast that they miss it**. This creates a stable, continuous path around the planet called an **orbit**.

Here’s a breakdown of how they achieve and maintain this, and what keeps them functioning:

### 1. The Core Principle: Staying in Orbit
A satellite doesn't stay up because it's "beyond gravity"—Earth's gravity is always pulling it down. The secret is its incredible **horizontal speed** (about 28,000 km/h for Low Earth Orbit).

*   **Imagine throwing a ball**: It arcs and hits the ground.
*   **Throw it harder**: It goes farther before falling.
*   **Throw it at just the right, incredibly high speed**: The ball's arc matches the curvature of the Earth. It falls *toward* the planet but keeps *missing* it. This is orbit.

### 2. The Machine Itself: Key Subsystems
To perform their mission and survive in space, all satellites need these core systems:

| Subsystem | What It Does | Why It's Critical |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Propulsion** | Small thrusters for adjusting position or orbit. | Corrects launch drift, avoids debris, repositions for mission. |
| **Power** | Solar panels convert sunlight to electricity; batteries store it for eclipses. | Provides energy for all computers, instruments, and communication. |
| **Attitude Control** | Wheels or thrusters that point the satellite precisely. | Keeps cameras pointed at Earth, antennas aimed at ground stations, solar panels at the Sun. |
| **Thermal Control** | Insulation, heaters, and radiators. | Protects electronics from extreme cold (in shadow) and intense heat (in sunlight). |
| **Communication** | Antennas that send data to and receive commands from Earth. | This is the satellite's only link to its controllers. |
| **Payload** | The mission-specific instruments (cameras, sensors, transponders). | This is the entire reason for the satellite's existence (e.g., to image crops or broadcast TV). |

### 3. The Ground Connection: Mission Control
A satellite is useless without its team on Earth.
*   **Ground Stations**: Large antennas send commands ("take a picture of this area") and receive the satellite's data.
*   **Control Centers**: Engineers monitor the satellite's health (power, temperature) and process the raw data into usable information, like maps of deforestation.

### 4. The Different "Jobs": By Orbit Type
How a satellite works is also defined by *where* it's placed:

*   **Low Earth Orbit (LEO - ~300-2000 km)**: Fast orbit (90 minutes per loop). Perfect for **Earth observation** (like imaging Angola's farms or coastlines) because it's close and gets high detail.
*   **Geostationary Orbit (GEO - ~36,000 km)**: Orbits at the speed Earth rotates, so it appears **fixed** over one spot (like over Central Africa). Ideal for **communications and weather monitoring** (like AngoSat-2 for TV/Internet).
*   **Medium Earth Orbit (MEO - ~20,000 km)**: Used mainly for **navigation** systems like GPS.

**In essence**: A satellite is a carefully balanced machine, hurled into space at a precise speed. It uses its subsystems to stay alive, pointed correctly, and powered, while its specialized payload (camera, radio) collects information and sends it back to us on Earth.

## what info do they gather

Satellites gather a vast range of information, and its use depends entirely on the instruments they carry. Here’s a breakdown of the major types of information and their transformative applications:

### 📡 Information Gathered & Its Practical Uses

| Type of Information | How It's Gathered (Sensor Type) | Primary Uses & Applications |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Visual & Multispectral Imagery** | **Optical Cameras** (capturing visible light and other wavelengths like infrared). | **Agriculture:** Monitoring crop health, predicting yields.<br>**Environment:** Tracking deforestation, urban sprawl.<br>**Disaster Response:** Mapping flood/storm damage. |
| **Radar & Microwave Data** | **Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR)** (actively sends and receives microwave signals). | **All-Weather Monitoring:** Seeing through clouds, day or night (crucial for tropics).<br>**Surface Change:** Detecting illegal mining, oil spills, ground subsidence.<br>**Soil Moisture:** Vital for drought assessment and farming. |
| **Communication & Broadcast Signals** | **Radio Frequency Transponders** (receiving, amplifying, and retransmitting signals). | **Global Connectivity:** Enabling TV, radio, internet, and phone services in remote areas.<br>**Data Relay:** Transmitting data from other satellites or remote sensors to Earth. |
| **Precise Location & Timing Signals** | **Atomic Clocks & Transmitters** (sending highly accurate time codes). | **Navigation:** Powering GPS in phones, cars, and ships.<br>**Precision Agriculture:** Guiding automated farm equipment.<br>**Financial Systems:** Time-stamping banking transactions. |
| **Atmospheric & Weather Data** | **Spectrometers, Sounders, Altimeters.** | **Weather Forecasting:** Measuring temperature, humidity, storm formation.<br>**Climate Science:** Tracking greenhouse gases, sea level rise, ice sheet melt. |
| **Scientific Data (Space Environment)** | **Magnetometers, Particle Detectors, Telescopes.** | **Space Weather Alerts:** Monitoring solar storms that can knock out power grids.<br>**Astronomy:** Observing distant galaxies without atmospheric interference. |

### 💡 Connecting Information to Real-World Impact
The power of satellite data lies in turning raw information into actionable knowledge. For a country like Angola:

*   A **multispectral image** showing poor crop health can prompt an early warning to farmers in a specific province.
*   **Radar data** revealing new ground movement near a diamond mine can trigger a compliance inspection.
*   **Communication capacity** from a satellite like **AngoSat-2** can directly provide internet access to schools and clinics.

**In short, satellites transform raw data about our planet and atmosphere into critical information for managing resources, ensuring security, connecting societies, and advancing science.**

## Where do they gather it what info do they gather and what is the use of this diferent information

Satellites collect information not by country, but by following specific **orbital paths** around Earth. The "accuracy per country" primarily depends on the **satellite's sensor technology** and the **availability of ground-based data** for calibration, rather than national borders.

Here is a breakdown of how and where different satellites gather data:

| Collection Pattern | Typical Orbits Used | Type of Data Collected |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Global Swath Coverage** | Low Earth Orbit (LEO), Polar Orbit | Weather, climate, low-resolution land mapping. Sensors scan wide areas. |
| **Targeted "Point-and-Shoot"** | Low Earth Orbit (LEO) | Very high-resolution imagery for specific cities or projects. Requires precise targeting. |
| **Fixed Regional View** | Geostationary Orbit (GEO) | Weather monitoring, communications. Satellite is stationary over one part of the globe. |

### 📏 Understanding Satellite Data Accuracy
Accuracy varies significantly based on the measurement type. Here is what you can typically expect:

| Data Type | Typical Accuracy / Resolution | Application & Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Optical Imagery (Visual)** | **30 cm to 15+ meters** | Urban planning, infrastructure. 30 cm can show cars; 15m shows larger land features. |
| **Radar & Microwave (e.g., Soil Moisture)** | **~9 km grid resolution** | Agricultural and drought monitoring. Measures conditions like soil moisture over large areas. |
| **Precipitation Estimates** | **~25 km grid, with local bias** | Hydrology, drought forecasting. Accuracy improves when combined with local ground data. |
| **Settlement & Land Cover Mapping** | **10 meter resolution** | Urban planning and population studies. Identifies urban vs. rural areas. |
| **Elevation Models (from LiDAR/Stereo)** | **Centimeter to meter-level vertical accuracy** | Engineering, flood modeling. Accuracy depends on sensor type and flight parameters. |

> **Note on Accuracy in Africa**: Studies show that for certain data like rainfall estimates, accuracy can be lower in regions like Central Africa's Congo Basin compared to East Africa. This is mainly due to a scarcity of ground-based weather stations to calibrate the satellite algorithms.

### 🌍 Satellite Data and Angola: A Case Study
Angola serves as a practical example of how different satellite data types are applied:
*   **High-Resolution Imagery**: Commercial satellites like **Maxar's WorldView constellation** can provide 30 cm resolution images of Angolan cities like Luanda for detailed mapping.
*   **Agricultural Monitoring**: Systems use **moderate-resolution data** (like NASA's SMAP at 9km) to track soil moisture and drought conditions weekly, helping to build resilience.
*   **Precipitation Analysis**: Research using the **TRMM satellite** data in Angola found it slightly underestimated heavy rainfall, but with a standard correction (+10.5 mm), it became reliable for engineering and design purposes.

### 🔭 How to Determine Accuracy for Your Needs
If you are working on a specific project, follow these steps:
1.  **Define Your Question**: What do you need to see or measure? (e.g., "Monitor crop health in a province," "Map informal settlements in a city").
2.  **Identify the Data Type**: This determines the sensor needed (e.g., optical, radar, thermal).
3.  **Check Sensor Specifications**: Look for the **spatial resolution** (e.g., 30 cm, 10 m, 1 km) and **temporal resolution** (revisit time).
4.  **Seek Validation Studies**: For scientific use, find papers that assess a dataset's accuracy in a similar region or landscape.
