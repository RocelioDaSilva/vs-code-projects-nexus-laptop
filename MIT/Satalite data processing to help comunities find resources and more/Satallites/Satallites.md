# Satellites — clear and simple (in English)

## What is a satellite?

An **artificial satellite** is a human-made object intentionally placed into orbit around Earth (or another body). It has a **bus** (the platform: power, structure, computers, comms, thermal control) and a **payload** (the instruments — cameras, antennas, sensors).

## How do satellites stay in orbit?

A satellite is continuously falling toward Earth but also moving sideways fast enough that Earth’s curvature “drops away” beneath it. The balance between gravity pulling it inward and its forward velocity keeps it in orbit. Small thrusters or reaction wheels are used for adjustments and attitude (orientation) control.

## Common orbital types

- **LEO — Low Earth Orbit (≈160–2,000 km):** used for Earth observation, many small satellites, the ISS, and large Internet constellations.
    
- **MEO — Medium Earth Orbit (≈2,000–35,786 km):** typically used by navigation systems (e.g., GPS).
    
- **GEO — Geostationary Orbit (~35,786 km):** satellites appear fixed over one point on the equator — ideal for communications and weather satellites.
    
- **Sun-synchronous / Polar orbits:** cross each point at the same local solar time — great for consistent lighting in Earth imaging.
    
- **Highly elliptical orbits (HEO, Molniya):** give long dwell time over high latitudes or other special coverage needs.
    

## Types of satellites by purpose

- **Communications:** TV, broadband internet, phone links.
    
- **Earth observation / remote sensing:** optical cameras, radar (SAR), multispectral sensors for mapping, agriculture, disaster response.
    
- **Navigation:** GPS, GLONASS, Galileo — provide precise location and timing.
    
- **Science & exploration:** telescopes, planetary probes, space-science experiments.
    
- **CubeSats / smallsats:** compact, lower-cost platforms for education, tech demos, and constellations.
    

## Main components (short)

- **Power:** solar panels + batteries.
    
- **On-Board Computer (OBC):** mission control, data handling.
    
- **ADCS:** attitude determination & control (reaction wheels, magnetorquers, star trackers).
    
- **Communications:** radios and antennas.
    
- **Payload:** mission sensors, cameras, transponders.
    

## Launch and operation

Satellites are launched on rockets into their target orbit (sometimes via transfer orbits). Post-launch: deployment, checkout/calibration, then normal operations. At end of life, satellites may be de-orbited for controlled reentry, moved to a “graveyard” orbit (GEO), or passively left to decay.

## Current challenges & concerns

- **Space debris:** more satellites (especially mega-constellations) increase collision risk. Mitigation: planned de-orbit, better tracking, and norms for end-of-life disposal.
    
- **Interference with astronomy:** bright satellite trails and radio interference affect ground-based observations.
    
- **Regulation & coordination:** orbital slots and radio frequencies are coordinated internationally (e.g., ITU) but are increasingly contested.
    

## How to track or see satellites

Use websites and apps like Heavens-Above, N2YO, CelesTrak, or satellite-tracker apps to find passes visible from your location. Large clusters (e.g., newly launched Starlink trains) can be visible to the naked eye shortly after deployment.
